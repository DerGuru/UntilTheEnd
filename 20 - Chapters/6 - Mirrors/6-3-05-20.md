Days.

The days had a quality. Present but uninhabited. I occupied them the way furniture occupies a room: spatially, without contributing to the room's meaning. I walked. Ate when you put food in front of me. Slept when darkness suggested sleeping. The operations of a body that had been maintaining itself for millennia and saw no reason to stop now.

The guilt was not an emotion.

It was absence. A space where something had been and wasn't. Like a tooth pulled — the tongue finds the hole, the mouth reorganizes around the missing thing. The guilt was a hole in the center of my experience and everything else, perception and thought, the relentless data of being alive, flowed around the hole and left it untouched. I was a vessel with a puncture. Everything drained toward the center and vanished into the space where the question lived.

*Have I killed Wei?*

The question didn't repeat. It didn't need to. It had been asked and the asking was permanent. The permanence was the punishment. The question existed in the same way that the evidence of my power existed. Retroactively, immune to revision. It had always been asked. It would always be asked. The asking was a feature of my landscape now, like the metallic taste or the teeth-vibration. Except that the vibration was silent and the only thing remaining was the question and the hole.

You cooked.

You cooked with the determined precision of a man who had decided that the world's most essential response to catastrophe was rice. You made it three times a day. You seasoned it with whatever you found on the trailside. Not those herbs. Different ones. You maintained the domestic protocol with the quiet heroism of someone who understood that normalcy was necessary.

You talked about weather.

"Rain tonight," you'd say. Or: "The wind's shifted east." Saying things that didn't matter was a way of filling the silence with sound that didn't wound.

You counted provisions.

"Three days of rice. Five of dried meat. We'll need water tomorrow."

The logistics of survival. The practical, material requirements of two bodies continuing to exist in a world that required food and shelter, all the mundane inputs that consciousness couldn't override. Or could it? Could I believe us fed? Could I believe the rice replenished, the provisions permanent?

Could I believe Wei alive?

The thought arrived and the hole received it, unchanged. The walking continued.

The anomalies stopped.

This was the detail that frightened me more than the guilt itself. Since the question — since the asking, since the night on my knees with your hand on my back — the anomalies had stopped. No trees appearing. No retroactive insertions of things that had always been there into positions they'd never occupied.

The belief-engine had frozen. The grief and the guilt had overwhelmed the process, had flooded it with a conviction so total that no other conviction could operate alongside it.

The sect on the ridge waited.

They were still there, distant and patient, the hundred and twelve figures maintaining their parallel path. But the feeding had stopped. The anomaly-pulses that had been nourishing their cultivation had dried up. The source was frozen. The farm was fallow.

I noticed them the way I noticed everything. Present but uninhabited. Data received but not processed. They were on the ridge, waiting. They would continue to wait. The information was accurate and irrelevant.

I combed my hair.

The gesture was — I didn't know where it came from. I sat by the dead fire and pulled my fingers through the strands and counted. One hundred. Two hundred. Three hundred. The counting was the point. The mechanical, repetitive action of assigning numbers to things. A practice of control where control had become meaningless.

You watched me count. You didn't intervene. Didn't offer comb or comment. You recognized it — the body's attempt to handle what the mind couldn't.

Four hundred.

I stopped. The stopping was as arbitrary as the counting and the arbitrariness was the point.

I put my hands in my lap. The hands that shaped reality. The hands that might have shaped Wei's death.

The hole received the thought. The hole was unchanged.

You started a fire. The physical way — flint and patience. The mortal method. The method that didn't require belief or the kind of certainty that I was no longer capable of producing.

The fire caught.

We sat.

The silence between us was not empty. It was full — full of the question and the guilt, all the things that mattered and didn't matter, living side by side in the space between two people who had nothing to say and everything to carry.
