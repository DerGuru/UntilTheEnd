You were sewing your pack.

The strap had torn during the approach. The moment when I'd sent you back and you'd gone reluctantly, you had gripped it with strength that exceeded the strap's engineering. The leather had split. A clean tear, diagonal, the failure line of material subjected to stress it wasn't made for.

You sat by the fire with a needle and thread and the careful, focused expression of a man performing surgery on luggage. The needle went in and out. The thread pulled. The strap's edges came together under your hands.

I also sat by the fire, scratching Xiǎohú behind her ears and I felt — no pressure.

The absence was strange. Since I met Wei, there had always been something — some external pressure that gave shape to the internal process. That had pushed. Now, there was nothing. The sect was no more. The anomalies had quieted. The wall was transparent. Every external pressures had been removed and what remained was the internal landscape, unobstructed, unpressured.

Just me, the question and a man sewing his pack.

You finished your repairs and glanced at me sideways. A glance that said "You're sitting there doing nothing." You held out the needle and a length of thread. I took them without thinking. My hands found my sleeve and the tear at the elbow that I'd been ignoring for a while.

I could have closed the tear without thread. A thought, a moment of conviction and the fabric would knit itself — like I had dried my clothes after the river. But that somehow felt like cheating. The act of pushing steel through cloth felt more honest. Also the needle was already in my hand and the thread was already cut. Go for it.

In absence of more scritches, Xiǎohú decided that it was time for a little hunting and took to the underbrush.

I hadn't sewn in — I didn't know how long. Decades. Centuries. The action was not in my recent inventory of behaviors. It belonged to an earlier version — a version that performed domestic tasks, that maintained clothing, that engaged with the physical world through the physical operations of repair and maintenance. A version that had existed before the walking became permanent which had made maintenance meaningless.

The needle went in and out. The thread pulled. The tear's edges met. I was repairing.

Repairing. 

Fixing what was torn. Closing what was open. The domestic version of what I'd been trying to do to myself — close the gap, repair the tear, fix the thing that was broken. Except that sewing a sleeve was simple. You saw the tear. You had thread. You put the thread through the fabric and pulled. The fabric came together. Done.

Why couldn't the self be sewn?

The question was absurd, which was kind of an answer in itself. Selves aren't fabric. Beliefs aren't tears. The needle that closes cloth doesn't reach inside to the level where convictions live. The metaphor was seductive but wrong.

Yet the sewing was real. The thread was real. The repaired sleeve was real. And the experience of repairing — the physical engagement with a broken thing, the hands performing the operations of fixing, the quiet satisfaction of edges meeting — was, itself, something.

I was productive. Without deciding to be.

I'd worked on your meridians that morning. The routine had shifted — shorter sessions now, less resistance, the channels holding their shape between cleanings instead of collapsing back. Your breathing was different afterward. Deeper. The kind of breathing a body does when something that was pinched has been unpinched.

You sewed. I sewed. The fire cracked. Xiǎohú came back and napped at the fire's edge, curled tight, eyes closed. The afternoon was warm, quiet and domestic. And the domesticity was not a distraction from the work but the work itself.

"There's a story they tell in the lowlands," you said. Not looking up. The voice of a man filling silence the way you filled your notebook — methodically, without urgency. "About a woman who made a hundred warriors vanish. Swallowed their souls, supposedly. Consumed them. They say she opened her mouth and the soldiers disappeared into her."

The needle stopped in my hand.

"Who tells this?" I said.

"Traders. Innkeepers. The version varies. In one, she's ten feet tall. In another, she has silver eyes. In all of them, she consumes."

I pulled the thread through the fabric. The stitch was tight. Too tight.

"I moved them," I said. "Not consumed. Moved. From one place to another. Most of them anyways. And there was no mouth involved. No swallowing. No souls."

"Besides." I continued. "It was yesterday and I saw no other witnesses."

You looked at me. The long, still look of a man who had stopped sewing.

"The story I was talking about is over five hundred years old," you said. Quietly. 

"Oh." I recognized my mistake.

"I guess it was a border dispute then. One of many." I said. "Two villages. Maybe sixty men between them, not a hundred. They had farming tools, not swords. I darkened the sky over the valley and created shadows around them and me, thick enough to blind. Then wind. A column of it, strong enough to lift a man off the ground."

The needle went through the fabric. The stitch was steady.

"I dropped them across the countryside. Some of them over a fair distance." I paused. "They were farmers. They weren't cultivators. They didn't know how to land."

The fire cracked. You said nothing.

"Not all of them died," I said. "But enough. Enough for a story about a woman who swallows souls."

The fire cracked. You held the needle in your hand, forgotten. The leather strap hung between your fingers.

"You're telling me," you said.

"Yes, that was me, too. Unless there was another woman who really swallowed souls centuries ago."

You returned to your pack. The needle went in and out. The leather strap's edges continued their reconciliation. Your face was the face of a man who had carried a theory for years and was hearing it confirmed not with evidence but with a voice that said *I* and meant it.

The fire cracked. Xiǎohú slept. I sewed my sleeve and listened to the afternoon. 

I wondered how many versions of me were being told around how many fires and how many of them involved consuming and mouths and ten-foot women with silver eyes. And how many of those stories had started the same way — with a woman who thought she was solving a problem and found out afterward what the solution had cost.
