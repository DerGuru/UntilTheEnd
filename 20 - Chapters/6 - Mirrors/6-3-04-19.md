The memory broke through that night.

This was whole. This was complete. This was Wei's death in its entirety, delivered with the full clarity of a wall that had lost the fight.

The self-protection didn't intervene. Perhaps it couldn't. Perhaps the question had weakened it more than the creation memories, more than the convergence, more than anything. Perhaps the question was the thing the wall had been built to prevent and asking it had broken the mechanism and the memory was the flood that the broken mechanism could no longer contain.

Wei died at the settlement gate.

The settlement's eastern gate, wooden and weathered.

He was standing. I hadn't seen him standing — I'd been on the west wall, back turned, drowning in qi that wasn't mine. The Hermit had flooded everything. The entire settlement saturated, every sense overwritten, every subtle signal buried under the sheer volume of a presence that treated the battlefield like a pond and filled it to the brim. I couldn't feel Wei's core through that. Couldn't feel anything through that. But the bootprints told me where he'd stood. Shen told me how it happened and I knew Wei well enough to fill the space between the words, the evidence and images. He stood stubborn and upright. The posture of a child who had decided to stand and would not be persuaded by anything as trivial as the physics of violence. The cultivator's technique hit him above the collarbone. Shen saw the impact. He saw the force travel through Wei's body. And Shen saw, how Wei fell.

The falling took an amount of time but barely any. The duration of a fall is proportional to height and Wei was not tall. 

He hit the ground.

I didn't hear the sound. I was on the west wall. The Hermit's qi was still everywhere — thick, suffocating, a flood that made the air itself opaque to sensing.

But his core screamed.

The scream cut through the saturation — through the Hermit's flood, through the interference, through the noise of a battle I'd already lost control of. His core's scream was not a sound. It was a sensation. The feeling of something small and bright tearing itself apart at a distance I couldn't close in time.

I ran.

The saturation thinned. Sensing returned in fragments — the wall, the gate, the field beyond. My feet found the steps. Found the ground. Found the direction that the scream had come from.

He was not breathing when I reached him.

His eyes were open. The dark, sharp, too-intelligent eyes that had screamed *help me* from a forest path — the first words, the first sound, the voice that had arrived in my life as a demand and never stopped demanding. The eyes that saw everything and forgave nothing. The eyes that were seeing nothing now. Fixed. Still. The interior landscape already departed, the view already empty, the operations already concluded.

"Wei."

His name in my mouth. The taste of it. Too late.

He smelled like blood. Like herbs, the sharp ones, too much of them, the ones he always used too much of. Like earth. The three smells combined into a scent that was specifically, irreducibly his. The olfactory signature of a child who had lived briefly but loudly and stubbornly in a world that had not been designed for children like him.

His face was too young. That was the detail that the memory insisted on, the detail that the memory carried with the weight of a physical object. Too young. The face hadn't finished becoming what it was going to become. The jaw was still forming. The cheekbones were still emerging. The architecture of the adult he would have been was visible in the scaffolding of the child he was.

He was quiet.

That was wrong. Wei was never quiet. Wei was noise. Constant, irritating, relentless noise. Opinions, objections and questions and the unending commentary of a child who experienced the world at a volume that matched his conviction. That the world owed him an explanation for everything it did. Wei was the loudest thing in any room, any forest, any conversation.

He was quiet now. Had been quiet before I arrived. And the quiet had started without me.

I tried to heal him. Or was I trying to bring him back?

The belief. Whatever it was, the thing that had moved stones and redirected water and shifted thirty cultivators and warmed tea. I wanted him to be alive. Whole. That he was breathing with his body intact and the damage repaired. That he stand and walk and be loud again.

I wanted it with everything I had.

It didn't work.

I hadn't known about belief or mechanism or the architecture beneath. I'd just wanted. Wanted with the full force of a consciousness that had spent millennia getting what it wanted without knowing how.

And now — now, in this clearing, with the memory finally whole — I could see what had been underneath the wanting. The bedrock conviction. The foundational certainty that lived beneath all other certainties, beneath the conscious beliefs, beneath the accessible thoughts, in the basement of the basement of the structure I'd built over millennia.

*Everything I love dies.*

The belief had won.

I knelt at the gate. My hands on him. His body, still warm, still present, still occupying the space that a living body occupies. But empty. The emptiness of a container whose contents have departed. The warmth already leaving, already cooling toward the temperature of the earth beneath him.

I tried again. Wanted it again. Meant it again. Threw everything I had at the wanting, every ounce of desperation, every particle of refusal.

Nothing.

I hadn't understood then. Now I did. You cannot override bedrock with surface. You cannot contradict the foundation by repainting the walls. The deep belief held. Had always held. Was the oldest thing in me, older than memory, older than the wall, older than the walking and the forests and the centuries.

Wei was dead.

And maybe — maybe — my belief had killed him.

My mind came back to the clearing. The fire. The night. The present.

I was on my knees. The grass was wet with dew. My face was dry. No tears. Grief required understanding and I didn't understand.

You were beside me. Your hand on my back. Resting, only resting. The weight of a human hand. The warmth of a mortal palm. The anchor.

Xiǎohú pressed against my knee. The warm, solid weight of a small body choosing proximity. She didn't look up. Didn't demand anything. Just leaned, the way a creature leans against something it has decided belongs to it, and stayed.

The metallic taste was gone. The teeth-vibration was silent. For the first time since the teeth-vibration started, the hum had stopped. As if whatever it was, the thing that seemed to shape reality, had frozen. Locked. Seized up on the question it couldn't answer.

I rubbed my arms. The gesture mechanical. Back and forth, back and forth, the self-soothing motion of someone who had run out of more sophisticated coping strategies. The metallic taste was bitter now. Everything was bitter.

Your hand stayed on my back.

You said nothing.

There was nothing to say.
