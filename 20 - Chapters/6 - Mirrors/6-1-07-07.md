Over the next three days the changes accelerated. Three days of trees appearing, streams rerouting and stone formations emerging from hillsides with the full authority of geological time compressed into a night's sleep. Three days of you remembering each change as permanent, as original, as the way things had always been. Three days of the sect on the ridge, watching, feeding, growing stronger with each pulse of reality-edit that I couldn't control, couldn't stop and didn't understand.

Three days of calmness.

It frightened me more than the changes themselves, but it persisted. The deep, inexplicable settling in the space behind the crack, the hands-in-light quiet that had replaced the Thought-Echoes' noise. I walked through a world that was rewriting itself around me and I felt — at peace. As if the changes were natural. As if the retroactive editing of reality was something that should have been happening all along and the only thing unusual was that it had taken this long to start.

That thought should have triggered the wall.

It didn't.

On the fourth morning, you unrolled a map. One of your maps — hand-drawn, meticulous, annotated in your small, precise script. You'd been recording the terrain with the compulsive accuracy of a man who needed to know where he was in order to know who he was.

"Wrong," you said, looking at it.

"What?"

"The map. It's wrong. I must have made a mistake. The trail I drew last week — it doesn't match the trail we walked yesterday. The creek is in a different place. The elevation changes are — look."

You held the map toward me. I looked. The lines you'd drawn — careful, accurate, drawn from direct observation by a man who prided himself on direct observation — didn't match the landscape visible from where we sat. The creek had moved. The ridge had shifted. A valley that your map placed to the east had now moved southward.

But they matched my memories of them. The changes had rewritten what your map conserved.

The ink was where you'd placed it. But the reality it described had moved. The map was now a historical document from six days ago, describing a landscape that had retroactively never been.

You stared at the map. The staring had a quality I recognized — the stillness of someone whose instruments had failed. The map. The objective record. The thing that was supposed to be true regardless of memory, regardless of perception, regardless of the treacherous subjectivity of human experience.

"It's changing faster," I said.

"Yes."

"We can't stay here."

You looked at me. "Where would we go?"

"Forward. South. Toward whatever this is leading to."

"You think it's leading somewhere?"

I didn't think. I felt it. The calm felt it. The images pressed toward it. The metallic taste, the teeth-vibration and the hands in the light. The crack in the wall and its silence. Xiǎohú's tracking and the bird's new song — all of it pointed the same direction. The slow approach of something meeting something else, drawn together by a force that didn't ask permission.

I was approaching, what the wall had hidden. 

"We can't prepare for this," I said. "There's nothing to prepare."

"Then what?"

"We move. We go toward it. Because standing still is just letting them farm the changes while whatever's coming takes its time arriving."

You folded the map. Careful, precise, along the original creases.

"The sect," you said.

"Will follow."

"The anomalies."

"Will continue."

"Your wall."

"Dormant."

You stored the map. Stood and looked south, toward the mountains that rose beyond our valley — higher, steeper, older. The direction Xiǎohú had been pulling since yesterday, straining at the invisible boundary of her orbit like a compass needle pointing at something more fundamental than north.

"You know where you're going?" you asked.

"I know where I'm being pulled."

"Same thing?"

"I don't know yet."

You shouldered your pack. Adjusted. Checked the knife at your belt.

"Alright," you said. "South it is."

Xiǎohú was already moving. She had turned south before I'd said the word, before you had agreed, before the decision had been made in any way that a decision could be observed. She knew. Like animals know weather or the way water knows downhill. The way the creating hands in the images had known exactly what they were making.

We walked. South. The trail rearranged itself ahead of us — subtly. The path opened, the terrain smoothed, the route clarified itself with each step as if the mountain itself was agreeing to let us through.

Or leading us.

Behind us, the sect followed. A hundred and twelve figures moving parallel to our path, maintaining distance, maintaining patience, maintaining the careful, sustainable harvest of power that my existence provided.

In my mouth, the metallic taste intensified. In my teeth, the vibration hummed. In my chest, behind the crack, in the space where the wall was thinning, the images pulsed — hands, light, creating — quiet and constant and absolutely certain.

Forward.

We moved.
