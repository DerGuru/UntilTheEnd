Gone in a blink. The absolute gone of something that had been here and was now not-here anymore, with no transition between the states.

Thirty cultivators. One moment in the square. The next moment not.

Their absence was louder than their presence.

The fires still burned. The smoke still rose. The child still cried. The world continued its physical operations with perfect indifference to the fact that thirty people had just been removed from it. The flames didn't notice. The smoke didn't adjust. 

But the square was empty.

I stood in it. You stood beside me. The villagers stood in their doorways and behind their walls and in the spaces where they'd been when the cultivators had arrived. Nobody moved. Nobody spoke. The silence had the weight of understanding — the collective recognition that something had happened that shouldn't have happened, couldn't happen, but had happened.

The fires still burned.

I looked at them. The nearest one was eating the wall of a house — someone's house, someone's kitchen, someone's morning. The flame was orange and indifferent to the people's home it was consuming.

*The fire is out.*

Not a wish. Not a command. Not a manipulation of temperature or oxygen or any of the physical mechanisms by which fire is extinguished. A statement. The same kind of statement that had moved thirty cultivators away.

The fire went out. All of them. Every flame in the square, simultaneously, as though they had never been lit. The smoke stopped. The heat withdrew. The coals went dark.

The house was still damaged. Blackened beams. A collapsed wall. The scorch marks like handwriting on stone.

*The wall is standing. The house is whole.*

The wall was standing. Had always been standing. The scorch marks faded — not disappeared, faded — the way a bruise fades, the way a memory softens, the way reality absorbs an edit and smooths its edges until the seam barely shows. The beams straightened. The thatch resettled. Not perfect. Not new. But whole.

I did this for each house. One by one. The stating, the believing, the quiet revision. Each correction cost something — not pain, not effort, but a thinning. The world's patience wearing down by a fraction each time I asked it to accept another change.

Somewhere between the second house and the third, a thought arrived. Every revision fed the sect's instruments. 

So be it.

A woman came out of a doorway with a flour-stained apron. She looked at her wall. Touched it. Her fingers traced the place where the scorch mark had been, finding only stone and wood, cool and solid. Unburned.

She looked at me. The fear was still there. But underneath it — gratitude. The complicated gratitude of someone who is thankful and terrified in equal measure and doesn't know which feeling to offer first.

She bowed. Low. I didn't bow back. I didn't know how to receive that particular combination of fear and thanks. I nodded, turned away and left the village.

I found the cultivators two hundred meters away, in the forest, scattered among the trees like leaves after a storm. Disoriented. Some standing, some kneeling, some on the ground. One was vomiting. Another was touching his own face with the slow, wondering investigation of someone who wasn't sure it was still there.

They weren't hurt. I hadn't attacked them. I had believed them elsewhere and they were elsewhere and the believing was all it took.

The vomiting cultivator was on all fours. I could feel his confusion. I did not empathize, not with any emotional resonance. His confusion was a feature of his current state, as measurable as his body temperature or his heart rate. He didn't know what had happened. He didn't know where he was. He had been in a village square and now he was in a forest. The gap between the two locations was not a journey but a revision.

I looked at my hands.

They were steady. Whatever I'd done hadn't required force. Hadn't required effort. Only the belief — the complete, absolute belief that thirty people were not where they appeared to be.

Conviction, not will.

The distinction crystallized. I'd been pushing against things my whole life — pushing against the wall, pushing against the erasure, pushing against the world's insistence on being one way when I needed it to be another. Pushing was will. Pushing was effort. Pushing was the paradigm. Apply force, overcome resistance, achieve change.

This wasn't pushing. This was stating.

*They are not here.* A fact. The same kind of fact as *the sun rises* or *water flows downhill*. Except that this fact hadn't existed until I believed it and the moment I believed it, it became retroactively, permanently, unquestionably true.

I was the thing that had made thirty people disappear.

I was the thing that could make anyone disappear.

I was the thing that reality rearranged itself to accommodate.

We left. Xiǎohú first, then me, then you. The order was unplanned — instinctive. The animal leading, the anomaly following, the human guarding the rear. We walked away from the cultivators the way you walk out of a room where something has broken — quickly, without looking back, without checking whether the pieces could be reassembled.

The water from the village's well would taste bitter for a week. Not because of contamination or any physical or chemical process that could be identified and corrected. Because the world was adjusting. Pushing back. The subtle, cumulative cost of reality being edited by someone who hadn't asked reality's permission.

Bitter water. And the taste of metal in a woman's well.

I'd saved them. And they would never forgive me for it.
