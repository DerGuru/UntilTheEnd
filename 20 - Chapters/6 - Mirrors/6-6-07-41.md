The rice burned. You'd been distracted — notebook open, the pen moving with the urgency of a man who needed to capture something before it evaporated from your memory. You'd positioned the pot wrong, or the fire had shifted, or the universe had arranged a minor domestic catastrophe for reasons that defied the effort you'd put into preventing exactly this.

I smelled it. The acrid, caramelized-then-charred scent of starch meeting too much heat for too long. The pot, when you pulled it off the fire with the cloth-wrapped handle, had the telltale dark crust at the bottom — still edible, but definitively burned. The kind of burned that announced itself in every bite.

"Zāo Gāo!" you exclaimed, staring into the pot with the expression of a man who had committed an error of calibration and suffered a localized reduction in pride — which was objectively funny.

Still I did not laugh.

I looked at the burned rice and I was in a different place.

Wei's kitchen. Our kitchen — the small room in the house we shared. Wei, crouching, holding a bowl of rice that was burned worse than yours — darker, crustier, the catastrophe that happened when a fourteen-year-old boy didn't pay attention and was stoking the flames to hot.

His grin. That grin — the one that took up too much of his face, the one that was disproportionate to whatever had prompted it, the one that made you want to either grin back.

He ate a bite. Chewed. The crunch was audible.

"Not bad," he said.

"It's burned."

"It's crunchy."

He pushed a bowl toward me. A bowl of burned rice. Pushed with both hands, the gesture that was somewhere between offering and challenge, the physical language of a boy who expressed everything through motion because words were too slow.

"Be quiet," he said.

Not mean. Not dismissive. The be quiet that meant that I should stop analyzing, stop evaluating, stop being the person who identifies the flaw before experiencing the thing. Stop thinking. Eat.

I'd eaten it.

The memory was complete. Full. Every detail preserved in the amber of an immortal memory that had kept this moment perfect while time dissolved around it. His hands. The bowl. The grin. The crunch. The particular quality of silence that existed between a boy who had burned rice and a woman who was eating it.

Wei had never tried to be anything but himself.

The recognition arrived and sat. Not the philosophical recognition at the river — the personal, exact, gut-level recognition of looking at a dead boy and seeing the thing you've been trying to understand reflected in someone who understood it without trying.

Wei had never gripped. Never held. Never willed himself into a shape. He was stubborn — he was so stubborn, the most stubborn person I'd encountered in any era, the kind of stubbornness that bent reality through sheer refusal to bend itself — but the stubbornness was not will. It was nature. He was stubborn the way the river was wet. Existing as a stubborn thing, naturally, the way things are what they are without deciding.

He had just been.

Alive. Present. Unanalyzed. A boy who burned rice, ate it anyway, grinned and said be quiet. And meant it. And he didn't ever, not once, in all the time I knew him, ask what he should be. He was what he was. The water flowing because flowing was its nature. The open hand that didn't know it was open because it had never been a fist.

That was what I had to learn.

The practice. The lived, bodily, daily practice of being what you are without gripping yourself into shape. Wei knew this. Wei had known this at fourteen. Wei had known this while burning rice. Or maybe he didn't and just didn't care to anything else. The result was the same.

And Wei was dead.

And the letting-go might show me why.

The question, whether my belief killed him, was still open, still sitting in the space between me and the truth like a stone in a river that the water flowed around but could not move.

"You're smiling," you said.

I looked at you. The burned pot in your hand. The rice, salvageable. The evening, ordinary.

"No," I said, surprised by myself.

But I was. The way the reflection in the river had smiled — with something underneath.

I was smiling because the universe, despite everything, despite eons and loss and the unbearable weight of immortality, still contained moments where someone burned the rice and looked at you with that expression.

"Eat," I said.

You served. The burned parts were distributed without comment — some to your bowl, some to mine, the democratic allocation of failure. We ate. The crunch was there, the texture-sound of burned starch and it was terrible and it was fine.

Xiǎohú appeared from wherever she had been and sat near the fire. She looked at the pot and sneezed.
