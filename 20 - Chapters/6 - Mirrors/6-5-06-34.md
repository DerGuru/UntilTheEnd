The day had become night and you were asleep.

You came back shortly after dusk — pack heavier than when you'd left, expression closed. No explanation offered. None requested. You'd built the fire, eaten something from your pack without offering conversation, and shut down with the efficiency of someone who'd found what they were looking for and needed time to absorb it.

Good. What I was about to do needed to happen without audience. It was an experiment and experiments conducted under observation produce different results than experiments conducted alone.

I picked up the knife.

Your knife. Small. Practical. The blade kept sharp because you maintained your tools the way you maintained everything. Carefully, regularly, as an expression of the principle that the condition of your instruments reflected the condition of your thinking.

I didn't hesitate.

Hesitation was for people who weren't sure what they were testing. I was sure. The hypothesis was clear: *My belief in my own invulnerability operates automatically, involuntarily, continuously. This belief will prevent the wound from persisting. The wound will close. The blood will vanish. The skin will be unmarked.*

I drew the blade across my left index finger.

Pain.

The pain was real — sharp, immediate, the honest signal of nerve endings encountering an edge. My hand flinched. Since the wall of numbness had opened, everything arrived unfiltered. The blade's edge. The skin's resistance. The moment when resistance failed and the blade entered and the blood began.

Blood.

Red. Warm. The red of arterial blood. Bright, oxygenated, the color that painters can never quite reproduce because blood's red is a living red, a red that comes from chemistry and motion.

I watched.

The wound closed while I watched. The skin's edges moved. Found each other and joined. The gap that the blade had opened sealed. Not scarred, not healed, SEALED. As if the wound had been an error and the error was being corrected. The blood on my finger thinned, paled, vanished. Absorbed back through the skin that was no longer broken.

My finger was perfect. Unmarked. The same finger it had been before the cut. The same lines, the same nail, the same unremarkable anatomy of a human digit that had never been cut. Retroactively undone.

I hadn't healed. I'd been un-wounded. The distinction was — I sat with it, felt its edges, understood its implications. Healing was a process. What my body did was not a process — it was a revision. The wound had existed and then had never existed. The blood had appeared and then had never appeared. My body didn't repair damage — it denied damage with the same absolutism that denied trees their original positions, that denied water its original direction and reality its original configuration.

I couldn't stop being immortal.

The cliff had told me. The flame had told me. But the knife made it undeniable. The moment of closing flesh and vanishing blood demonstrated, with brutal clarity, that my immortality was not a status I maintained but a condition I was.

Not doing. Being.

You don't change what you are by wanting to change what you are. You don't become mortal by willing mortality. You don't override bedrock by painting the surface. The belief was not in my mind — it was in my structure. In the architecture. In the foundation that preceded thought and exceeded will and constituted the self that thought and willed.

I held the knife and took a closer look. The blade was clean. No blood, because the blood had never existed. The knife was the same knife it had been before I'd used it, because my body had retroactively denied the use.

I put the knife down.

The gesture was finished. Finished with this experiment. Finished with the hypothesis and its confirmation. Finished with the physical testing of a truth that didn't need tests because the truth was older than the tools used to test it.

I went to the stream.

In the dark, by starlight, I knelt and washed my hands. The water was cold, biting my skin, always biting now, the cold that the crack had given me. I washed the blood from my finger. The blood that wasn't there. I washed anyway, the way you wash after contact with something that leaves no visible residue but changes you nonetheless.

The washing was ritual. Not sanitation. The hands-in-water gesture of someone who needed the water to carry something away. Not blood. The knowledge. The certainty. The confirmed, demonstrated, experimentally verified understanding that she could not stop being what she was.

The frustration was different this time.

Not the empty frustration of the early days, the hollow, post-guilt numbness that had frozen the engine and stopped the anomalies. This frustration was hot. Burning. The focused frustration of someone who had been given a puzzle and had tried every piece and every piece fit everywhere except the place it needed to fit.

I knew everything — what I was, how it worked, why it worked, when it started, what maintained it. I had the complete map. I had the full diagnosis. I had every piece of information that should have been sufficient to produce change.

And nothing changed.

Knowing wasn't enough.

Understanding wasn't enough.

The gap between comprehending and transforming was infinite — wider than the cliff, hotter than the flame, sharper than the knife. The gap said that I could know myself completely and still be unable to change what I knew. Self-awareness is not self-transformation. The map is not the territory.

I washed my hands in the stream and the water carried nothing away because there was nothing to carry. I knew this and I washed anyway.

Morning came. You found me at the stream. Still awake. Wet to the elbows. Dark circles under my eyes — the evidence of a night spent at the intersection of knowing and not-being-able-to.

"Have you slept at all?" you said.

"No."

You nodded. You'd known. Or guessed. The same way you knew everything — pattern recognition, logical inference.

You sat beside me. The stream ran past us. Morning light. The quality of mountain dawn. Clear, cold, honest.

"If knowing isn't enough," I said. "What is?"

You didn't answer. The question wasn't for you. It was for the stream and the morning and the woman sitting in both.

You sat beside me and let the stream be loud enough for both of us.
