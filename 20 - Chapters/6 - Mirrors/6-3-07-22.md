Morning. Weight on my chest. Warm, slight, deliberate. I opened my eyes to amber ones — Xiǎohú's muzzle resting between my collarbones, her body curled against my ribs. She hadn't slept there before. Not like this. Not with the full commitment of a creature choosing proximity over distance.

I breathed. She breathed. The rhythm was synchronized in a way that suggested she had matched hers to mine sometime during the night.

I reached up and scratched her ears.

You stirred. A single breath drawn slightly too deep, held for a fraction, then released as your body remembered where it was and what it was doing there. Your eyes opened and found me, found Xiǎohú on my chest. Something crossed your face — acknowledgment. The kind of expression that said *noted* without needing to say it aloud.

We ate the leftovers in silence before you started packing. The methodical, precise process of reducing a campsite to a carried load — bedrolls, provisions, fire. You moved through the routine with the efficiency of a man who had made and broken camp a thousand times and considered the making and breaking a form of prayer. Each object in its place. Each strap at its tension. Each item accounted for and secured.

I finished packing my stuff and stood by the dead fire.

"So," you said. Adjusting a strap. "Did you?"

The question was careful.

I shook my head.

Not *no*. The headshake was not a negative. It was something else — the physical expression of a cognitive state that existed between yes and no, between knowing and not-knowing, between the answer and the absence of an answer.

"I don't know," I said.

The third option — the one that stories don't prepare you for.

*I don't know.*

I let the question stay open.

The realization was quiet. A settling. Some questions are not meant to be answered. The asking is what matters.

The first act of letting go.

Letting go of the answer. The need for it. The demand that the universe produce a verdict.

The universe owed me nothing.

The ambiguity was stronger than certainty. I knew this now — knew it the way I knew belief shaped reality, the way I knew the stone was there because I believed it was there. Certainty was brittle. Certainty could be broken by new evidence, new perspective, new understanding. Ambiguity was flexible. It contained every possible answer and excluded none.

I picked up my pack.

The pack's weight was familiar — the physical, measurable, gravitational weight of objects. Not belief-weight. Not question-weight. Real weight. The kind that could be distributed, accommodated and carried.

I carried it.

We walked.

South. The trail was not opening anymore — it was just a trail. The terrain was cooperating the way terrain cooperates when no one is shaping it. Imperfectly, with rocks and roots and the normal obstacles that paths produce when paths are left to be paths. The belief-engine was still frozen. Still seized on the question. Still unable to produce the casual, unconscious edits that had been my signature.

But I was walking. We were walking.

It felt different from the walking of the previous days. Those had been automatic — the body in motion because motion was the default, the legs moving because legs had always moved, the forward progress a mechanical consequence of not-stopping rather than a deliberate choice to continue. 

That walking had been inertia, but this walking was decision.

I decided to walk. Decided to carry the pack and the question. To carry the ambiguity, the guilt, the memory and the maybe. I had decided that walking was what I could do and that doing what I could do was enough. Even if enough was not the same as sufficient. But it was better than nothing. And nothing was the only alternative.

Xiǎohú walked beside me. Her ears barely reached my knee, her fur warm against my calf when the trail narrowed. She had changed position. Since the anomalies had stopped and the world had stopped being shaped by my unconscious certainties. Xiǎohú had moved closer. As if my diminishment had made me approachable. As if the silence in my machinery had created space for companionship that the machinery's noise had previously filled.

I walked. The question hung in the air behind me — invisible, permanent, the weight of maybe trailing like a shadow that the sun couldn't eliminate.

You walked behind. Three steps. Your distance. Your place in the formation.

"South?" you asked.

"South," I confirmed.

The mountains rose. The trail climbed. The world continued, imperfect and unshaped and ordinary and I continued with it.

The question followed.

It would always follow.

That was alright.
