I was certain that the tree hadn't been there yesterday. As certain as I was of anything now. Which was less certain than it used to be but still certain enough. We'd camped at a clearing's edge, where the trail curved around a granite outcrop and the ground flattened into a natural circle of packed earth and sparse grass. The clearing had held our fire, our packs, our sleep. The clearing had not held a tree.

But now there was a tree.

An old jujube — thick trunk, weathered bark, the kind of age you measure in centuries rather than decades. Its roots had penetrated the packed earth with the confidence of something that had been growing there since before the trail existed. Its branches spread wide, casting shadow across half the clearing and its leaves were the deep green of late summer, each one veined, particular and individual.

I stared at it the way you stare at a sentence that has appeared in a manuscript you thought you'd finished. Simply there, with the quiet authority of permanence.

"We should eat," you said. You were sitting on one of the tree's roots, your back against the trunk, the morning light filtered through its canopy. Your notebook was closed on your knee. You looked comfortable. You looked like a man who had been sitting under this tree every morning of his life.

"That tree."

"What about it?"

"It wasn't here yesterday."

You looked up at it the way someone looks up at a ceiling. Mildly, acknowledging its presence without questioning it. The tree provided shade. The shade was welcome. The shade existed.

"It's obviously been here for years," you said.

*Obviously*.

The word carried complete sincerity. The rewriting had finally reached you. For you, the tree had been there. Had always been there. As natural and unremarkable as the granite outcrop, the packed earth, the sparse grass. Your memory contained the tree. Your reality contained the tree. Your morning had begun with the tree.

Mine hadn't.

One of us was wrong. I was wrong, or reality was wrong, or the distinction between the two had stopped mattering.

I walked to the trunk. Touched the bark. It was real. Rough under my fingers, warm from the morning sun, textured with the hundreds of small irregularities that genuine age produces. I pressed my palm flat against it. The vibration in my teeth shifted, altered in quality rather than strength, as if the frequency had adjusted to accommodate a new variable.

The tree was a fact. Verifiable by every sense I had learned to distrust but couldn't stop using.

"How long?" I said. "How long has it been here?"

You looked at me with the patience of a man who had learned not to answer rhetorical questions from people who were having a crisis. "You planted it," you said.

"I didn't."

"Spring before last. After the rain."

A pause. You kept looking at the trunk — the thick, weathered, centuries-old trunk. The pause lasted one breath. Then it closed, and you believed it again.

I could see the memory in your face — my hands in the dirt, the sapling, the rain. The memory was real. And it was about me doing something I had never done.

I stepped back from the tree.

The morning continued around us with perfect indifference. Birds. Wind. Xiǎohú stretched in a patch of sun, her fur catching light that the tree's shadow didn't reach. Everything was normal. Everything was fine. A tree had appeared overnight and reality had rewritten itself to accommodate its presence, retroactively inserting centuries of growth.

Everything was fine.

We'd been walking for an hour, south along the trail, toward the ridge where the path narrowed and the elevation climbed.

The stone at the trail's bend had turned. Like a cup someone had rotated to face a different direction. The moss grew on the sun-facing side. Yellowing, sun-bleached, dying the slow death of something exposed to conditions it should never have met. Moss grows in shade. This moss was in full morning light and had been long enough to start dying but not long enough to be dead. The weathering was wrong too: rain erosion on the sheltered face, smooth stone on the exposed one. The stone's history disagreed with its position.

Something must have rotated it. And then reality had forgotten the rotation.

I knelt and touched the stone. Cool, solid and factual. The earth around its base was undisturbed. Consistent with a stone that had been sitting in exactly this position for exactly as long as stones sit in positions before weather, time and erosion take them elsewhere.

The vibration in my teeth intensified.

I stood. Looked back at the trail behind me, the trail ahead. The tree in the clearing. The stone at the bend. Two things wrong in one morning, each one carrying the full weight of a past that hadn't happened.

Xiǎohú padded past the stone without looking at it. Her eyes tracked something I couldn't see — something above the treeline, something in the air, something invisible and present and moving through the mountain with the same quiet, retroactive certainty as the tree and the stone.

She tracked something that wasn't there.

Or something that was there now.
