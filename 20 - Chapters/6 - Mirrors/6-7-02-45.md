 The tree was old. Older than you, younger than me — a comparison that applied to most things, a taxonomy so broad it was functionally useless. Old in the tree-sense. The trunk was wider than two men standing shoulder to shoulder, bark ridged deep enough to lose a finger in. Thick-trunked, root-tangled, the kind of tree that had been growing long enough to stop being a tree and start being geography. The roots had wrapped around stones and incorporated them. The canopy was its own weather system.

We rested beneath it. The shade was cool, the filtered-light-and-temperature that large trees create beneath themselves. I worked on your meridians first. The channels were mostly clear now — clean enough that the qi moved through them like water through an unblocked stream. What remained was the core itself. The crack. The fracture I had caused, that everything else had been compensating for. The system around it was ready. The core wasn't.

You sat against a root afterward. Your pack beside you. The knife in your hand — a small folding blade you used for everything from peeling ginger to cleaning your nails to the activity you were performing now.

Carving.

A stick. Pine, probably. You'd been shaping it since we sat down — long strokes with the blade, the shavings curling off and falling, the rhythm of someone who knew how to use a knife on wood but was not invested in the outcome. The last time you'd carved, months ago, you'd never finished it. Whatever it had been not-becoming remained between you and the wood, but it had burned bright when you fed it to the fire.

You stopped. Looked at the shape. Whatever it had been becoming, it wasn't that anymore. You snapped it and tossed the pieces.

The gesture did not look like frustration. Casualness. The disposal of something that had served its purpose — hand-occupation — and no longer needed to exist.

"I knew," you said.

The words came with the same casualness as the tossed wood. 

I waited.

"Not the details." You folded the knife. Put it back in your pocket. Looked at the roots, the stone, the tree's patient architecture. "The feeling."

You turned your head. Looked at me. The look — direct, unguarded, the openness that you allowed yourself once every now and then when the clinical mask served no purpose and the rawness underneath was the more accurate communication.

"I've known since the beginning that something was — not right isn't the word. Not wrong. Different. That you were different in a way that wasn't just ability or cultivation or any of the explanations I pretended were sufficient. I didn't write it in the notebook. Not explicitly. But the feeling — the feeling was there from the start."

I looked at the tree. The roots, wrapping stone, incorporating what couldn't be moved. The patience of organic architecture.

You continued. "When you knelt in the river, when you said it, I wasn't surprised. I was confirmed. The way you confirm a diagnosis that you've suspected since the first symptom. Relief and grief simultaneously. Relief because the uncertainty ends. Grief because the certainty is large."

No shock.

I nodded.

The leaves rustled. A breeze — nothing else.

What you didn't say, was why you had stayed.

Maybe because it didn't need saying? Maybe because the saying would have been smaller than the fact. Some truths are best expressed through their continued demonstration rather than their articulation. Maybe you stayed because you had no other option you wanted. 

You could have walked away at any point — you had legs and a mind and the competence of a scholar who could survive alone in any terrain — and you chose, each morning, each step, each day, to walk with me, into the mountains, toward the thing I needed to do, which was also the thing that might end everything.

You stayed because staying was your answer.

The leaves rustled again. We stayed silent for a while. 

"The blossoms are early," you said.

"You already mentioned that," I said.

"I guess I did."

Almost a smile. Yours. Mine. The shared, micro-expression of two people who had been traveling together long enough that their humor had synchronized, whose references had become a private language that no one else would find funny or even comprehensible.

We sat under the tree that was older than you and younger than me and the silence was not silence but communication and the breeze moved the leaves and the leaves made sounds and the sounds were enough.

Xiǎohú appeared from wherever she had been investigating. She sat near us. The triangle — woman, man, fox — that constituted whatever we were. Something without a name that was no less real for being unnamed.

After a while, we packed and continued. The path climbed.

You walked beside me.
