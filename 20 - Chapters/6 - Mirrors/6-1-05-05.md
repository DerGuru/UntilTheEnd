The trail curved east around a stand of bamboo that had no business growing on a south-facing granite slope. Old. Thick. Established — the kind of stand that takes decades to develop. But the soil was wrong for it, the elevation was wrong for it, and the worn edge of the trail ran straight toward where the bamboo now stood. As if the path had once gone through that space and the bamboo had grown into it afterward. Except the bamboo was older than the trail.

You walked through it without hesitation. Your feet found the path automatically, your body moving through the green shadows with muscle memory I knew you didn't have.

Another edit. Another rewrite. The mountain was accumulating them like snow.

Then the bamboo thinned and the ridge opened ahead of us and I stopped counting anomalies.

They stood on the ridge like a painting.

A hundred and twelve — I counted. A hundred and twelve cultivators arranged along the ridge's spine in a formation that was something between military and ceremonial. Patient. Organized. Still.

They'd reformed.

The sect that should have scattered after the field, after the near-death, after the complete unraveling of whatever operational structure they'd maintained, had reformed. Come back stronger, tighter, more integrated, as if the catastrophe had compressed them, the way pressure doesn't destroy carbon but rearranges it into diamond.

Pencil stood at the center.

I called him Pencil because that was how I'd first seen him: thin, angular, precisely drawn, with the kind of bone structure that looked like it had been designed for efficiency rather than aesthetics. He hadn't earned a real name because that required proximity, and proximity to Pencil was something I'd been systematically avoiding.

He was different.

The last time I'd seen him — through the haze of the collapse, through the field's dissolution, through the smoke and the noise and the general chaos of reality reasserting itself after a prolonged argument with its own rules — he'd been diminished. Pale. Shaken. The look of a man who had pushed too close to something he couldn't control and had been reminded, violently, of his limitations.

Now he looked fed.

He looked nourished, strengthened, saturated with something that humans didn't normally have access to. His cultivation signature was different. Thicker. More resonant. As if the anomalies — my anomalies, the retroactive changes, the reality-edits that had been accumulating around me like sediment — had fed him. Had fed all of them.

The anomaly-boost.

The phrase arrived with the understanding. They'd absorbed the excess. The changes I'd been producing: the tree, the stone, the flowers, the bamboo. Each one had released something. Energy. Potential. The residual force of reality being overwritten. And they'd caught it. Collected it. Refined it. Used it the way cultivators use everything: as fuel. As resource. As the raw material of power.

A hundred and twelve cultivators, boosted by my own unconscious edits, arranged on a ridge overlooking the valley where Xu Ran and I had been walking for three days.

Watching.

Watching. The way you watch a storm approach — with respect, with caution, with the certainty that it will arrive whether you watch or not.

They were patient.

In every previous encounter, the sect had been aggressive — driven by urgency, ambition, the cultivator's inability to let power exist without claiming it. They'd attacked. Manipulated. Schemed.

Now they waited.

"How many?" you said. You were standing beside me, looking at the ridge. Your voice was level, the controlled calm of a man who had learned to be calm in situations that didn't deserve calm. Your hand was near your belt, where the small knife you carried hung.

"A hundred and twelve."

"More than before."

"Yes."

"They look —"

"Different. I know."

Pencil was looking at me. At me. The directed attention of someone who saw exactly one thing worth watching. His face was still. His cultivation energy was reined in, controlled, the disciplined restraint of someone who had learned that power displayed was power wasted.

He was waiting for me.

They were all waiting for me. The entire formation, the hundred and twelve bodies arranged along the ridge with their anomaly-boosted cultivation and their reformed structure and their devastating, unprecedented patience. They were waiting for me to do something. To change something. To produce another retroactive edit that would release another pulse of residual energy that would feed them, strengthen them, compress them further into whatever they were becoming.

They were farming me.

The realization was cold. Clean. Mathematical. They didn't need to do anything except wait for me to exist, to live, to walk through the mountains and unconsciously edit reality around me and then they would absorb the excess and grow stronger and wait some more.

A sustainable model. A closed circle. Me as the source. Them as the collectors. The anomalies as the medium.

I stood on the trail and looked at Pencil and Pencil looked at me and between us was the valley and the air and the understanding that we were no longer adversaries in any meaningful sense. We were participants in an equation. Variables in a function that neither of us had written and both of us were learning to read.

"We're out of time," I said.

You looked at me.

"The changes are accelerating. The anomalies are getting bigger. They're getting stronger from the anomalies. And I can't stop producing them because I don't know how I'm producing them."

"Can you —"

"No."

The refusal came before the question finished. I didn't know what you'd been about to ask — stop them, leave, fight — but the answer was the same. No. I couldn't stop producing anomalies I didn't know I was producing. I couldn't fight a hundred and twelve cultivators strengthened by my own unconscious power.

Xiǎohú stood at the trail's edge, her body tense, her eyes fixed on the ridge. Every hair on her back was raised. Her tail was low and still, the posture of a predator that has recognized a threat it cannot engage.

She had been tracking them. All day. The invisible things she had been watching, the things above the treeline, the things in the air, hadn't been anomalies.

They'd been scouts.

Pencil raised one hand. A small gesture, economical, precise, the cultivator's equivalent of a nod. Acknowledgment. Recognition. The gesture of a man who had seen what he came to see and was satisfied with what he'd seen and would be back tomorrow to see it again.

The formation dissolved. Quietly. They simply turned and walked, a hundred and twelve figures moving along the ridge's spine and then over it and then gone, absorbed into the mountain's terrain with the ease of something that belonged there.

Gone.

The ridge was empty. The valley was quiet. The evening light performed its ordinary transformation of the ordinary world and I stood on an ordinary trail with an ordinary man and Xiǎohú and the knowledge that nothing about any of this was ordinary and the time I'd been using to not-think about what was happening had just run out.

"We need a plan," you said.

You were right.

I had no idea what the plan could be.
