Morning came and I was hearing things. Thoughts — my own thoughts, arriving with the unhurried cadence of someone who had always been speaking and was only now being listened to.

The fire was rebuilt. Rice again. Xiǎohú curled near the embers, eyes half-closed, the laziness of an animal that had decided the universe was acceptable for the time being. You crouched near the fire with the expression of a man solving architecture. The placement of wood, the angle of the pot, the alignment of elements that would produce heat and rice and the continuation of bodies.

I sat down across from you and the thought came. *She wanted to keep you alive.*

She? Me. 

The distinction was dissolving. The self-protection, the mechanism I'd cursed, fought, resisted through the fragments and the field, had not been a prison. A wall between me and the weight of truth, erected by a part of me that had calculated the timeline and determined that it wasn't time yet. That I couldn't hold this yet. Giving me time.

I'd spent eons fighting the part of me that was keeping me alive.

The protection was dropping, but whatever it had been shielding me from was still there.

"Rice?" you offered, handing me the bowl. The word as delivery mechanism.

I took it. The warmth through the ceramic. Plain, slightly burned at the bottom where the pot had been too close to the embers, the imperfection that distinguished real cooking from idealized memory. I held the bowl with both hands and ate a bite. The rice was good.

The thought continued.

She — I — had known. Since the beginning. Since before the beginning, since the moment the conviction crystallized and immortality became not a condition but a fact. She— I had known what that meant, known the scope of it, the weight of it, the endless accumulation of time and loss. The slow erosion of everything except the thing that could not erode. And I had looked at everything and decided to go with it anyway. To keep going. To keep believing. 

Patience measured in millennia.

Patience so vast it looked like cruelty.

I ate the rice. Slowly. Each bite an act of presence rather than fuel. The self-tenderness was there, unnamed, a warmth that had nothing to do with the fire or the bowl but sat somewhere in the space between recognition and acceptance. I could not name it because naming it would make it smaller. It existed as sensation, as the quality of attention that accompanies the act of being gentle with something you've hurt.

I had hurt myself. Not through action — through misidentification. I had looked at my deepest self and called it Other. I had felt my own protection and called it Prison. I had heard my own voice and called it Enemy.

Xiǎohú opened one amber eye. She ley there unbothered by my inner turmoil.

"You're eating slowly," you said.

"Am I?"

"You usually finish in three minutes. It's been seven."

"Counting?"

"Observing."

The distinction mattered to you. It shouldn't have mattered to me. It did. The way you attended to me — it mattered. Not as validation. As mirror.

You watched me the way I should have been watching myself.

I looked at the rice. Four bites left, maybe five. The portion was adequate, the meal was functional, the morning was ordinary in every way except the one that made it extraordinary. I was hearing myself. The full, clear, unsevered voice of a woman who had been speaking for eons and was only now being recognized for what she was.

*You did what you had to do.*

The thought arrived and I let it sit. I didn't fight. I didn't analyze. The thought was mine. The thought had always been mine. I had done what I had to do and the part of me that had done it had done what she had to do, too.

We had both been trying to survive.

I finished the rice. Set the bowl down. The fire was settling into its middle phase, the bright urgency fading, the coals establishing their longer, steadier warmth. You watched the fire as you watched everything.

I picked a grain of rice from the rim of the bowl. Ate it. It was burned, crunchy, the bottom-of-the-pot kind that Wei would have eaten first because he liked the texture and I would have left last because who eats the burned parts.

I ate it.

You were watching.

"What?" I asked.

"Nothing." You reached for the pot. "There's more, if you want."

I considered. The hunger was real. Body-hunger, the kind that came from walking and sleeping on ground and being a thing that required fuel. Regular, physical hunger.

I stretched out my arm, with the bowl in hand. "Split?"

You served and we ate. Slowly. 

And the voice — my voice, the only voice, the voice that had never been two and was no longer pretending — was quiet. The conversation was done. The speaker and the listener were both satisfied and there was nothing left to say that the silence wasn't already saying.
