We walked in silence for most of the morning.

This silence had texture. Weight. The pressure that precedes weather.

You were building a sentence. You walked behind me, three steps back, your breathing measured, your footsteps deliberate and the sentence grew between us like the tree had grown in the clearing: slowly, then all at once, then retroactively always.

You said it at noon.

We'd stopped at a stream, a normal stream, flowing downhill, obeying the fundamental laws that most water still obeyed. I was filling the water skins. You were sitting on a rock. Xiǎohú was drinking from the stream's edge, her tongue quick and precise.

"You're different," you said.

I didn't look up. "Different how?"

"Calmer."

The word hung. I finished filling the water skin. Capped it. Set it beside the other one.

"That scares me more than your anger," you said.

I looked at you then.

You were watching me with the expression I'd learned to read over the past months. The observer who cared.

"My anger?"

"The field. The deductions. The rage when you found the rules and the rules were wrong. That was — I understood that. That was a person finding out they'd been lied to. That was proportionate."

"And this isn't."

"This is you sitting by a stream filling water skins while reality rewrites itself around you and a hundred cultivators harvest your unconscious power from a ridge and you, you're filling water skins."

You were right. I was calm. A calm I didn't recognize — deeper than performance, deeper than shock. It came from the crack, from the space where the images had taken up residence. The hands. The light. The creating. Since the images had arrived, since they'd refused to leave, since I'd stopped pushing and let them stay, there had been a quiet in me that I didn't recognize. A settling. As if something inside had stopped struggling against something else and the cessation of struggle had produced a calm that was either peace or surrender.

I didn't know which.

"I don't understand it either," I said.

"That's what scares me."

"That I'm calm?"

"That you don't understand why." You leaned forward, elbows on your knees. "You understood your anger. You understood your fear. You understood the wall and the erasure and the field and everything that happened there. Not immediately, but eventually, you understood it and the understanding gave you something to work with. A lever. A position."

"And now?"

"Now you're calm for no reason while things happen that should terrify you and you're — accepting it. Like someone who's stopped fighting a current. That's either wisdom or it's drowning."

The stream moved past us. Normal stream. Normal water. Normal direction. Xiǎohú finished drinking and settled on the bank, her eyes half-closed. The noon light was warm and even, without the theatrical quality that dramatic moments usually demanded.

I considered your words. You were right — wisdom and drowning looked identical from the outside.

But you were wrong about one thing.

"I'm not accepting it," I said. "I'm not fighting it either. I'm — waiting."

"For what?"

"I don't know."

"That's not better."

"I know."

You sat with that. The sitting: your gift, the willingness to hold discomfort without moving. The stream ran. The sun moved. A bird — maybe the new one, the three-note one, the one that hadn't existed until yesterday — called from somewhere in the canopy.

"The images," you said. "From yesterday. The hands."

"They're still there."

"Still the same?"

"Quieter. Like — background noise. Something running constantly that you stop hearing after a while."

"But you haven't forgotten them."

"No."

"And the wall?"

"Doesn't react to anything. Hasn't since the field. It's still there. I can feel it. But it's not — active. Like a guard sleeping at his post."

You processed this. The notebook was closed now — the data had outgrown the medium. But the model-building continued behind your eyes.

"You said the editing is retroactive," you said. "That when something changes, everyone's memory changes with it. Except yours."

"Yes."

"And you said you don't know how you're producing the changes."

"Yes."

"But you said — last night. You said something and then you stopped."

I remembered. The thought that had arrived fully formed — *I was immune to the editing because the editing was mine* — and the push, the reflex, the habit of not-thinking-further that had carried me safely through centuries of not-knowing.

"You heard that?"

"I heard you start a sentence and swallow it. I've been watching you do that for six months. I know what it looks like."

Silence. The stream. The bird. Xiǎohú's slow breathing.

"What were you going to say?" you asked.

"Something I'm not ready to say."

You nodded.

"When you're ready," you said.

"When I'm ready."

You stood. Picked up your pack. Adjusted the straps with the practiced efficiency of a man who had carried weight across mountains before and would do it again and considered the carrying a reasonable price for the arriving.

"I'd prefer it if you were ready soon," you said. "The calm ones are always closer to the edge than they think."

You walked. I watched you walk: the human gait, the mortal steps, the back that carried its years without complaint.

You weren't wrong.
