We walked in silence for most of the morning.

This silence had texture. Weight. The pressure that precedes weather.

You were building a sentence. You walked behind me, three steps back, your breathing measured, your footsteps deliberate. The sentence grew between us like the tree had grown in the clearing. Slowly, then all at once, then retroactively always.

It was noon when we stopped at a stream, a normal stream, flowing downhill, obeying the fundamental laws that water obeyed. No anomalies here. I surely was expecting water to flow uphill at some point. But for now, I was filling the water skins. You were sitting on a rock. Xiǎohú was drinking from the stream's edge, her tongue quick and precise.

"You're different," you said.

I didn't look up. "Different how?"

"Calmer."

The word hung. I finished filling the water skin. Capped it. Set it beside the other one.

"That scares me more than your anger," you said.

I looked at you then.

You were watching me with the expression I'd learned to read over the past months. The observer who cared.

"My anger?"

"The field. The rage. The tree that burst when we spotted the three scouts. That was — I understood that. That was proportionate."

"And this isn't."

"This is you sitting by a stream while reality rewrites itself around you and more than a hundred cultivators harvest your unconscious power. And you, you're just filling water skins."

You were right. I was calm. A calm I didn't recognize — deeper than performance, deeper than shock. Maybe it came from the crack, from the space where the images had taken up residence. The hands. The light. The creating. Since the images had arrived, since they'd refused to leave, since I'd stopped pushing and let them stay, there had been a quiet in me that I didn't recognize. A settling. As if something inside had stopped struggling against something else and the cessation of struggle had produced a calm that was either peace or surrender.

I didn't know which.

"I don't understand it either," I said.

"That scares me even more."

"That I'm calm?"

"That you don't understand why." You leaned forward, elbows on your knees. "You understood your anger. You understood your fear. You understood the wall, the erasure, the field and everything that happened there. Not immediately, but eventually, you understood it and that gave you something to work with. A lever. A position."

"And now?"

"Now you're calm for no reason while things happen that should terrify you. And you're just accepting it. Like someone who's stopped fighting a current and now floats with it. That's either wisdom or it's drowning."

The stream moved past us. Normal stream. Normal water. Normal direction. Xiǎohú finished drinking and settled on the bank, her eyes half-closed. The noon light was warm and even, without the theatrical quality that dramatic moments usually demanded.

I considered your words. You were right — wisdom and drowning looked identical from the outside.

But you were wrong about one thing.

"I'm not accepting it," I said. "I'm not fighting it either. I'm — waiting."

"For what?"

"I don't know."

"That's not making it better."

"What would you have me do? Fight them? I won't do that."

"No. I'd have you care that it's happening."

I sat with that. The stream ran. The sun moved. A bird — maybe the new one, the three-note one, the one that hadn't existed until yesterday — called from somewhere in the canopy.

The water caught my eye. Something about the surface — the way the light moved on it. I watched it for a moment. Still flowing downhill. Still normal.

"The images," you said. "From yesterday. The hands."

"They're still there."

"Still the same?"

"Quieter. Like — background noise. Something running constantly that you stop hearing after a while."

"But still present." You paused. Thinking. "And the wall?"

"Doesn't react to anything lately. Not since you asked me a question, that I forgot and something had spoken through me. It's still there. I can feel it. But it's not — active. Like a guard sleeping at his post."

You processed this. The notebook was closed now — the data had outgrown the medium. But the model-building continued behind your eyes.

"You said the editing has changed," you said. "That when something changes, everyone's memory changes with it now. Except yours."

"Yes."

"And you said you don't know how you're producing the changes."

"Yes."

"You were going to say something last night. You started, but then you stopped before anything came out."

I remembered. The flicker from the night before — half-formed, too quick to hold. The sense that two things belonged together.

"You saw that?"

"I saw you start a sentence and swallow it. I've been watching you do that for six months. I know what it looks like."

Silence. The stream. The bird. Xiǎohú's slow breathing.

"What were you going to say?" you asked.

I looked at the water. Still flowing downhill. Still obeying. Then I said it.

"The immunity and the editing. They're not separate things."

You were quiet. Waiting.

"Everyone's memory changes when the world changes. Except mine. And I don't know how the changes are being produced. But what if those two things are connected? What if I'm immune because —" The sentence wanted to stop. The old reflex, the push, the habit. I finished it anyway. "Because I am truly the one editing?"

You didn't move. Your face did something complicated — several reactions passing through it in sequence, each one considered and discarded before it could settle.

"Say that again," you said.

"I think I'm the one doing it. Not something in me, me."

The fire-maker, the data-collector, the man who built models from observations and tested them against evidence — I watched him receive the hypothesis. You didn't reject it. You didn't accept it. You held it carefully, at arm's length, turning it, looking for the seam where it didn't fit.

"That would explain the immunity," you said slowly.

"Yes."

"But if it is truly you, why can't you stop it?"

"I don't know." 

"So, that's what you weren't ready to say."

"I'm still not sure, that I'm ready. But you—."

The stream had stopped.

I noticed it mid-sentence — the surface had gone still. Flat. The water wasn't flowing downhill anymore. It wasn't flowing at all. It sat in its bed like glass, reflecting the sky with the perfect calm of something that had forgotten it was supposed to move.

Then it reversed.

"—aaasked." I stretched the word. My eyes on the water.

Gently. The water began flowing uphill — from low ground to high ground, climbing the slope with the quiet confidence of water that had always flowed in this direction. No turbulence, no hesitation. Just water deciding that gravity was a suggestion.

"Yun?"

You were watching me. Not the stream — me. The stream was fine. The stream had always flowed this way. What concerned you was my face, whatever my face was doing while I watched water climb a hill.

"What is it?" you said.

"Nothing."

You didn't believe me. But you let it go.

The metallic taste coated the inside of my mouth. The teeth-vibration settled into a continuous hum. The water climbed the slope beside us, quiet and wrong, and you filled your water skin from it without hesitation because for you this was how water had always moved here.

*Not something in me. Me.*

You stood. Picked up your pack and adjusted the straps with the practiced efficiency of a man who had carried weight across mountains before and would do it again, considering it a reasonable price for arriving.

"Well, I'd prefer it if you were ready soon," you said. "The calm ones are always closer to the edge than they think."

You walked. I watched you walk. Human gait, mortal steps, a back that carried its years without complaint.

You weren't wrong.
