Our rice, the evening portion, cooked with whatever herbs the trailside offered, seasoned with the care of a man who treated meals as architecture. The rice was warm. The herbs were adequate. The eating was routine.

Until I heard it. A voice that said: *You already know.*

A voice. Someone else's words, arriving from inside, uninvited, clear the way a stranger's voice is clear when they speak to you across a quiet room.

I stopped chewing.

*You already know.*

The voice was — 

It wasn't like before. Not compressed, not some vague impression that arrived like morse code from behind the wall. Nor felt it like an internal dialogue between my surface self and someone else deep inside, arguing across a divide.

This was something else. Clear. Conversational. Like a part of me sitting across from me, speaking in my own voice but from a chair I hadn't known was occupied. A voice arriving from somewhere that wasn't the place where I was thinking. But recognizable. The way you can hear your own voice on a recording and know it's yours even though it sounds wrong.

I set the chopsticks down.

The thoughts continued. Quiet. Present. Available. Like a conversation partner who had always been sitting at the table but whom I'd been unable to see because the chair was facing the wrong way. Now the chair was turning. Slowly. Not yet face to face. But I could see the outline. I could hear the words without having to decode them first.

The split that had defined my interior experience was closing. The two voices were no longer arguing across a wall. They were in the same room now. Speaking the same language. And I was beginning to suspect they were the same person.

The split had been an illusion. Or was becoming one — the recognition arriving in stages as a series of small corrections, each one making the previous understanding slightly less wrong.

The voice had been real. The content had been accurate. The split itself, the perception that there were two of me, that the surface self and the deeper self were separate entities, that those fragments had come from somewhere other — that was beginning to look like an illusion. The wall's last defense. The final trick. If you can't hide the truth from someone, convince them that the truth is coming from outside, so they can disbelieve it.

My self-protection had made me think my own voice was someone else's.

I sat with the recognition. Let it settle. The voice that had said *Not now* at the field. The voice that had said *Enough*. The voice that had said *Keep going*. They were all me. No guardian, nothing separate. Me, that heard the words and attributed them to an external source. Me, speaking to me, through the only channel the wall left open, three words at a time, compressed, urgent, disguised as foreign.

I'd been talking to myself. I guess that's what happens, when you are alone for too long.

The foreignness was fading. What replaced it wasn't certainty — not yet — but a growing familiarity. The voice that had felt like someone else's was starting to fit. The way a glove that's been in a drawer for years resists the hand at first, then remembers the shape.

"You stopped eating," you said.

I looked at my bowl. Rice. Half finished. The chopsticks resting across the rim. An object that had been sitting in front of me while my attention was elsewhere. It was there, it was unchanged, the world had continued its physical operations while the interior landscape reconfigured.

"Hearing something," I said.

Your face shifted. "Hear what?"

"Me." The word felt almost right. Almost.

You processed. You narrowed your eyes and held your breath, the momentary cessation of motion that preceded your most careful responses.

"What's different?"

"It's clearer now. Not just fragments. Just — thoughts. Mine. But from deeper inside." Each word a small act of claiming. Of trying the voice on, the way you try on a coat that might be yours.

Something shifted. Quiet. Almost invisible. The recognition that the voice I'd feared had been my own voice all along, and the voice had been trying to help.

The self-protection had been me.

A part of me — the oldest part, the part that remembered, the part that knew — trying to protect me from knowledge I had decided I wasn't ready for. Hiding the truth out of care, not malice. The way a parent hides sharp objects from a child, not to deny the child's agency but to preserve the child's safety until the agency can handle the sharpness.

I had been protecting myself from myself.

And now the protection was no longer needed — not because the truth was less sharp but because the self was ready for sharpness.

Or perhaps just no longer able to look away.

I picked up the chopsticks. Found the rice. Continued eating.

Slower than before. Deliberately slower. Each grain a presence, each bite a choice, each swallow an act of attention that the previous version of eating hadn't contained. I finished the rice. Slowly. Completely.

You watched me eat the way you watched everything — with the careful, sustained attention of a man for whom observation was devotion.

*You already know*, the voice had said. And it was right. I did know. I had known. In retrospect, I'd known since I knelt in the river. But knowledge and experience are separate things, like the distance between reading about fire and holding your hand over a flame.

But here and now with rice eaten and the sound of Xiǎohú's breathing, I was beginning to *experience* it. And that was something else entirely.
