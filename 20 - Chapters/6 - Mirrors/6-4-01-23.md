We made camp where the trail levelled. A shelf of rock, half-sheltered by pine, the kind of place that offered itself to travellers without asking anything in return.

You made fire. The mortal method — flint, steel, patience. The routine of a man whose routines were the architecture of his sanity. I sat with my knees drawn up, chin on forearms and watched the flames find their rhythm.

I'd done your meridians before the light failed, while the last warmth of the day still clung to the rock. The lower channels were responding now — not healed, but less resistant. The core was still the problem. The crack ran deep and the pathways around it had adapted to the damage, rerouting qi the way a river reroutes around a boulder. Fixing the core would mean undoing every adaptation the body had built to survive without it. I wasn't ready for that yet.

Everything was converging.

I could feel it — the way you feel a wave before you see it, the way you feel the ground's tremor before the earthquake announces itself. The tests. The guilt. The Wei memory. The creation fragments. Your mirror-words. The mechanism frozen. The question open. The wall thin. Everything that had been accumulating was moving toward a center, gathering speed, gathering mass, gathering the gravitational inevitability of a convergence that had been building since the metallic taste appeared one morning.

A spark jumped from the fire.

Small. The physics of combustion — heated gases distorting wood fiber, releasing particles, the particles becoming briefly incandescent and then dark. The spark arced through the gray air and landed on my hand.

I didn't flinch.

I didn't flinch because there was nothing to flinch from. The spark landed on my skin and extinguished. No heat. No burn. No reddening. No pain.

Xiǎohú flinched for me. She'd been dozing by the fire and the spark's arc had crossed her line of sight and she'd jerked back, ears flat, the honest animal reaction that my body had failed to produce. She sniffed at my hand. Sniffed again. Looked up with the expression of a creature who had just confirmed that something was wrong with me and was choosing not to elaborate.

I looked at my hand.

The skin was unmarked. Smooth. The same skin that had been unmarked for centuries — the skin that didn't scar, didn't burn, didn't age, didn't display any of the evidence that the passage of time and the accumulation of experience normally inscribed on human surfaces.

I'd always known this. The invulnerability — the casual, automatic, unremarkable invulnerability that I'd attributed to cultivation, to affinity, to talent, to the mysterious external force that my now-burned notes had documented with such careful dishonesty. I'd always known that sparks didn't burn me. That cuts didn't scar. That falls didn't break.

I'd never known WHY.

I stared at the hand. The same hand that had shaped stones, that carved meridians and pressed against surfaces that yielded because I expected them to yield. The same hand that belonged to a mind that had shifted cultivators and believed reality into compliance. The same hand that had, according to the creation fragments, shaped light in a place before places. The believe that the wall was losing its ability to hide from me.

The spark hadn't burned because I believed I couldn't burn.

The cuts hadn't scarred because I believed I couldn't scar.

The falls hadn't broken because I believed I couldn't break.

Belief. My belief.

The recognition began. Something inside me shifted, something heavy, something that had been balanced on an edge. Slowly. The way a leaf falls to ground — visible, trackable, governed by physics but cushioned by medium, the descent observable but unhurried.

I stared at my unmarked hand and felt the leaf begin its descent and knew — with the same certainty that had moved the stones — that the leaf would reach the bottom. That the falling was unstoppable. That what waited at the bottom was the thing I had been approaching since the first morning on this ridge.

The fog pressed against the clearing. Gray. Textureless. Waiting.

You put food in front of me. I didn't eat.

Xiǎohú climbed into my lap. The full weight of her, slight but undeniable, curling into the space between my crossed legs and my chest. Her nose pressed into the crook of my elbow. Her breathing slowed.

She slept. I didn't.

The leaf fell.
