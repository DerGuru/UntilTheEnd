Morning came the way mornings had been coming since the river — slow, soft, the light filtering through the canopy in stages.

Xiǎohú was already gone — hunting, probably, or patrolling whatever perimeter she'd decided was hers. The fire was embers, so I rebuilt it. Found water. Set rice. The pot still wore last night's burn and I scraped it with a stone before filling it.

You woke to the smell. Sat up and blinked. The expression of a man assembling consciousness one piece at a time. You pulled on your boots. Stretched and looked at the pot.

"I'm watching it this time," I said.

"I didn't—"

"You did."

The morning was warm and the air smelled like pine resin and damp earth and the particular freshness that existed in forests near rivers, the humidity that made everything softer.

After eating, I gathered what needed washing. I went to the river and started washing.

My own robe. The under-layer. Your spare shirt. The sash — creased at the knot, soft where the tail hung, the textile autobiography of repetitive use. The cloth we used as a pot holder. Everything that could be washed, I washed.

The river was the same one. We hadn't moved far — a half-day's walk downstream from yesterday's washing spot, the water wider here, shallower, the stones smaller and rounder. Xiǎohú had found a patch of sun on the far bank and was lying in it with the committed indolence of an animal that understood the value of warmth.

I stood in the water up to my calves. The fabric was heavy with water. My hands were full.

I wrung out your shirt. Set it on the flat rock I'd designated as the drying surface and picked up the sash. I scrubbed. The motion was becoming familiar again — not the muscle memory of eons, but the fresh familiarity of something relearned, the satisfaction of a skill that had been dormant and was waking up.

The sash was clean. I held it.

Wet fabric, heavy, dripping. Both hands. The weight of it pulling down — not much, the sash was light even wet, but enough. The pull of gravity on water-logged cloth, the most ordinary force in the world.

I opened my hands.

The sash fell into the river.

Quickly. Wetly. It hit the surface, sank halfway, caught the current and began to move downstream. A piece of wet fabric in a river, doing what wet fabric in rivers does.

I watched it go.

My hands were open. Empty. The air on my wet palms. The absence of the fabric's weight — the lightness of having held something and now not holding it. I had been holding a piece of wet cloth and I had opened my hands and the cloth had left.

The letting go was that simple.

The letting go was that hard.

Because the impulse came — reach, grab, retrieve — the automatic response of a mind and body calibrated for holding. The grip-reflex. The fist that wanted to close because fists were what hands did, because holding was the default, because millennia of holding had made holding feel like the natural state and opening feel like an aberration. The impulse was strong. The impulse was screaming.

I did not close my hands.

The sash floated. Caught on a stone, spun, freed itself, continued. Two meters downstream. Three. Moving with the current's unhurried inevitability, the pace of water that had nowhere exact to be and eternity to get there.

"Yun."

You. Downstream. I could see you — you'd been sitting on the bank, notebook, the usual arrangement — and you were standing now, watching the sash approach, your expression the precise combination of concern and bemusement that accompanied most of my unconventional moments.

"We still need that," you said.

I almost laughed.

You waded in. Two steps, your boots already off from sitting on the bank, the water to your knees. You caught the sash as it drifted past, plucked it from the current with an unhurried motion and held it up.

"Got it," you said.

The sash dripped. You dripped. Xiǎohú on the far bank lifted her head, assessed the situation and put her head back down. Not interesting enough.

"You could have caught it," you said.

"Yes."

"You chose not to."

I looked at my hands. Still open. Still empty. The palms cooling in the air, the water evaporating, the skin tightening as it dried.

"I chose not to close my hands," I said. "It's different."

You considered this. Standing in the river, holding a wet sash, water around your knees.

"Is it?" you said.

"Choosing not to reach is still choosing. Letting go is — not choosing. It's the absence of a grip. If I had chosen to let the sash go, that would have been will. Directing the outcome through decision. But I didn't let go of it. I stopped holding."

You looked at the sash. Looked at me. You wrung the sash out — efficiently, practically, the wringing of a man who intended to use the sash again and was not interested in letting it complete its symbolic journey to the sea.

"You stopped holding?" you repeated.

"And it left."

"Because that's what wet sashes in rivers do?"

"Yes."

You waded back to the bank and spread the sash on your drying rock. You stood there for a moment, looking at the arrangement of wet fabric — your shirt, my robe, the pot cloth, the sash — with the evaluative expression of a man assessing whether the distribution of cloth-to-sun ratio was optimal.

"Hm," you said. Looking at the sash drying on the rock. Then at me, still standing in the river. "I guess you did stop holding it. It did leave."

Not quite true. But close enough. The impulse to reach was still there — would always be there, probably, the fist's memory of being a fist. But the impulse had not become action. The hands had stayed open. The sash had gone.

Xiǎohú yawned on the far bank. The sun continued its slow, indifferent transit. The wet fabric drying in the warmth.

I stood in the river with empty hands and thought about the nothing of space, the emptiness that existed between letting go and whatever came after, the pause between one state and the next that could only be occupied by waiting.

I waited.

The water flowed through.
