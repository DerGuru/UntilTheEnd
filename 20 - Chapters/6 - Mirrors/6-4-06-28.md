My eyes were open. After the onslaught of memories, a calmness took hold of me. The water was cold against my hips. The river had resumed its current, moving again, flowing south, the ordinary behavior of water freed from the suspension my belief had imposed. My clothes were soaked. The fabric clung to my skin with the heavy, intimate weight of wet cotton and the coldness of the water had become the coldness of the cloth against my body.

Every nerve registered it. All of it. The way I'd been feeling things since the breach, acutely, intensely, with the heightened sensitivity of a body whose numbing mechanisms had been disabled. The cold. The weight of the water. The texture of the river stones beneath my knees. The air moving across the wet skin of my arms.

Sunlight fell on the river, on the bank, on the trees, on you. Unfiltered, undiffused, the honest light of a day in a mountain valley. No theatrical quality. No symbolic relevance. Just light — the kind that shows things as they are.

As they are.

The phrase was different now. Before I found my true self — *as they are* had meant the world as given. The world as received. 

Now *as they are* meant the world as made. As believed. As sustained by the continuous, unconscious, unwavering conviction of a self that could change it all on a whim but would maintain it until the conviction ceased. And I wondered, what would happen if the conviction ceased? If I stopped believing in the world, in the river, in the trees, in you? In all of it? Would all disappear? Would all collapse into nothingness? Or would it continue without me? Would it continue because I believed — deep inside — it would?

The same tree on the bank. The same pine, the same trunk, the same roots gripping the same earth. But I knew now that it stood there because I believed in its standing. That its rootedness was my rootedness. That its reality was my reality, generated, maintained, sustained. Was this godhood?

The same blue, cloud-streaked, sky. The ordinary sky of an ordinary afternoon. But I knew now that the sky was blue because I believed it to be blue. That the clouds moved because I believed in their movement. 

This should have been terrifying. The solipsistic horror of a consciousness discovering that everything is itself — that the other, the external, the independent reality that provides the ground on which identity stands, is a projection. An invention. A belief.

It wasn't terrifying.

It was exhausting.

My muscles ached. My head ached — the ordinary headache of a mind that had processed too much. My eyes were dry. The body's response to a revelation that the body hadn't been designed to absorb.

You were still at the bank. Notebook closed now. Pen in your breast pocket. Sitting cross-legged on dry ground, watching me with the expression I'd spent months learning to read and still couldn't fully translate. The expression that combined observation with concern with something else.

"Yun," you said. Quietly.

I came back on my feet with much effort. Knees protesting, muscles stiff, the body's complaint against prolonged immersion in cold water. I waded toward the bank. Each step sent ripples outward, small, normal, the ordinary disturbance of a body moving through water.

Or was it ordinary? Was any ripple I produced ordinary anymore? Did I produce ripples because my body displaced water, or because I believed in displacement? Was there a difference? Had there ever been?

I reached the bank. You extended a hand. I took it — mortal hand, warm, the calluses of a man who used his hands for practical things. You pulled. I stepped onto dry ground.

My clothes were wet. The fabric heavy, clinging, cold against my skin.

I looked down at it. At the dark cotton pressed against my thighs. At the water dripping from the hem.

I knew how this worked now.

My clothes were dry.

Not gradually. Not through some process of evaporation or wringing or waiting. Dry. The fabric light against my skin, warm, as if the river had never touched it. As if *wet* had been a suggestion and I'd declined.

You looked at my clothes. At the dry fabric. At the river behind me. Your hand went to your breast pocket where your pen was. Then stopped.

No scream. No tears. No dramatic moment.

Just knowing.

The knowing was enough. The knowing was everything. The knowing changed everything and nothing because the world was exactly the same world it had been an hour ago — the trees, the river, the stones, the sky. The only thing that had changed was the woman looking at it and the woman's understanding of why it existed.

"Shall I make rice?" you said.

The question was perfect. What made it perfect was its timing, its tone, its absolute refusal to treat this moment as anything other than what it also was. A day on a mountain and two people with need for food.

I nodded.

You stood. Went to your pack. Retrieved the pot, the rice, the water skin. Began the ritual, the mortal ritual, the human ceremony. Pot over fire. Water measured. Rice poured. The simple chemistry of sustenance.

Xiǎohú was by the fire. She'd been there the whole time — curled near the stones, nose tucked under her tail. When I stepped into the clearing she lifted her head. Looked at me. Not the cautious look of the past days, not the worried sniffing. Just looked. Then put her head back down.

I sat by the fire. Beside you. Close — closer than I usually sat, close enough that my shoulder nearly touched yours, close enough that the warmth of your living body reached mine through the dry cotton of your shirt.

And I waited for rice.

Like every other day. Like every other meal. Like the thousand meals before this one and the however-many meals that would come after.

The truth sat between us. Just present. Like a third person at the fire. A guest who had arrived and would not leave. Their presence changed the shape of the conversation without dominating it.

*I am it. I was always it.*

The sentence hummed. Not in my teeth — in my chest. In the space where the crack had been, where the wall had been, where the guardian had patrolled and prevented and managed. The space was open now. The wall was transparent now. I could see through it. See my true self on the other side.

The humming was recognition. The self recognizing the self. The mirror seeing the mirror. A woman sitting by a fire, waiting for rice, knowing that her belief held more weight than she'd ever dared to measure.

The rice cooked.

Xiǎohú trotted over. Sat down at my knee. Looked at the pot with the unmistakable expression of a creature who had decided that cosmic revelations were fine but dinner was dinner.

We ate.

And then I told you what had happened. Not everything — not the millennia, not the details, not the shape of the place before places. But enough. The mechanism. The belief. The river. The why.

You listened. You didn't write.
