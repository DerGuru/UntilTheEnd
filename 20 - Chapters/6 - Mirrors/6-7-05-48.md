The fire was mine, built from my kindling, my stones, my arrangement. The breath that coaxed the flame was cam from my lungs. I cooked. The pot was positioned at the angle that experience, ancient, body-stored, hands-that-remembered, had determined was optimal for the size of the fire, the amount of water and the quantity of rice.

I measured the water. Too much. I knew it was too much even as I poured, but that didn't stop the pouring. We'd been trading the cooking back and forth since you'd burned yours — some mornings you, some mornings me — and the ratio should have been familiar by now. But the mountain was louder tonight. The direction. The closeness. Whatever waited ahead was pulling at the part of me that should have been watching the water level.

The water was still before the heat reached it. My face floated on the dark surface — reduced, essential. I looked at myself looking back. Then the first bubble rose and the reflection scattered into something the pot could use.

The rice cooked.

Slowly. The water rose to a boil with the patient inevitability of physics operating on liquid and heat. The rice expanded. Softened. Became food. I watched the process as I'd watched you unpack, with the attention of someone learning to see the ordinary as sufficient.

It burned.

Barely. The slow, bottom-of-the-pot kind. The smell arrived before the color did.

I pulled the pot off the fire.

Brown-black at the bottom. Now I was the one holding the pot. Different hands. Same result. Bad rice is bad rice regardless of who burns it.

You appeared. You'd been tending to the camp, setting up the sleeping arrangement, checking the water skin, the minor logistics of evening. You looked at the pot. At me. At the pot.

You took your portion. Without comment. Served yourself the burned rice and ate without complaint, with the quiet of a man who understood that the food was not the point.

I served myself. The burned part. The worst of it — the charred crust that had to be broken from the bottom with the edge of the spoon, the pieces that crunched when bitten, the flavor that was acrid and bitter and tasted like failure.

I ate it.

And it tasted like something.

The taste was memory. But memory was bearable now. Memory was even, at certain angles, in certain light, with certain rice even good.

You finished your portion. Set the bowl down.

You pulled out the notebook. The pen tapped your knee twice — the thinking rhythm, the one that meant the words hadn't arrived yet. I scraped the pot clean. The charred crust came off in flakes. I carried it to the stream we'd camped near and scrubbed it with gravel and cold water until the ceramic showed through.

When I came back you were writing. The fire had settled into the low, steady stage — the one that gave light without demanding attention. I sat. Stretched my legs toward the warmth.

"How far tomorrow?" you asked without looking up.

"Depends on the trail."

"That's not an answer."

"It's been a long time, since I was here the last time. I recognize some things, but everything looks different. I can't even say, where we are exactly, I just feel, you know, the direction. The way you feel north without thinking about it."

You wrote something. I couldn't tell if it was my words or your own.

The night sounds were layering in — crickets first, then something larger in the underbrush, then the stream. Xiǎohú appeared at the edge of the firelight and settled into the space between us with the deliberate placement of an animal choosing her spot. She turned twice. Lay down. Her tail curled over her nose.

I mended a strap on the pack where the stitching had frayed. The needle was too large for the thread, but serviceable. You watched me work for a while, then went back to writing.

The fire settled. The stars remained.
