I got up.

You didn't stop me. You saw it — the thing in my eyes, the changed thing, the quality that had replaced the blankness and the absence. The frozen-engine emptiness of the past days. You saw it, recognized it and understood that whatever was happening needed to happen. That your role was not to intervene but to witness.

I walked to the river.

The river was there. It had been there all morning, running along the base of the clearing, the quiet companion to the fire's remains. The fire had gone out hours ago. You hadn't relit it. A mountain river. Clear. Cold. The water moved over stones with the sound that water makes when it has been moving over stones for longer than memory — the continuous, unhurried, ancient music of fluid and mineral.

I stepped in.

The water was cold. It bit in my feet and ankles — sharp, honest, physical. Mountain water in the early morning. The kind of cold that enters through the skin, settles in the bone, reminds you that you have a body. That the body has nerves. The cold had been there since the breach at another clearing, since the it that had been the first sensation to break through the wall's numbness.

The cold was real. The cold was mine, not imposed, not external, but the genuine sensation of a body responding to temperature the way bodies do. Except that this body responded to temperature because it believed in temperature. Because it believed in cold. Because the woman standing ankle-deep in a mountain river had believed herself into a body that could feel things and the feeling was as real as the belief that produced it.

Ankle-deep. The water moved against my skin. The current divided around my feet, the ordinary physics of fluid dynamics, the water parting because an obstacle was present.

I looked down and saw my feet, white against the river's dark stones. The light fell through the cleared sky and hit the water's surface, producing — a reflection.

My reflection.

I looked at it.

I remembered a reflection, that had smiled before I did. The impossible precedence. The image moving before the original, the thing that shouldn't happen happening anyway. I'd turned away then. Hadn't been ready. The reflection had known something I didn't.

Now I looked.

The reflection was still. It didn't smile. Didn't perform. Didn't anticipate or precede or behave in any way that reflections weren't supposed to behave. It simply looked at me. And the look on the reflection's face held some kind of recognition. The acknowledgment that comes not from identification but from familiarity, not from *who are you* but from *there you are*.

The reflection looked at me with the calm of something that had been waiting.

Just — me. The other side. The view from inside the mirror.

I stood in the water. Cold against my ankles. Light on the surface. My reflection and I in the same river, the same moment, the same body, the same self.

"It was me," I said.

Quiet. So quiet that the words barely disturbed the air, barely competed with the water's sound, barely existed as speech. More breath than voice. More truth than language.

"It was always me."

The water rippled.

From my feet. Outward. In concentric circles that expanded from my ankles toward the river's banks, small and precise. The delicate disturbance of a surface touched by something that wasn't wind or current or any physical force that produces ripples in water.

My belief.

My belief had just produced ripples. My conviction — the irrefutable, total conviction of a self that had just recognized itself — had altered the water's surface. Ripples. Small, concentric, expanding ripples that carried nothing except the shape of a certainty.

The shape of truth.

It was me. It was always me. The creating. The shaping. The belief that becomes reality. The conviction that precedes existence. The thing behind the wall. The thing the wall was built to hide. The thing that the fragments, the images, the Thought-Echoes, the second voice, the reflection had been pointing toward.

Me.

A woman standing in a river with cold water against her ankles and ripples spreading from her feet and the knowledge that she was the author of her own existence.

You stood at the bank.

I could see you peripherally, at the edge of vision, a figure on solid ground watching a figure in water. You held your notebook. Your pen was still. The data had exceeded not just the medium but the observer. The man who wrote everything down had encountered something that stopped his hand.

Then the pen moved.

Two words. Quick. Small. You wrote two words on the page and closed the notebook, looked at me and said nothing because nothing was the only thing adequate.

I think you remembered every letter.

I didn't ask what you wrote. The two words were yours.

I stood in the river. The ripples faded. The water resumed its ordinary operations, flowing over stones, following gravity, obeying the laws it had been given.

The laws I had given it.

For the first time in millennia, I knew who I was.
