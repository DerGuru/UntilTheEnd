Another morning a few days later. The kind of morning that had existed before language — before someone named it dawn, before the categories of light and dark became concepts, before the word morning was invented to domesticate the raw, uncivilized fact of the world becoming visible again. The light was grey-gold. The mist was low, sitting in the valley like something spilled. The air smelled like dew and pine and the mineral coldness of ground that had been dark for eight hours and was only now remembering warmth.

I had gotten up, left the camp and walked to the edge of the clearing. My feet were bare, like they always had been. The grass was wet. The dew between my toes was cold. The sharpened, genuine cold that the breach had returned to me and that still surprised me with its presence, the way a guest who has been away for years surprises you by knowing where the cups are.

I stood in the grass, the morning was happening and I was in it.

Xiǎohú was awake. Sitting at the treeline, facing the valley, her posture the alert stillness of an animal monitoring something she could sense but I could not. Her eyes caught the early light and held it. The tail was wrapped tight around her paws. 

You were still asleep. Your sleeping form was a shape in the camp behind me, indistinct in the mist, human-sized, breathing.

I pressed my feet into the grass.

I was basking in the sensation, that was once overwhelming. Each blade a separate pressure, each drop of dew a distinct temperature, the soil beneath giving slightly with the weight, the whole foot a sensor array. My feet in the grass. Wet. Cold. Present.

How do you let go of something you've held for millennia?

How do I stop holding onto immortality, rather than becoming mortal? How could I open myself, rather than gripping? I understood the mechanism. I understood the theory. I understood the shape of it the way you understand the shape of a mountain from a distance. 

But understanding the shape of a mountain is not the same as climbing it.

How do you open a fist that has been closed so long it has forgotten it's a fist? How do you release a grip that has become the structure of the hand? How do you let go of the thing that is doing the letting? The paradox was not philosophical. It was practical. It was my daily problem of standing in wet grass, knowing exactly what I needed to do and having no idea how to do it.

I didn't know that yet. But I knew the question's shape. I knew its weight. I knew that it would not be answered by thinking, or by will, or by another dramatic confrontation or revelation. It would be answered — if it could be answered — by the same slow, physical, bodily process that had brought the insight in the first place. By hands in water. By feet in grass. By the daily, mundane, unremarkable practice of being a thing in the world without gripping the world into shape.

The mist was lifting. The grey-gold becoming gold. Xiǎohú turned her head and looked at me with amber evaluation.

"Breakfast," I said.

To no one. To the morning. To Xiǎohú, who didn't care. To myself, which was, for the first time in a very long time, one self, speaking with one voice, hearing clearly.

I turned and walked back to the camp. The dew on my feet picked up debris. Pine needles, a fragment of leaf, the small accumulations of walking barefoot on forest floor. The fire from last night was embers. I crouched, added kindling and blew. Not too hard, not too soft, the breath that feeding a fire required.

The fire caught.

You stirred and opened your eyes. The transition from sleep to waking. You sat up. Looked at me. At the fire. The morning assessment. The quick, comprehensive evaluation of status, environment, companion. Everything accounted for. Everything acceptable.

You put the pot on. Water from the skin. Rice from the bag. No measurements required. A handful, a splash, the calibration of someone who had been cooking rice long enough that the body knew the ratios without consulting the mind. The water warmed. The rice sat in the warming water, doing whatever rice does in the minutes before cooking begins. Absorbing, softening, preparing.

The rice cooked. Slowly, inevitably, without requiring belief or will or conviction. The heat did the work. The water did the work. You held a stick and stirred while I watched.

The rice was done. You served two bowls. Set one near the fire for me and kept yours.

We ate.

The rice was adequate. The adequacy of rice cooked by a competent but uninspired — or sleepy — chef. Each bite was a bite. Each swallow was a swallow. I still didn't really need to eat. The body that ran on conviction rather than biology didn't require fuel. But it felt good.

I ate because I wanted to.

Not needed. Wanted. The distinction was there, clear and whole. I wanted the rice. I wanted the warmth. I wanted the morning and the fire and the taste and the act of eating with another person while Xiǎohú watched and the mist lifted and the world continued its patient, unrequested operation.

Wanting was a beginning.

Not the answer. The question was still there, the how of letting go, the shape of the mountain from the base. But wanting was a beginning. You could want rice and eat rice and finish rice and set the bowl down and the wanting was complete and the letting go was natural.

I set the bowl down.

The mountain was ahead.

Xiǎohú, who was watching us both with amber eyes that held the early light like something precious.
