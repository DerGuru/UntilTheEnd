"I've been thinking," you said.

Morning. Trail south. The rhythm of walking: your boots, my bare feet, Xiǎohú's silent pads on stone and earth. The mountains rose ahead, each ridge higher than the last, the terrain ascending toward something that geography alone couldn't explain.

You'd been quiet since dawn. The processing silence; I recognized it now, the weight of a man whose model had been updated and who was reorganizing the data into a new structure. You walked and processed and the processing had a duration that you respected and I respected your respect and the walking filled the time.

"About everything," you said.

I waited.

"About the five stages."

The framing was yours: the segmentation, the numbered accounting of time and events that his methodical mind imposed on the chaotic, continuous, fundamentally unnumbered experience of living. Five stages. Five sections. Five accumulations of data that he had recorded and observed and cataloged in notebooks that he no longer opened because the data had exceeded the medium.

"The healings in the first," you said. "When you were hurt and then weren't. When things that should have killed you didn't. The timing: too precise for coincidence. Too consistent for luck."

You weren't asking. You were listing.

"The coincidences in the second. The paths that opened when you needed paths. The weather that changed when you needed change. The people who appeared at the exact moment they needed to appear. I wrote them down. All of them. Forty-seven instances."

Forty-seven. You'd counted. Of course you'd counted.

"Wei's death," you said.

The words landed differently from the others. Heavier. The name alone carried weight, the gravity of a child's name spoken in the context of everything I'd been learning about my own power. The name anchored the list, gave it a center, a point of maximum density around which all other observations orbited.

"The one time it didn't work," you said. "The one time the healings stopped, the coincidences failed, the protection wasn't there. The one time someone you cared about died and the thing that had been keeping you alive for centuries stood aside and let it happen."

I walked. The trail was clear; the terrain had been smooth since we'd turned south, the path opening with the suspicious ease of a road that was being prepared for us. Xiǎohú moved ahead, her gaze tracking the invisible.

"The shockwave in the fourth," you continued. "When you lost control. When the mountain — the MOUNTAIN — cracked. I was there. I saw it. That wasn't cultivation. That wasn't qi." The certainty of someone who'd reached Nascent Soul and knew the difference. "That was something else entirely."

Your breathing was steady. Controlled. The breath-management of someone who was building toward a conclusion and needed the architecture of his delivery to support the weight of what he was about to say.

"The field in the fifth. The rules. The deductions. The breakthrough. The second of consciousness that should have killed you and didn't."

You stopped walking.

I walked three more steps before the absence of your footsteps registered. Turned. You were standing on the trail, arms at your sides, your face showing the expression I'd seen exactly once before: the night he'd decided to stay, the night he'd looked at everything I was and decided that staying was worth the cost of staying.

"You've always done this," you said.

Not a question.

"The healings. The coincidences. The protection. The shockwave. The field. All of it. All of it was you. Not something external. Not something acting on you. YOU. Acting on the world. Unconsciously. Automatically. The way the heart beats, without decision, without awareness, without the subject's knowledge or consent."

I stood on the trail.

"And yesterday," you said. "In the village. That was the first time you did it consciously. The first time you SAW yourself doing what you've always done. But the mechanism — the power — the whatever-it-is that makes reality bend when you believe hard enough. That's been there since the beginning. Since before the beginning."

The wind moved through the trees. A bird — the three-note one, the retroactive one, the one that hadn't existed until last week — called from somewhere in the canopy.

"Only without knowing it," you said.

The sentence settled.

I heard it. Every word. The logic was — yours. Clean, linear, evidence-based, the careful accumulation of observations assembled into a conclusion that the observations demanded. You hadn't guessed. You hadn't intuited. You'd counted forty-seven coincidences and recorded every healing and measured the shockwave's force and documented the field's behavior and arrived, through the patient, relentless application of methodology, at the conclusion that all of it pointed to the same source.

Me.

"I'm not saying this to frighten you," you said. "Or to accuse you. I'm saying it because you need to hear it from outside. You need someone standing at a different angle to show you what you can't see from yours."

The mirror.

Your function here: not companion, not adversary. Mirror. The angle I didn't have. The external view that the wall couldn't block because the wall only controlled what I saw from inside. You stood outside. You saw the shape from the front while I saw it from within. And the shape he saw was consistent, was coherent, was the same shape from every angle.

I wanted to argue. The reflex was old, centuries old, embedded in the pattern of self-protection and denial and the wall's careful curation of what I was allowed to know. The argument was ready: coincidence, luck, cultivation affinity, environmental factors, systematic bias in his observation methodology. The counter-evidence was available. The objections were formed.

I didn't voice them.

Because the village. Because thirty people, shifted by belief. Because my hands on the earth and the vibration and the almost. Because the tree that grew overnight and the water that ran uphill and the reality that rewrote itself around me and the metallic taste and the teeth-vibration and the images of hands in light creating something with the absolute certainty of something that had been creating since before creating was a word.

The objections died.

"I don't know what that means," I said.

"Neither do I."

The admission was your gift: the willingness to arrive at a conclusion without demanding that the conclusion explain itself. You'd identified the pattern. You'd named the source. You didn't know what the pattern meant or what the source was or what came next. You knew what you knew and you didn't know what you didn't know and the boundary between the two was clean and precise and honestly maintained.

We stood on the trail. The mountain ahead. The path opening.

I didn't argue. Didn't deny. Didn't push away. The words seeped in — slowly, the way water seeps into stone, finding the cracks, filling the spaces, reshaping the structure from within without disturbing the surface.

*You've always done this.*

The wall didn't intervene.

*Only without knowing it.*

Nothing. No headache. No spike. No guardian.

The wall was letting me hear it.

Or the wall had given up.

Or the wall was me and I was letting myself hear it and the distinction between the wall and me had been the last lie and the lie was dying and the truth — not born yet, not quite, but crowning — was a thing I had always been and had never known and was about to know.

We walked. South. The path opened. The mountains rose.

Xiǎohú's eyes caught light from a source I couldn't see.
