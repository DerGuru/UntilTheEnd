We walked until the village was a concept rather than a location. Then we walked more.

You didn't speak. Your silence was different from the village's silence; theirs had been shock, yours was processing. The methodical, internal recalibration of a man whose theory had just been confirmed at a scale he hadn't anticipated. You walked behind me, three steps back and I could hear you thinking. I could hear the rhythm of your breathing change the way it changed when you were working through something difficult.

We stopped at a clearing farther south, where the canopy opened to sky and afternoon light fell in long, slanted columns that turned the dust into gold.

I sat on a rock and looked at nothing in particular. The dirt in front of my feet, the place where stones marked the spot for a fire. A broken tree, that was lying across the ground, its stump still rooted at its base. At my hands.

They were the same hands. The same lines, the same absence of calluses, the same short and practical nails of a woman who didn't care about nails. The same hands that had held chopsticks. That pulled weeds, touched bark and had pressed flat against the earth in the dark two nights ago. I turned them over. Closed the fists. Opened them. The mechanics were identical. Tendons, muscle, bone, the standard engineering of the human hand. Nothing had changed.

An hour ago, I had believed thirty people elsewhere.

*I. Have. Done. It. Consciously.*

The sentence arrived in its components, each word separate. I had stood in a village square and believed thirty people elsewhere.

The wall didn't intervene.

I waited for it — the headache, the spike, the guardian's gentle or forceful reminder that thinking about this was not permitted. Nothing came. The wall was there — I could feel it, the way you feel a presence in a dark room, something between you and the far wall. But it was inert. Watching, perhaps. Waiting, perhaps. But not intervening.

Either I'd outgrown its jurisdiction or the thing behind the wall had decided I was allowed to know this much.

"Thirty people," I said. To the air. To the clearing. To the dust-gold light, the trees and to Xiǎohú that lay at my feet with her eyes open and her warmth against my ankles. "Same effort as one. Same effort as none."

You were leaning against a tree. Arms crossed. Face neutral. The expression of a man who had decided to be present without being intrusive.

"Not killed," you said. "Not fought. Not even touched."

"No."

"So how did you do it?"

"I just decided they weren't there."

You let that sit for a moment. Your breathing did the thing it did when you were filing something.

"That's not a technique," you said.

You uncrossed your arms. Pushed off the tree. Walked two steps closer, close enough that your shadow fell across my hands. I looked up at you. Your face was doing the thing it did when you were about to say something you'd been building toward — the subtle pre-arrangement of features, the musculature of a man whose mouth was about to form words his body hadn't fully accepted.

"When you believed them gone," you said, "did you choose where they went?"

I thought about it. The believing — the moment of conviction, the instant of absolute certainty that the thirty cultivators were not in the village square — hadn't included a destination. I hadn't believed them *somewhere*. I'd believed them *not here*. The two hundred meters in the forest had been — what? Default? The universe's own interpretation of *not here*? The shortest possible distance that satisfied the condition of absence?

"No," I said. "I just believed them not there."

"And the not-there became somewhere." You continued your observations. "Not dead. Not injured. Placed two hundred meters in the forest."

You were making a point.

"Your belief did not exclude harm," you said. "So you didn't choose that consciously. But something in the way you believe seems to default to the least harmful outcome."

I stared at you.

"This could have been used as a weapon, but you aren't one," you said.

The words arrived and sat in the clearing between us and the dust turned gold around them.

A memory surfaced. Uninvited, the way they always did.

A courtyard. Flagstones dark with something that wasn't rain. A hand on my shoulder — heavy, proprietary, the hand of someone who had learned that pointing me at a thing was the same as removing the thing. He'd said something. A name, a direction, a city. I had gone. I had always gone.

The city had stopped being a city by the time I was done. The hand had stopped being a hand shortly after and I walked away from the ashes.

I walked for a very long time. Until I found a boy with a basket full of the wrong mushrooms and herbs, being attacked by a spirit boar.

I looked at my hands again. The same hands. Always the same.

The metallic taste was fading. The teeth-vibration had quieted to its baseline hum. The afternoon was warm, the clearing safe, my hands in my lap. I had done something that should have been impossible and the fact was settling into my body like a sinking ship into water. Slowly, completely, irrevocably.

I had edited reality. Consciously. Deliberately. With belief rather than force.

And the wall had let me.

I closed my hands.

I Opened them.

The same hands.

Everything different.
