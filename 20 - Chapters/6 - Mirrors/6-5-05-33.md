I sat in the field.

The grass was growing. Not visibly, not the time-lapse acceleration of plant biology, but the grass around me was taller than the grass further away. The blades closest to where I sat were longer, greener, more vital. As if my presence nourished them. As if the excess energy of the mechanism, running continuously, involuntarily, leaked into the ground and fed whatever was nearest.

Where a hundred and thirty-nine people had stood — no. Where a hundred and thirty-nine people had *been*. There were no impressions. No trampled grass, no scuffed earth, no evidence. Just grass. Just wind. Just the empty field.

I let the memories come again. The thousands of years before Wei, the accumulated lifetime of a self that had believed itself into immortality and then walked the world it had shaped, night after night, millennium after millennium.

Battlefields.

A plain where two armies had met and I'd walked between them. I'd fought. I always fought — picked a side or didn't, it hadn't mattered, the result was the same. The arrows that should have killed me didn't. The spears slid past. The men on both sides died and I remained standing in a field of the dead and the war was over because I'd ended it.

Every time.

A siege that lasted four months until I walked through the gate. I'd been inside the walls, or outside — the details had compressed. The smell remained. Burning grain, wet stone, the particular sweetness of decay in summer heat. A river crossing where the water ran red for three days until I crossed it and the dying stopped.

I'd ended wars the way storms end droughts. By arriving. By being present. By the sheer weight of something that couldn't be killed walking into a space where killing was the point.

And then I'd stopped needing to fight. Somewhere across the centuries, I'd crossed a threshold that had no name. Cultivation couldn't describe it. Technique couldn't contain it. I'd walk onto a battlefield and the fighting would stop. Not because I intervened. Because I arrived. Because the sheer weight of what I was pressed into a space where killing was the point, and the killing lost the argument.

Someone had figured out what I was. Not the truth. Not the whole of it. But enough. Enough to know that I could be aimed. That a word and a direction were sufficient. He'd kept me for centuries, or I'd let him keep me, the distinction blurred by time. Being his weapon. His answer to every problem that had a location.

I remembered the day I stopped being aimed. There was only ash left. What remained was the walking — for a very long time.

Then faces poured into me again.

A woman who laughed too loudly in a market I couldn't place. A child who looked like Wei but wasn't. The chronology dissolving under the weight of quantity. An old man who told me his name but I'd forgotten it before he finished. A cultivation master I'd sought out — not because I needed the technique, I'd already known more than he could teach, but because I'd wanted the precision. The refinement. The vocabulary for what my body already did. He'd been proud to have such an apt student. He'd died believing he'd created something.

Gone. All of them. Dead. The mortality that I couldn't share, the ending that I couldn't reach, the natural conclusion that every face I'd ever seen had arrived at while I continued. While I walked. While I believed in my own continuation. I continued when the faces stopped and I walked past them the way I walked past stones and trees and rivers.

The pattern you had named.

Proximity. Attachment. Loss. Continuation. The four-beat rhythm of my existence. Get close, feel something, lose it, keep going. Repeat. Across centuries. Across millennia. The pattern was not chosen. The pattern was structural — the inevitable product of a immortal consciousness moving through a mortal world. Things near me lived. Things near me died. I continued.

And every time — every loss, every face that stopped, every name forgotten — the deep belief deepened. *Everything I love dies.* The evidence accumulated. Each loss confirmed the conviction. Each confirmation strengthened the belief. Each strengthening made the next loss more likely because my belief shaped reality and reality produced loss. And loss confirmed the belief.

A spiral. Downward. Self-reinforcing. Millennia deep.

You sat beside me. You'd arrived without announcement — walked across the field, settled in the grass, placed a bowl of rice between us. Xiǎohú circled around you twice, and lay down against my thigh. The bowl sat there. The rice cooled. Neither of us touched it.

You didn't speak.

You sat. You were present. The fact of your presence — mortal, temporary, the warmth of a human body aging in real time — was the anchor.

The faces continued. I let them come. Let them pass. Let the tide of many thousand years wash through me and recede.

Loneliness.

So comprehensive that it wasn't experienced as loneliness anymore because I had no reference point for non-loneliness. I hadn't known I was lonely because I'd always been lonely because immortality was loneliness. The two were the same condition described in different vocabularies.

The rice cooled.

The shadows moved. Time passed — mortal time, human time, time measured by the angle of light, the temperature of rice and the slow, reliable progression of a day through its phases. I sat in the empty field letting the memories flow. The grass grew and the world continued its operations around a woman who was remembering how long she'd been alone.

Eventually, the tide receded.

The faces dimmed. The losses stepped back from the foreground. I was left with the residue — the emotional sediment of millennia compressed into an afternoon, the exhaustion of a consciousness that had just reviewed its entire portfolio and found it heavy.

The rice was cold.

You hadn't touched it either. You'd sat, present, patient, through the entire tide. Your face was worried. Mine tired. 

I picked up the bowl. Rice was rice.

I ate.

Xiǎohú must have fallen asleep and her ear twitched.

You stood. Brushed grass from your knees.

"Their camp is still out there," you said. "South. I saw the smoke yesterday."

You walked toward the southern ridge. Your pack was light. Your steps had direction.

I didn't ask. The field was enough company. Xiǎohú was warm against my leg.
