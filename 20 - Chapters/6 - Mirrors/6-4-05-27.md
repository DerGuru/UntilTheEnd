The weight of eons arriving all at once and my knees received it. They surrendered and I went down.

I knelt in the water. It rose to my hips — cold, immediate, the physical insistence of a river that didn't care about revelations or transformations or the cosmic significance of the woman kneeling in its current. The fabric of my clothes spread around me, darkening with water, the material performing its simple chemistry of absorption.

You stepped toward the bank's edge. But stopped.

I felt the hesitation from the water. The weight of your step, the shift forward, then the halt. You wanted to come in. Every line of your body said *help her*. But something held you at the edge — the same instinct that had kept you writing instead of shaking me awake hours earlier. You recognized the difference between drowning and diving.

You stayed on the bank.

The memory came. All of them. The entire sequence, from creating to now, from the place before places to the river where I knelt. Emotional, not chronological. The memories arrived in the order of their impact, their weight, their gravitational pull on the center of what I was.

My hands — the original hands, the hands before the hands I had now — shaping light into form, believing something formless into structure, saying *exist* and existence complied. The solitude of that act. The absolute, cosmic, incomprehensible solitude of a self creating. Creating because creating was what I was. Creating because the alternative was the ending I feared.

Then came the fear. The fear that preceded immortality, the vertigo of a consciousness confronting its own potential nonexistence. The dizziness. The grasping. The desperate, irrational, overwhelmingly powerful belief that I MUST NOT END. And the belief becoming true. Becoming the foundation. Becoming the bedrock upon which form and identity were built.

Then the millennia. The fighting. The killing. The walking. Faces. Thousands of faces, faces I'd seen and spoken to — walked past and forgotten. Forgetting was the worst. Not the wall's erasure, but the natural, organic forgetting of a mind that had lived so long that most of its content had been overwritten by newer content. I'd forgotten more lives than most civilizations had produced. I'd forgotten languages, cultures, continents, epochs. The forgetting was not a failure of memory but a feature of duration.

And among the thousands — closer than the rest, sharper, recurring — the ones who stayed. The ones who walked beside me. Who watched. Who wrote things down. Faces I couldn't hold, names I couldn't retrieve, but the *shape* of their presence was identical every time. Someone patient, someone precise, someone who believed that the record mattered more than the recorder. They appeared in the scroll-margins. In the field reports. In the neat, obsessive notation of dates and locations, the subject's state before and after each observation. They had always been there. One after another, across the millennia, each one finding me or being found, each one picking up the work where the last had been erased.

The scrolls, the tablets, the carvings in shrines, caves and temples. Written by *them*. About *me*.

The understanding landed and I looked at the bank where you sat with your notebook, your pen and your desperate documentation. The understanding became specific, became personal, became a weight I was not prepared for.

Wei's face was there. Among the thousands, exact, unforgettable, the one face that the wall could not erase because the attachment that produced it was stronger than the mechanism that managed it. His face. Small. Fearful. Eyes wide with the desperate clarity of a child who knows he cannot save himself. *Help me!*

His death was there. Again. The gate. The fall. The silence. The belief that failed — *he will live* — defeated by the deeper belief that *everything I love dies*. The structural, mechanical, systematic murder-that-wasn't-murder. The pattern.

My pattern.

The memories washed through me. Not sequentially. Simultaneously. Each one occupying its own frequency, its own channel, the way a river carries a thousand different currents within a single flow. I was all of them at once. The creator, the afraid, the walking, the loving, the losing, the forgetting.

My breath went shallow.

The river — the river I was kneeling in, the real river, the now-river — was still. I noticed this in the way you notice details during seismic events. Peripherally, absurdly, the mind cataloging irrelevance while the main process consumes everything. The river had stopped flowing. The current had stilled. The water around my knees was motionless. Still. As if the river had stopped breathing.

I'd done that. Unconsciously. The engine, unfrozen by the knowledge, reactivated by the certainty, had produced a new edit. The kneeling required stillness and the belief required compliance.

I didn't drown as the water settled.

Of course I didn't. Because I believed I couldn't drown. Because the belief was the reality and the reality was the belief and the circularity was the point.

The irony was structural. A woman who couldn't die because she believed she couldn't die, kneeling in a river that couldn't flow because she believed in stillness.

You sat on the bank and didn't approach. You'd positioned yourself at the water's edge, close enough to reach, far enough to not intrude. Your notebook was on your knees. Open. But the pen had stopped.

There was nothing to write. A woman kneeling in a river. A river that had stopped flowing. 

Still, unknown to you, you were witnessing the longest memory of the series.

You were witnessing me remembering myself.

The weight settled. The memories didn't end. They didn't have endings, because the timeline they described was ongoing, was still happening, would continue happening until I stopped believing it would. But the flood receded. The memories took their positions. Present now. Arranged around me like furniture in a room I'd just entered.

My breath steadied. The river resumed. Slowly, tentatively, the current returning with the caution of a thing that had been interrupted by something it didn't understand. The water moved against my knees. Cold. Real.

I closed my eyes. I opened them.

Light. Water. Sky. Trees. Stones. You.
