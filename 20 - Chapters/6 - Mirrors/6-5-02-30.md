The flame bent around my fingers like water around a stone.

It was nighttime. Xiǎohú had returned from her day's wandering precisely at dusk, a small rabbit hanging from her jaws. She'd dropped it at the edge of the fire, sat down, and looked at us with the patient expectation of a hunter who had done her part and was now waiting for the kitchen to catch up. You'd cleaned it. I'd watched. We'd shared. Three portions — two human, one fox. Xiǎohú had eaten hers in the grass, methodical, unhurried, then curled up by the fire and closed her eyes.

Now you were asleep — or rather performing sleep. I recognized the controlled breathing of a man who had learned that some experiments needed to happen without witnesses. The fire was mortal-fire, flint-lit, burning on the chemistry of wood and oxygen and the reliable physics of combustion.

I held my hand over it.

Xiǎohú opened one eye.

"I am mortal."

The words a whisper. Different this time. Quiet, concentrated, the careful formulation of someone who had refined the experiment. Close-range. Controlled conditions. Precise intention.

The flame bent.

The fire reached for my hand and diverted, curving around my fingers with the gentle, absolute authority of a force encountering an object it could not touch. The flame's light moved across my skin — warm, orange, visible — but the heat did not arrive. The glow without the burn. The appearance without the substance.

I pressed my hand into the fire.

Into the center of the flames, where the combustion was hottest, where the wood was brightest, where even cultivators with their reinforced bodies would have felt the cellular destruction of tissue exposed to extreme temperature.

Nothing.

The flame wrapped around my palm and fingers. The hand sat in fire the way a stone sits in water — immersed and surrounded, yet untouched. My hand should have been cooking. Instead I was experiencing the warmth as sensation without consequence.

You were up.

"What—" You grabbed my arm and pulled. The motion was urgent, instinctive, the response of a man who had seen a hand in fire. Your instincts had overridden your knowledge. You pulled my hand free, held the wrist and looked at the skin of my fingers and palm.

Nothing. Perfect, unmarked, impossibly intact skin.

You breathed. Heavy. Saw the hand. Saw the fire. Saw me.

Let go.

"It doesn't work," I said.

"What — believing you're mortal?"

"That's the point." I looked at my hand. Perfect. Unmarked. The hand of a woman who could say *mortal* as many times as she wanted and never mean it where it mattered. "I can say it. I can want it. But I don't really believe it. Not in the way it counts."

You sat back. The fire crackled between us — the fire that had just demonstrated, with absolute physical clarity, that my belief in my own invulnerability was more powerful than my will to be vulnerable. The fire didn't care about my intentions. The fire cared about my convictions. And my convictions — the deep, structural, millennia-old convictions that constituted my being rather than my thinking — said that this woman does not burn.

"It's a loop," I said. The words arrived mechanically, the product of logic rather than emotion. "I know I'm making myself immortal. I want to stop. But wanting to stop requires believing I can stop. And believing I can stop requires believing I'm not fundamentally immortal. And I am fundamentally immortal. So the belief in stopping contradicts the belief in being and the being wins because the being is older and deeper and more total than anything I can generate by wanting."

"To believe you don't believe," you said.

"Exactly."

"That's — a problem."

"Yes."

The fire burned. The impossibility sat between us — the logical trap, the philosophical prison, the infinite recursion of a consciousness trying to override itself. You can't stop believing something by believing you don't believe it. The believing-you-don't-believe is itself a belief and it doesn't cancel the original — it adds a layer. Beliefs don't subtract. They only add.

You looked at my hand. Still holding it, absently, the way you hold something after the urgency has passed but the impulse hasn't. Your fingers were warm. Mortal-warm. The warmth of a body that could burn.

"Then how?" you said.

"I don't know."

Silence. The fire. The night. Two people at the edge of an impossibility, looking at it the way you look at a wall without a door.

"Yet," I added. Because the *yet* was important. Because the frustration was a fuel, not a conclusion. Because the inability to change now was not the inability to change ever. After all, as long as I was immortal, I had all the time in the world.
 
Xiǎohú watched with eyes that reflected a fire that couldn't burn the woman she watched.

You let go of my hand.

"But I will figure it out. I have to. I want to. I need to."
