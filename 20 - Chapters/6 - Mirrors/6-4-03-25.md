You shook my shoulder.

Gently first. Then harder. The desperation of a man who had spent three hours talking to someone who wasn't home, who had tried everything. Voice, touch, the name spoken in every tone from whisper to shout. You'd arrived at the physical option because the other options had been exhausted.

I blinked.

The present arrived. Sharp and violent, the sensory assault of a consciousness returning to a body that had been running on automatic. Everything was too much. The light was too bright. The air was too cold. The grass was too green. Each stimulus arrived at full volume, unfiltered, unmodulated, as if the senses had been recalibrated again during the absence and the new calibration was set to maximum.

"How long was I gone?"

"More than three hours."

Your face was tired with the deep tiredness of sustained concern. Three hours of watching, writing and not knowing. Not being able to help. Your eyes were red. Your hand, when you removed it from my shoulder, trembled.

I reached for the ground. My hand found the grass. Individual blades, each one vivid, each blade impossibly exact in its greenness and its shape and its relationship to the soil that held it. I gripped. Hard. The grass resisted, then yielded, then held. Real. Solid. Present.

I needed the earth under me. Needed the physical, tangible, gravitational confirmation that I was here. Past the before, past the place of creation. Here. In a clearing. On a mountain. With a man who carried a notebook and a knife. With a patience that exceeded the patience of normal persons.

"You were speaking," you said. "Words I didn't recognize. But I wrote what I could."

Your notebook was in your lap. Open. Pages filled with tight, rushed handwriting. Three hours of documentation. Three hours of doing the only thing you could.

I didn't look at what you'd written. Not yet. The memory was still reverberating. The echoes of creation, the afterimage of the place before places, the residual sensation of a self that had existed before names and forms and had believed with a certainty that the memory insisted was indistinguishable from making.

The reframing happened fast. Like a lens clicking into focus. The entire history of my life, all my lives in every age, every experience, every moment recorded and unrecorded, rotated. Shifted. Showed its other face.

A wound healing — mine, the belief that I couldn't be harmed — and at the same time Wei's face, too young, too stubborn — and at the same time the mountain cracking, the shockwave, stone splitting because I — the paths opening, weather shifting, the world rearranging itself around the shape of my — Wei falling, the settlement gate, the cultivator's technique, the fall happening because I believed everything I loved — the voice, *Not now*, *Enough*, *Keep going*, the voice that was mine, had always been mine, speaking from behind the barrier in three-word fragments because the wall only allowed — the healings, every healing, every survival, not a guardian, not an external force, me, my conviction translating itself into invulnerability the same way it translated stones into new positions and water into new — the reflection that smiled before I did and the field where the rules broke and the coincidences that weren't coincidences, the paths clearing because I believed in clear paths, the weather because I believed in favorable weather, the people who appeared because I believed in outcomes that — Wei, always Wei, the deep conviction overriding the surface wish, the pattern not the murder, the belief that everything I loved died operating with the same automatic unconscious authority as the belief that I couldn't burn and both of them mine, mine — the second voice not foreign, not external, a part of me that knew, knew, that had always known, speaking from behind the wall in the only way the wall allowed — the anomalies, every impossible thing, every breach of physics pouring through simultaneously, the missing mountain flank the reversed water the shifted stones the reflection the shockwave the field the rules the — all of it at once, layered, overlapping, each one illuminating the others, each one the same truth wearing a different face.

It wasn't just a thought. It was an experience. Physical, visceral, the sensation of everything you've ever known turning inside out and revealing that the inside had always been the outside. That the thing you'd been looking at was the thing you'd been looking from.

I hadn't been surprised. I should have been.

That was the strange part. The reframing produced confirmation. The terrible confirmation that arrives when the thing you suspected turns out to be true.

I'd known. Some part of me — the part behind the wall, the part that smiled in reflections — had always known. The reframing didn't provide new information. 

It was old information finally acknowledged.

I sat in the grass. The clearing was bright. The fog was gone. The sun was direct and honest and showed everything without mercy.

I knew.
