I sat by the fire. Hands on knees. Eyes open. Xiǎohú still in my lap. I drifted.

The transition was seamless. No gap, no threshold, no moment of departure. I was in the clearing and then I was in the memory and the two states coexisted the way the two sides of a mirror coexist. Opposite, simultaneous, each one reflecting the other into infinity.

The memory was clear.

This was glass-clear. The kind of clarity that doesn't belong to memory because memory is always reconstructive. This clarity was primary.

I was there.

In the place before places. Like I was being before being. Light surrounded me. It immersed me and I was unable to identify the boundary between self and medium.

I created. Or so the vision insisted.

I saw it now, through the wall's remaining interference, through the fragments' abbreviation. I saw myself. Not the self I was now, not Yun the wanderer, me as a cultivator, as a woman standing in rivers and moving stones. An earlier self. A self that existed before Yun, before names, before forms, before the categories that consciousness uses to divide experience into manageable portions.

I created because creating might have been what I was. The creating was the being. Or so the memory insisted.

I believed.

The same mechanism that moved the stones and redirected the water, but at a scale so vast that the word *mechanism* collapsed under its own inadequacy. I believed things into existence. The absolute, irrefutable certainty that preceded language, that exceeded thought and constituted reality itself.

But the memory was warped. Distorted by age, by the wall, by the sheer incompatibility between what I'd been and what I was now. It insisted on showing me creation — light, form, time — as if I'd made them. As if the world had started with me. The images had the grammar of origin stories, the syntax of myths, the structure of a consciousness so old that it had confused *being present at the beginning* with *being the beginning*.

I didn't trust it. Not fully. The memory was real but the interpretation was mine, and my interpretation was of a mind that had just learned it could move stones by believing, that had just asked whether its beliefs had killed a child. Of course the memory scaled upward. Of course it reached for the largest possible frame. That was what minds did with incomplete data — they filled gaps with the biggest available story.

But it also showed me the *why*.

Fear. The oldest fear. The fear of ending, of the creating stopping, of the being ceasing, of the self dissolving back into the state from which it had emerged. The fear was irrational, because the self that feared ending was the self that had created beginning and a self that could create beginning could presumably create continuation. But fear is not rational. Fear is the foundational conviction that precedes logic and my fear — the fear of ending — was the deepest conviction I held.

So I believed myself immortal.

I believed myself immortal as — in the memory — I'd believed light into existence. Absolutely, foundationally.

The belief became true. As all my beliefs became true. My immortality was not discovered. Not bestowed. Not achieved. Created. By me. From me. The product of a fear so old that it had become invisible.

And the rest — the rest the memory insisted on, the vast claims, the cosmic scale — I held at arm's length. Noted. Not accepted. The wall was thin but not gone, and the part of me that remained Yun, that remained a woman sitting by a fire with a fox in her lap, refused to swallow the story whole. Not yet. Maybe not ever.

My lips were moving.

In the clearing — the other reality, the one where my body sat with its hands on its knees, its conscious mind absent — my lips were forming words. Not my words. Words from the memory. Fragments. Syllables from something older than language, delivered in a voice that didn't sound like mine.

"Yun?"

I heard your voice. It was distant. Arriving through the membrane between the memory and the present, filtered, attenuated, like sound through water.

No response.

"YUN."

Loud. Sharp. Wrong. Wrong name, wrong tone, the voice of a man whose calm had fractured against the surface of a situation his calm wasn't designed for. Three hours of sitting beside a woman who wasn't present, who was somewhere else, somewhere unreachable.

No response.

Fog had gathered while I was gone. The mountain kind — rising from the valleys after dark, filling the spaces between trees, settling into clearings with the patience of something that had nowhere else to be. Three hours of absence and the world had wrapped itself in grey.

The fog stopped at the edge of the clearing. A wall of grey, dense and motionless, pressing against the treeline as if something inside the clearing was holding it there. The air within was sharp, clear, unnaturally still.

The moon had risen. Its light was silver and cold, and it fell into the clearing the way water fills a bowl — completely, without effort.

I could see her. The woman by the fire with the fox in her lap, hands on her knees, lips moving. I could see her from above, from outside, from the place where the memory held me. She looked small. She looked like someone I'd known a long time ago.

The light on her face intensified. Sharpened. Became something more than moonlight — sudden, total, filling the space the darkness had vacated with the completeness of something that had been waiting for permission. Her open eyes saw nothing that was in front of them. Not the clearing. Not the fire. Not the night. Not the mountain.

Something older.

Something that was me.

You sat beside her. Your notebook was open, open for the first time since the data had exceeded the medium. You were writing. Fast. Catching the words her lips formed. Catching the changes in her expression. Catching whatever you could catch of a process you couldn't see, couldn't access, couldn't participate in.

Your handwriting was tight. Rushed. Nearly illegible. You were trying to hold her in ink. Pin the moment to paper before it dissolved.

But she was somewhere before memory, remembering something that memory couldn't contain.

You wrote.

I remembered.
