They found us on an open field. Grass. A few wildflowers. The kind of space that exists in mountains because mountains occasionally produce flatness. Unremarkable.

A hundred and thirty-nine cultivators. I counted because counting gave me something to do with the part of my mind that wasn't preparing for what I was about to do. A hundred and thirty-nine. Twenty-seven more than the ridge. Six weeks of feeding on my unconscious edits and they'd grown by a quarter. They'd recruited, or merged, or produced new members through whatever mechanism sects used to grow.

The Pencil stood at the center. Lian Zhuo — the name arrived, unbidden, from somewhere I hadn't stored it. His real name. The name I'd avoided knowing because knowing names meant knowing people and knowing people meant caring. And I didn't want to care with what I was about to do.

He looked at me. Curious. The curiosity of a man who had been studying something from a distance and was now close enough to see its details.

"We want to understand," he said.

The words were clear. Audible across the field's distance, carried by the resonance of a cultivator's controlled voice. Simple words. Honest words, possibly. The words of someone who had been harvesting my unconscious edits and wanted to know the nature of the source.

I stood between them. Xiǎohú at my ankle, pressed against my leg.

You were fifty meters behind. I'd sent you back, my hand on your chest, the physical push that said *not you, not here, not now*. Your face had shown the argument you didn't make, the objection you didn't voice, the protest you swallowed. You'd looked at my face and whatever you saw there was enough. You swallowed the protest and stepped back.

Lian Zhuo waited. Patient. They were always patient.

"You don't understand," I said. My voice boomed across the field without effort. I didn't need a cultivator's resonance. "I want to be left alone."

Simple. Clear. The same thing I'd wanted since the mountains. Since the ridge. Since the first time I'd noticed them following, collecting, building their little empire on the residual energy of my existence.

Lian Zhuo's expression didn't change. The curiosity remained, but something harder moved behind it. Something that had been patient for six weeks and had decided that patience was no longer the strategy.

"We've been following you for months," he said. The voice was different now. The scholarly politeness stripped away, replaced by the tone of a man who had committed too much to walk away. "Tracking your movements. Mapping your patterns. Adjusting our entire sect's trajectory to accommodate your wandering."

He stepped forward. One step. The seventeen behind him shifted with him, a formation move, practiced.

"You will come with us."

Xiǎohú hissed.

The sound cut across the field — small, sharp, disproportionate. A fox-spirit the size of a cat, ears flat, teeth bared at a hundred and thirty-nine cultivators. Absurd. Brave. The hiss of something that didn't calculate odds.

Hadn't he learned from the stasis field? Was he so sure now of himself, with his gathered power — with my power — that he could force me? 

Me?

Something broke. Like a rope that has been fraying for weeks — one strand, then another, then all of them at once, and the thing it held crashes down.

Months. Months of being followed. Months of them farming the excess. Months of walking south and knowing they walked behind. Of adjusting routes. Of pretending the shadow wasn't there. Of being someone's crop, someone's resource, someone's *project*.

And now demands. Now *you will come with us*. As if I were something to be collected.

Lian Zhuo didn't look at the fox. His eyes stayed on me. "The energy you produce unconsciously could advance our entire—"

I looked at Lian Zhuo. At the seventeen around him — his inner circle, the ones who understood what they'd been harvesting, who had built their methodology around it, who had turned parasitism into practice.

*Gone.*

A single thought. A statement of fact directed at eighteen people who had made themselves into a problem I no longer intended to have. Whatever the Pencil was about to say, he would never finish his sentence. The seventeen around him — the architects, the strategists, the ones who had known exactly what they were doing. Gone.

I looked around me.

A hundred and twenty-one faces. The outer ring. The followers, the joiners, the ones who had come because sects offered purpose and structure and the illusion of progress.

"Scatter."

Gone as well. Distributed across the land like a hand sweeping pieces off a board. One to a southern province. One to a mountain pass. One to a river delta. A hundred and twenty-one people in a hundred and twenty-one different locations, spread everywhere, alone, separated, the sect dissolved not by confrontation but by geography.

The field was empty.

Grass. Wind. Wildflowers. Morning light on nothing.

The silence was absolute. The silence of absence. The silence of a stage that had characters playing and was now empty. 

I stood on the empty field. Grass beneath my feet. Wind in my hair. The metallic taste overwhelming. My hands were steady. That was the worst part — the steadiness.

You walked toward me. Fast. The pace of a man who had watched over a hundred people vanish and needed answers from the only person left.

You reached me.

"What did you do?" You asked in the voice of a man who was afraid.

"I removed them."

"Where?"

"The inner circle — from everywhere. I don't know." I gestured at the field, the treeline, the mountains, the nothing. "The rest are scattered. Across the land. Individually."

"You don't know?" You repeated it. Testing the words. "You don't know where eighteen people are?"

"No."

"Are they alive?"

Wind. Grass. The empty field.

"I didn't believe them dead," I said. Which wasn't the same as believing them alive and we both knew it.

You looked at the field. The grass, untrampled. The wildflowers undisturbed. As if a hundred and thirty-nine people hadn't stood here thirty seconds ago. Even the evidence was gone.

You didn't sit down.

Power outward. Failure inward.

I'd solved the external problem. Completely. Permanently. With casual efficiency.

And nothing inside me had changed.

I was still immortal. Still unable to burn. Still shaping the world. Still running the machinery. But at least there was nobody left to harvest.

I sat down in the grass. The field was empty. The sky was clear. The world was exactly what I believed it to be.

I was cold. Inside. Lodged somewhere behind the sternum, the residue of what I'd just done with the ease of someone brushing crumbs off a table. A hundred and thirty-nine people. Brushed away. And my hands hadn't even trembled. The absence of hesitation where hesitation should have been.

You stood beside me. You didn't sit down.

"Now what?" you said.

I didn't answer. Didn't have a real answer.

That was the problem.
