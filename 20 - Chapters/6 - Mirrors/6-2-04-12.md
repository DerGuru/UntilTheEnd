I worked on your meridians while the water heated. The blockages were stubborn — the same ones, the same places, the damage repeating itself like a sentence the body couldn't stop writing. But the secondary channels were cleaner now. Slowly.

You were cooking. The evening ritual, the human ceremony of preparing food over fire, the domestic anchor that kept us tethered to the ordinary. The pot first, the water second, the rice third, the herbs last. Always last. The herbs went in at the end because that was when they released the most flavor. Principle, not luxury.

The herbs smelled wrong.

They were mountain herbs. Wild rosemary. A bitter root and something sharp and green that grew where the altitude thinned the air. You mixed them with the confidence of a man who had learned to cook from necessity and had converted necessity into competence.

But the proportions were wrong.

Too much of the sharp one. Too little of the bitter. The ratio was — familiar. Not from your cooking, not from any meal I'd eaten in the last several months. From before. From a kitchen that wasn't a kitchen, from a fire that wasn't this fire, from a boy who couldn't cook but cooked anyway because stubbornness was his primary relationship to the world.

Wei.

The memory arrived like the creation images — present, complete. The wall didn't intervene. The wall had been quiet all day, since the village, since the conscious editing that should have triggered every defensive mechanism it possessed and had triggered none.

The memory was clear. A continuous, coherent sequence of moments, preserved the painful, absolute clarity.

I stopped eating.

The herbs' smell filled the clearing, wrong in the way they were wrong, right in the way they were right, the paradox of a scent that belonged to two different times and neither of them was now.

Wei.

The forest had been different. Denser. Southern. The kind of forest where the canopy closed completely and the light that reached the ground was green and filtered and heavy with moisture. I'd been walking. I was always walking, the eternal transit, the perpetual motion of someone who had nowhere to be and all of time to not be there. The walking was old. Centuries old. Millennia old. The forest was one of thousands and the trail was one of millions where I was passing through it the way light passes through glass. Present, transient, leaving nothing.

He was sitting on a rock.

Small. Dirty. A child. Twelve. The age where humans are large enough to be independent, small enough to be vulnerable and stubborn enough to be both at once. His clothes were torn. His hair was a catastrophe. His hands were in the basket, sorting and arranging mushrooms and herbs. Like he had done the last few days before.

I almost walked past him.

That was the detail that the memory emphasized — the *almost*. The fraction of a second between seeing and not-seeing, between noticing and ignoring, between the reflex of continued walking and the decision to stop. I had spent centuries not-stopping. Not-seeing. Not-noticing. The wall made it easy. The erasure took care of the things that might have made me pause, the guardian removed the hooks that might have caught my attention. I walked. I passed.

I almost walked past him.

But I stopped.

The stopping was the choice. A small choice. The smallest possible unit of decision: I saw a child and instead of walking, I stopped.

He looked up.

His face was — I remembered every line. The jaw set in the permanent defiance of a child who had been told *no* so many times that *no* had become his operating system. The eyes — dark, sharp, too intelligent for his face, the eyes of a boy who saw everything. The smear of dirt across his left cheek. The scratch on his right hand, half-healed, evidence of a fight with something that had probably been inanimate.

He looked up at me and smiled. His mouth opened, "You're standing in my light."

As if I were the intrusion. As if the forest belonged to him and the relevant issue was the shadow I was casting on his basket.

I should have walked on. The wall should have intervened, should have smoothed the hook, blunted the attention, redirected the interest toward the trail, the walking and the centuries of transit that were my assigned pattern. The wall should have made me forget him.

Yet, it didn't.

The choice had been mine. Not the wall's. Not the guardian's. Nor any of the mechanisms' that managed my experience, that curated my memory and decided what I was allowed to care about. Mine. I chose to stop. I chose to look. I chose to help a dirty, stubborn child sitting on a rock in a forest and I chose — for the first time in millennia — to be interested.

That was the word. Interest. Pure, undecorated interest. The simple, radical act of paying attention.

I stepped out of his light.

He went back to his digging. Didn't thank me. Didn't acknowledge the courtesy. Simply returned to his mushrooms and herbs, as if I had been a minor atmospheric disturbance that had resolved itself and required no further attention.

I sat down.

Not close. Ten meters away. On a rock of my own. The sitting was as unreasonable as the stopping, as inexplicable as the looking, as unprecedented as the interest. I had no reason to sit. I had nowhere to sit toward. I had centuries of walking ahead of me, behind me and around me. The temporal geometry of an immortal life made all directions equivalent.

I sat and I watched.

"You're still here," he said, without looking up.

"Yes."

"Why?"

I didn't answer. I didn't have an answer. 

He shrugged. The shrug of someone who had cataloged me as a minor mystery. He went back to his sorting. His small hands worked with the absolute commitment of someone who believed that what he was doing mattered.

His hands smelled like herbs. The wrong ones. Too much of the sharp. Too little of the bitter.

"You stopped eating," you said.

I blinked. The clearing. The fire. The pot of rice and herbs. Evening. Now.

I looked at my plate. The herbs smelled wrong. Smelled right. Both.

"The herbs," I said.

"What about them?"

"He mixed them the same way."

You didn't ask who. You knew.

You poured more rice into my bowl. The gesture was practical, domestic, deliberately ordinary. A man maintaining the rituals that maintained the world.

"Eat," you said.

I ate. The herbs tasted wrong. Tasted right.

You just poured more rice.
