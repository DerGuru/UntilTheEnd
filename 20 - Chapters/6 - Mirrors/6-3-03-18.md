The question took three days to form.

The logic was simple — three premises stacked like stones, the conclusion on top. The words were common. A child could have formed the sentence.

It took three days because I was afraid of it.

The fear was personal. Mine. The fear of a woman who had arrived at the edge of a drop and could see the bottom.

I walked. Ate. Slept. Functioned. The trail continued south, the path opening, the terrain cooperating with the suspicious ease of a world that was being shaped by someone who believed in clear paths. I walked through the world I was unconsciously sculpting and thought about the question I wasn't ready to ask.

You could see the approach. The way my face changed when the thought came close, how I stopped mid-sentence, how my hands found each other and held, the unconscious self-grip of someone bracing for impact. You saw these things and said nothing. Cooked. Counted provisions. Discussed weather. Built the scaffolding of normalcy around a space that was anything but normal.

On the third evening, the fire was burning.

I hadn't lit it. I'd believed it should be burning. And the thought came, quiet and terrible, that the belief had produced the fire. That the fire had always been burning. I sat across from the flames. They moved and I thought about what fire was and what belief was and whether fire knew it was being believed.

The fire had gone out earlier that day. I'd put it out unconsciously, without deciding, as I'd been probably putting out and relighting fires for millennia without knowing. The fire going out and coming back. Could that be my belief fluctuating? My attention wandering? My convictions shifting as convictions shift? Not dramatically, but in the small, constant adjustments that consciousness makes as it moves through the world.

The fire was back now. I'd relit it unconsciously. The fire had always been burning.

The logic arrived.

What if my belief shapes reality?

I have always believed, at the deepest level, in the absolute, foundational certainty that constituted not my thought but my identity — I have always believed that everything I love dies.

Wei died.

The conclusion sat on top. I could see it. The way you see a shape in the dark. The outline was there. The terrible, simple outline of a conclusion that the premises demanded.

My lips moved.

I formed the sentence. In the air. Without voice. I formed the sentence the way you form a word in a language you're afraid to speak — the shape in the mouth, the presence in the throat, the readiness at the lips.

I didn't say it.

You saw.

You were across the fire, sitting on a log, your hands on your knees. You saw my lips move. Saw the shape of the unsaid sentence. Saw the thing forming in my face — the understanding, the comprehension, the quality of a realization that spreads like cracks across glass. You saw the glass cracking.

"Yun?"

Your voice. The name an anchor. The syllable a rope thrown to someone standing at the edge of something deep and dark and honest.

"Have I killed Wei?"

Quiet. Almost inaudible.

Have I killed Wei?

Me?

If belief shapes reality. If my deepest belief was that everything I love dies. If I loved Wei. Then my belief — my unconscious, automatic, foundational belief — killed him. Causally. Structurally. The way a riverbed kills by directing water.

The fire burned.

You didn't breathe. I could the held breath. You heard the question. You understood the question. You understood what the question meant and the premises that needed to be there, to support it. And the evidence that supported the premises.

You didn't answer.

The world didn't answer.

The question hung in the air between us — over the fire, through the smoke, in the space where answers were supposed to form. The space stayed empty. The question remained. Because the question itself was the endpoint.

Had my belief killed Wei?

Maybe.

Maybe not.

Wei had been mortal. Fragile. A child in a world of cultivators, carrying a body that bruised easily, broke easily and died easily. He would have died anyway. Eventually, inevitably, in the normal way that mortals die. The cultivator who struck him would have struck him regardless of my beliefs. The circumstances that placed him in danger existed independently of my convictions.

Or didn't they?

Or had my belief in loss created the circumstances? Had the cultivator appeared because my conviction required an instrument? Had the danger materialized because my deepest certainty demanded that Wei die, the way my deepest certainty demanded that water flow and stones sit and fire burn?

I didn't know.

And the not-knowing was the cruelest possible truth. Worse than yes, worse than no. The question would never close.

I would carry this.

The weight of a maybe. The unresolvable weight of a question that could not be answered and could not be dismissed. Yet could not be forgotten.
