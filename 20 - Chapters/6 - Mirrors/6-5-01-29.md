The trail went up. Steep, narrow, the kind of path that goats use and humans borrow. We climbed in silence for the first hour, the mountain doing what mountains do — testing the legs, punishing the lungs, reminding bodies that altitude is earned.

"I want to try something," I said. "At the top."

"What kind of something?"

"The kind where I stand at the edge and tell myself I'm mortal."

You climbed three steps behind me. Always three. "You think that will work?"

"No."

"Then why?"

"Because I need to know that it doesn't."

You were quiet for a while. The trail switchbacked through pine and scree. Then you stopped. Reached into your jacket pocket. Took something out — small, grey, round. River-smoothed. The kind of stone a child would pick up and keep because it fit perfectly in a palm.

"I've been carrying this since the settlement," you said. "From Wei's garden."

I knew the stone. Wei's lucky stone. The one he'd found in the creek and placed at the edge of his vegetable patch, the one he'd insisted kept the rabbits away. It hadn't kept the rabbits away. He'd believed it did.

I took it from your palm. It was warm from months against your chest. Small and heavy the way things are heavy when they carry more than their weight.

I closed my hand around it. We kept climbing.

Near the top, you stopped. Sat down on a flat stone. Took out your water skin.

"I'll wait here," you said.

I looked at you. At the stone. At the trail that narrowed ahead into wind and exposed rock.

You didn't explain. You didn't need to. Some things need to be done alone.

I kept climbing until I reached the top. The summit was a small plateau with a sheer drop on one side. The wind was strong, cold, the kind of wind that made you feel like you were standing in the middle of a storm even on a clear day.

I stood at the edge and yelled into the wind.

"I am mortal."

The wind didn't care. The wind was wind — it blew because atmospheric pressure differentials created movement, because temperature gradients produced currents, because the physical world operated on principles that didn't include my declarations. The wind blew. The words dissolved.

Below me: rocks. Water. The honest physics of falling — mass times acceleration, the equation that governed stones and bodies and everything that gravity claimed. Below me: the end, for anyone who wasn't me. The simple, reliable, available end that every mortal carried as a default option, the exit that existence built into every form it produced.

Except mine.

I opened my hand. Looked at the stone. Grey, smooth, warm. Wei's stone. Wei's belief in lucky stones and rabbit-proof gardens and a world that made sense if you just found the right pebble.

I don't need luck. I need conviction.

I let it go.

It fell. Of course it fell — stones don't believe they can fly. Stones don't believe anything. Stones are subject to physics without the complication of consciousness, without the interference of conviction, without the fundamental immunity that belief provides.

Wei's stone hit the water below. A small sound. Barely audible over the wind. Gone.

I didn't fall.

I wasn't going to fall. Because I believed I couldn't fall. The belief was deeper than the declaration — deeper than the words, deeper than the conscious intention. The belief was bedrock. The declaration was paint on the surface.

You can't change a building's foundation by repainting its walls.

You waited below. On the trail, fifty meters down. You were sitting on a rock — small, human-shaped, mortal from above.

I stepped back from the edge.

The stepping back was recognition of a category error. I had been trying to change a belief by declaring its opposite, the way a liar tries to become honest by saying *I am honest*. The declaration doesn't change the structure. The words don't reach the foundation. The surface and the core speak different languages and telling the surface *you are mortal* doesn't translate into the core because the core has been saying *you are immortal* for longer than language has existed.

The gap between knowing and being.

I knew I made myself immortal. The knowledge was complete, verified, confirmed by creation memories and river ripples and years of accumulated evidence that Xu Ran had counted and I had burned. I KNEW. The knowing was intellectual, cognitive, fully articulated, sitting in the part of my mind that processed information and produced conclusions.

The being was somewhere else. The being was in the part of me that didn't process but simply WAS — the part that believed without deciding to believe, that convicted without reasoning toward conviction, that held immortality not as a thought but as a condition. The way I held my height, my face, the color of my eyes. Not chosen. Not maintained. Simply: was.

You don't change the color of your eyes by declaring them different.

I walked down to you. The descent was easy — the path cooperated, the terrain smoothed, the mountain yielded. Because I believed in smooth paths. Because the belief operated regardless of my frustration with it.

"Well?" you said.

"Nothing."

You nodded. The declaration didn't work. The conscious override didn't work. The gap between knowing and being was real, was physical, was as wide as the cliff was tall.

"Interesting," you said.

"Frustrating."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

We walked. South. The trail continued its suspicious cooperation.
