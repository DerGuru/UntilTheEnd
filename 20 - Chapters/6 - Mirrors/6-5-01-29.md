The trail went up. Steep, narrow, the kind of path that goats use and humans borrow. We climbed in silence for the first hour, the mountain doing what mountains do — testing the legs, punishing the lungs, reminding bodies that altitude is earned.

"I want to try something," I said. "At the top."

"What kind of something?"

"The kind where I stand at the edge and tell myself I'm mortal."

You climbed three steps behind me. Always three. "You think that will work?"

"No."

"Then why?"

"Because I need to know that it doesn't."

"Why can't you do it somewhere else? Does it have to be a cliff?"

"I don't know. It just feels right." I looked up at the ridge. "And the view must be amazing."

You were quiet for a while. The trail switchbacked through pine and scree. Then you stopped, reached into your jacket pocket and took something out. Small, grey, round. River-smoothed. The kind of stone a child would pick up and keep because it fit perfectly in its palm.

"I've been carrying this since for a while now." You said. "It's from Wei's garden."

It clicked. I recognized the stone. Wei's stone. The one he'd found at the shrine in the hills and placed in the garden, between the dock grass and the lemongrass. Smooth, grey-blue, the size of a palm. *Good stone*, he'd said, patting it. The right stone in a garden of wrong plants.

I took it from your palm. It was warm from months against your chest. Small and heavy the way things are heavy when they carry more than their weight.

I closed my hand around it.

You sat down on a flat stone beside the trail. From here, the cliff face was visible — the sheer drop, the rocks below, the full vertical truth of what I was walking toward. You took out your water skin.

"I'll wait here," you said.

You didn't explain. You didn't need to. Some things need to be done alone. And some things need a witness who watches from a distance.

I kept climbing. The trail narrowed, switchbacked through loose scree, then opened onto a small plateau with a sheer drop on one side. The wind was strong, cold, the kind of wind that strips pretense.

I stood at the edge and yelled into it.

"I. Am. Mortal."

The wind didn't care. The wind was wind — it blew because atmospheric pressure differentials created movement, because temperature gradients produced currents, because the physical world operated on principles that didn't include my declarations. The wind blew. The words dissolved.

Below the cliff were rocks. Water. I mused. The honest physics of falling — mass times acceleration, the equation that governed stones and bodies and everything that gravity claimed. Below me was the end for anyone who wasn't me and took a step further. The simple, reliable, available end that every mortal carried as a default option, the exit that existence built into every form it produced.

Except mine.

I opened my hand. Looked at the stone. Grey-blue, smooth, warm. Wei's stone. The right stone from a garden of wrong plants. The stone a boy had found and patted and placed between herbs because a space he'd decided was important deserved decoration.

I let it go.

It fell. Two meters. Three.

And then it was in my hand again. Warm. Smooth. The same weight, the same shape, as if it had never left. As if *falling* had been a suggestion and I'd changed my mind.

I hadn't reached for it. Hadn't moved. The stone had simply returned — called back by the part of me that couldn't let go, that didn't know how to let things fall, that believed in keeping. The same part that believed in immortality. The same bedrock.

I closed my fingers around it. Wei's stone. Still warm.

I wouldn't fall either or rather would have a similar experience like at the other cliff. Because I believed I wouldn't end. And the belief was deeper than the declaration — deeper than the words, deeper than the conscious intention. The belief was bedrock. The declaration was paint on the surface.

But you can't change a building's foundation by repainting its walls.

I stepped back from the edge. Put the stone in my pocket.

You waited below. On the trail, fifty meters down. Small, human-shaped, mortal from above.

I sat down. Cross-legged, looking over the drop. The view was worth the climb. Valley floors in green and brown, a river threading silver through the distance, ridgelines stacking behind ridgelines until the farthest ones dissolved into haze. The kind of view that makes you forget the size of what you are and reminds you of the size of what you're looking at.

The wind pulled at my hair. The stone pressed against my thigh through the fabric.

If declaring didn't work, maybe stillness would. Maybe the foundation couldn't be argued with but could be — settled past, breathed through and reached beneath. Maybe meditation could take me to the place where the belief lived and I could touch it there, in its own language, without the translation error of words.

I closed my eyes.

The mountain held me. The wind moved. The stone in my pocket carried a boy's warmth and a garden's memory and the stubborn conviction that some things are worth keeping even when keeping is the problem.

I sat for a while, but nothing changed. I couldn't focus. So I walked down to you. The descent was easy — the path cooperated, the terrain smoothed, the mountain yielded. Because I believed in smooth paths. Because the belief operated regardless of my frustration with it.

"Well?" you said.

"Nothing." I touched my pocket. The stone was there. "Declaring doesn't work. Meditating doesn't work. I can't even let a stone fall without catching it."

You looked at my pocket. At the shape pressing through the fabric.

"Interesting," you said.

"Frustrating."

"Those aren't mutually exclusive."

We walked. South. The trail continued its suspicious cooperation.
