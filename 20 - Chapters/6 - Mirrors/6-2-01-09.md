I smelled smoke and you stopped.

Your body registered it before your mind did — the shift in stride, the lifted chin, the nostrils flaring. A human response. A mortal response. The animal recognition of fire where fire shouldn't be.

"Fire!" you exclaimed and dropped your pack. You ran. I followed.

The village was small. A market square. A handful of houses arranged around a well with the casual geometry of settlements that grow rather than plan. The kind of village where Wei had lived. Where you and I had bought rice and salt. The kind of village that existed because people needed to exist somewhere and this somewhere was as good as anywhere else.

Three houses were on fire. The flames were consuming the wood and the thatch with the voracious appetite of a fire that had been given a gift. The smoke was thick and black and billowing.

The sect cultivators were in the square.

Thirty of them. A detachment. The operational arm of the larger patience. They stood among the villagers with the casual authority of people who had done this before and expected to do it again. Their presence was the weapon. The subtlety was, as usually with sect operations, nonexistent.

The Pencil was not among them. The leadership was elsewhere — watching, presumably. Observing from the ridge. Measuring the response.

The villagers were afraid. Their fear had a quality — the dull, heavy weight of comprehension. They understood they were instruments.

A child was crying. Small — four, five years old. Held against its mother's chest with the desperate grip of someone who had run out of other options. The mother's face was blank. Decided. She'd looked at the situation, calculated the probabilities and arrived at the conclusion that the best possible action was no action. Hold the child. Be still. Wait.

The cultivators weren't attacking. They were waiting. Standing in the square, among the smoke. In the midst of crying and three burning houses. Waiting for me to arrive.

And I arrived.

Thirty faces turned. Expecting. They'd set the fire at the right places, to keep it from spreading immediately. It produced the right amount of smoke to be visible and smellable in the down wind. The operational competence was genuine.

I stood at the square's edge. You were beside me, breathing hard from the run, your hand on the knife at your belt. Xiǎohú was behind us — tense, low, amber eyes tracking the cultivators with the focused attention of a predator that knew she was outmatched.

The smoke rose. The child cried. A beam collapsed in one of the burning houses, sending sparks and a gust of heat across the square.

The impulse arrived.

An impulse — raw, immediate, pre-verbal. The same impulse that had put my hands on the ground in the dark, the same impulse that had produced the images, the same impulse that lived in the crack behind the wall.

Not *I want them to leave*.

*They are not here.*

Belief, not will. Conviction, not desire. The stone isn't moved because you want it moved. The stone IS there because you believe it is there. The belief comes first. The reality follows.

*They are not here.*

I didn't say it. Didn't think it. BELIEVED it.

And they were gone.
