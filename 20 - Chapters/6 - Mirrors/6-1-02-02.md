The trail wound south through pines. Narrow, loose-stoned, the kind of mountain path that required just enough attention to keep the feet honest. The pack sat heavy on my shoulders. The morning was cool and the walking was good — the steady, unthinking rhythm of legs and ground, the metronome that filled the space between thoughts.

Suddenly I stopped.

I hadn't decided to. One moment I was moving and the next I was standing still, mid-stride, right foot forward and left foot back. The trail continued ahead. The pines continued around me. My body had stopped and my mind hadn't been consulted.

Images came.

They couldn't have been memories. The wall would have caught memories. But I was awake — eyes open, the morning trail visible in all its physical detail. Pines, stones, path.  So not a dream either. Something else. Something inbetween. 

I saw hands. My hands. Or hands that were mine in the way that an earlier draft of a sentence is still yours. They were shaping something — moving through light and the light was bending. It followed the gesture. Almost obeying. Responding. The way water responds to a thrown stone, the way air responds to motion, the way reality responds to the thing that shapes it. They were—

*Creating.*

The word arrived with an image, inseparable, a caption burned into the frame.

I — the other I, the version in the images, the hands in the light — was creating with a technique that looked ancient. Older than the practices you call cultivation now. It looked deliberate. And she — or me — seemed certain about what was being made and why and how.

The images stacked like Thought-Echoes, additive, each one building on the last, each one carrying more information than the frame could hold. Hands in light. Light bending. Something emerging from the bending — a shape, a structure, a principle made visible. And beneath it all, a feeling. Intention. Pure, concentrated, undiluted intention, the kind that doesn't come from wanting but from knowing.

Then they were gone.

The images withdrew. As if someone had decided that they were enough, that the dosage had to be precise.

I stood on the trail. Right foot forward. Left foot back. The morning was exactly as it had been. Pines, stones, path, sky. Nothing had moved. Nothing had changed. The world was stubbornly, obstinately real.

"Why have you stopped," you asked from behind me. Your voice was calm, curious, the way you asked questions you believed you already knew the answer to but wanted to hear from me.

I looked at you. The looking took effort — the images were still resonating, their afterglow filling my vision the way bright light fills your vision after you look away. The trail was there but the hands were there too, superimposed, a palimpsest of present and something-else.

"Did I?"

The question was genuine. I didn't know how long I'd been standing.

"About thirty seconds," you said. You were watching me with the attention of someone who had seen this before. The field had taught you what it looked like when I went somewhere else without moving. Xiǎohú sat beside you, watching me too, tilting her head slightly, as if she understood more than she let on.

"I thought I saw something," I said.

I pushed it away. The instinct was automatic, the habit of dismissal, the reflex of someone who had learned that seeing things, acknowledging things and thinking about things led to the wall, to erasure and the gap. Push it away. Don't examine. Don't engage. Don't give the guardian a target.

It came back.

Ten minutes later, walking, the rhythm of footsteps filling the space between thoughts. The images returned. Softer. Less vivid this time. But still present. The hands. The light. The bending. The creating.

I pushed again and it returned.

Pushed. Returned.

The cycle was new. Before the crack, pushing had worked. The wall had been absolute. Content dismissed was content destroyed, thoughts rejected were thoughts erased, the guardian's authority had been total and the subject's compliance had been irrelevant. But now the pushing wasn't sticking. The images were persistent — refusing to leave. Sitting outside the threshold. Waiting.

Like a cat at a closed door. Patient. Certain that eventually it would open.

I walked and the images walked with me.

The metallic taste became even stronger. The teeth-vibration had settled into a constant hum, the sensory equivalent of a tone held too long. My jaw ached from the constant micro-resonance of my bone receiving a signal it hadn't been designed to receive.

You walked behind me. Three steps. The distance of companionship and caution. I could hear your breathing, the measured rhythm of a man who was fit enough for the trail but not so fit that the trail was effortless. Human breathing. Mortal breathing. The kind that starts and stops and counts and ends. We continued our session, where I cleaned your meridians, but there was still a lot to work on.

Two cracks. I thought about that while I worked. I had cracked your core and you had cracked my wall — or the woman, belonging to the hands in the images had cracked it. Or was it me? Your crack leaked potential — qi bleeding through fractures it should have contained. Mine leaked images, feelings. Maybe some residue of a past self I didn't remember? Both of us walking the same trail with damage we hadn't chosen, carrying the consequences of each other's doing. Both of us changed.

"Are you going to tell me what you saw?" you asked, losing your patience.

"I'm not quite sure what I saw."

"Just tell me your observations, then."

I appreciated it the way you appreciate a handrail on a steep descent. The walking is different without it.

"Hands," I said. "Creating something. Light that bent."

You were quiet for a moment. Processing. "Your hands?"

"I don't know. Mine but not mine. Like — an earlier version."

"An earlier version?"

"Older. Before."

The word hung. *Before* had been accumulating weight since the crack — a time before my time, implications about my being that the wall was specifically designed to prevent me from thinking about.

"Before what?" you asked.

"That's the thing. I don't know. I don't remember doing something like that. But they felt like my hands." The headaches grew, but relaxed again. "Something must have happened that made me lose that memories. Not that it would be the first time. The wall had already taken a lot from me. Maybe it took that too."

The wall did not intervene. I'd said *before* and *creating* and *older* and the wall had done nothing. Either the wall was losing its grip or the thing behind the wall had decided that these words were permitted. Had decided that I was allowed to approach.

We continued walking. The images pulsed, faint, persistent, the visual equivalent of the feeling in my chest, present without clarity, real without resolution. Xiǎohú padded beside me. Her gaze flicking between me and the trail. The morning opened into day. The clouds thinned further. I thought, that the sun might actually come out.

I pushed the images away one more time.

They came back.

I let them stay.
