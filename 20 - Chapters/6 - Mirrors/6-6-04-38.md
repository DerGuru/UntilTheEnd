We were walking. The trail — whatever it had been, whatever it would become, the perpetual uncertainty of mountain paths that existed in a state of ambiguity between road and suggestion — wound through pines. Xiǎohú trotted ahead, tail low, the casual patrol of an animal that had decided which direction was correct without consulting the humans. We followed. We always followed.

You walked beside me. Your stride — the measured, three-quarter pace of a man who had calibrated his walking speed to precisely match mine despite having longer legs and more efficient biomechanics. As if I were the slower one. I wasn't. But I walked deliberately at the pace I chose, so you would be able to match it and neither of us had ever discussed the arrangement.

"The second voice," you said. "How does it sound?"

You said it casually. Tossed sideways, thrown over the shoulder of a more ordinary sentence, curiosity disguised beneath the tone of someone making conversation.

The trail narrowed. Pine roots crossing the path like knuckles, the ground uneven with the treachery of organic architecture. I stepped over a root while I considered.

"It sounds a lot like me."

You nodded. Walking. Looking past me.

I adjusted. "More precisely — like I sound when I'm not thinking."

You were quiet for four steps. I counted without meaning to, the residual hyper-awareness that the field had installed and that hadn't fully decalibrated. Four steps, pine needles, the crunch of dry ground, Xiǎohú's tail disappearing around a bend.

"That makes sense," you said.

I glanced at you. You were watching the trail, but the hand not holding your walking staff was doing the thing — the curl-and-release. You would write it down tonight.

"How so?" I asked.

Xiǎohú reappeared on the trail ahead, paused, looked back at us with the amber evaluation of an animal confirming that the slow creatures were still following. Confirmed. Turned. Continued.

"Even though it is a separate part of you. It is still you."

"It was never separate," I said. The thought arriving as speech before it completed itself as thought, the verbal processing that happened when the interior landscape was reconfiguring and the mouth became a pressure valve. "None of it was separate. It was a deeper part of me, the part that knew. But it was me. I just didn't know."

You listened.

"The part that knew and decided I wasn't ready. So it packaged the knowledge into fragments, compressed them, pushed them through whatever cracks the wall allowed. Just a few words at a time. *Not now.*, *Keep going.* and *Enough.* It was — she was — I was — protecting myself from collapsing under the weight of what I am. What I did to myself."

My voice was steady. Genuinely so, the steadiness of someone describing something they'd accepted rather than performing composure. The metallic taste was there, faint, the low background hum that had been almost constant for weeks now. I'd stopped noticing it the way you stop noticing the sound of a river when you've been walking beside it long enough.

"She sounds like me when I'm not thinking because she is me when I'm not thinking. Not just a part. Me. The deep self. The original. The one who existed before the wall, before the whole apparatus of self-protection was constructed. She's been talking this whole time. I just — attributed the voice to something external. Something other. Because that was the easier assumption."

Four more steps. A bird. The particular silence that follows an admission that has been building for years.

"You said she *decided,*" you said.

I looked at you.

"You said the deeper part decided you weren't ready. Decided to package the knowledge. Decided to protect."

"Yes."

"That implies agency."

I considered. The trail climbed. The pines were thinning into something less managed, the tree line pulling back to reveal a wider sky.

"Agency, yes, but internal," I said. "Below conscious decision-making. The way the body decides to flinch before the conscious mind registers the threat. The intelligence of the organism. Faster than thought. Deeper than thought. Operating on protocols that the surface self doesn't have access to."

You nodded. Walked. Your hand stilled — the curl-and-release resolved, the processing complete or at least paused.

"Self-organization," you said.

I waited.

"The whole — you — organizing around the need to survive, allocating knowledge and experience to different levels based on what each level can carry."

You said it as if you were commenting on the weather or the quality of the trail.

"Yes," I said.

We walked. Xiǎohú was waiting at the next bend, sitting, tail wrapped around her paws, the patient irritation of an animal that had been ready to move for several minutes and was being inconvenienced by bipedal conversation.

You reached for your water. Drank and offered me the skin. I drank. The water tasted like leather and mountain — the mineral quality of streams that ran through granite.

"I have to write that down tonight," you said.

"I know."

You almost smiled. It was there and then it wasn't, like a ripple.
