I woke to the taste of metal.

Not blood. The distinction matters, though both are iron. Blood tastes of salt and warmth and the organic sharpness of something that belongs inside and has come out. This was different. Clean metal. Cold. The taste of pressing your tongue against a coin or a blade or the tines of a fork. Present without source, without origin, without any physical explanation for why my mouth tasted like I'd been chewing on old iron in my sleep.

The fire was dead. Not dying. Dead, the greyness of ashes that have surrendered their last heat hours ago and are now just the remains of what burning leaves behind. You were still asleep on the other side of the pit, your head resting on your notebook, the waterproof case making an uncomfortable pillow that you'd been using since you'd run out of clothes to fold beneath your skull.

The morning held a grey sky. The kind of overcast that isn't weather so much as absence. No clouds precisely, just a uniform dimness that covers the sky like a lid.

I sat up. The cold was there immediately, a parting gift from the crack, you had caused with a question I did not remember. It was still the permanent sensory adjustment of a body that had turned temperature from data into experience. The air against my face, the ground beneath my hands, the chill of mountain morning pressing through fabric and skin with the insistent physicality of something that wanted to be felt.

And then there was the humming.

Not in my ears. In my teeth. A low and steady vibration, the frequency of something mechanical operating just below hearing range. I could feel it in my molars, in the bones of my jaw, in the subtle resonance that travels through solid material when sound is too deep for air. As if someone had pressed a tuning fork to the base of my skull and left it there.

This was new. The feeling — the nameless frequency, the bass note vibration that had lived behind the sternum — was old and familiar, a companion I'd carried since the moment I couldn't remember. But the teeth-vibration was different. Higher. More physical. More present. As if whatever lived inside the feeling had turned up its output and the overflow was leaking into my bones.

The metallic taste was its aftertaste. The residue. The sensory equivalent of exhaust from an engine that was running harder than before.

I stood and stretched. My body complained in the catalogued way that bodies complain when they've slept on ground and are old enough to have stiffened for it. Shoulders tight. Lower back aching. The headache — persistent and permanent, the cost of carrying whatever I carried — pulsing behind the eyes with the rhythm of my heartbeat.

Xiǎohú was awake. Lying beside my bedroll where she had been every morning for the past week, amber eyes open, watching me with the patient attention of something that was waiting. Not for food. Not for movement. Just waiting, the way an animal — if you could call a fox-spirit an animal — waits when it knows something is coming and hasn't been told yet, what it would be.

I walked to the river.

The water was cold and clear and ran over stones with the constant murmur that rivers use to fill silence. I knelt. Cupped some water and brought it to my face.

The cold hit like contact. Not the gentle sensation of water on skin, but the full, sharp, detailed experience of temperature meeting nerve endings that had been calibrated for maximum sensitivity. Every droplet was distinct. Every rivulet that ran down my cheeks and dripped from my jaw carried its own exact coldness.

I opened my eyes.

My reflection was there.

In the still pool at the river's edge — the same kind of sheltered pocket that had held my face before, the same calm water that had shown me the smile — my face looked back.

I turned away. Fast. The quick, instinctive aversion of someone who has seen something they're not ready to see again and has chosen distance over engagement.

This time, the reflection had been just a reflection. My face. Tired, thin and honest. Neither smiling nor doing anything it shouldn't have been doing. Just there. But the memory of the last time was still present. The smile that preceded mine, the warmth, the understanding. And that presence made looking at my own face feel dangerous. Not because the reflection might do something again. Because I might feel something if it did.

I washed my face. Quickly. Without looking at the water.

You were awake when I returned. You'd coaxed something from the dead fire. The optimism of a man who was adding kindling to ashes and breathing on them with the stubborn determination that had gotten fires lit a thousand times before.

"Good morning," you said.

"Good Morning." I looked at the fox-spirit. "And good morning to you too, Xiǎohú," I added.

Against my prediction, the fire caught. Small, tentative, the first orange tongue licking at dry bark with the nervous energy of flame finding its conditions met. You fed it. Pine needles first, then twigs, then the careful architecture of larger pieces that would sustain the burn.

You looked at me with a quick assessment. The automatic scan that you'd been performing every morning since the clearing, the calibration of how-is-she-today. Your eyes tracked my posture, my face, the way I held my hands.

"Something new?" you asked.

"I taste metal."

You nodded. Reached for your notebook — then stopped. The notebook was new. Barely used yet. And still unopened. You kept it closed since you bought it, after the day you'd decided that documentation had reached its limits and whatever came next was mine.

You opened it now.

The gesture was significant. A discipline, a boundary, a statement about the limits of observation. But something in what I'd said had overridden the discipline.

You wrote one line and closed it again.

We ate breakfast. Cold rice from last night, because the fire was too young for boiling water and I was too hungry for patience. The rice was the rice. Grain-neutral. The one constant in a life of variables.

The humming pulsed in my teeth. It had become stronger than when I'd woken. The metallic taste was denser, coating my tongue with the aftertaste of something that was neither food nor drink but was being processed by the same systems.

Something had changed. The crack was still there, stable, the loose tooth in the wall's architecture that I could press against and feel give. The feeling was still there, the nameless frequency, the thing behind the sternum that the deletion couldn't reach.

Something else was there, too. Something additional. As if the feeling had been running at constant speed for a while and had, overnight, shifted gears. The output was higher. The resonance was wider. 

I didn't know what it meant. The wall was quiet. No headache-warnings, no preemptive interventions, no gentle refusals disguised as pain. Whatever was happening was either below the wall's threat threshold or above its jurisdiction.

Xiǎohú stood, stretched, padded to my side and sat, close enough that her fur brushed my leg. I started petting her. She leaned into the touch, the way she had since the first day, when she'd come to me and curled against my leg as if she knew that I was the one who could feel her. The warmth of her body against the cold, the softness of her fur against the rough fabric of my pants, the steady weight of her presence — all of it was a comfort that I hadn't expected but had come to rely on in a way that was different from relying on you.

The morning opened like mornings do, gradually, without announcement, the grey lid of overcast thinning until the light was light and the day was day and the business of being alive resumed its usual priority of forward motion.

I packed. You packed and killed the fire. We left the camp and the river where my reflection had been just a reflection and walked.

My teeth hummed. My mouth tasted like metal. The world was sharper and louder and colder than any world should be. And somewhere deep inside — below the wall, below the crack, in the bedrock where the ancient thing breathed — the feeling pulsed harder.

Something was coming. 

Was I ready for it?

*Almost.*
