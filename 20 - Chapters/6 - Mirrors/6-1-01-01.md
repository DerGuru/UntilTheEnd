I woke to the taste of metal.

Clean metal. Cold. The taste of pressing your tongue against a coin or the tines of a fork — present without source, without explanation.

The fire was dead — grey ash, hours cold. You were still asleep on the other side of the pit, your head resting on your notebook, the waterproof case making an uncomfortable pillow that you'd been using since you'd run out of clothes to fold beneath your skull.

The morning held a grey sky. The kind of overcast that isn't weather so much as absence. No clouds precisely, just a uniform dimness that covers the sky like a lid.

I sat up. The cold was there immediately, a parting gift from the crack you had caused with a question I did not remember. Still there — the permanent sensory adjustment of a body that had turned temperature from data into experience. The air against my face, the ground beneath my hands, the chill of mountain morning pressing through fabric and skin with the insistent physicality of something that wanted to be felt.

And then there was the humming.

In my teeth. A low and steady vibration, the frequency of something mechanical operating just below hearing range. I could feel it in my molars, in the bones of my jaw, in the subtle resonance that travels through solid material when sound is too deep for air. As if someone had pressed a tuning fork to the base of my skull and left it there.

This was new. The feeling — the nameless frequency, the bass note vibration that had lived behind the sternum — was old and familiar, a companion I'd carried since the moment I couldn't remember. But the teeth-vibration was different. Higher. More physical. More present. As if whatever lived inside the feeling had turned up its output and the overflow was leaking into my bones.

The metallic taste was its aftertaste. The residue. The sensory equivalent of exhaust from an engine that was running harder than before.

I stood and stretched. My body complained in the catalogued way that bodies complain when they've slept on ground and are old enough to have stiffened for it. Shoulders tight. Lower back aching. The headache — persistent and permanent, the cost of carrying whatever I carried — pulsing behind the eyes with the rhythm of my heartbeat.

Xiǎohú was awake. Lying beside my bedroll where she had been every morning for the past week, amber eyes open, watching me with the patient attention of an animal — if you could call a fox-spirit an animal — that was waiting as if she knew that something was coming but hadn't been told what to expect, yet.

I walked to the river.

The water was cold and clear. It ran over stones with the constant murmur that rivers use to fill silence. I knelt, cupped some water and brought it to my face.

The cold hit me on contact — full, sharp, every droplet distinct. Every rivulet that ran down my cheeks and dripped from my jaw carried its own exact coldness.

I opened my eyes.

My reflection was there. In the still pool at the river's edge — the same kind of sheltered pocket that had held my face before, the same calm water that had shown me the smile — my face looked back.

I turned away. Fast.

The reflection had been just a reflection. My face. Tired, thin, honest. Just my face. But the memory of the last time was still there — the smile that preceded mine, the warmth. And that made looking feel dangerous. I might feel something if it happened again.

I washed my face quickly. Without looking at the water.

You were awake when I returned. You tried to coax something from the dead fire. The optimism of a man who was adding kindling to ashes and breathing on them with the stubborn determination that had gotten fires lit a thousand times before.

"Good morning," you said.

"Good morning." I looked at the fox-spirit. "And good morning to you too, Xiǎohú," I added.

Against my prediction, the fire had caught. Small, tentative, the first orange tongue licking at dry bark with the nervous energy of flame finding its conditions met. You fed it. Pine needles first, then twigs, then the careful architecture of larger pieces that would sustain the burn.

You looked at me — the quick scan you'd been performing every morning since the clearing. Your eyes tracked my posture, my face, the way I held my hands.

"Something new?" you asked.

"I taste metal."

You nodded, reached for your notebook and stopped. The notebook was new. Still unused. Still unopened. You kept it closed since you bought it, after the day you'd decided that documentation had reached its limits and whatever came next was mine.

You opened it now. Something in what I'd said must have overridden the decision. You wrote one line and closed it again.

We ate breakfast. Cold rice from last night, because the fire was too young for boiling water and I was too hungry for patience. The rice was the rice. Grain-neutral. The one constant in a life of variables.

The humming pulsed in my teeth. It had already become stronger than when I'd woken. The metallic taste was denser, coating my tongue with the aftertaste of something that was neither food nor drink but was being processed by the same systems.

Something had changed. The crack was still there, stable, the loose tooth in the wall's architecture that I could press against and feel give. The feeling was still there, the nameless frequency, the thing behind the sternum that the deletion couldn't reach.

Something else was there, too. Something additional. As if the feeling had been running at constant speed for a while and had, overnight, shifted gears. The output was higher. The resonance was wider. 

I didn't know what it meant. The wall was quiet. Whatever was happening was either below its threat threshold or above its jurisdiction.

Xiǎohú stood, stretched, padded to my side and sat close enough that her fur brushed my leg. I started petting her. She leaned into the touch, the way she had since the first day, when she'd come to me and curled against my leg as if she knew that I was the one who could feel her. The warmth of her body against the cold, the softness of her fur against the rough fabric of my pants, the steady weight of her presence — all of it was a comfort that I hadn't expected but had come to rely on in a way that was different from relying on you.

The morning opened like mornings do, gradually, without announcement, the grey lid of overcast thinning until the light was light and the day was day and the business of being alive resumed its usual priority of forward motion.

I packed. You packed and killed the fire. We left the camp and the river where my reflection had been just a reflection and walked.

My teeth hummed. My mouth tasted like metal. The world was sharper and louder and colder than any world should be. And somewhere deep inside — below the wall, below the crack, in the bedrock where the ancient thing breathed — the feeling pulsed harder.

Something was coming. 

Was I ready for it?

*Almost.*
