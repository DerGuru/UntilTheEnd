I woke before you. Before Xiǎohú — the amber-eyed shape still curled near the dead fire, breathing the small breaths of a sleeping animal. The sky was the grey before grey, the pre-dawn absence of dark that preceded light.

I packed.

Your bag first. The straps tightened, the weight redistributed — the dried meat to the bottom, the water skin accessible, the notebook where you could reach it without stopping. Then mine. The small bundle that constituted everything I carried, which was almost nothing, which was enough.

I set them side by side near the trailhead. Two bags. Yours heavy. Mine light. The morning was cold and the cold was good.

You woke to the sound of me scraping the pot. Sat up. Blinked. The expression of a man assembling consciousness one piece at a time.

"I packed yours," I said.

You looked at the bags. At the trail. At me.

"You repacked the meat to the bottom."

"I left some out for later."

You pulled on your boots. Stretched. Looked at the sky the way you did every morning — checking, measuring, the meteorological assessment of a man who had learned weather through walking. "A clear sky."

"For now."

You stood. Dressed. The morning motions. I heated water over a small fire — not for rice, we'd eat the dried meat cold, but for warmth. Something to hold. You took your cup when I offered it and wrapped both hands around it.

Steam rose between us.

"I know what I have to do," I said.

The same volume I'd use for *it will rain* or *the trail goes north*. Factual. The way you state something that has been true for a while and is only now being said aloud.

You drank and stored the cup away.

"Okay," you said.

You shouldered your pack. The weight settled across your back with the familiar distribution — the shape so customary now that its absence would have felt stranger than its presence.

"There is a cave uphill," I said.

You nodded. "How far?"

"Higher."

You accepted that. Higher was a direction. Higher was sufficient.

I picked up my bundle. Xiǎohú woke, stretched and joined our departure.

The trail narrowed as we climbed. You walked beside me, the pen in your hand, tapping against your thigh.

We walked. Xiǎohú trotted ahead, then fell behind, then found a position at our flank that apparently satisfied whatever tactical calculation governed her movement. Three beings on a mountain path heading higher.

The morning brightened. The sky found its color — the high-altitude blue that exists above the haze of lower elevations.

I knew what I had to do. The doing would be what remained.

The trees were gone. The tree line had passed beneath us somewhere in the day's climb, the last struggling pine giving way to scrub giving way to rock, which was all there was. Grey stone, veined with quartz, the surface alternating between rough and smooth with the unpredictability of geological process. The sky was enormous, the dome of it swelling as the horizon dropped, the vastness that altitude reveals by removing everything between you and the edge of the visible world.

And the cold was overly present.

Real cold. Not dangerous cold. The ambient, persistent cold of high altitude in early spring, the temperature that informs every breath and sits against the skin like a second garment.

A hollow in the rock held rainwater — a palm's width of accidental mirror. I passed it without looking down. I already knew the face that would look back now.

I walked.

The path became barely a path now. More a suggestion inscribed in rock by the passage of animals. Single-file. You had fallen back, not to your three-step distance but to the practical spacing that single-file terrain demanded, close enough that I could hear your breathing, your footsteps, the small sounds of a mortal body navigating difficult ground.

I knew where I was going. I couldn't have pointed to a spot on a map, couldn't have given coordinates or described the entrance or provided any of the concrete information that navigation typically required. But I knew the direction. The deep-body sense of a destination that the conscious mind couldn't locate but the whole organism oriented toward.

Higher. Toward the place where the mountains folded in on themselves and the rock became hollow and that was a cave and the cave was — 

I didn't finish the thought. The thought didn't need finishing. The cave was what it was. The cave was where it was. I was walking toward it. Each step was a placement, a decision, the body's continuous negotiation with terrain that wanted nothing and offered nothing and required everything.

Your breathing behind me was slightly elevated. The altitude, the effort, the mortal lungs doing mortal work in thinner air. You didn't complain. Had never complained. The walking staff tapped, a rhythm, the metronome of your progress, each tap a confirmation of presence. I am here. I am behind you. I am walking where you walk.

Xiǎohú went ahead. A shape against the grey rock, amber eyes visible when she turned to check on us, then invisible again as she continued. Either she knew the way too or she was just going the same direction. 

There was no sign of human presence beyond the vestigial path. The world had simplified, stripped to its components, reduced to the basic vocabulary of stone, sky and cold. The complexity of the lower world — towns, sects, relationships, the entire social architecture of civilization — existed below us now, in the valleys we couldn't see, behind the curve of the mountain.

Up here was essential.

Two people. A fox. A path. A direction.

And somewhere ahead — ahead and above, in the place where the mountains kept their oldest secrets, there was a cave. The place I wanted to go to. My final resting place or the place I wanted to stop not-dying in. The place where a lifetime of walking would either arrive at their destination or discover that the destination was a different kind of question.
