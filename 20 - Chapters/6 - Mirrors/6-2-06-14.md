We were on the trail. The southern trail, the opening trail, the trail that arranged itself ahead of us with the eager courtesy of a road that wanted to be walked. Midday. The sun was high, the shadows short, the metallic taste constant and the teeth-vibration a hum I'd stopped noticing the way you stop noticing your heartbeat.

A stone on the trail. A small one. Not a special stone, not a significant stone, a stone the size of a fist that sat in the center of the path and would have required stepping over or around. I walked toward it and first the stone was not there and then had never been there. The edit happened between one step and the next, automatic, involuntary, as natural as blinking. The trail was clear because I believed the trail was clear, or the thought arrived like that, unbidden and strange. Belief had preceded reality. And reality had conformed.

Something prickled across my scalp. In the village, the warping had been dramatic. Thirty people, a conscious belief. Yet, this was different — the unconscious version. Small. Precise. Automatic.

Your words were still seeping. *You've always done this. Only without knowing it.* The words had been settling for hours, finding the cracks in the stone of my self-understanding, filling spaces I hadn't known were empty. Each minute brought another connection, another coincidence explained, another healing accounted for, another lucky break revealed as something other than luck. The architecture of my entire life was being revised, not by new information but by new interpretation. The same events. A different author.

The creation memory broke through while I was walking. While the stone was being edited. While your words were seeping. While the three converged in the space behind the crack and produced a pressure that the wall — weakened, thinning, half-asleep — couldn't contain.

The images came back. Stronger. Longer. Clearer.

Not just a few frames this time. A sequence, playing behind my open eyes. I saw me. Not as I was now, but as I had been. Before becoming a weapon. Before walking. Before the forests and the mountains and the centuries and the names and the losses.

Before everything.

The place was everywhere. A condition, a state. I stood in a space that was simultaneously vast and intimate, And the contradiction didn't matter because the concept of size hadn't been invented yet.

I stood in the raw material.

And I shaped it.

The images showed hands, because the memory was translating itself into forms I could comprehend. Bit I sahped with something that felt like belief. With what seemed like the same force that had moved the stone and shifted the cultivators. I believed and the believed thing became. I said with the concentrated certainty that precedes words that *this exists now* and it existed. I said *here is light* and light arrived. I said *here is form* and form condensed from formlessness.

I stopped on the trail. My hands went to my temples. The metallic taste was suddenly overwhelming, no longer a taste but a flood, copper and iron and something older, something that tasted like the inside of the first bell ever cast in the first forge of a world that I —

Everything crashed together. Fused. Becoming more than the sum of its parts. Superimposed like a force that I could feel in my bones, in my blood, in my skin. The world around me was vibrating, as if reality itself was responding to the pressure of something it couldn't contain.

Warping. Words. Memory. A stone cleared by unconscious belief. *You've always done this.* Hands shaping light in a place before places. The convergence was chaotic. Physical. Pain.

My knees hit the trail.

The world shimmered. Not the visual distortion of dizziness or the perceptual warping of illness. The reality around me vibrated at a frequency I could see. The solid world revealing itself as a held note, a sustained conviction that matter existed and would continue to exist.

My architecture?

My conviction?

My —

The wall FOUGHT.

A spike of pain went through my forehead. A clamp on my thoughts. The memory imploded. It compressed, contracted, collapsed from a sequence to a point and from a point to nothing. The hands in the light vanished. The place before places vanished. The shaping vanished.

Pain.

My nose bled.

Warm, sudden, the copper-taste and iron-smell of internal fluid finding an external exit. It ran from my left nostril across my upper lip and dripped onto the trail's dust, red on brown, life on earth.

I'd never bled from a memory before. The headaches had been a warning. This was the wall defending a perimeter that had been breached.

The thing behind the wall.

The thing that was a part of me.

"Breathe." You were there. Beside me. On the trail. Your hands on my shoulders. Your face close, your eyes searching.

"Breathe," you said again, calmingly.

I breathed. It was difficult. The wall's intervention had seized more than my thoughts, had tensed the muscles of my chest, had constricted the pathways that air used to reach the places where air was needed. I breathed like I was held held underwater. Desperately, greedily, the body's primitive demand for the thing it needed most.

The blood slowed. The pain receded, pulling back from the spike to a throb to a presence to a memory of a pain that had been.

Your hands trembled.

I noticed through the pain and the blood and the collapsed memory.

"I'm alright," I said.

"You're bleeding from your nose and you just collapsed on a trail."

"I'm alright."

You didn't argue. You didn't tell me I wasn't alright. You took your hands from my shoulders, slowly, and sat back on your heels. Looking at me.

The metallic taste was blood now, mixed with metal, the two flavors indistinguishable.

I wiped my nose. Red on my sleeve. Red on the trail.

The memory was gone. The wall had taken it, compressed, sealed, locked behind the barrier with renewed force. But the edges remained. The residue. An afterimage of a place before places and hands in light. The irrefutable knowledge that I had been there. Had done that. Had shaped.

The convergence had been too much. The wall had fought and won. And the cost was blood and pain and a certainty that the wall was running out of victories.

"Almost," I whispered.

"I know," you said. "I saw."

You'd seen nothing. You'd seen a woman stop walking and fall to her knees and bleed from her nose. From outside — from the mortal angle, from the mirror's surface — that was all there was to see.

But you said *I know* and meant it.

And meaning it was enough. 

For now.
