I was done with revelations, done with the theatrical. This was a body in a river, arriving at something the river had never needed to learn.

I stood in the water. Your robe in my hands, half-washed, the ink stain still visible because ink was stubborn and the river was patient. Some things took longer than a single washing. The sun moved. The patterns on the water shifted. Xiǎohú downstream had finished drinking and was crouched at the shallows, one paw poised over the water, watching something beneath the surface with the absolute stillness of an animal that believed patience was a hunting strategy. A fish, probably. 

The insight crystallized. Not in language. Language came second, arrived after, the translator that showed up late and tried to claim credit for the original thought. The insight crystallized as sensation. As the physical knowledge of what happened when fists became open hands.

I was not trying to become mortal.

I was trying to stop holding myself immortal.

The distinction sounded like wordplay. But it was structural. Fundamental. The difference between building a wall and removing one. Between adding and subtracting. Between the muscular effort of gripping and the release of opening.

For eons — since the moment my conviction had made immortality a fact — I had been holding. The way a fist holds. Without thinking about holding, without being aware that it's holding, the grip so old and so constant that the hand had forgotten it was a fist and instead assumed this is just the shape hands are.

My will was holding.

The belief that made reality — that made stones appear and mountains disappear — was an act of will disguised as conviction. Deeper than the surface will, deeper than the conscious I-want-the-stone-to-move. The structural will, the foundational grip, the conviction that was so deep it had become invisible. I am immortal. That was not a thought. Not a declaration. A grip. A fist closed so long it had calcified.

And I had been trying to open the fist by gripping harder.

Every attempt to become mortal, every test, every experiment, every deliberate effort to believe differently, had been an act of will. Will directed against will. Fist fighting fist. The very effort to change the conviction was itself a conviction, the conviction a grip and the grip the opposite of what was needed.

Letting go was not willing.

The water in the river did not will itself to flow. It flowed because that was its nature, because gravity existed and the channel existed and the water existed. Flowing was what happened when nothing prevented it. The water didn't decide to be water. Didn't will itself downstream. Didn't grip itself into motion.

It let go. It never held on in the first place. It was water and water moved.

I looked at my hands. Open. The fabric resting across them, the water sitting in the weave, unhurried, present. My hands were not gripping the fabric. They were not holding the water. They were open and the fabric was there, the water was there, my hands were there. Nothing was being forced or held or willed.

This was the shape of letting go.

An absence. The removal of the grip rather than the application of a different grip. Releasing the old belief the way an open hand releases water — by ceasing to hold.

The problem — and I could feel it, could feel the edges of it the way you feel the edges of something you're about to trip over — was that the grip was me. The fist was not a thing I had. The fist was a thing I was. The conviction of immortality was not a belief I held. It was the structure of my being, the foundational architecture of whatever I was, the load-bearing wall that held up everything else. To open this fist was to disassemble myself.

To let go was to let go of the thing that was doing the letting.

You were still watching. Observing my face and my posture. Trying to read the process in my head. 

The paradox sat in my chest like a stone — present, immovable. The weight of a truth that was both clear and impossible. I understood what needed to happen. I understood that I could not make it happen. I understood that the inability to make it happen was itself the proof that the understanding was correct, because making was will and will was grip and grip was the opposite of letting go.

I stood in the river.

The robe in my hands. The water. The sun.

I did nothing.

That was deliberate. No. The nothing was not deliberate. The nothing was nothing. I stood there and did not grip and did not release. I stood there the way the water stood in the fabric of the robe, present, unhurried, subject to forces that didn't require my participation.

After a while, I lifted the robe. Wrung it. Not hard. The gentle, efficient wringing of someone who knows the fabric will dry in sun and wind and time, who doesn't need to squeeze out every drop because some things can be left to outer forces.

"You done?" you called from upstream.

"With the robe," I said.

The implication sat between us. You heard it. You didn't respond. You closed your notebook, stood and stretched, stiff from sitting too long on a rock by a river, watching someone learn something you cannot learn for them.

Xiǎohú left the water and shook herself. Water flew from her fur, catching the sun, each drop a small lens that showed the world inverted for the instant before it fell.

We left the river. The robe over my arm, damp, heavy with the weight of water that would evaporate in its own time.
