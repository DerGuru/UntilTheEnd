We arrived at a crossroads — a point on the mountain path where the trail split. Left went downward, into the valley, toward the distant suggestion of rooftops and smoke that indicated a town. Right was upward, into the higher ranges, where the vegetation thinned and the rock became exposed and the sky was closer than the ground.

I stopped.

Something in the right-hand path. The mountain's silhouette against the sky, the particular fold of ridge meeting ridge, the angle of the light falling across exposed stone. My feet knew this. This *direction*. My body recognized it the way your body knows the direction of home before your mind assembles the coordinates. Old. Older than language. A deep-body certainty that the place I was walking toward was up there, somewhere in the ranges where the rock became hollow and the cold became the kind of cold that settles into stone and stays.

I was turning to the right path.

"I see you want to go right," you said. You looked at my face, then at the right-hand path, then left toward the town. Smoke rising, thin and domestic. "We have rice for two days. Maybe three if we stretch. We need to stock up if we're going to go into the mountains. Town first, then up."

My jaw tightened. The mountain was right there. Right *there*, behind the sky, behind the ridgeline, close enough that my skin prickled with it.

But you were mortal. And mortal bodies required rice.

I nodded. "Town first."

We went left. Down into the valley. Xiǎohú trotted beside us until the first rooftops appeared, then stopped. She sat at the road's edge where dirt became cobblestone, the precise boundary between her territory and theirs. 

The town was small. A market, two streets, chickens in the road. The smell of frying oil, woodsmoke and the particular staleness of grain stored too long in too little air. A woman sold rice from wooden bins, scooping it with a tin cup, weighing it on a balance that had been recalibrated so many times the markings were illegible. You haggled. Politely, methodically, the haggling of a man who respected the ritual. She added a handful extra. You bought dried meat, a small bag of salt.

From somewhere came a burst of squawking. A henhouse in sudden disagreement with the world. A boy ran past us toward the sound, barefoot, shouting something I didn't catch.

I stood near the last stall and looked up to the mountains. They were still there. Patient. They could wait. They had been waiting since before this town existed, since before the valley was a valley, since the rock folded and a hollow formed.

You found me there. Pack heavier now.

"Ready?" you said.

"Since before you asked."

We climbed back. The valley behind us. Xiǎohú was waiting at the road's edge where we'd left her, sitting in the same spot, tail around her paws. There was a feather on her muzzle. A small one. Brown. The kind that belonged to a chicken.

She licked her muzzle. The feather drifted to the ground.

Somewhere behind us, a woman was going to count her chickens tonight and arrive at a number that didn't match yesterday's.

We took the right path.

It climbed and with every step the recognition deepened. The trail was unfamiliar, the trees were right but placed differently, the stones had shifted, settled and shifted again. But the direction was right. The way a compass knows north — below thought, below memory, in the body's oldest architecture.

You walked beside me. The pen was in your hand, tapping against your thigh the way it did when you were composing something you hadn't written yet.

"You know where we're going," you said.

"Yes."

"You've been here before."

"The mountains have. I might have."

You considered this. Four steps. The pen stopped tapping.

"How long ago?"

I looked at the ridgeline. The fold of stone against the sky. The hollow that I could feel ahead the way you feel a room's shape in the dark.

"Long enough that the forests changed species."

You put the pen away. You'd write it down tonight. For now, you walked.

The path narrowed as it climbed. Pine gave way to scrub, scrub gave way to rock with tenacious vegetation clinging to cracks. Xiǎohú trotted ahead, tail low, pausing at each switchback to confirm we were still following.

The answer to what you had spent your life looking for was walking beside you, messy and unfinished, heading toward a place she'd left before language had a word for leaving.
