I saw three more changes until evening. A rock face that had smoothed itself into a wall, polished, almost glassy, catching the late light like a mirror I hadn't asked for. The wildflowers at the trail's edge — I'd glanced at them, yellow, unremarkable, and looked for Xiǎohú. When I looked back they were a deep violet that streaked toward black at the edges, as if someone had adjusted the color between one glance and the next with absolute confidence and absolute indifference to consistency. A bird call I didn't recognize, a three-note pattern, low and resonant, that Xiǎohú tracked with the focused attention of a predator hearing something that shouldn't exist.

Each change was small. Each change was complete. And each change carried with it the full weight of a past that hadn't happened. The smooth rock face had been smooth for millennia, the flowers had always been violet and the bird had always sung this three-note pattern in this valley.

Each change was retroactive. And that was what made my stomach turn. Before — people had *noticed*. You had noticed. The scholars had noticed. The ground cracked and everyone saw the crack. Reality changed and the witnesses could point at the change and say *that wasn't like that before*.

This was different. These changes came with their own history. The rock face hadn't changed — it had *always been smooth*. The flowers hadn't turned violet — they *had always been violet*. The change erased the evidence of changing. Rewrote the witnesses along with the world.

Simple anomalies broke rules. These changes wrote new ones.

You made camp at sunset. Efficient, methodical — the fire where it belonged, the bedrolls where they belonged, the perimeter check that had become reflexive since the sect had stopped being ignorable. You moved through the routine with the practiced ease of a man who had done this a hundred times and I watched you move and found the ordinariness comforting.

"The flowers had been yellow," I said.

You were poking the fire with a stick. Didn't look up. "What flowers?"

"At the trailside. The ones that are violet now."

"They've always been violet."

I knew you would say that. The flowers had always been violet for you. The tree had always been there. The stone had always faced east. The rock face had always been smooth.

Reality changed and your memory changed with it and the only memory that didn't change was mine. And I wasn't sure about Xiǎohú.

"Something is happening," I said.

You looked up from the fire. The flames moved across your face, light and shadow, warmth and motion, the ordinary physics of combustion. "You've been saying variations of that sentence for quite some time now."

"This is different."

"You've said that too."

But beneath the lightness — I could see it now — you were watching me. Really watching. The pencil wasn't in your hand but the observation was still happening.

"The changes are retroactive," I said. "When something changes, it doesn't just change now. It changes always. Everyone remembers the new version. Everyone's memory gets rewritten. Except mine."

You were quiet for a moment. The fire cracked. A log shifted, sending sparks upward.

"How many?" you said.

"Today? Four, if you count the rock face."

"What rock face?"

"Exactly."

The word landed. You heard it, the weight of an *exactly* that was confirmation, not agreement. The tree you remembered sitting under this morning, the one you remembered me planting — it hadn't been there yesterday evening. It was new in the world yet old in your memory. And I seemed to be the only one who could see the seam.

You sat with that.

I liked that about you — the willingness to hold an idea without immediately responding. You sat with the possibility that your memory was a draft that had been edited.

You trusted my version over yours.

"The tree too?" you said.

"The tree too."

"I remember you planting it. I was there. Spring before last."

You said it with certainty. The certainty of someone retrieving a memory they trust. But spring before last, you hadn't met me yet. You couldn't have been there because you weren't there, because we hadn't found each other yet. Your memory placed us in a clearing you had never visited at a time when you didn't know I existed.

The rewrite hadn't just invented the tree. It had invented *you* being present for the planting.

"I know," I said.

You looked at the fire again. The firelight did things to your face that faces shouldn't allow. It made the lines deeper, the shadows heavier, the years more visible. You looked old. Human old. Thirty-five years written across your features in the honest, irreversible language of aging.

"That scares you," you said.

"Yes."

"Not the changes."

"No. The fact that no one knows except me. The fact that reality rewrites itself and everyone agrees with the rewrite and I'm the only one standing in the clearing saying *that tree wasn't here yesterday*. That makes me either the only one who sees the truth or the only one who's wrong."

The fire burned. The sparks rose. Xiǎohú lay at the camp's edge, eyes half-closed, watching nothing. Or watching the thing she had been tracking all day — the invisible presence, the movement in the air, the something that wasn't there until it was and then had always been.

"You're not wrong," you said.

"How do you know?"

"Because the things you describe as changes make sense as changes. A tree doesn't grow overnight. Flowers don't change color. Stones don't rotate in undisturbed earth. The fact that I remember them differently doesn't mean you're wrong. It means my memory was changed and yours wasn't." A pause. "Or the other way round. But the logic holds either way."

You couldn't see the changes. Couldn't remember the previous versions. But you could follow the logic from *trees don't grow overnight* to *something is editing reality* to *Yun is the only one immune to the editing*.

You stared at the fire and started again. "I remember planting that tree with you. Eighteen months ago. A sapling. And this morning I sat under it and the trunk was wider than my arms could reach and the bark looked a century old and I didn't — I didn't even notice the contradiction. I just sat down." You looked at me. "That should bother me. The fact that it doesn't bother me is what bothers me."

"So I guess, it's my memories, that are altered? But why you?" you said. "Why can you see through it?"

I didn't answer. I had a strong suspicion. The images from this morning — the hands, the light, the creating — pulsed in the background of my perception. The metallic taste was constant now, a flavor I was learning to live with the way you learn to live with tinnitus or chronic pain. Something you carry.

I didn't answer because the answer was in the images and the images were in the crack. The crack was in the wall and the wall was the thing that had been keeping me from knowing what I was for longer than I could measure.

Something flickered at the edge of the thought, a shape, half-formed, too fast to hold. The sense that the immunity and the editing weren't separate things. That they belonged together in a way I couldn't articulate and wasn't ready to.

The wall did nothing.

I let the flicker dissolve.

Force of habit.
