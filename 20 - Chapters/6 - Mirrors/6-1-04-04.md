Three more changes by evening.

A rock face that had smoothed itself into a wall, polished, almost glassy, catching the late light like a mirror I hadn't asked for. The wildflowers at the trail's edge, which had been white yesterday and were now a deep violet that streaked toward black at the edges, as if someone had adjusted the color with absolute confidence and absolute indifference to consistency. A bird call I didn't recognize, a three-note pattern, low and resonant, that Xiǎohú tracked with the focused attention of a predator hearing something that shouldn't exist.

Each change was small. Each change was complete. Each change carried with it the full weight of a past that hadn't happened. The smooth rock face had been smooth for millennia, the flowers had always been violet, the bird had always sung this three-note pattern in this valley.

Each change was retroactive.

The retroactivity was the part that made my stomach turn. The changes themselves I'd learned to expect. But this — the past rewritten without scars, without even a trace of what had been.

Simple anomalies broke rules. These changes wrote new ones.

You made camp at sunset. Efficient, methodical — the fire where it belonged, the bedrolls where they belonged, the perimeter check that had become reflexive since the sect had stopped being ignorable. You moved through the routine with the practiced ease of a man who had done this a hundred times and I watched you move and found the ordinariness comforting.

"The flowers were white yesterday," I said.

You were poking the fire with a stick. Didn't look up. "What flowers?"

"At the trailside. The ones that are violet now."

"They've always been violet."

I knew you would say that. The flowers had always been violet for you. The tree had always been there. The stone had always faced east. The water had always run uphill. The rock face had always been smooth.

Reality changed and your memory changed with it and the only memory that didn't change was mine.

"Something is happening," I said.

You looked up from the fire. The flames moved across your face, light and shadow, warmth and motion, the ordinary physics of combustion. "You've been saying variations of that sentence for six months."

"This is different."

"You've said that too."

But beneath the lightness — I could see it now — you were watching me. Really watching. The pencil wasn't in your hand but the observation was still happening.

"The changes are retroactive," I said. "When something changes, it doesn't just change now. It changes always. Everyone remembers the new version. Everyone's memory gets rewritten. Except mine."

You were quiet for a moment. The fire cracked. A log shifted, sending sparks upward.

"How many?" you said.

"Today? Six. Seven, if you count the bamboo."

"What bamboo?"

"Exactly."

The word landed. You heard it, the weight of an *exactly* that was confirmation, not agreement. The bamboo that he remembered walking through, that his body knew, that his feet had found automatically — the bamboo didn't exist yesterday. Hadn't existed at all. Was new in the world and old in his memory and I was the only one who could see the seam.

You sat with that.

I liked that about you — the willingness to hold an idea without immediately responding. You sat with the possibility that your memory was a draft that had been edited.

You trusted my version over yours.

"The tree too?" you said.

"The tree too."

"I remember you planting it."

"I know."

"I can see it. Your hands in the dirt. The rain."

"I know."

You looked at the fire again. The firelight did things to your face that faces shouldn't allow. It made the lines deeper, the shadows heavier, the years more visible. You looked old. Human old. Thirty-five years written across your features in the honest, irreversible language of aging.

"That scares you," you said.

"Yes."

"Not the changes."

"No."

"The retroactivity."

"The fact that no one knows except me. The fact that reality rewrites itself and everyone agrees with the rewrite and I'm the only one standing in the clearing saying *that tree wasn't here yesterday*. That makes me either the only one who sees the truth or the only one who's wrong."

The fire burned. The sparks rose. Xiǎohú lay at the camp's edge, eyes half-closed, watching nothing. Or watching the thing she had been tracking all day — the invisible presence, the movement in the air, the something that wasn't there until it was and then had always been.

"You're not wrong," you said.

"How do you know?"

"Because the things you describe as changes make sense as changes. A tree doesn't grow overnight. Water doesn't run uphill. Flowers don't change color. The fact that I remember them differently doesn't mean you're wrong. It means my memory was changed and yours wasn't."

You couldn't see the changes. Couldn't remember the previous versions. But you could follow the logic from *trees don't grow overnight* to *something is editing reality* to *Yun is the only one immune to the editing*.

"Why you?" you said. "Why can you see it?"

I didn't answer. I knew why. The images from this morning — the hands, the light, the creating — pulsed in the background of my perception. The metallic taste was constant now, a flavor I was learning to live with the way you learn to live with tinnitus or chronic pain. Something you carry.

I didn't answer because the answer was in the images and the images were in the crack and the crack was in the wall and the wall was the thing that had been keeping me from knowing what I was for longer than I could measure.

Something flickered at the edge of the thought, a shape, half-formed, too fast to hold. The sense that the immunity and the editing weren't separate things. That they belonged together in a way I couldn't articulate and wasn't ready to.

The wall did nothing.

I let the flicker dissolve.

Force of habit.
