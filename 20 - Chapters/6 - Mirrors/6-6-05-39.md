We arrived at a river. It was wide here. Shallow, clear-bottomed, the kind of water that showed every stone individually and made you think you could count them. Sun coming down through the tree cover in patches. Mid-morning. The air smelled like wet rock and pine sap and the freshness of water that has been running for a long time without interruption.

You prepared camp and I was washing clothes. Choosing the mundane task of scrubbing fabric against river stones as a way to keep my hands busy while my mind moved through landscapes that words couldn't adequately map.

The fabric of your outer robe, the one with the ink stain on the left sleeve that you claimed was intentional and I knew was a dropped brush, went into the water and I scrubbed. The motion was ancient. The kind of physical labor I had performed in lives I couldn't fully remember but whose muscle memory remained in my hands like notation in a margin.

I hadn't done this for someone else in — I couldn't even date it. When you walk alone for centuries, who are you washing clothes for? You wear a robe until it falls apart, replace it, wear the next one. Maintenance stops mattering when there's no one to maintain for and no version of yourself that cares about the state of fabric.

It didn't matter. My hands remembered.

Scrub. Lift. Wring. Submerge. The rhythm was meditative in the original sense — consciousness-altering. The repetition created a channel, which allowed things to move through it that the active mind would have blocked or deflected before they could finish arriving.

Water through fingers.

I noticed the fabric in my fists, the water squeezing out, running between knuckles, escaping through the gaps that tightened hands couldn't close. The harder I squeezed, the faster the water ran. Not resisting, the water wasn't fighting my grip. Moving. Flowing. Being what it was. Water's nature was to move and my fists were irrelevant to that nature. And squeezing didn't hold the water — it accelerated the water's departure.

I stopped wringing and opened my hands.

The fabric lay flat across my palms, wet, heavy, dripping. The water that hadn't been squeezed sat in the fabric. Soaking, present, unhurried. Neither escaping nor running. Sitting. The way water sits when nothing is forcing it anywhere. Gravity or the air would take it eventually. But in the moment between opening my hands and gravity's patient claim, the water was simply there.

Fists — the water escapes.
Open hands — the water stays.

I looked at my hands again. The fabric. The river moving around my ankles. Cold, the temperature a genuine sensation. My hands were pruned, the wrinkled fingertips of prolonged immersion, the body's irrelevant preparation for better grip on wet surfaces. My body still did this. Still went through the motions of being a body, adjusting, adapting, responding to stimuli with the automatic competence of a body that didn't know it was running on conviction rather than biology.

The insight didn't arrived as the water. As the physical, sensory, body-located experience of water flowing through fingers and water sitting in open palms. As the difference between gripping and holding. The non-verbal understanding that a body learns before a mind can articulate.

Something was beginning to form, to crystallize. The raw material of understanding, present but unprocessed, the way ore exists in rock before anyone identifies it as metal. I couldn't name it yet, but some things needed to exist as experience before they could accept the reduction of language.

You were upstream. Sitting on a flat rock, after finishing your tasks. Your notebook was open, pen in hand, doing the thing where the notebook was open but the attention was aimed sideways. You were watching me. Watching the water. Watching the way I moved. Watching the way the water moved around me. Watching the way I interacted with the water. The way I interacted with the fabric. The way I interacted with my own hands. Observing.

Xiǎohú was downstream, drinking. She raised her head. Water dripping from amber-lit whiskers. She looked at me. The look of an animal that had seen a human doing something obvious and was waiting for the human to catch up.

I looked back at her.

Then I looked back at my hands. At the fabric. At the water.

I submerged the robe again. Held it this time. Open-handed. The water moving through the fabric, through my fingers, through the space between all of it. Moving the way water moved. My hands were incidental. My intention was irrelevant. The water moved because it was water.

Something was crystallizing. Not yet. Almost.

I waited for it. 

Patient.
