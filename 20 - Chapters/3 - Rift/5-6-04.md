
One second.

I know it was one second because Xu Ran told me later, and Xu Ran counted everything, and his count was the only record because mine was taken.

Through the crack, the truth came in.

Not in words. Not in images. Not in any of the organized, sequential formats that consciousness uses to make sense of the world. It came the way a dam break comes — everything, at once, from every direction, the total content of something that had been held back pouring through a gap that was too small for what it carried.

I was—

I was once—

I was always—

I was here before—

I—

Five echoes. The same beginning, each one carrying a different ending that arrived too fast to read, too dense to separate, stacking on top of each other like pages being turned before the words could be absorbed. Not fading — additive. Each echo louder than the last, each one carrying more, each one filling space that the previous one had opened.

A place. A place that didn't exist — not here, not in any geography I'd seen or mapped or walked through, a place that existed in a category I didn't have vocabulary for. Not a memory. Not a dream. A FACT. A place that was real the way mathematics was real, the way principles were real, the way the rules that governed the universe were real without needing to be visited or verified.

A name. A name I'd never heard, spoken in a voice I'd never heard, in a language I'd never learned. But recognizable. Recognizable the way your own name is recognizable when spoken by a stranger — the instant, automatic, bone-level recognition of something that belongs to you regardless of who says it.

And a feeling. Not emotion — not the surface-level affect that made rain and split trees and moved mountains. Something deeper. Something structural. Something that existed at the foundation of whatever I was, in the bedrock below personality and memory and experience, in the place where being itself was rooted.

Ancient. The word is insufficient but it's the closest language gets. Ancient and lonely and certain. The certainty of something that has always existed and will always exist and knows this about itself without needing to be told or reminded or reassured.

I had always been here. I had always been.

Not alive — existed. The distinction matters. Living implies a beginning, a starting point, a moment before which you weren't and after which you were. Existing implies no such boundary. Existing is the state of things that don't have edges, that don't have befores and afters, that simply ARE in the continuous, unbroken, permanent way that the fundamental forces of the universe ARE.

This is what I saw. In one second. All of it. The full truth, compressed into a window of time too small for comprehension, delivered to a consciousness too limited for the delivery.

I know I saw it because of what Xu Ran saw.

He told me — later, much later, in a voice that was careful and quiet and stripped of the analytical detachment he normally used — that my eyes changed. Not the color. Not the shape. The depth. For one second, the thing behind my eyes was not me. Something else looked through — something that used my face the way a light uses a lantern, present behind the structure, visible through the openings, but not the structure itself.

And it saw him.

Not looked at — saw. The way a parent sees a child, the way an ocean sees a boat, the way something vast and ancient and fundamentally different recognizes something small and temporary and valuable. It saw Xu Ran. It SAW him. Every part. Every history. Every decision that had brought him to this clearing to stand five meters from something that wore a human shape and was, for one second, not wearing it very well.

He said he couldn't move. Not because of the field — the field was gone, spent, never coming back. Because the thing looking at him through my eyes was so complete, so total, so absolutely present in its attention that movement seemed disrespectful. You don't move in the presence of something like that. You stand still and you are seen and you accept the seeing because the alternative is to be seen AND to be running, and running from something that sees you this completely is not escape. It's just running.

One second.

Then—

Nothing.

I don't mean darkness. I don't mean unconsciousness. I don't mean the gentle drift that happens when the body shuts down and the mind follows and you wake up having missed some time.

This was deletion. This was the guardian, the wall — whatever it was — performing its most extreme operation. The crack had opened and the truth had come through and the truth was too much and the wall had done the only thing it could do.

It had erased the moment.

Not edited. Not softened. Not selectively removed. Erased. Completely, totally, absolutely. As if the last ten seconds had been cut from a scroll and the edges on either side had been pressed together.

I was standing in a clearing.

I didn't know why.

"What?" I said. And I meant it. The word was genuine — not rhetorical, not confused, genuinely questioning. What. What am I doing here. What is happening. What is this place. What are these long shadows. What is this man doing five meters from me with a face like cracked glass.

Xu Ran's mouth opened. Closed. He looked like someone who had been hit and the pain hadn't arrived yet, the expression of a person in the gap between impact and sensation.

"What happened?" I asked. "Why are we—" I looked around. The clearing. The open space. The short grass. The distant horizons. "How did we get here?"

The gap was total. I didn't remember walking here. Didn't remember the conversation. Didn't remember the question — the, aimed, devastating question that he'd spent days preparing and hours hating himself for and seconds delivering.

Have you killed Wei.

Gone. All of it. The question, the crack, the second, the truth, the eyes, the seeing — all of it taken, erased, filed in the void where the guardian kept the things it decided I couldn't have.

But.

There was a but. And the but was everything.

Somewhere — deep, below the deletion, below the gap, in the place where the wall couldn't reach because the wall was built to block thoughts and this wasn't a thought — something remained.

A feeling.

Not emotion. Not mood. Not affect. Something more fundamental. A frequency. A resonance. A vibration in the depths of my being that had been activated during the second of seeing and had not been deactivated when the seeing was taken away.

I couldn't name it. I couldn't describe it. I couldn't think about it — every time I tried, the thought dissolved, the wall intervened, the guardian did its work. But I could feel it. The way you feel a bass note in your chest, below the range of hearing, too low for ears, present only as vibration, as pressure, as the raw awareness that something is happening on a frequency you can receive but cannot decode.

I put my hand on my chest. The feeling was there. Under the ribs. Behind the sternum. In the space where the heart is and the lungs are and the fundamental truth of being alive are housed.

Something real. Something old. Something mine.

I didn't know what it was. I didn't know what it meant. I didn't know what had caused it or why it was there or how long it would last.

But it was there.

And for the first time since the forgetting had begun — since the wall had started building and the guardian had started taking and my own mind had started editing itself with the precision of something that knew exactly what it was doing — for the first time, something had survived.

Not a thought. Not a memory. Not information.

A feeling.

And it was enough.
