I tried to find it again.

The substrate. The warmth. The sensation that the cliff had provided — the stirring below the qi, the energy from the foundation, the hand pressing on the heart from inside. I stood at the crater's center, on the basalt, on the cold stone and I closed my eyes and I looked inward.

Looked was the wrong word. The searching was tactile. I searched for the substrate the way one searched for a sound heard in another room: by quieting everything else and listening.

The qi: present. Cycling. The autonomous humming that constituted the second breathing, the constant background process that the substrate operated through my channels. The qi was there. The qi was always there. The qi was not what I was looking for.

Below the qi. Where the cliff had shown me. Below the channels, below the architecture that cultivation built. I pushed my attention deeper — not physically, not with force, but with concentration. Descending through the layers the way a diver descended through water: each meter deeper producing greater resistance.

Deeper. Past the qi infrastructure. Past the core. Past the energetic structures that millennia of cultivation had built. Into the space where the substrate lived.

I found the boundary.

Not a wall — a threshold. The sensation of attention encountering something that was not part of the same architecture. The boundary felt like the space between wakefulness and sleep — not a surface but a transition.

I pushed.

The substrate responded.

Not the way the cliff had provoked it. The crater response was: no.

No. A locked door. The thing being sought did not want to be found.

*Not yet*, something said. The wordless impression that doors made when they were not going to open.

I pushed harder.

The qi spiked. Not gradually. Instantly — from baseline to maximum. The qi spiked and the spiking was not from me.

Stone.

The basalt beneath my hand — the hand I had placed back on the surface, trying again, pressing harder this time — cracked. A single line. Slow. Precise. The crack traveled through the stone the way a crack travels through ice: deliberate, following a grain that should not have been there. Six inches. Eight. A foot.

No sound. No tremor. No eruption. Just: stone splitting where stone should not split. Cleanly. As if someone had drawn a line with a tool that did not exist.

Then: silence.

I stood. In the center. My hand still extended toward the surface, the stone beneath it now divided by a line that had not been there before. A crack in four-hundred-year-old volcanic basalt produced by nothing I had directed and nothing I could explain.

Something in me had said: do not look here. And the not-looking-here was enforced with precision, not violence. A single impossibility. Quiet. Controlled by something that was not me.

The control was the frightening part. Not the force — the specificity. Whatever had done this had not erupted. It had *corrected*.

You. At the rim. You had seen it. Or seen enough — the crack appearing in stone that did not crack, the line traveling through basalt without tool or force.

"What—" You stopped. "What was that?"

I looked at the stone. The crack. Clean. Impossible.

"I don't know," I said.

You came down carefully. Knelt. Examined the crack. Ran your finger along it. Your face: the expression of a man encountering data that invalidated his framework.

You reached for your notebook. You wrote what you had seen. Stone. Crack. No force. No sound.

"Don't do that again," you said.

You said this without looking up.

"I wasn't trying to," I said.

"Which part?"

"Any of it."

You looked up. Assessed. Concluded: she was not lying.

You closed the notebook. You stood among the cold basalt. I stood in the crater. Between us: silence and a crack in stone that should not exist.

Something had happened at the crater's rim that you did not tell me about. Something I would learn much later, from a detail in your notebook. You had been approached. You had said no.

In the weeks that followed, your handwriting became slightly less steady. Your head would turn, occasionally, to check the path behind us. Your notebook would be held closer — against the chest, inside the robe.

I would notice these things. I would not connect them.

The crater settled. The dust cleared. The trees remained broken.

We left.
