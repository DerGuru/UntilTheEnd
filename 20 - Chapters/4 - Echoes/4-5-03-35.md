I tried to find it again.

The substrate. The warmth. The sensation that the cliff had provided — the stirring below the qi, the energy from the foundation, the hand pressing on the heart from inside. I stood at the crater's center, on the basalt, on the cold stone and I closed my eyes and I looked inward.

Looking was the wrong word. The searching was more tactile or aural. I searched for the substrate the way one searched for a sound heard in another room, by quieting everything else and listening.

My qi was present and cycling. The autonomous humming that constituted the second breathing, the constant background process that the substrate operated through my channels. The qi was there. The qi was always there. And the qi was not what I was looking for.

I had to look where the cliff had shown me. Below the qi, below the channels, below the architecture that cultivation built. So I pushed my attention deeper and concentrated. I descended through the layers the way a diver descends through water. Each meter deeper producing greater pressure and resistance.

Deeper and deeper I went. Past the qi infrastructure. Past the core. Past the energetic structures that millennia of cultivation had built. Into the space where the substrate lived.

And there I found a boundary.

Not a wall — more like a threshold. The sensation of attention encountering something that was not part of the same architecture. The boundary felt like the state between wakefulness and sleep — not a surface but a transition.

I pushed.

The substrate responded.

Not the way the cliff had provoked it. The crater response was a clear no.

No. A locked door. The thing being sought did not want to be found.

*Not yet*, something said. The wordless impression that doors made when they were not going to open.

I pushed harder.

The qi spiked. Instantly from baseline to maximum. But the spiking was not from me.

I touched the stone.

I placed my hand back on the surface, trying again, pressing harder this time. The basalt beneath my hand cracked. A single line. Slow. Precise. The crack traveled through the stone the way a crack travels through ice. Deliberately following a grain that should not have been there. Six inches. Eight. A foot.

No sound. No tremor. No eruption. Just the stone splitting where stone should not split. Cleanly. As if someone had drawn a line with a tool that did not exist.

Silence.

I stood before the stone. My hand still extended toward the surface, the stone beneath it now divided by a line that had not been there before. A crack in a thousands-of-year-old volcanic basalt produced by nothing I had directed and nothing I could explain.

Something in me had said to not look here, enforced with precision, not violence. A single impossibility. Quiet. Controlled by something that was not me.

The control was the frightening part. Not the force — the specificity. Whatever had done this had not erupted. It had *corrected*.

You stood beside me. You had seen it, too.

"What—" You stopped. "What was that?"

I looked at the stone. The crack. Clean. Impossible.

"I don't know," I said.

You came down carefully. Knelt and examined the crack. You ran your finger along it. Your face had the expression of a man encountering data that invalidated his framework.

You reached for your notebook. You wrote what you had seen. Stone. Crack. No force. No sound.

"Don't do that again," you said.

You said this without looking up.

"I wasn't trying to," I said.

"Which part?"

"Any of it."

You looked up, assessed and concluded, that I wasn't lying.

You closed the notebook. Between us was silence.

Then you said, "What are we doing now?"

"We move to the next one."

Something had happened at the crater's rim that you did not tell me about. Something I would learn much later, from a detail in your notebook. You had been approached on your way to the crater, while I was already ahead. You had said no.

And you had said nothing to me about it.

We moved on.
