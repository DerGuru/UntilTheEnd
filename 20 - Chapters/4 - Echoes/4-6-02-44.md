Morning light arrived through the canopy gap — the kind of light that found open ground wherever trees had fallen. The clearing was sixty paces across. Flat. Compacted earth, sparse grass. Good visibility. Good footing. A bird was singing somewhere in the canopy — the repetitive, two-note call of something small and territorial that did not know what was about to happen in its clearing.

I had arrived before dawn. The phial had needed warmth to liquefy and I had kept it against my skin through the night, but the compound still moved sluggishly when I tilted it. I sat on a fallen trunk at the clearing's edge and waited for the sun to do its work. Dew on the grass. The smell of wet earth and last night's cold. A spider had built a web between two stalks of grass — the kind of small, precise architecture that existed because the spider believed it should exist, and the world had no reason to disagree.

The phial warmed. The compound thinned. The spider finished a radial thread.

The sun climbed. The dew burned off in patches — first the exposed grass, then the shadowed edges, the clearing drying from center outward like a slow exhalation. The bird changed its song. A third note now, lower, as if it had decided the morning warranted elaboration.

I sat with the phial in my lap and watched the clearing exist. The light moving across the grass. An ant carrying something pale along the edge of the fallen trunk — methodical, unhurried, the commute of a body that had work to do and no opinion about the cosmic significance of the work. The air smelled like resin and distance and the particular cleanness that mountain mornings produced before the day warmed enough for the forest floor to start composting audibly.

It was a good clearing. Quiet in the way that mattered. The kind of place where nothing had happened for a long time and nothing was expected to.

I stood. Walked to the center. The phial was in my hand. Ceramic. Warm. The wax seal intact. The weight of two mouthfuls. Ninety seconds to dissolve immortality.

I heard you before I saw you. Footsteps through the undergrowth — not cautious, not quiet. The footsteps of a man who had woken to an empty camp, checked his pack, found the absence, and understood.

You emerged from the tree line. Your notebook was in one hand, the pen in the other. Your eyes found the phial immediately.

You stopped at ten paces. Not because I had asked you to — because you had calculated the distance yourself. The same distance you always maintained. The observational distance. Even furious, you defaulted to method.

"You said you'd tell me," you said.

I had. And I hadn't.

Your posture was wrong. Not the posture of recording — the posture of someone standing where they did not want to stand, arriving too late to prevent what they had come to prevent. Your weight was forward. Not observation-forward. Intervention-forward. As if the ten paces were a leash and you were testing its length.

"Ten paces," I said. "And your notebook."

You looked at the phial. You looked at the clearing. You looked at the far tree line — fifty paces from where you stood. You raised the pen slightly. The gesture that meant, that you were there. You were recording. And you had not agreed to this.

I broke the seal. The wax crumbled. The phial was open.

Absorption at fifteen seconds. Onset at thirty. Death at ninety. I knew the numbers. 

I drank.

One motion. The phial to the lips. The tilt. The liquid — thin, the consistency of water, the temperature of ambient — entered my mouth and I swallowed and the ceramic was empty.

The clearing held its features. The light. The trees at thirty paces. You at ten. The phial in my hand, empty.

I waited.

The cliff had been instantaneous — the fall was the threat, the response was the stop. But the poison was different. Internal, not external. Through the blood, not through gravity.

I waited.

Five seconds. I felt nothing. The liquid was in my stomach. Someone had put real craftsmanship into this.

Ten seconds.

Your pen moved. You were writing. 

Fifteen seconds. The absorption should be complete.

Twenty seconds.

The onset was not what I expected. The old records had described a burning — the meridians responding to the compound's approach the way nerve endings responded to acid. Burning. Progressive. Peripheral to central.

What I felt was coldness.

Not the cold of temperature. The cold of absence. Something was being eaten away. The Shadow-Eater was eating.

I felt my peripheral channels go. The minor meridians in my fingers, my toes. One by one. Not pain — absence. Coldness.

Twenty-five seconds. You held your pen at the ready, waiting for the next data point. The next observable change. 

"Fingers," I said. "The peripheral channels are dissolving."

You wrote.

"It feels cold. Like a void where the channels used to be. Not pain. Just — nothing."

Thirty seconds. 

"Secondary channels."

The compound was progressing inward. On schedule. I observed myself die from the edges. Layer by layer. Channel by channel.

It was cold. So cold.

The poison hit the primary channels.

Thirty-five seconds. The large meridians. The load-bearing structure.

I felt it. The sensation of becoming less.

"Primary channels," I said.

Forty seconds.

"The dantian."

The compound reached my core.

"Forty-five," you said. Your voice was wrong.

The Shadow-Eater ate.
