The night came and went. So did the days. Uneventful. You healed and meditated. You could carry your pack now on your own.

The morning was windless. The ridge held the cold from the night — the stone slow to warm, the grass stiff with frost that had not quite committed to being frost. More of a suggestion. The valley below was filled with mist, white and flat, and the peaks on the far side rose out of it like islands.

You coughed. Once. The short, suppressed, the sound of a body that had learned the cost of depth. You sat up from the bedroll and looked at the sky measuring, the way you looked at most things.

I kindled the fire. The last of the rice from the settlement — enough for two portions, barely. I served yours. You ate. I ate from the pot.

The volcanic terrain began an hour south of the ridge. The soil changed first — darker, grainier, scattered with fragments of basalt that caught the light in dull, glassy facets. The grass thinned. The trees stopped. The landscape became stone. Old stone, shaped by heat that had passed through it and left its signature in every surface.

The air was different. Drier. A mineral edge to it, like breathing near a forge that had been cold for years but still remembered fire. The wind picked up as the vegetation dropped away — nothing to slow it, nothing to absorb it. It pushed against us from the southwest, steady, carrying dust and the faint sulfur-smell of hot springs somewhere below the surface.

You leaned into the wind. Your coat pressed flat against your ribs. Your eyes narrowed against the grit. We crossed a field of cooled lava — the surface rough, ropy, frozen mid-flow. The basalt was dark grey, almost black, and warm to the touch despite the morning air. Residual geothermal heat. The ground remembering what it had been.

I stopped.

Something had changed inside me. A vibration — below the qi, below the meridians, below anything I had a name for. Quiet. Below the threshold of hearing. I felt it in my sternum, in my teeth, in the bones of my feet where they pressed against the warm basalt.

You stopped and turned.

I put my hand against my chest.

"What is it?" you asked.

I did not have words for it. The thing-below-qi, the substrate, the defended architecture — it was humming. As if the proximity to the crater had woken something. As if the basalt beneath my feet and whatever was inside me recognized each other.

"Nothing, not yet." I said. "It's just..."

A hawk circled above the lava field — high, patient, riding the thermals that the warm stone produced. The sulfur-smell was stronger here.

You looked at me. The fading bruises, the careful posture that broken ribs imposed. You did not ask further. You strapped your pack tighter.

We moved.

The crater was ahead. I could not see it yet, but the hum intensified with each step south — the vibration rising from below-hearing toward something that was almost sound. Almost a note. The basalt field stretched toward the horizon and somewhere beyond it the ground dipped and the stone curved inward and the lake that had filled the crater would be waiting. Still water in a bowl of old violence. And at the edge of that water, a stone I had touched once and pulled my hand back before I understood why.

The hawk had moved on. The wind had settled. The hum continued — quiet, steady, like the second breathing that I have still not gotten used to.

*Inward.*

I noticed it for the first time that morning. Not the qi — the qi was above, in the meridian structure, in the cultivated channels that the Shadow-Eater had tried to dissolve. This was below the qi. This was from the place where the warmth had originated during the poison experiment. The place where the thing-below lived.

The hum was quiet. Below the threshold of sound. I felt it rather than heard it — the vibration that occurred at the frequency where hearing became feeling, where the ear’s range ended and the body’s resonance began. A hum that lived in my bones.

It followed me. Or it was me. The hum was internal. Produced by the thing-below. The thing that was part of me. The thing that the direction pointed toward. The rhythm of something that was not my lungs but that shared my body and that had been breathing all along. The thing that I was only now hearing because my hearing had been changed by the cliff, the poison, the shockwave and the direction.

Echoes.

That was the word for what was happening. Sound reflecting off surfaces and returning changed. The things you said that came back to you altered. The things that happened that resonated through subsequent events and arrived at the present diminished but present.

The hum was the echo. Of what — I did not know. Of the thing-below. Of the warmth that woke when the poison reached the dantian. Of the voice that said *not yet* in the dawn after the shockwave. Of the always-there that I was only now beginning to hear.

I walked to the edge. The stone was there. Dark basalt, half-submerged, the size of a man's torso. Unremarkable. I put my hand on it again. The hum peaked. Not pain — recognition. The stone was warm. The water was still. The crater held us.

"Here," I said. "This is where we start."

You set your pack down. Looked at the lake, the stone, my hand on it. Looked at me.

"Start what?"

The water reflected the sky. The hum held its note — steady now, no longer climbing, arrived.

"The journey inward."

*Inward.*
