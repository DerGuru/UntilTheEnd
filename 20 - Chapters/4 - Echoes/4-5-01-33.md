The search had a direction.

Not a map. A direction. Internal. The compass the cliff had calibrated, expressing itself geographically as— 

There. And there. And there.

Old places. Places where I had previously died without dying. Places where the substrate had intervened before I knew it existed, before the question had a name.

"What are we looking for?" you asked, on the second day of the search.

"Traces."

You wrote it down. The notebook always open. The pen always moving.

"Where?"

"Places I've been before. Old ones."

"How many?"

"Several."

Your pen paused. Then continued.

"What are you looking for there?"

"Signs. That something intervened."

"How long ago?"

Wind from the east, carrying dust and the smell of dry grass. I walked a few steps before answering.

"The oldest has been many millennia. I don't remember exactly."

You wrote that down without comment. But your grip on the pen changed.

"What will we find?"

The path narrowed between stone ridges.

"I don't know," I said. "Hopefully answers."

"How will you know?"

"We'll see."

You wrote that down too.

I quickened my pace.

You noticed. Your broken core limited your physical capacity in ways mine was not limited. The distance sometimes stretched to twelve paces. Sometime to fifteen. You did not complain. You walked.

The terrain shifted. We descended from the mountain foothills into the river provinces — lowlands again, cultivated terrain that human civilization had domesticated over centuries. Roads replaced paths. Settlements became visible in the distance.

We avoided the settlements out of habit. The impulse to avoid human contact was strong, the result of a long period of isolation and the knowledge that human interaction produced complications I did not want.

"We could resupply," you said. You were looking at a village below the ridge. Smoke from cook-fires. The sound of a market bell.

"Fine. I'll wait here. You go down and get what we need."

You adjusted your pack straps. Said nothing else.

I waited on the ridge while you went down. You came back with dried meat, rice, a new water skin. The transaction had taken less than an hour.

A pass road, worn by commerce. It didn't follow the direction exactly, but close enough, and faster than you cutting through open terrain.

Near a small marketplace I saw someone.

A man. Walking the opposite direction. Alone. Ordinary clothing, ordinary stride — and underneath, heavenly qi so dense and clean it was hard to look at directly. His masking was excellent. Better than most Immortals I'd encountered. But his body was wrong — or right, in a way I'd only seen once before. Soul and flesh fused into one thing. Not a soul living inside a body. A soul that *was* the body. Nascent Soul achievement, the real kind. And the qi's shape — narrow, vertical, edged. Like a blade that had opinions about justice.

Judgement's Gale. I'd heard the name.

He glanced at me as we passed. A brief hitch in his stride — not alarm, not recognition. Confusion. His qi-sense had swept the road ahead and found a hole where a person should have been. A woman-shaped absence walking south.

I inclined my head. The classical greeting. Polite, minimal, forgettable.

He returned it. Still frowning slightly.

We kept walking. Neither of us slowed down.

The direction continued. The First goal was three days ahead.
