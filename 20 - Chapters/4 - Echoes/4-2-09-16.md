Morning. The quality of morning light that indicated the sun was above the tree line and that the mist had dissipated.

I stood.

This was not new. Standing was the default transition between sitting and the next state. But the standing contained a direction. Categorical, not geographic. No compass bearing, no destination, no named location toward which the body oriented. The direction was: not here.

The disconnection — the gap, the absence of will that had characterized the weeks since the forest — was not repaired. The will was still absent in the form that produced hunger and sleep and the desire for particular outcomes. But below the severed line, in the layer where need operated, in the stratum where the body breathed and the heart beat and the involuntary functions performed their maintenance. Below all of that, something had shifted.

Not a decision. Decisions required the architecture that the disconnection had dismantled. Something else. A vector. The directional impulse that indicated: the current position is no longer sustainable and any other position is preferable and the preference is not a choice but a necessity.

I had no possessions to pack. The recognition of this — the awareness that packing was the action that preceded departure and that packing required possessions and possessions were a category I no longer inhabited — was ironically the first thing that felt familiar. I had often left with nothing. Leaving with nothing was the oldest form of departure. The form that predated luggage.

I moved. Not walked — moved. The distinction was the direction. Walking was the undirected locomotion that the body performed when standing was exhausted. Moving was the directed locomotion that the body performed when the direction existed and the direction was: away from here.

You watched.

You were sitting at the fire, the morning fire, the one you made each day with the routine that characterized your mornings. Rice steaming. Notebook open. The pen: paused. You had been writing and the writing had stopped when I moved.

"You're packing," you said.

"I have nothing to pack."

"You're making the motions."

You were right. The body — the muscle memory that countless departures had worn into the bones — was performing the departure sequence. Shoulders: adjusted. Weight: shifted to the balls of the feet. Gaze: forward, elevated, the visual orientation that preceded sustained travel. The motions of packing without the possessions to pack. The ghost of departure.

You closed the notebook. Not the closure of someone finishing a thought. The deliberate closure of someone recognizing that the phase had changed. You did not put it away. The notebook was never away, never inaccessible, because inaccessible produced forgetting and forgetting was the thing you fought. You held it. Closed. Against your chest.

"Where?" you asked.

The question was valid. Direction was the variable that the departure-sequence required and that the vector provided and that the vector did not specify. The vector said: away. The vector did not say: toward what. Away was the beginning, not the map.

I did not answer. The silence came from genuine absence, not from the disconnection. The question of *where* would fill itself as the movement continued, the way a river's destination was determined by the terrain and not by the water. I would go. The going would encounter geography. The geography would produce the where.

You stood. The fire: abandoned. The rice: untouched, still steaming. You looked at the rice. The rice steamed. You looked at me. You left the rice.

You did not abandon rice lightly. You did not abandon routine lightly. Routine was your defense and you were setting it aside.

"Forward," you said.

One word. A direction, not a question. A statement, not encouragement. The word contained everything about you in a single syllable: forward, next, continue. The word that opposed stopping. The word that refused the static. The word that said: whatever happens now, it happens in the direction of further and further is the only direction available.

*Forward.*

You picked up your pack. The worn pack with the patched seams and the ginger pouch and the notebooks — plural, I now realized, you carried more than one — and the scratched pot and the provisions that sustained a body that needed sustaining. You picked it up with the practiced economy of someone for whom the picking-up was as automatic as my standing.

The morning air was cold. Your body creaked when you stood: the left side, the compensation, the cost of carrying a broken core through mountain terrain for weeks. You did not complain. You adjusted the pack on your shoulders, favoring the right side. The injury talking.

The body oriented. East, or whatever direction the body chose without the mind's participation. East, because the face pointed that way, because the tree line broke there, because that was the path of least resistance. The route the direction-absent body selected.

I walked. The first step: the same as every step since the hut. The second step: different. Different because behind me, ten paces behind me, you walked too. The ten-pace distance maintained with the precision that had characterized every day of your presence. Ten paces. The gradient. Brown to yellow to green. The ecological distance between what I was and what lived.

Ten paces behind me, someone followed. I did not lead. You followed. The distinction was your choice, not mine. Following was your verb. Your choice. Accepted the moment you picked up your pack.

The forest opened ahead. The forest closed behind. Between us: ten paces. Between me and the world: three. The math was simple. The math was the architecture of every future interaction: close enough to hear, far enough to survive, the distance that coexistence required between someone who killed grass and someone who wrote about it.

Two figures on a path. One ahead. One behind. The morning light between them, casting shadows that fell in the correct direction.

For now.
