Morning. The third since the shockwave. A thin mist hung between the trees — the kind that burns off by mid-morning but makes everything look soft until then. The fire was embers. A wren was singing somewhere in the undergrowth, the rapid, disproportionately loud song of a bird smaller than a fist.

You cooked breakfast. One-handed, stirring with slow, careful motions — the ribs governing the radius of every gesture. The rice was unevenly heated, some grains soft, others still crunchy. You served it in the wooden bowl and ate it slowly, each breath between bites measured against the damage.

You were slow about it. Not the slowness of the ribs — that I knew by now, the careful measured pace that broken bone imposed. This was different. You stirred longer than the rice required. You adjusted the fire when the fire did not need adjusting. You ate in small bites with long pauses between them, watching the mist thin between the trees as if the mist were interesting.

"Your observations are correct," I said. "The notebook. All of it."

You looked up from your rice.

"You read it," you said.

"I did."

A pause. You resumed eating. But something had shifted — the set of your shoulders, the angle of your attention. The investigation should have been mine, but since I forgot all about it had been yours. The confirmation made it ours again.

"What do you remember?" you asked. Casually. Between bites. The question of a man who was not stalling.

"A direction," I said. "Inward. And a feeling that I understood something once. The shape of the understanding is there. The content isn't."

You nodded. Chewed. Swallowed carefully — the ribs.

"The second notebook," you said. "The summary. Have you read it today?"

"While you were cooking," I said.

You ate another bite. Small. The pause between bites growing longer, not shorter.

"Rice is running low," you said. Inspecting the bag. Weighing it in one hand. "Three days. Maybe four if we stretch it."

"And the ginger?"

You checked. "Two roots. A week if I use it sparingly." You looked at me. "Where's the nearest settlement?"

I considered. The geography was a blur — the months before the shockwave existed as fragments, directions without landmarks. But somewhere beneath the blur, older knowledge persisted. The kind that accumulated over time and that the erasures did not bother with because knowing where towns were threatened nothing.

"South. Three days at your pace. Maybe more."

"My pace," you said. The dry tone.

After breakfast you meditated. On the ground, the lotus position modified for the ribs — your torso tilted slightly, the breathing shallow, controlled. The mist was thinning. Sunlight came through in pale columns, catching the moisture in the air, illuminating small drifting particles that were either dust or pollen or both. The meditation that had once been audible and disruptive was subdued now. Quiet enough that I could hear the wren still going, and beyond it, the distant knock of a woodpecker working a dead trunk.

I sat beside you. Three paces. The moss on the root was cool and damp through my clothes. I watched the light shift and listened to you breathe and the breathing was a kind of metronome — the slow, careful rhythm that the ribs allowed.

You finished earlier than usual. Opened your eyes. A spider had built a web between the root and a low branch overnight — you looked at it for a long moment, the geometry of the silk catching the new light. Then you registered me sitting three paces away, and said nothing.

"Could you have another look at the shoulder? And the head wound."

You turned. Presented the shoulder — the one I had reset the first night. I placed my hand on the joint. Moved it gently. The range of motion was limited but improving — swelling down, the tissue knitting. The shoulder would recover.

"Better," I said.

"It doesn't feel better."

"It's better."

You tilted your head. The temple laceration — the scab was dark, edges clean, no heat, no redness. Healing well. The concussion was the real concern, not the surface wound. The dizziness that still caught you when you stood too quickly. That would take longer.

"The cut is clean," I said. "The dizziness?"

"Comes and goes. Worse in the morning."

I sent a thin qi thread through the tissue around the wound. Not the channels — just the surface, the damaged capillaries, the bruised bone beneath. You held still. The thread did what it could, which was not much at the surface level but was more than nothing.

"The shoulder needs more time," I said. "Don't lift anything heavy with that arm."

"I'm cooking rice one-handed in the wilderness with broken ribs." The corners of your mouth moved. Almost. "Heavy lifting is not imminent."

I figured it out. You were stalling. You did not want to walk. The four hours from yesterday — the negotiation of every step with a torso that punished motion — had not been forgotten. Your remembered and you were making your position clear by stretching everything into something that resembled permanence.

"We go."

Two words. This time the words were mine.

You looked at me. The look lasted longer than the words warranted. Then you picked up your pack — the slow, one-armed motion that the ribs allowed.

"Hand me your bag. Today, I will carry it." I said, taking the bag from your hands and shouldering it.

The day settled into its shape. We moved slowly — your ribs dictating the pace, your pauses dictating the rhythm. The path wound through mixed forest. Birch and oak, the undergrowth sparse enough to walk through without detour. I plugged some mushrooms from a fallen log — bracket fungi, the shelf-like kind, pale orange against the dark bark. A stream crossing where you paused and drank and I refilled the water-skin. The water was cold and clear and tasted like stone.

Your breathing was careful all day. I heard each one and walked within its rhythm. When you stopped — to rest, to breathe, to wait for the dizziness to pass — I stopped too. We stood and the forest went on around us. A pause in the forward motion, like a rest in music.

By afternoon, a light rain came. Brief. The kind that barely wets the canopy before it stops. The air afterward smelled sharp and green — the forest exhaling.

By evening, we had covered a distance I would normally cover in two hours. I built the fire in the shelter of a granite outcrop that deflected the breeze.
