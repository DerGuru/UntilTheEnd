The granite outcrop held the warmth of the fire. The last light was bleeding through the trees in long, amber strips, and the first moths had found the flame — three of them, copper-winged, circling without urgency. I had cooked — the rice, the routine, the serving. Your bowl was beside you. You ate slowly, the careful rib-governed bites that I had stopped counting. Somewhere below the outcrop, a cricket started. Then a second.

There was rice left in the pot. And for the first time in longer than I cared to measure, I wanted some. I walked to the fire. Took the pot and sat down with it. Near the fire, near you, near the warmth. I ate from the pot with the wooden spoon.

You saw it. The arrested motion of a man mid-bite. Your eyes on the pot, on me, on the absence of ceremony. 

You resumed eating. The corners of your eyes did something — not quite a smile. The suppression of one. The suppression was respect. Don't name it. Don't comment. I looked at you. The spoon against my lips. 

The eating continued. The fire settled — a log shifted, sent a brief column of sparks upward, and the moths scattered and returned. The air had cooled. The granite beneath me was still warm from the afternoon sun, holding it the way stone does: slowly, reluctantly. The silence was not empty, not heavy, just: present. Two people who had walked together and now sat together.

The grief was not gone.

The question, the *Why not protect Wei, too?*, the clamped-off condition that had characterized my state since the he died. The grief was still present. But it had been contained. The search — the investigation, the direction, the inward pull — it had given the grief a container. A vessel. A purpose. The grief was not less. It was housed. It lived in a structure now and the structure gave it boundaries, which meant that the grief did not occupy the entire cognitive space anymore. There was room left for other things now. For eating. For sitting. For walking to the pot and taking it.

The balance was progress and loss, simultaneously. I tried to reach for a Wei-memory — deliberately, consciously, the voluntary recall that should have produced the image of Wei cooking, Wei's hands, Wei's way of cutting vegetables with a blade that was too large for the task.

Static.

Not silence — static. Like a signal that existed but could not be received. Like a room where someone had turned off the light, but with the additional quality of noise. The static was new. The memory-gaps had been silent. Empty rooms. This was a room with something in it that I could not see.

The static lasted for two heartbeats. Then it was gone. The attempt at recall ended. The memory did not arrive.

I could not tell if the memory was being erased or if it was being protected. The static was ambiguous. Defensive or protective — I could not tell.

Three sentences of experience. The static, the two heartbeats, the absence. I did not analyze it. I did not tell you. I returned to the rice. It had cooled. The wooden spoon clicked against the rim of the pot — a small, domestic sound in a forest that was filling with night.

The eating continued. The fire burned. The balance — progress, loss, simultaneously — was the condition of all my existence and I did not know if the sum was positive or negative and I did not try to calculate it.

The fire burned lower now. One of the moths landed on the rim of your bowl, stayed for three breaths, and left. You went to sleep and I took watch.

The fire needed tending — small branches, placed carefully, the geometry of airflow. The crickets had multiplied. A chorus now, rhythmic, overlapping, the sound of a forest that had decided the day was over.

I softly placed my hand against your ribs. Qi. Enough to ease the inflammation around the fractures without waking you. Your breathing changed and became a fraction deeper, the exhale losing its careful edge. Two minutes. Then I withdrew.

An owl called from the ridge above us. Not the same species as the one two nights ago — this one had a lower pitch, a doubled note. Something rustled in the undergrowth to the east. Stopped. Continued. Too heavy for a mouse, too light for a deer. A badger, perhaps, or a fox measuring its options.

The moths had gone. The fire had shrunk to a core of embers, orange and grey, and I fed it another branch. The wood was damp — it hissed before it caught. The smoke changed direction twice, following currents I could not feel.

An hour passed. I worked on your shoulder, where the tissue was still swollen and hot beneath the skin. Qi in small pulses, timed to your heartbeat. Encouraging the body to do its own work.

The forest shifted. The crickets stopped — all of them, simultaneously, as if a conductor had lowered a baton. Three seconds of absolute silence. Then they resumed. Something had passed through.

Your breathing was steady. The scab at your temple had darkened further, the skin around became lighter — good. Dry, closed, no infection. I checked the meridians near the wound, the minor channels that fed the scalp. Bruised but open. No qi needed there.

The deepest hours were the quietest. The owl had gone. The rustling had moved west and faded. Only a few stars were visible through the canopy. Just the brightest ones, framed by oak branches that looked like cracks in a dark ceiling. The fire was embers now and I let it stay that way. Your body heat and the residual warmth of the granite were enough.

I placed my hand against your ribs again. The same thread of qi. The inflammation had receded — measurably. Progress in fractions. I held the thread a bit longer this time. Your face did not change. You slept the way injured people sleep. Deeply, completely, the body commandeering all resources for repair.

Then the air changed.

The temperature dropped by a degree, maybe two. Moisture was gathering. Not rain — not yet — but the precursor. The kind of humidity that settles into fabric and hair and makes everything heavier. And with it, rising from the forest floor, from the granite, from the moss and the dead leaves and the soil beneath came the best scent in the world: petrichor.

Not the petrichor of rain. The petrichor of anticipation — the earth exhaling in advance, the oils from the leaves and the minerals from the stone releasing into air that was finally wet enough to carry them. It was the smell of four in the morning in a forest that had been dry for days. It was specific and unrepeatable and it filled the clearing with something that was not quite scent and not quite temperature and not quite memory but all three.

I breathed it in. That was all. I breathed it in and inhaled the forest itself.

The grey came gradually. No sunrise yet, but the absence of full darkness. The canopy turned from black to charcoal. The oak branches became visible as shapes rather than silhouettes. 

You stirred. The grey light found your face — the bruise at the temple, the line of the jaw, the careful way you held your left side even in sleep. You opened your eyes. Looked at the sky. Looked at me.

"Morning," you said.

I had water warming on the rekindled embers. And fresh rice, enough for two portions. I served yours first — the bowl, the spoon, the routine. You sat up slowly, ribs announcing themselves, and ate.

I ate from the pot again. The second time was less remarkable than the first. That was the point.

The night was gone. The moisture was burning off — wisps of mist curling between the tree trunks, thinning as the light strengthened. The birds resolved into a proper chorus. The forest was awake.

We packed. I took the pack. You stood without assistance — slower than before the cliff, faster than three days ago. Progress in fractions.

We walked south.
