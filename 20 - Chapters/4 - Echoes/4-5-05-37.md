We were on the road again. You wrote faster now.

Not faster in the sense of rushed — faster in the sense of urgent. The notebook was no longer your project but my lifeline.

You wrote before I spoke. You wrote during speech. You wrote afterwards, filling gaps. My posture, my expression, the behavioral markers my words did not contain. You wrote as if constructing a backup of me — a Yun-on-paper who would exist even if the Yun-in-person forgot.

"The next site should be east. Past the ridge, where the soil changes," you said, at the fire, on a morning that followed a night that I mostly remembered.

"When did I say that?"

You showed me the notebook. Third entry from the top. The date. The time. My words.

I had given a direction. With specifics. Past the ridge, where the soil changes — the instruction of someone who was navigating from knowledge she couldn't account for. The investigation instinct, still operational. Still producing output.

I did not remember saying that.

This was the pattern. Not consistent erasure — selected erasure. The memories that disappeared connected to the investigation, to the substrate, to the cognitive territory the self-protection targeted.

Other memories remained. I remembered your meditation from last night. It was still loud, still irritating, still the acoustic assault that constituted your evening procedure. I remembered the playful argument about ginger.

But the investigation-adjacent memories were all gone.

You were my memory now. Not metaphorically. Your notebook contained the record that my mind no longer reliably maintained. Your pages contained the thoughts I had thought and forgotten and would think again and might forget again.

Millennia of self-sufficiency and my memory was being maintained by a man whose evening breathing exercises sounded like a water buffalo with swollen nose.

You adapted.

The notebook entries changed format. Previous entries had been observational. Your perspective. Your analysis. "Subject touched basalt. Withdrew hand." "Subject spoke eleven words." "Subject shows behavioral markers consistent with —"

New entries became instructional. My perspective. My words, quoted exactly. The format of someone who was writing not for your own reference but for the reader who would need the content to reconstruct herself.

"Read this every morning," you had written. At the top of a new section. In handwriting deliberately larger than your usual miniature — readable at first glance, without squinting.

The section contained everything from what we were doing over what we had found to our conclusions. The briefing that restored awareness.

You had turned your notebook into my morning briefing.

You were also paranoid.

I noticed over the days. You looked behind us. Not often — not dramatic. Intermittently. The frequency of head-turns that indicated background monitoring, passive surveillance of a threat you were not discussing.

Your handwriting, when you noticed me watching, steadied. The shake was present when you were unobserved and absent when you were observed. Control.

Something had happened.

I did not know what. It must have happened within the memory gap. But something had happened to you during my investigation and it had changed your behavior. You showed fear. Not of me. Not of the substrate. Fear of something external.

"Are you alright?" I asked.

"I'm fine," you said. I detected the lie. It had the same shape as mine. Small, necessary, protective. I let it slide.

We continued. You wrote. I forgot. You reminded. I continued.

You compensated. I failed. You compensated. I failed.

The dependency was not comfortable. Millennia of self-sufficiency, now conditional on a broken-core researcher with a notebook, a pen and a fear you wouldn't name.

But the dependency was functional.

And functional was more than I had managed alone.
