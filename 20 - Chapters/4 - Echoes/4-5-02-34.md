The crater was smaller than I remembered.

Four hundred years of erosion and vegetation had softened everything. The rim: rounded, no longer the sharp edge that a volcanic eruption left when it carved a bowl into the mountain's side. The slopes: covered in scrub grass and succession-growth. The interior: a depression, bowl-shaped, thirty meters across. It looked natural unless you knew what had made it.

I knew what had made it.

The lava had been the incidental detail. I had fallen into a volcanic eruption. Wrong place, wrong time.

The lava had been hot enough to destroy everything it touched. I had been in it. My body should have ended there.

It didn't.

I stood at the rim. Wind moved through the scrub grass, dragging damp air up the bowl. A volcanic eruption and the worst part was the nostalgia.

The air here was different — four hundred years of cooling had not erased the mineral taste that volcanic activity left. Sulfuric. Below normal detection but not below mine.

You stood behind me. Fifteen paces this time — my faster pace stretching the standard gap. You were breathing heavily. The trip down the mountain had been steep and your broken core made steep things harder.

"This is one of the places?" you asked.

"Lava."

"When?"

"Four hundred years."

Your pen moved. Four-hundred-year-old lava. Date, location, substance, outcome.

I descended into the crater.

The slope was gentle — vegetation and erosion had converted the sharp interior walls to walkable angles. I went down into the bowl. 

At the bottom: stone. Volcanic basalt — dark, dense, the kind of rock that remembered being liquid. I knelt. Put my hand on the stone.

I pulled it back.

Immediately. The way one pulled a hand from a hot surface — but the stone was cold. Four hundred years of cold. The reaction was not thermal. Something in the contact had produced a response that bypassed thought and went directly to: withdraw.

I looked at my hand. Normal. I looked at the stone. Normal. Whatever had happened existed in the space between the two — in the contact itself, in the milliseconds of touch before the withdrawal.

I did not put my hand on the stone again.

I climbed back to the rim.

You showed me the notebook. Your entry: date, location, observation. "Subject touched basalt surface at crater center. Withdrew hand immediately. No verbal report. Duration of contact: <2 seconds. Reaction appeared involuntary."

The clinical language was accurate. The clinical language was the kindness.

"Now what?" you asked.

I looked at the crater. One old site. One contact. One involuntary withdrawal that I could not explain.

"The next one," I said.

You closed the notebook. We moved.
