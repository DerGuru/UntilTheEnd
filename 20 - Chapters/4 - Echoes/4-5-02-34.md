The crater had become a lake.

Four hundred years of rain and groundwater had filled what was left of the bowl. The rim — rounded now, softened by erosion and succession-growth — curved around a body of still, dark water perhaps fifty meters across. Temples lined the eastern shore. Small ones. Stone foundations, wooden roofs, copper ornaments gone green with age. Incense smoke drifted across the water. A path wound down from the ridge, worn smooth by the feet of pilgrims who came here to pray at what they believed was a sacred site.

None of them knew they were praying on a grave.

I knew what had made this place. The last time I had stopped someone's ascension, the release of energy had carved this bowl into the mountainside. The lava had been incidental — a consequence, not a cause. I had been in it. My body should have ended there.

It didn't.

We stood on the western rim, away from the temples. Wind moved across the lake, carrying incense and the mineral taste that volcanic ground never fully lost. Sulfuric. Below normal detection but not below mine.

You stood behind me. Fifteen paces — my faster pace stretching the gap. You were breathing heavily. The descent had been steep and your broken core made steep things harder.

"This is one of the places?" you asked.

"The first."

"When?"

I looked at the lake. At the temples. At the green copper.

"The crater — a long time ago. The lake took four hundred years to fill. The temples came after."

Your pen moved. Date, location, geological history, current state. You looked at the temples across the water and added something. I didn't ask what.

I descended to the waterline.

The western shore was rockier than the temple side — no path, no pilgrims, no reason for anyone to come here. Volcanic basalt broke through the soil at the water's edge. Dark, dense, the kind of rock that remembered being liquid.

I knelt. Put my hand on the stone.

I pulled it back. Immediately. The way one pulled a hand from a hot surface — but the stone was cold. Centuries of cold. The reaction was not thermal. Something in the contact had produced a response that bypassed thought and went directly to withdrawal.

I looked at my hand. Normal. I looked at the stone. Normal. Whatever had happened existed in the space between the two — in the contact itself, in the milliseconds of touch before the withdrawal.

I knelt there for a long time. The water lapped against the basalt. Across the lake, a bell rang in one of the temples — afternoon prayers. The sound carried cleanly over the water, lingered, faded.

I put my hand on the stone again.

The withdrawal came faster this time. Not my decision. My hand moved before my mind registered the contact. Like touching a surface the body had already classified as dangerous.

Third attempt. I pressed my palm flat and held it. The impulse to withdraw cam again. I fought it down and forced my hand to stay. Two seconds. Three. The stone was cold and smooth and underneath the cold and the smooth there was — 

Something. Not heat. Not qi. Not anything I had a word for. A pressure that existed in a direction I could not point to. Like the stone remembered what had happened here and the memory had texture.

Four seconds. Five. My fingers began to tremble. Not from cold.

I lifted my hand. The trembling stopped immediately.

The stone was unchanged. No mark, no crack, no residue. Whatever I had felt was not physical. Or not physical in ways I understood.

I moved along the waterline. Touched other stones. Basalt, all of it — the same formation, the same event. Some produced the withdrawal response. Others did not. The ones closest to the center of the old crater — now the deepest part of the lake — were the strongest. The response faded with distance from the center, like ripples in reverse.

I mapped it. Not on paper — in my body. Stone by stone, contact by contact, building a topology of something I could not name.

You watched from the rim. Your pen never stopped moving.

When I climbed back, my hands were wet from the lake and shaking slightly. I pressed them against my thighs until they stilled.

You had the notebook open. 

"The first time you touched the stone. You pulled your hand back."

"Yes."

"Why?"

I opened my mouth. Closed it. Tried again.

"It wasn't hot. It wasn't painful. It was —" I looked at my hand. "Like the stone didn't want to be touched. No. Like something between me and the stone didn't want the contact. A —" I stopped. The words were wrong. All of them. "A direction. A pressure. But not from the stone. From —"

I gestured vaguely. At the lake. At myself. At the space between.

"From inside?"

"I don't know."

"From the stone?"

"I don't know."

You waited. Pen still.

"The third time, when I held on. There was something underneath. Not qi. Not heat. Something that had —" I pressed my palms together. "Texture. Like a memory that wasn't mine. Or was mine but I couldn't —"

I stopped. Shook my head.

"Sorry. I— I can't explain it. Not now. But there was something there. I know it was there. It was real."

You wrote that down. Then read back what you had observed: the three contacts, the timing, the tremor, the forty minutes along the waterline, the pattern fading with distance from the center.

The clinical language filled the gaps my words could not.

"Now what?" you asked.

I looked at the lake. The temples. The pilgrims on the far shore, carrying offerings to a place whose real history would terrify them.

One old site. Multiple contacts. A response I could feel but not explain. A pattern I could map but not interpret.

It was more than I had expected. It was not enough.
