"The answer is inside me," I said.

We were sitting by a river. Not a river — a generic, unremarkable, functionally-adequate river that provided water and a place to stop and the ambient sound of moving fluid that the environment offered. The river was: context. The words were: the thing.

"The answer is inside me. So I have to go in."

The longest sentence I had spoken in months. From silence to one word (*Me*) to two words (*You too*) to a complete declarative statement with subject, verb, object and logical conclusion.

You looked at me. You were sitting against a tree — the posture that your ribs demanded, the vertical support that external structure provided. Your arm was improving. The swelling had diminished. The bruising around your eye had progressed from purple-yellow to the greenish-brown of late-stage resolution. Your ribs were: unchanged. Ribs healed slowly.

"Sounds unpleasant," you said.

You meant it. Going inward — into the defended space, into the thing-below-qi that had nearly killed you — that sounded unpleasant. The assessment was: accurate.

It was the first humor in a long time. Not because the situation was funny. Because the people in the situation had reached the relationship density where humor could occur.

You strapped on your pack. One-handed — the ribs precluding two-handed motion. You adjusted the weight. You stood up. You stood beside me.

I hesitated.

The direction was clear. The purpose was clear. The course was clear. But the first step was: difficult. Not mechanically — my legs worked, my muscles obeyed, the body that survived shockwaves and poisons could certainly walk. Difficult because the first step toward the inward direction was the first step toward the thing that headached and erased and hid.

I stood at the river. Hesitating. The water moved. The day was: present.

"Forward," you said.

The word arrived from beside me. Three paces. The distance that the arrangement had settled into since the shockwave — closer than ten, farther than two, the negotiated proximity that accounted for your injury and my danger.

*Forward.*

The third and final time. The guide-word that was yours — the one-word directive that governed your approach to: everything. Forward. Continue. Keep going. The word you had first spoken when the walking began. The word you had spoken when the direction crystallized. And now: the word you spoke into my hesitation, into the gap between purpose and motion, into the interval where I knew what to do but had not yet begun doing it.

*Forward.*

I walked.

You followed. Limping. Your breathing calibrated for the ribs. Your pack adjusted one-handed. Your notebook — recovered from the debris, repaired with twine, functional — against your chest.

We walked toward the old crater. The four-hundred-year-old site where lava had cooled and basalt had formed and I had once laid my hand against the stone and felt a vibration that was ambiguous. The place where whatever lived inside had first been suspected — or intuited. The place where I would begin the inward turn.

The crater was: the beginning. The shockwave clearing was: the reckoning. The crater was: the next step.

Three paces. On the ground. Legs crossed. Silent. Facing the same direction — forward, toward the fire.
