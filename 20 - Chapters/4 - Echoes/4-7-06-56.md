We were in the room. The narrow bed, the straw mattress, the rough blanket. The river audible through the thin walls. The light through the window had shifted from amber to grey. Somewhere downstairs, a door closed. A child's voice, muffled, said something and was answered by a woman's laugh.

"The answer is inside me. So I have to go in."

You were sitting on the bed, back against the wall. Your arm was improving. The bruising around your eye had progressed to the greenish-brown of late-stage resolution. Your ribs were unchanged. The bed had helped, though. You looked less like someone held together by discipline alone.

"Sounds unpleasant," you said.

You strapped on your pack. One-handed — the ribs precluding two-handed motion. You adjusted your weight. Stood up and moved beside me.

The river flowed below the window, catching the last light in shifting fragments. A dog barked twice from one of the houses and stopped. The smell of woodsmoke drifted in — someone else's fire, someone else's evening meal, the ordinary rhythms of a place that did not know us and would not remember us.

"Forward," you said.

I moved.

We went down, out and left the settlement soon after the sun rose above the horizon. The bridge creaked under our weight. The woman was not hanging laundry yet — too early, the mist still sitting on the river like gauze. A rooster called from behind one of the houses. The sound followed us into the trees and then the trees swallowed it.

You followed. Limping. Your breathing calibrated for the ribs. They had gotten better, but there was still a way ahead of it. I carried your pack. You had your notebook against your chest.

The path climbed through birch and pine. The ground was drier here — sandy soil, scattered needles, the crunch of it under our feet replacing the soft give of forest floor. A jay announced us from three trees ahead, hopping from branch to branch, staying just out of reach. It followed us for a quarter hour before losing interest.

We stopped twice and you leaned against things — a boulder, a fallen trunk. I waited. You straightened. We continued.

By midday the pines had thinned and the landscape opened — a ridge, a valley below, the kind of long view that the usually forested hills rarely permitted. Grass moved in the wind. The sky was high and pale and carried the smell of warm stone. In the distance, south and east, the terrain changed color — darker, rougher, the volcanic geology beginning to assert itself. We stood there for a moment taking it all in.

We moved back toward the old crater, where lava had cooled, basalt had formed and I had laid my hand against the stone. I had pulled it back before I understood why. The place where whatever lived inside had first stirred. The place where I would begin the inward turn.

You meditated that evening. On the ground beside the fire. The lotus position, modified for broken ribs. The air smelled different here — drier, mineral, the pine resin giving way to something older. A moth found the flame — not the copper-winged kind from the outcrop, a smaller one, pale, orbiting with the patience of something that had nowhere else to be.

I sat beside you. Legs crossed. Facing the fire. A cricket started somewhere in the grass behind us. Then stopped, as if reconsidering.

You finished. Opened your eyes. I could see the faintest trace of a smile.

The moth had landed on a stone near the embers. Wings folded. Still. The valley below us was dark, but the ridge still held the last light — a thin line of amber along the rock, fading as we watched.

The fire burned.
