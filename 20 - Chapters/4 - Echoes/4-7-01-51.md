The evening came gently. The light shifted from the long afternoon into the warm tones that preceded dusk — amber filtering through the bare trunks at the destruction's edge, the shadows of the fallen trees stretching until they merged with each other and with the general dimming.

We did not speak. The silence between us was occupied — not empty. You lay beneath the lean-to and breathed your careful breaths and watched the light change. I tended the fire. Added wood. The cycle from the night before, resuming.

I heated water. You drank. Small sips. The ribs still governing everything — depth of breath, angle of swallow, the geometry of existing inside a damaged frame. I watched you drink and I waited until you had finished.

"I'm going to feed you some qi, to help with the ribs," I said. "Like last night — you were asleep for those."

You looked at me. The right eye steady. The left eye still swollen half-shut. You nodded.

I placed my hand on your chest. Over the ribs. You flinched — not from the touch but from the anticipation. I sent the thread. Thin. Along the primary meridian. The qi settled into the tissue around the ribs. Warm. Gentle. Your breathing changed — not the sharp punishment, but a softening. The inflammation releasing its grip by a small degree. Your face registered it.

I sent a second thread. Deeper. Along the secondary meridian toward the shoulder. The cleared paths from earlier that day accepted it easily — the sediment I had removed during the morning had stayed removed. But the channel itself was narrower here, more scarred. Residual buildup from the old damage, the years since the tribulation. I pressed against a constriction. Gently. The silt shifted. The channel widened.

You went still. A slight twitch in your shoulder — the involuntary response of a channel remembering what flow felt like. Something opening. Your eyes found mine.

"That's not just the ribs," you said.

I held the thread steady but said nothing.

"You're clearing the channels." Not a question. The tone of a man who had spent his life studying qi and who knew what channel work felt like even through a broken system. Your hand moved to your chest — not touching where mine was, but near it. The instinct of a former cultivator sensing qi movement inside himself for the first time in a long time.

"The old damage left residue," I said. "The healing threads work better through clean paths."

You were quiet. Processing. Then:

"The channels. Not the core."

"Not the core."

A pause. The fire crackled. An ember popped and sent a small shower of sparks upward into the deepening sky.

"Could you?" you asked. "The core."

I withdrew my hand. Sat back. I thought about the collapsed structure at the center of your system — the sharp-edged wreckage, the instability, the places where any intervention would cascade into secondary collapses.

"Not yet," I said. "Maybe."

You accepted that. The way you accepted data — without emotion, without argument. Filed. Stored. Available for future reference. Maybe was not no. Maybe was a door that was closed but not locked.

I continued the channel work. Three more paths. Slow. Careful. You lay still and said nothing. But I could see in your face that the feeling was strange. Familiar and strange simultaneously. The sensation of qi moving through channels that had been inert for quite some time now. Not the full flow of an active cultivator — nothing close to that. A trickle through ruins. But present.

When I was finished I sat back. Your breathing was marginally easier. A fraction. The channels carrying the healing qi more efficiently now, the cleared paths doing their work.

"Thank you," you said.

I said nothing. There was nothing to say. I had broken your system. Cleaning the debris was not generosity. It was the minimum.

Dusk settled. The amber light dimmed through grey into the blue-dark of early evening. The fire became the dominant light source again — the warm circle against the cooling air, the two of us inside it.

The night sounds began. Not abruptly — gradually, the way the light had left. The crickets first, resuming from the night before, their numbers growing. More confident now. The damaged forest less strange to them on the second night. Then the deeper sounds. A frog from the direction of the stream — the low, rhythmic call that meant standing water nearby. An owl. The first owl — a single low note from somewhere far enough away that the shockwave had left its territory intact. It called and waited and called again. The ancient pattern. Question. Silence. Question.

Something moved in the undergrowth beyond the fallen trunks. Not large — the rustling of something small, ground-level, navigating the debris field. A mouse or a vole. The nocturnal scavengers testing the new terrain. The shockwave had created a habitat for the small things too. Overturned earth meant exposed roots, exposed roots meant insects, insects meant the things that ate them.

You slept. The transition from conscious to sleeping was brief — the exhaustion of a damaged body pulling you under within minutes of closing your eyes. Your breathing found its pattern. Shallow. Steady. The ribs permitting this much and no more.

I fed the fire. The night was colder than the last — markedly. The first night's ground heat from the shockwave had dissipated, and the open clearing offered nothing against the sky. No canopy. No windbreak. The cold settled from above, the way it does on cloudless nights when the heat radiates upward and nothing sends it back. Your blanket was insufficient. I could see it in the way you curled tighter, the ribs protesting the contraction.

I fed qi into the fire. Not into you — into the wood, the embers, the air around the flames. A thin, steady current that raised the temperature of the warm circle by a few degrees. The fire burned no brighter but the heat carried further, wrapped around the lean-to, settled against the blanket.

The owl called again. Closer now. Perhaps investigating the clearing, the open ground, the good hunting that devastation produced. A bat crossed the firelight — a quick dark shape, silent, pursuing something too small to see.

The night deepened. The stars visible through the open sky above the clearing — unfiltered. A clear field, slowly rotating. The cold pressed down from that vastness and the fire pressed back, and between them was a small warm space where you breathed.

I sat with the stillness. Fed the fire. Checked on you. The cycle of care that had become the rhythm of my night. Somewhere in the hours between midnight and dawn, the frog stopped calling and a different bird began — a nightjar, the churring repetition that filled the dark. It was close. In one of the fallen trunks, perhaps. Nesting in the wreckage already. Two days, and life was moving in.

Dawn arrived without announcement. The same slow dilution — dark thinning to grey, grey accepting color. The dew had formed on everything — the fallen trunks, the grass between the fissures, the fabric of your blanket, the wood beside the fire. Wet. Clean. The world washed overnight.

You coughed before you woke. A deep, wet sound, immediately punished by the ribs. Your body curled — the protective fold, involuntary, the architecture of a torso defending itself. Three coughs. Each one producing a grimace. Each one continuing regardless of the grimace.

The coughing settled. You straightened — slowly, incrementally, the negotiation with pain that had become your method of achieving vertical. You sat up. The blanket fell from your shoulders. The bruise around your left eye had advanced — purple-yellow now, the color progression that marked two days of healing. Your right eye was clear.

I handed you the water-skin. You drank. Small sips. Careful. The ribs.

You looked around. At the clearing. At the fallen trees. At the fire. At me, sitting where I had sat for two nights.

You said nothing about the destruction. Nothing about the night.

You reached for your pack. Found the rice. Looked at it. Put it down.

"Not yet," you said.

The ribs. Cooking required motion. Motion required breath.

I took the rice. I took the pot. I built the cooking fire from the embers and the broken wood that surrounded us.

I cooked.

The practiced motions of food preparation. Water. Rice. Heat. Duration. A sequence stored in the kind of memory that the erasures did not reach because cooking threatened nothing.

You watched. From your position on the ground. Your right eye tracking my motions. Watching without documenting. Your notebook was still in my pack — you had not asked for it, and I had not offered.

I served the rice. Placed the bowl beside you and moved back to my place by the fire.

You ate. Slowly. Each breath between bites calculated for the ribs.

We did not speak.

After — after the eating, after the fire burned lower, after the morning progressed from grey to the diffused warmth of a clouded sun — you stood. You swayed and caught yourself but you stood.

"We should move," you said.

We should move. Because staying in the circle of destruction was not sustainable. Because the location was evidence and evidence attracted attention.

"I can stabilize you," I said. "Qi. You wouldn't have to walk."

You looked at me. The look of a man who understood exactly what was being offered and who had already decided.

"No. I want to— I need to walk."

You picked up your pack — slowly, the ribs dictating the arc of the motion — and put it on. The left strap first, then the right, each one a controlled negotiation with damaged infrastructure.

You walked. Slowly, because your body allowed nothing faster. I moved at your speed. The matching was automatic. An adjustment I did not name and you did not ask about.

You noticed. The sideways glance. The assessment. My pace locked to yours.

You said nothing.

We moved. You coughed occasionally. I waited when you paused. We moved.
