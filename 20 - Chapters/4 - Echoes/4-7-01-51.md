The morning was grey and physical and true.

Ash from the fire. Grey residue of wood burned through the night — white at the center, grey at the edges. The ash covered the ground around the fire pit and beyond: devastation. Fallen trees. Fissured earth. The radius of my protection made visible in snapped trunks and displaced soil.

I sat on a stone. My shirt was torn — the sleeve missing, used for your bandage, the remaining fabric insufficient and dirty and bearing the evidence of a night spent tending a fire and a man. Dust in my hair. Your blood still on my hands, the dried-brown patina that resisted casual washing.

You were under the blanket I had taken from your pack. The blanket covered everything except your face, which was: pale. Pallor of blood loss. The laceration bandage had bled through during the night — a dark stain through my sleeve-fabric that indicated the wound was deeper than the initial assessment had suggested. Your breathing was shallow, the ribs dictating depth.

You woke.

The transition — a change in breathing, a facial contraction, the eyes opening with the squint of someone returning to consciousness through pain. You woke and the waking was accompanied by: coughing.

The cough was deep and wet and immediately punished by the ribs. You curled — the involuntary protective response, the body folding around the broken structure. The cough continued. Three repetitions. Each one producing the facial expression of: stop. Each one continuing regardless.

Eventually the coughing settled. You straightened — slowly. The careful, incremental motion of someone for whom every degree of change was a negotiation with pain. You sat up. The blanket fell. The bandage was visible. The bruise around your left eye was: advanced. Purple-yellow. Two days of bleeding under the skin.

You were dizzy. I could see it — the way your eyes tracked and then overcorrected. The concussion was still working. You sat upright but the uprightness was precarious. Your hand went to the ground. Stabilizing.

"Water," I said. I handed you the water-skin. You drank. Small sips. Careful. The ribs would not let you breathe deeply enough for large swallows.

You looked around. At the destroyed clearing. At the fallen trees. At me. At the stone I sat on. At the grey and physical and true morning.

You said nothing about the destruction. Nothing about the night. Nothing about the word.

You reached for your pack. Found the rice. Looked at it. Put it down.

"Not yet," you said.

The ribs. Cooking required motion. Motion required breath. Breath required ribs that were not broken but yours were.

I stood. I took the rice. I took the pot. I built the cooking fire from the residual embers and the broken wood that surrounded us in abundance.

I cooked.

The cooking was: mechanical. The practiced motions of food preparation that I had performed for millennia. Water. Rice. Heat. Duration. The sequence was stored in the kind of memory that the erasures did not reach because cooking threatened nothing.

You watched. From your position on the ground. Your good eye — the right one, the unswollen one — tracking my motions with the observational intensity that was your nature. Your notebook was still missing. Somewhere in the debris. You could not document.

I served the rice. In the bowl. Placed it beside you. Moved back to my stone.

You ate. Slowly. Each breath between bites calculated for the ribs.

We did not speak.

After — after the eating, after the fire burned lower, after the morning progressed from grey to the slightly-less-grey of overcast — you stood. The standing was an event. The project of a man with broken ribs achieving vertical. You stood and swayed and caught yourself and stood.

"We should move," you said.

We should move. Because staying in the circle of destruction was not: sustainable. Because the location was evidence and evidence attracted attention and attention was unwelcome. Because the pragmatism that governed your behavior was also operational through broken ribs.

We walked. You walked. Slowly.

I matched your pace. The matching was automatic. You walked slowly because your ribs were broken and your balance was off and your body was running on less than it needed. I walked slowly because you were walking slowly.

I simply: walked at your speed.

You noticed. I saw you notice — the sideways glance, the assessment. My pace matching yours. The adjustment I had not mentioned.

You said nothing.

The morning continued. Grey. Physical. True. Two people walking through a forest — one hindered, one unhurt. The unhurt one walking at the hindered one's pace. Something I did not have vocabulary for. I did not try to find it.

We walked. You coughed occasionally. I waited when you paused. We walked.
