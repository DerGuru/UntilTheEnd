The poison was called Yinshi — Shadow-Eater. The substance consumed the qi-channels from within, eating the meridian structure the way shadow eats light. Replacing it with nothing.

I had known about it for centuries. I had found it through observation, long ago, passing through cities that no longer existed, abandoned sect libraries that had since become forests, a ruined apothecary whose ruins had been ruins even then. I had read scrolls that crumbled at my touch, their age exceeding their structural capacity. The information existed in the gaps between official records, which indicated this subject was suppressed.

Yinshi killed Immortal-level cultivators. Reliably. Completely. In approximately ninety seconds.

The poison's work was documented in a scroll I had found beneath a collapsed shelf in the ruins of the Qinzhou Apothecary, a sect destroyed eight thousand years ago for possessing, among other things, this knowledge. The scroll described the mechanism in precise, clinical language. The compound entered the bloodstream, identified the qi-meridian structure and dissolved it. From the periphery inward. The peripheral channels first, then the secondary, then the primary. The dantian last.

For an Immortal-level cultivator, the dissolution of the meridian structure was fatal. Not because the body couldn't survive without qi — mortal bodies survived without qi permanently. But at Immortal level, what held the body together had been rebuilt around the qi-channels themselves. The channels bearing load. Remove them and the body collapsed.

Ninety seconds. Complete dissolution. Unrecoverable. Not the cliff — the cliff had been impulse, accident, discovery through falling. This was different. This was method.

You had the poison.

That fact was itself significant. You had carried it — in your pack, in a small ceramic phial wrapped in oil-cloth, positioned against your hip where the body heat would keep the compound liquid. You had carried it since before you found me.

One evening we stopped at a stream. The routine of it — water, fire, the small negotiations of campsite geography. You swung your pack off your shoulder and set it against a boulder. The pack tipped sideways, the weight shifted, and a
phial threatened to crash onto the rock.

You reached for it. But I was faster. I caught it. The ceramic was cold and smooth in my hand. The phial was small — the size of a teacup, maybe smaller. The weight was significant. I knew what it was before my hand fully closed around it. The ceramic. The weight. The specific gravity of a phial designed to contain something that ate meridian structures from the inside out. You don't carry a thing like that for centuries without recognizing its siblings.

I held it up.

Your face did what your face did when it was calculating — the brief stillness, the assessment of options, the rapid discarding of lies in favor of the version of truth that preserved the most.

"How long have you carried this?"

"Since before I found you."

"For me."

You met my eyes. No flinch. "I didn't know what I would find. A cultivator at your level, alone, after what happened — I didn't know what state you'd be in. Whether you'd recognize me. Whether you'd be hostile."

The honesty was clinical. The honesty of a man who had packed a poison beside his notebook and considered both tools of equal necessity.

"You didn't know it wouldn't work," I said.

"No." A pause. "I know now."

He did. He had seen the cliff. He had seen the substrate respond. He understood, better than anyone alive, that whatever held me together operated on a level that Yinshi could not reach. The poison dissolved meridian structures. The thing inside me was not a meridian structure.

You took the phial from my hand and placed it at the bottom of your pack — deep, beneath the cooking pot and the spare cloth and the second notebook. You buried it with the deliberate thoroughness of someone closing a chapter.

I watched where you put it.

That night, while you slept, I took it. The pack was beside your head. I threaded a thin line of qi through my fingers — just enough to dampen the vibrations, to absorb the small sounds that fabric and ceramic produced when they moved against each other. The technique was old. Older than sects. The kind of thing you learned when you had lived long enough to steal from people who could hear a heartbeat change rhythm.

The oil-cloth came out without a sound. The ceramic was cold now — hours away from your body heat, the compound inside thickening toward its storage state.

I put it in my belt. Against the skin. Beside the folded page with its single word.

I did not tell you.

I selected a location. A clearing south of our camp, open ground, no structures within the range I had estimated from the cliff data. The cliff-jump had produced the substrate response at approximately the moment of lethal threat. The poison would produce the lethal threat chemically rather than kinetically. The response, if it came, would be observable.

The clearing was sufficient. Flat. Open. The nearest trees at thirty paces.

The calculation was wrong.

But I did not know that yet.

The folded page was against my skin. The question — *Why* — pressed into me by belt and cloth and the physical persistence of paper against flesh. Since the substrate stopped my fall and I'd wondered not just *what* it was but why it had bothered. The *why* had lived in the shadow of the *what* for weeks, patient, waiting for the *what* to exhaust itself against the wall. Writing it down had not created the question. Writing it down had given the question a body.

Not yet an answer. But the beginning of the mechanism that would produce the answer.

I did not sleep that night. Not the usual not-sleep — from anticipation. The wakefulness that meant the patched-together will was holding. The will was anticipating. The will was planning. The will was alive.

The poison needed some warmth to liquefy first.

At dawn.
