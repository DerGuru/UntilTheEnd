The poison was called Yinshi — Shadow-Eater. The substance consumed the qi-channels from within, eating the meridian structure the way shadow eats light. Replacing it with nothing.

I had found it through observation alone. I had passed through three cities, two abandoned sect libraries and a ruined apothecary. I had read scrolls that crumbled at my touch, their age exceeding their structural capacity. The information existed in the gaps between official records, the absences that indicated: this subject was suppressed.

Yinshi killed Immortal-level cultivators. Reliably. Completely. In approximately ninety seconds.

The poison's work was documented in a scroll I had found beneath a collapsed shelf in the ruins of the Qinzhou Apothecary, a sect destroyed four hundred years ago for possessing, among other things, this knowledge. The scroll described the mechanism in precise, clinical language: the compound entered the bloodstream, identified the qi-meridian structure and dissolved it. From the periphery inward. The peripheral channels first, then the secondary, then the primary. The dantian last.

For an Immortal-level cultivator, the dissolution of the meridian structure was fatal. Not because the body couldn't survive without qi — mortal bodies survived without qi permanently. Because at Immortal level, what held the body together had been rebuilt around the qi-channels. The channels were load-bearing. Remove them and the body: collapsed.

Ninety seconds. Complete dissolution. Unrecoverable.

I knew all of this. I had researched it. The knowledge the controlled experiment required. Not the cliff — the cliff had been impulse, accident, discovery through falling. This was different. This was method.

You had the poison.

That fact was itself significant. You had carried it — in your pack, in a small ceramic phial wrapped in oil-cloth, positioned against your hip where the body heat would keep the compound liquid. You had carried it since before you found me. You had acquired it through contacts that I did not ask about and you did not explain. You had calculated the possibility: the scenario in which an Immortal-level cultivator required termination and the standard methods would be insufficient.

You had carried it for me. Or against me. The distinction was unclear and the unclarity was: honest.

I found the phial on the third morning after I tore the page from your notebook. You were at the stream, washing. Your pack was open — deliberately, I understood later. You had left it open. You had positioned the phial at the top, visible, accessible. You had made the discovery: easy.

You had understood my intention before I articulated it. You did not hide the means. But you did not offer them either. The phial was visible because hiding it would not have worked — I would have found it regardless. You chose the version of losing that preserved your dignity.

I took the phial. I held it. Ceramic, warm from your body heat. Small. The volume of two swallowed mouthfuls. The oil-cloth wrapping was stained with years of proximity to skin and sweat and the faint chemical residue that the compound produced through its container.

I examined it. Turned it. Removed the wax seal. Smelled it.

Nothing. Yinshi was odorless. The absence of smell was itself a design feature. The compound had been engineered for assassination. The craft of people who understood that killing had quality standards.

I replaced the seal.

"How long have you carried this?"

You looked at the phial in my hand.

"Since before I found you."

"For me."

"For whatever was necessary."

The distinction you maintained. Not *for-you*, not *against-you*. For-whatever-was-necessary. The pragmatism of a man who had searched for three months with a poison in his pack and a notebook against his chest.

I put the phial on the flat stone between us. The transition zone. The cooking-and-negotiation space.

"Tomorrow," I said.

You looked at the phial. You looked at me.

"No," you said.

The word was simple. The tone was not. You had said *Don't* at the cliff. This was its sibling — harder, more specific. *Don't* had been instinct. *No* was: considered.

"You said you'd stay until you understood," I said.

"Understanding doesn't require your death."

"It might require the attempt."

Your jaw tightened. The muscle beneath the ear. You were composing arguments — I could see them forming and being discarded, one after another, because each one arrived at the same wall: you could not stop me. I was stronger. I was faster. I was the thing you were studying and the thing you were studying did not require your permission.

"Ten paces," I said. "That's what I need from you. Ten paces and your notebook."

You sat with this. The fire between us. The phial on the stone. Your hands were still and the stillness was effort.

"Ten paces was not enough at the cliff," you said.

"The cliff was uncontrolled. This is method."

You did not answer for a long time. Then you reached for your notebook — the first one, the personal one — and wrote something that I could not read from this distance and that I did not try to read.

I spent the rest of the day preparing. I selected a location: a clearing south of our camp, open ground, no structures within the range I had estimated from the cliff data. The cliff-jump had produced the substrate response at approximately the moment of lethal threat. The poison would produce the lethal threat chemically rather than kinetically. The response, if it came, would be observable.

The clearing was sufficient. Flat. Open. The nearest trees at thirty paces. You would stand at ten.

Ten paces. The distance you always maintained. The observational distance. Close enough for detail, far enough, I calculated, for safety.

The calculation was wrong.

But I did not know that yet.

The folded page was against my skin. The question — *Why* — pressed into me by belt and cloth and the physical persistence of paper against flesh. The question waited. Tomorrow would begin the answer.

Not the whole answer. But the beginning of the mechanism that would produce the answer.

I did not sleep that night. Not from forgetting — from anticipation. The wakefulness that meant the patched-together will was holding. The will was anticipating. The will was planning. The will was alive.

Tomorrow.
