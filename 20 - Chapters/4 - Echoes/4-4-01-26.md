The path had brought us back into the mountains. The lowlands had ended gradually — terrain rising over days, the flat giving way to folds, the folds to hills, the hills to a mountain ridge. We had been walking it for two days now. The ridge was not too high — the kind of elevation where trees still grew but the horizon opened up and the world fell away on the sides.

A fault line had sheared the face clean. We stood at the edge of a wall that dropped eight hundred meters from the top to the bottom without intermediary stages. Eight hundred meters of air between where I stood and where the ground resumed.

All we could see below was rock. Flat, grey. The kind that shattered what it caught. The honest kind. The kind that made no promises about what would happen upon contact.

I stood at the edge. The path led here and I followed it. Here was the edge.

My toes — actually the front third of my feet — extended past the edge. Balance was automatic. The body knew edges. I had stood at edges before. A thousand years of edges accumulated over my long life. Fortress walls, canyon rims, temple rooftops at the periphery of dynasties I could no longer name. The body remembered all of them. The toes held.

Wind. There had been always been wind for the last few days, the consistent horizontal pressure of operating at this altitude. The wind had been present during the approach, during the last hundred meters where the terrain announced what it was about to do by becoming thinner and more exposed.

The wind had stopped.

Not gradually. The air that had been moving was no longer moving and the stillness was absolute.

Silence.

It was different from the silence I carried. The world seemed to pause. Everything at once. Wind, insects, birds, the hum of air itself. Gone in an instant.

My own breathing was the only sound I could hear. But in that utter silence, my breathing was loud. Too loud. It seemed almost foreign. My breath sounded like someone else's breath heard from close proximity.

I looked down and came to the conclusion that this was a long way to fall.

You were not here. Behind me — at the camp, or on the path, or wherever your trajectory had taken you. You would not see me standing here with my toes past the edge and the silence around me and the rock below.

Or you would. Later. The timeline was not my concern.

Why weren't you here?

I looked down at the rock. Eight hundred meters down. Grey. Patient. Stone waited because waiting was all it could do and stone was good at it.

I didn't know then that patience was the wrong word for what the rock was doing. But I would learn. Soon.

The silence held. My breathing continued. The world was a held breath and I was standing on the edge where the breath could be released.

I shifted my weight to my left foot, lifted my right one and placed it down half a meter in front of me.

A step. Except beneath the step was nothing.

I saw you on the switchback behind me. You came just around the corner into my field of vision. You were looking at me with shock in your eyes.

I was calm. My thoughts in that moment contained neither a farewell nor a statement. Instead I had an experiment's description in two syllables on my mind.

*Let's see.*
