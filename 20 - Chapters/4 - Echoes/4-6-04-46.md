I tore my sleeve.

The motion was automatic. Thousands of years of automatic — the practiced mechanics of someone who had dressed wounds in wars, in villages, in the repetitive emergencies that immortality presented. I tore my sleeve at the seam. The fabric separated. I folded it. I pressed it against the laceration on your temple.

Blood. Your blood. On my hands. On the fabric. On the ground. Around us, the dust was still settling. Fine particles drifting through shafts of light that had no business being this beautiful.

Your breathing was shallow and wet and the wetness was blood in the airways.Your ribs were broken because of me.

/ʔh:/. The sound arrived in the old tongue — the language I'd helped shape when words were still forming, a linguistic fossil from a period that memory had not erased because the sound was attached to muscle memory and muscle memory was deeper than thought. /ʔh:/. Pressure. The first word for pressing something closed.

I pressed the fabric against the wound and the wound against the skull. My hand against the fabric. Pressure through medium.

I could have healed this with qi. But your core was broken. Your meridian structure was fractured, incomplete — the channels that should have carried qi were damaged in ways that made them unpredictable. Threading qi into a broken meridian system was not healing. It was pressure into a cracked pipe. The energy would go where the cracks directed it, not where I intended. I could accelerate the wrong thing. I could rupture what remained.

I had healed Wei with qi. It had saved him and made everything worse. I had no intention of doing that again. Not with you. Not when the risk was this high and the tools of qi were this unreliable. Your channels were not intact. Your channels were rubble.

Later, maybe. Small amounts. Carefully. When you were stable and the risk was manageable. But not now. Not with my hands still shaking and your body broken in ways I could not fully assess while you were unconscious. Now it was sleeves and pressure and fire and water. The tools of people who did not have qi. The tools that worked regardless.

I needed water to clean the wounds. The stream was two hundred paces west. Fire. You would go into shock if you were not already. Core temperature would drop. The water needed to be boiled else I'd risk infection. I needed fire and I needed water and I needed to be in two places and I could only be in one. The ground was still warm, but it was cooling rapidly. I had to move fast.

Fire first.

There were no insects. I noticed this while I was scanning for wood. The clearing was silent in the way that clearings were not — no hum, no buzz, no small wings. Everything that could flee had fled. Everything that could not was gone.

I released the pressure bandage — held in place by positioning your head so gravity kept force on the wound. I gathered wood. Thankfully, it was everywhere. The shockwave had produced a lot of it. The cracked and fallen trees providing fuel in abundance. I gathered it. I arranged it. I used some qi to ignite it. As a reflex I looked for you pack, It was blown further away by ten paces but intact.

Fire.

The flames took. The warmth began. Irregular, smoky, fresh-broken wood, but warm. I positioned you closer. Carefully. The ribs. The arm.

The grass had survived. Between the fissures, in patches, the blades bent flat to the ground but green. The shockwave had passed over it the way wind passed over wheat — pressing it down, but not destroying it. The grass would straighten in an day. The trees would not.

With the fire going, I needed water.

I rushed the two hundred paces. Fast. An immortal body had its uses. Speed without strain, distance without fatigue. At the stream the light broke on the water's surface in shifting patterns — white fragments on green current. A fish rose and snapped at a water strider. The small violence of ordinary hunger. The stream did not know about the clearing. The stream was continuing.

I filled your water-skin and returned as fast as I went.

I cleaned the wounds. Temple laceration. Deep enough for concern, shallow enough that the skull was intact. The blood was already slowing. The pressure bandage had worked, the clotting was beginning, the processes that mortal bodies used when damaged were functioning.

Your arm was only dislocated, not broken. I braced your shoulder, took the arm and pulled. The rotational force that relocation required, the technique that would have been painful but necessary.

The joint seated. The pop. The sound of bone finding socket.

You did not wake. The concussion held you under.

A bird called. Distant — from the zone where the trees had only bent, where the canopy was torn but the branches held. One bird. The first sound from outside since the blast. It called twice and stopped, as if reconsidering.

I sat back. On the churned ground. In the circle of destruction. The fire between us. Between me and the unconscious man whose ribs were broken by my protection.

My hands were still shaking. The tremor had nothing to do with temperature or fatigue or effort. I watched them tremble and I knew what it was.

My hands were shaking because of what I had done.

Neither the poison experiment nor the scientific procedure. What I had done to you. I had nearly killed you. My continued existence — the thing that the thing-below-qi maintained at any cost — had treated the nearest human being as irrelevant collateral. The shockwave had not discriminated. 

I looked at my shaking hands. I looked at the unconscious you. And I looked at the devastation all around us.

An I realized two things. Simultaneous. Neither arrived as thought. Both arrived as weight.

What kept me alive did not feel like an enemy. It knew where to push. It knew when to wake. It knew the architecture of my body the way only something that *lived* there could know it. The thing below the qi — the thing that woke and warmed and protected and destroyed — it was not foreign. It was not an invasion. It felt like: mine. The way a heartbeat felt like mine even though I had never told it to beat.

But it had not protected you. The shockwave had expanded outward from me. Through me and past me. And everything in its path was acceptable damage. 

I was the danger. I had always been the danger.

"/ø ʔm̩/," I said.

The old tongue again. The fossil language. The sounds that the deeper memory attached to the deeper understanding. I am doing this. Not I did this — I AM doing this. Present tense. Ongoing. Every moment of my continued existence was a moment of potential lethality to anyone in proximity.

Inward. The direction arrived without my permission. From the cliff. From the bearing that had been set before I understood what it pointed toward. Inward. The answer was inside. The danger was inside. The protection was inside. Everything was inside. 

And inside was me.

I sat beside the fire. I waited. Your breathing continued. The wet sound diminished over the hours. The blood was clearing from your airways. The ribs would take weeks. Mortal bodies healed at mortal speed.

At the far edge of the destruction, where the fallen trunks met the standing wounded, small branches still carried leaves. Green, intact, sheltered by the geometry of collapse — a branch angled just so, a trunk that had taken the force, the accidental architecture of survival. Ten paces closer, the branches were stripped bare. The line was sharp. Leaves. No leaves. The blast had drawn a border and the border was exact.

I waited. My hands shook. The fire burned the shattered wood. The sky moved, changed, indifferent to the small catastrophe on the ground below.

You were alive. I had done this. 

Both facts were simultaneously true and the things I needed to hold.
