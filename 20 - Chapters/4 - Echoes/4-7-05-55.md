We saw the smoke in the late morning. A thin column, grey-white, rising above the canopy to the south. Too steady for a forest fire. Too concentrated. A chimney. A settlement.

The last time I had walked this path, there had been nothing south. Forest, river, more forest. No chimney. No laundry lines. No smoke. A few decades, and the land rearranges itself around people who were not there before. We would have needed two more days at least.

You stopped walking and leaned against a birch. The ribs — you didn't say it, but the lean said it. We had been walking for three hours already.

"There," you said. Pointed south with your chin. "Half a day. Maybe less."

We walked south. I kept looking back.

The settlement was smaller than the smoke had suggested — a handful of houses along a river, a bridge, a woman hanging laundry who watched us approach without alarm. I found a room. A bed. Paid with a coin from the pouch you carried. The bed was narrow, the blanket rough, the walls thin enough to hear the river. It was still the first bed in weeks.

You sat down on it. The mattress gave — straw, not cotton — and the give made you close your eyes for a moment. The body's relief at a horizontal surface that was not earth.

"Stay," I said. "Rest. Eat something that isn't just rice."

You opened your eyes and looked at me.

"And what will you do?"

"I'll be back before nightfall."

You waited. The question had not been answered and we both knew it.

I took the pen from your bag. You watched me take it. I held up the second notebook — the summary, the one you had prepared for me.

"In case something comes back," I said.

You nodded once and drew your legs up onto the bed, carefully, ribs governing the geometry. Lay back. The ceiling was low, stained, unremarkable. You looked at it as though it were the sky.

"Be back before nightfall?" you asked, but I was already moving.

Qi-speed is not running. It is the removal of friction — air, gravity, the body's preference for slowness. The forest blurred. The trees became lines. The path we had walked in three days unspooled beneath me in minutes, the landmarks arriving and departing in reverse: the stream crossing, the granite outcrop, the slope with the bracket fungi. The world at qi-speed is not a place you pass through — it is a place that passes through you.

The clearing announced itself before I saw it. An absence of canopy. Light where there should have been shadow. The forest stopped, and what remained was a circle of devastation.

I had remembered it wrong.

That was the first thought. Standing at the edge — except the edge was not where I had placed it. I had remembered thirty paces of shattered trunks, another forty of felled trees. A circle. Regrettable but bounded.

The felled zone extended closer to seventy paces. And beyond it — another fifty paces of trees that were cracked but standing, held up by neighbors that were themselves cracked, listing at angles, canopies torn open. A hundred and twenty paces in every direction. Not a clearing. A crater in the canopy.

The fissures in the ground were deeper than I remembered. Some had widened — rain, maybe, or the settling of traumatized soil. The earth itself had not recovered. The exposed roots of the downed trees were pale, dry, already dead. Nothing had begun to grow. The clearing smelled of resin and split wood and, beneath it, ash.

I walked to the center. The exact spot. Where I had stood when I drank the poison. Where my body had produced the thing that did this. The ground beneath my feet was compressed — packed hard by the downward force of the shockwave, the vertical component that had driven into the earth while the horizontal component had driven into the trees, into the man who now sat against a birch tree half a day's walk south, breathing carefully around broken ribs.

I sat down. Cross-legged. The pen in my lap, the notebook closed beside me. I closed my eyes.

The meditation was not peaceful. It was deliberate. I went inward — past the qi, past the meridians, past the channels that I had been cleaning in your body for days. Into the deeper architecture. The place where the shockwave originated. The substrate. The thing-below-qi that I had no name for and that the erasures had taken my understanding of.

I reached for the three hours.

The understanding that the poison had unlocked — the clarity, the seeing, the brief window where everything had made sense and the connections had been visible and the architecture had been readable. Three hours of knowing. Erased by the thing that protected me. Taken back.

I reached for them deliberately. Not the emotions — the structure. The framework. What had I understood? What had I seen? The channels, the pathways, the reason for the shockwave, the origin of the protection — all of it had been clear for three hours and then all of it had been taken and the taking had left me with nothing.

I reached.

Static.

The same static, as when I attempted to remember Wei. But louder. Denser. Like pressing your ear against a wall and hearing machinery on the other side — present, operational. But inaccessible. The understanding was there. I could feel its weight, its shape, the outline of something enormous behind the noise. But I could not see it. I could not retrieve it.

I pushed harder. The static intensified. A pressure behind my sternum — not pain, not the shockwave, but its echo. A warning. The mechanism that had erased the understanding was still active, still watching, still prepared to take whatever I managed to recover.

I stopped and opened my eyes. The clearing was unchanged. The downed trees, the fissures, the exposed roots. My breathing had accelerated — I slowed it. The pen was still in my lap. The notebook was still closed. I had nothing to write.

The understanding was gone. The three hours remained erased. Whatever I had known, whatever I had seen — it was behind the static, and the static was a wall I was unable to tear down.

I sat with that for a while. The wind moved through the downed trees — the low sound, the intimate one, air threading through wreckage. Somewhere a woodpecker tested a dead trunk. Three taps. Pause. Three taps.

The ash-smell had been there since I arrived. Now, sitting still, breathing evenly, the smell deepened. Wood-ash and charred earth. The cooking fire we had built at the center, the night before the poison. The domestic context overlaid on the destructive one.

The smell opened a door.

Wei had said it once. In a morning that smelled like this — like ash, like the aftermath of something that had burned. A cooking fire. The domestic context that Wei inhabited when he was not dying.

"If you don't know where to go, just go."

The memory was short. One sentence. Wei was walking ahead of me. The morning was grey. He did not look back when he said it, because he wasn't saying it for my approval — he was stating a principle. A Wei directive that governed ambiguity: if you don't know, go. If you're uncertain, move. If the destination is unclear, the motion is still valid.

If you don't know where to go, just go.

I opened the notebook. Wrote one line: *Static. Wall. Active. Understanding present but inaccessible.* Everything the meditation had produced. Everything the return had given me.

I stood up. Looked at the clearing one more time. A hundred and twenty paces. Three rings. Worse than I had believed. More comprehensive than I had remembered. The thing inside me protected me at any cost — and this was the cost. This was what it looked like from the outside.

I turned away. The looking was done. The reckoning was complete. I had seen what I had done. I had stood at the center of my own destructive radius. I had not remembered.

Just go.

The return at qi-speed was even faster. The forest blurred, unblurred, resolved into fields, a river, rooftops. The settlement. The narrow room.

You were lying on the bed as I had left you, your notebook open on your chest. Reading. Your own observations, your own handwriting, the investigation continuing in my absence. The room smelled of straw and river-damp and something cooking downstairs.

You looked up. Measured my face. Whatever you saw there — you did not ask.

I handed you the pen. Opened my notebook to the line I had written.

You read it. Sat up — slowly, ribs — and took the pen. Wrote beneath my line, in your careful hand: *Confirmed. Active resistance. Not passive decay.*

You kept the pen, closed the notebook and looked at the window, where the light was amber and slanting.

"Before nightfall," you said.

An hour to spare.
