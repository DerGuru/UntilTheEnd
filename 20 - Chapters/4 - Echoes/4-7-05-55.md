We saw the smoke around midday. A thin column, grey-white, rising above the canopy to the south. Too steady for a forest fire. Too concentrated. A chimney. A settlement.

You stopped walking. Leaned against a birch. The ribs — you didn't say it, but the lean said it. We had been walking since morning and the distance was catching up.

"There," you said. Pointed south with your chin. "Half a day. Maybe less."

I looked at the smoke. Then I looked back — north, the way we had come. The trees closed behind us like a sentence that had already been read.

"I need to go back."

Your expression shifted. The careful blankness breaking — genuine alarm, poorly hidden. "To the clearing?"

"Yes."

A pause. You measured me the way you measured questions — with the eyes, not the mouth. Deciding whether the answer required argument.

"Alone," I said. "At speed. You rest here. I'll be back before nightfall."

You did not argue. You understood the difference — at my speed, three days became hours. At your speed, you would rest. The math was clean.

I took the pen from your bag. You watched me take it. I held up the second notebook — the summary, the one you had prepared for me.

"In case something comes back," I said.

You nodded. Once. There was something in the nod that was not agreement — it was the acknowledgment that I was doing this deliberately, that the return was not impulse but method. You sat down against the birch. Arranged your pack behind you as a cushion. Drew your legs up, carefully, ribs governing the geometry.

"Before nightfall," you said.

I went.

Qi-speed is not running. It is the removal of friction — air, gravity, the body's preference for slowness. The forest blurred. The trees became lines. The path we had walked in three days unspooled beneath me in minutes, the landmarks arriving and departing in reverse: the stream crossing, the granite outcrop, the slope with the bracket fungi. The world at qi-speed is not a place you pass through — it is a place that passes through you.

The clearing announced itself before I saw it. An absence of canopy. Light where there should have been shadow. The forest stopped, and what remained was: the circle.

I had remembered it wrong.

That was the first thought. Standing at the edge, looking in. I had remembered it wrong. Three days and the distance of walking and the softening that memory performs on everything — the clearing in my mind had been smaller. Contained. A local event. Thirty meters of damage. Regrettable but bounded.

The clearing was not thirty meters. It was closer to fifty. The trees at the perimeter — the ones I had categorized as still standing — were not standing. They were leaning. Cracked at their bases, held up by neighbors that were themselves cracked. The circle of total destruction was surrounded by a second circle of partial destruction, and that circle bled into a third ring of stress fractures and stripped bark and branches torn free.

I had not seen the outer rings. I had been at the center, looking out. From the center, the damage at the edges was invisible.

From the edge, looking in, the damage was: comprehensive.

The fissures in the ground were deeper than I remembered. Some had widened — rain, maybe, or the settling of traumatized soil. The earth itself had not recovered. The exposed roots of the downed trees were pale, dry, already dead. Nothing had begun to grow. The clearing smelled of resin and split wood and, beneath it, ash.

I walked to the center. The exact spot. Where I had stood when I drank the poison. Where my body had produced the thing that did this. The ground beneath my feet was compressed — packed hard by the downward force of the shockwave, the vertical component that had driven into the earth while the horizontal component had driven into the trees, into the man who now sat against a birch tree half a day's walk south, breathing carefully around broken ribs.

I sat down. Cross-legged. The pen in my lap, the notebook closed beside me. I closed my eyes.

The meditation was not peaceful. The meditation was: deliberate. I went inward — past the qi, past the meridians, past the channels that I had been cleaning in your body for days. Into the deeper architecture. The place where the shockwave lived. The substrate. The thing-below-qi that I had no name for and that the erasures had taken my understanding of.

I reached for the three hours.

The understanding that the poison had unlocked — the clarity, the seeing, the brief window where everything had made sense and the connections had been visible and the architecture had been readable. Three hours of knowing. Erased by the thing that protected me. Taken back.

I reached for them deliberately. Not the emotions — the structure. The framework. What had I understood? What had I seen? The channels, the pathways, the reason for the shockwave, the origin of the protection — all of it had been clear for three hours and then all of it had been taken and the taking had left: nothing.

I reached.

Static.

The same static from the Wei-memory attempt. But louder here. Denser. Like pressing your ear against a wall and hearing machinery on the other side — present, operational, inaccessible. The understanding was there. I could feel its weight, its shape, the outline of something enormous behind the noise. But I could not see it. I could not retrieve it.

I pushed harder. The static intensified. A pressure behind my sternum — not pain, not the shockwave, but its echo. A warning. The mechanism that had erased the understanding was still active, still watching, still prepared to take whatever I managed to recover.

I stopped. Opened my eyes. The clearing was unchanged. The downed trees, the fissures, the exposed roots. My breathing had accelerated — I slowed it. The pen was still in my lap. The notebook was still closed. I had nothing to write.

The understanding was gone. The three hours remained: erased. Whatever I had known, whatever I had seen — it was behind the static, and the static was not silence. The static was a wall.

I sat with that for a while. Not measuring how long. The wind moved through the downed trees — the low sound, the intimate one, air threading through wreckage. Somewhere a woodpecker tested a dead trunk. Three taps. Pause. Three taps.

The ash-smell had been there since I arrived. Now, sitting still, breathing evenly, the smell deepened. Wood-ash and charred earth. The cooking fire we had built at the center, the night before the poison. The domestic context overlaid on the destructive one.

The smell opened a door.

Wei had said it once. In a morning that smelled like this — like ash, like the aftermath of something that had burned. A cooking fire. The domestic context that Wei inhabited when he was not dying.

"If you don't know where to go, just go."

The memory was short. Four sentences. The image: Wei's back. He was walking ahead of me. The morning was grey. He did not look back when he said it, because the saying was not for my approval — the saying was a principle. A Wei directive that governed ambiguity: if you don't know, go. If you're uncertain, move. If the destination is unclear, the motion is still valid.

You had asked me about the phrase once. *Just go.* I told you it was Wei's. You wrote it down in a hand smaller than usual.

If you don't know where to go, just go.

I opened the notebook. Wrote one line: *Static. Wall. Active. Understanding present but inaccessible.* Four words and a sentence. Everything the meditation had produced. Everything the return had given me.

I stood up. Looked at the clearing one more time. The circle of my destruction. Fifty meters, not thirty. Three rings of damage, not one. Worse than I had believed. More comprehensive than I had remembered. The thing inside me that protected me at any cost — this was the cost. This was what it looked like from the outside.

I turned away. The looking was done. The reckoning was complete — not closed, complete. I had seen what I had done. I had stood at the center of my own destructive radius. I had seen what I was.

Just go.

The return at qi-speed was faster — I knew the path now. The forest blurred, unblurred, resolved into the birch where you waited. You were sitting as I had left you, pack behind you, legs drawn up. The second notebook open on your knee. Reading. Your own observations, your own handwriting, the investigation continuing in my absence.

You looked up. Measured my face. Whatever you saw there — you did not ask.

I handed you the pen. Opened my notebook to the line I had written.

You read it. Nodded. Took the pen and wrote beneath my line, in your careful hand: *Confirmed. Active resistance. Not passive decay.*

You handed the pen back. Closed the notebook. Looked south, toward the smoke.

"Before nightfall," you said.

We made it with an hour to spare.
