We headed north and rested at a stream two hours from the crater.

A short rest. We drank water, you had some dried fruit, the mechanical replenishment that travel required. You sat on a rock and wrote. I sat on a different rock and thought about nothing, which was itself unusual — the mind that catalogued and analyzed and never rested was, for a few minutes, quiet. The stream moved past with the patience of water that had been moving past this exact spot for centuries and would continue moving past it for centuries more.

I stood.

"South," I said.

You looked up from the notebook. The pen paused. Not the way it normally paused — the brief hesitation between observations, the breathing space between recorded thoughts. This pause had a different quality. The quality of a man who had heard something wrong and was deciding whether to name it.

"South," you repeated.

"There's a site. Volcanic. A crater — it became a lake. Temples on the eastern shore." The details came easily, the way landmarks came when you'd studied a map. "The foundation qi there should be strong. The basalt will have preserved whatever the original event left behind."

You looked at me. The look was not your usual assessment — not the clinical, diagnostic look of a scholar evaluating a subject. This was different. This was the look of a man watching someone walk toward a door she'd already walked through.

"Yun."

"What?"

You held the look for two seconds. Three. The stream filled the silence between us.

"Nothing," you said. You closed the notebook. You stood. You shouldered your pack with the resigned efficiency of a man who had decided that following was more informative than arguing.

We walked south.

The trail was familiar, though I couldn't have said why. The turns arrived before I expected them — a bend to the left before the outcrop, a descent through a grove where the soil changed from loam to volcanic ash. My feet knew this path. My mind did not recognize the knowing.

You walked behind me. Fifteen paces — my faster pace stretching the gap. You were breathing heavier on the inclines. But the pen was moving. I could hear the scratching. You were writing while walking, which you only did when the observation couldn't wait.

The sulfur arrived first. The mineral taste that volcanic ground produced — below normal detection, above mine. Then the incense, drifting from somewhere ahead, carried on the wind that moved across water.

We crested the ridge. Below us was a bowl. Four hundred years of rain and groundwater had filled what was left of it. Still, dark water, perhaps fifty meters across. The rim curved around it, softened by erosion and succession-growth. On the eastern shore, temples. Small ones. Stone foundations, wooden roofs, copper ornaments gone green with age. Pilgrims on the path. Incense smoke moving low across the surface of the lake.

I stopped.

The stop was not a decision. The body encountered a landscape it recognized and the recognition producing a halt the way a wall produced a halt — structurally, without negotiation. I knew this place. I knew the curve of the rim and the color of the water and the exact angle at which the temple roofs caught the afternoon light.

I knew it the way I knew a passage from a book I'd read but couldn't place. The knowledge without the context. The fact without the memory.

You arrived beside me. Breathing deeper than the walk warranted. You looked at the crater, at the lake, at the temples, with the expression of a man who had seen this exact view a few hours ago.

"Why are we here?" I asked.

Your pen stopped.

But your pen never stops. Your pen moves the way your lungs function — continuously, involuntarily, as a condition of being you. And yet, your pen stopped.

The silence between us contained something I could not read. You were looking at me with an expression that was neither surprise nor concern and not the diagnostic assessment of a scholar observing a subject. The expression was closer to grief. The specific grief of watching something break that you do not have the tools to fix.

"You don't know," you said.

Not a question.

"I know this place," I said. "I can see that I know it. The temples. The copper. The basalt at the waterline. I know the western shore is rockier — no path, no pilgrims. I know the sulfur smell doesn't fully dissipate from volcanic ground. I know all of these things. But I don't know —"

I stopped. The sentence had no ending.

"We were here this morning," you said. Carefully. The way you said things that might cause damage — with the surgical precision of a man who understood that words, placed wrong, could collapse a structure that was already fracturing.

"This morning."

"Three hours ago. You walked to the crater. The basalt. You touched the stone. You pulled your hand back."

You opened your notebook. The page: your handwriting. Miniature. Careful. Date — today. Location — this location. Observation: specific, clinical, the language of someone who did not know they were creating evidence.

*"Subject touched basalt at waterline. Withdrew hand immediately. Involuntary. Repeated contact three times. Third attempt sustained — five seconds. Visible tremor. Subject mapped stone-by-stone along western shore for forty minutes."*

One entry. Of me. My morning. The one I had no memory of.

"I didn't do that," I said.

I knew the notebook was not wrong. Your documentation was compulsive and precise. Your compulsion was your survival. Your precision was your identity. Neither produced falsehood.

But I did not remember.

The memory was gone. Not faded — gone. A room where the lights had been turned off and the furniture removed and the door sealed and the corridor leading to the door erased from the building's plans.

You showed the page again, pointing to another entry. Same date later.

*"Subject placed hand on basalt at crater rim. Stone cracked — single line, approx. one foot, no force applied, no sound. Subject visibly disturbed. Stated: 'I don't know.'"*

"The crack is still there," you said. "In the stone. You can go look at it."

I looked down at the crater. The stone you pointed out at the rim. But I couldn't see the crack from this distance. I went and checked. But you were not a man who offered evidence he couldn't produce.

Three hours gone — and the stone held the proof.

*Not yet*, something said. The locked door.

Something had erased three hours. Something had taken the morning and removed it the way a surgeon excised tissue — surrounding structures intact. I remembered the stream. The dried fruit. Standing up and saying *south.* Everything between the crater and the rest stop was gone.

The surgeon was inside me.

"Something is fighting me," I said.

You wrote. Fast. The urgency of someone who understood that the woman standing in front of you might forget this conversation too. The pen moved across the page with the speed of a man racing against an erasure that could arrive at any moment.

The thought was dizzying.

"How many times have we been here?"

"This is the second time."

You held the notebook open. Showing me. The external record of my own life, maintained by someone else, because the internal record was being edited by something that was not me.

I read your words again. My experiences, translated through your observation. The woman in the notebook that had touched the stones.

I did not recognize her.

This was the horror. Reading someone else's account of my own actions and finding the account alien. The me in the notebook was a stranger. The me standing on the ridge was the original.

I should have started writing then. I didn't. That cost me.

"We keep going," I said. "And you keep writing. Make sure, we arrive at the next place and you write down everything. Every detail. Every word. Every sensation. I want it all in the notebook."

You wrote that down too.
