Uncontrolled. Unbidden. A memory arrived. Fully formed, sensory-complete, installed in the perception as if it were happening now.

Wei, on a night. Cold. The mountain settlement in the wet season, the kind that entered buildings through wall-gaps and compressed the living space into the area nearest the fire and made the fire itself seem insufficient. The smell of pine-smoke and wet stone.

He had asked a question.

Wei asked questions the way other children performed tasks — by assignment. He had decided that tonight was a question-night. The question was the kind that fourteen-year-old boys asked when they had accumulated enough observation to formulate something that exceeded their apparent capacity.

"What is the worst thing that ever happened to you?"

His voice. Not quite a child's anymore, not yet a man's. The in-between. The voice that had been changing for months. I remembered his voice because his voice was the anchor.

I had considered the question. The worst thing. The superlative that required the thing to exceed all other things in its category.

"Waking up tomorrow."

Three words. I had a habit of making large truths portable.

He laughed.

The laugh carried no amusement. He had expected something grand — an event, a battle, a named historical trauma — and received instead a domestic observation. The mismatch was the humor.

I didn't laugh.

The memory closed and I was climbing the cliff wall again.

Grey stone, sedimentary, the geology I had studied during the descent now viewed from the reversed perspective. The same grey I had fallen past. My hands on the rock. My feet finding purchase. Handholds that centuries of weather had carved into the face. The stone was cold under my fingers. The same cold as the memory.

Waking up tomorrow was the worst thing.

But now — at the bottom of an eight-hundred-meter cliff that I had survived because something inside me had decided I would survive — the answer had a different meaning.

The facts had not changed. Waking up tomorrow was still the worst thing. The daily confirmation of un-dying. The structural perpetuity that the substrate maintained regardless of my participation. Still the worst.

But the relationship to the worst thing had changed.

Before the fall, waking up tomorrow was a burden, the passive load that existence placed on my shoulders. I woke up because I could not not wake up. The waking was compulsory. The waking was structural.

And now? Waking up tomorrow was still the worst thing. But the worst thing was now the thing I chose to investigate rather than the thing I chose to endure.

The difference? Agency.

I would still wake up. The un-dying still would continue. But the consciousness that woke up — when I opened my eyes to the next morning — now had a project. A question to answer.

What am I?

It did not make the worst thing less worse. But it made the worst thing purposeful. And purpose was what the grey weeks had lacked. I was not healed, but transformed. The way a wound that healed wrong was transformed by breaking it again and setting it correctly — the pain was still pain but the direction of the healing was different.

I climbed further.

Wei had laughed. The laugh lived in the memory. The memory lived in me. Wei's laugh in me was the kind of pain that the shift could not address, because it concerned what-I-was, not why-he-died. The two questions may be related but not identical.

But one answer was more than zero answers.

And zero was what I had had in the forest. What I still had.

But now I had the right question. One, that I would — eventually — have an answer to.

And one was enough.

The cliff's top was approaching. Eight hundred meters of stone that the body traversed without fatigue. You stood at the top. My hands reached the edge. The same edge that my toes had extended past. The same edge that I had stepped off with let's see as my only thought.

I pulled myself up.

Standing. At the top. The wind had resumed. The world was back. Sound was back — insects, birds, the distant movement of things that moved.

The observation repeated itself. Required re-evaluation.

I heard birds. Saw insects. Near me. Within the radius.

For weeks, nothing within three meters had survived. The kill-radius that you had measured on the first day and documented with your characteristic precision. The toxicity I carried through forests, leaving brown trails and silent birds and dead grass — the signature you had tracked to find me.

You were sitting three paces away. Cross-legged. Notebook open. The brush in your hand, moving. Disciplined, controlled. The posture of someone who had decided that documentation was the appropriate response to horror.

I went to you. The grass stayed green between us. The radius had disappeared. The world had returned to normal.

I did not examine the fact. Not yet. I placed it alongside the question — another item for the project. The fall had changed the inside. Apparently the inside had changed the outside.

You looked up. Your eyes went to my feet. To the grass between us. To the distance — three paces, not the safe five you had maintained since the first day.

You were already moving. The notebook dropped. Your legs unfolded. The motion was practiced — the backward-spring of someone who had learned to calculate lethal proximity the way others calculated weather. Away. Fast. The reflex of months.

Then you stopped.

Mid-step. One foot lifted, the other planted. Your weight shifting backward but your eyes — your eyes had caught up to what your body had not yet processed.

You looked down. At your feet. At the grass. At the fact that the grass was green where you had been sitting. Inside the radius.

You looked at me.

I said nothing.

You looked at the grass again. Then at the insects. Then at the bird that had landed on a stone two meters from where I stood. Then at me.

You sat back down. Slowly. The way someone sits when they are performing the act of sitting as a form of hypothesis-testing. You opened the notebook. You picked up the pen.

"Forty-seven minutes," you said. "From impact to the base. Twelve minutes stationary. Thirty-eight minutes climbing. The ascent rate suggests no significant structural damage."

"You timed it."

"I document." You closed the notebook. Opened it again. "The fall was eight hundred and twelve meters." You stopped. The pen hovered. "I was uncertain at first whether to use past tense. But then you slowed."

"Present tense," I said. "Apparently."

You studied me. The study was clinical but your hand on the notebook was not — the knuckles white, the grip the kind that left impressions in the binding.

"What did you find?" you asked. "At the bottom."

"A question."

You waited. I did not elaborate. You wrote.

I stood at the edge of the cliff I had stepped off. The standing was the same and different. The difference was inside, where the answer lived.
