The first second was the quietest thing I had ever experienced.

It was, as if my sense of weight was gone. There was no ground, no surface, no pressure against the soles of my feet, no phsyical contact to an object, no weight. The whole-body alarm of falling fired — vestibular to muscular, the cascade immediate. Firing into nothing. And beneath the alarm was weightlessness. The body moving along with gravity. For the next few seconds, gravity was not a force to be felt.

The cliff wall barely moved. Grey rock, sedimentary, exposed to weather for geological time. At this speed — a walking pace, perhaps twice — I could have counted the striations. The autobiography of a mountain, readable to anyone falling past it slowly enough.

*One.*

Wind. The first real wind. Air resistance building against my face, my chest. The robes caught the current and fluttered. The cliff wall slid upward — accelerating. Or rather, I gained downward speed. The distinction was academic.

*Two.*

Faster. The wind pressed my robes flat against my body. Loose fabric made tight by the speed of my descent. The striations blurred from legible to decorative. The sky above me receded. Blue. High-altitude blue. Getting farther. 

The thing I was approaching was grey and final and patient.

*Three.*

The wind roared like a storm. My hair whipped upward. The cliff wall was a grey current, featureless at this velocity. Tears streamed horizontally across my temples and vanished into the air behind me.

I closed my eyes.

*Four.*

The wind against my skin was a surface. A wall of air pressing against every exposed inch. My robes had stopped fluttering — pressed too tight to catch the wind, clinging like wet cloth. The sound filled everything. A roar without edges, without gaps. Not wind heard from shelter. Wind heard from inside it.

And something else. Something returning. The weightlessness from the first second was thinning. The wind pressing against me was not just noise — it was resistance, and resistance meant the body was being carried. Not by ground. By air. A fraction of my weight, handed back.

*Five.*

The roar shifted. Not louder — deeper. The frequency dropping as the velocity steadied. I felt it in my bones before I understood it. The acceleration was slowing. Two forces — gravity pulling, air pushing — approaching equilibrium.

The weight returned further. Half, perhaps. The wind held me the way water held a body — distributed, imprecise, but real. I was heavy again. Not standing-heavy, not ground-heavy. Falling-heavy. The air remembering what the first second had forgotten.

*Six.* 

The wind stopped increasing.

Terminal velocity, or the approach to it. The body had reached its agreement with the air. No more acceleration. This was the speed at which I would meet the rock.

The weightlessness was gone. The wind carried my entire weight now — not from below, but from the front, pressing against every surface. I was as heavy as I had been on the ridge. Heavier, perhaps. The air was the ground, and the ground pushed back.

The rock-dust smell reached me. Mineral and dry and ancient. Drawn upward by displaced air, carried into my nostrils by the wind of my own descent.

*Seven.*

Constant. The wind held. The roar held. The pressure on my skin held.

Nothing was changing. The outside had become static — the same force, the same sound, the same speed. Second after second after undifferentiated second.

*Eight.*

Terminal velocity. Fully achieved.

The outside had nothing left to tell me. Nothing would change on the way down.

*Nine.*

I was down already about 300 meters and had reached a velocity of approximately 50 meters per second. The rock was 500 meters below me. At this speed, I would meet it in ten seconds. Ten seconds of waiting. 

I stopped counting and turned inward.

The qi was present. The signature of the autonomous cycling that operated without my direction. Functioning. Continuous. Present the way the heart was present — maintaining, not responding to the crisis.

Nothing about the qi suggested it knew I was falling.

But there was something else, too.

Below the qi. Below the channels. Below the framework that your cultivation theory describes. Something at the foundation. Not qi, not body, not mind, but the thing all of those rested on. There wasn't a name for it, but it felt like a substrate.

And the substrate stirred.

Like a sleeping animal stirs when a noise reaches it. Pre-waking. The preparatory movement before the eyes opened. Something down there — at a level where everything dissolved — shifted.

I felt a warmth bubbling up in me.

Warmth from the foundation. Energy from a source that had no name I knew of. It expanded from the center — the place your cultivation theory calls the core, but beneath the core — outward. Into the channels. Along the body. Through paths that usually carried qi and now carried something older.

I still sensed the same wind, the same roar, the same pressure, the same smell of ancient rock. The world of the senses had become a single held note. Yet inside, the warmth moved — slowly, deliberately — reaching one meridian, then the next and the next.

I waited for the ground.

The rock was coming. Any second. Any moment. How many seconds had passed? Around twelve?

The warmth became pressure. A hand pressing on the chest from inside. A hand made of warmth. The force increasing.

The waiting continued.

Seconds that felt like minutes. The wind roared. The burning grew. The rock-dust smell thickened — dry on the tongue, mineral in the throat. Close. The ground had to be close now.

It must have been hours. The same wind. The same burning. The same waiting. The hand on the chest pressing harder, the warmth exceeding the comfortable range, energy building toward something I could not name.

The ground should have come by now. Eight hundred meters was not infinite. The math was simple. The rock should have met me.

Why hadn't it?

The wind changed.

It became weaker. The pressure against my face less. The roar diminished. By a fraction, by a degree — but less.

I was not accelerating for a few seconds now. But I was not even maintaining speed. I was — slowing down?

The wind weakened further. The roar dropped. The pressure softened, degree by degree, the wall of air dissolving into something gentler.

I opened my eyes.

Something seemed to stop my fall.

The relationship between my body and the air changed. Gravity weakened. A force opposed by another force, the opposition incremental and enough.

I slowed further.

One hundred meters from the ground. Slowing. Terminal velocity becoming sub-terminal. The grey rock still approaching, gentler now.

Fifty meters. The warmth in my chest became more intense. The hand on my heart pressed hard. Energy engaged in an operation that biology did not describe and physics did not permit.

Twenty meters. Much slower. I was falling like a leaf.

Floating.

For two seconds — or three — I floated. Eight meters from the grey rock. Suspended in the air. Held in an equilibrium. Nothing moved.

I hung in air until I finally descended. Gentle. Settling. My feet touched rock. Soles on grey stone. Ground.

I stood.

On the rock. At the bottom of eight hundred meters. Upright. Intact.

I wiped my robes. The gesture after contact with stone. There was nothing to wipe — the wind had taken everything long before the landing.

The silence was also back. The silence from the top was here, too. Or a different silence — the silence that came after. The silence that followed the impossible.

Then coldness arrived. The warmth that had burned in my chest withdrew. Heat left and was replaced by ice. Cold in the joints. Cold in the fingers. Cold in places cold should not reach, because this cold was internal. The price. Energy deployed; heat extracted.

I shivered. Hands. Knees. Jaw. Every part of my body shivered.

Standing on the grey rock. At the bottom of the cliff. Alive. As always. Trembling. Not as always.

I had survived. This was not news. But this time, I had observed the intervention. The substrate stirring, the warmth deploying, the hand pressing, the fall stopping.

I had observed how I survived.

And the how was that something inside me had decided I would not die. Something with its own judgment. Something that operated on my behalf without asking.

Something in me had decided and told the world: no. Not this. Not now. Not ever.

The shivering continued. The cold held.

But something had changed. After millennia, I had seen it.

But what was it?
