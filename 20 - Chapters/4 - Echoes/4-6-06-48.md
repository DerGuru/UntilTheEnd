The grey arrived slowly. The transition from firelight to dawn was not a switch — it was a dilution. The warm orange thinning into something cooler, paler, the shadows losing their depth degree by degree. The fire had burned to embers. I did not add wood. The morning would be warm enough.

You were still sleeping. The last qi thread had settled an hour ago. Your breathing was steady — the deep, regular rhythm of a body that had decided to live and was doing the work of living without consulting the mind. I had left the laceration to air. The scab was forming well. Dark. Tight. The skin around it bruised but intact.

I sat where I had sat all night. Next to you. The destroyed forest acquiring the daylight clarity that turned devastation from atmospheric to precise. In firelight, the fallen trees had been shapes. In morning light, they were evidence.

The thought arrived the way dawn had arrived — gradually, without announcement. A drift. The analysis from the night hours had wound down, the data points sorted, the questions defined but unanswered. And in the quiet that followed, in the space where the analytical mind released its grip, something else surfaced.

I should leave.

The calculation was simple. You were injured. I was dangerous. The shockwave had demonstrated — in broken ribs and blood — what proximity to me cost. Being near anyone was lethal — to them. The pattern demanded distance. Maximum distance. The only responsible conclusion.

I sat with the calculation. It was correct. Every variable supported it. The logic was clean.

And then:

*Not yet.*

Two words. Not thought. They arrived from somewhere below the calculation. Below the analysis. Below the part of me that had spent the night sorting data points and feeding qi through broken channels.

*Not yet.*

I sat very still.

The words had come from me. Or had they? That was the problem. They had come from me and they had not come from me and both were true simultaneously.

I listened. The way you listen after a sound in an empty room. Holding the breath. Holding the body. Waiting for it again.

Nothing.

The embers ticked. Your breathing continued. A bird tested a note from somewhere in the wounded canopy.

I was listening. Really listening. And what I heard was not a voice. It was the shape of a voice. An impression. The way a hand leaves warmth on a surface after it lifts. Something had pressed two words into the space where my thoughts lived and then withdrawn. And the words were still warm.

The familiarity was the part that tightened my skin. Not strangeness — familiarity. The words felt the way my own breathing felt. The way the heartbeat I never directed felt. Present. Intimate. Belonging to me the way things belonged to a body without asking permission.

I had never heard this voice and I had always heard this voice and the contradiction did not resolve.

Who spoke?

I did not ask the question aloud. I sat with it. The grey dawn. The embers. The sleeping man beside me. And the question living in the same space as the two words that had arrived without origin.

Something inside me had an opinion about whether I should leave. Something inside me knew words.

I checked the laceration. The scab held. The mechanical motions of care — the motions that had carried me through the night. I performed them like normal but the normalcy was thin.

You'd wake, your breathing said soon — I would not mention this. Not because I chose secrecy. Because I did not know what *it* was. What would I say? That something inside me disagreed with my decision to leave? That the disagreement arrived as two words in a voice I recognized without ever having heard?

I tended the fire. I added a branch from the wreckage. The flame took.

The morning progressed — light increasing, temperature rising, the sounds of a forest adjusting to what the shockwave had done. Birds arrived. Life, adjusting. The resilience that living things demonstrated when their world was broken.

The fire burned. The man breathed. And somewhere inside me, in the space where the two words had arrived and withdrawn, something waited. Patient. Familiar. Unspeaking.

I tended the fire. I checked the scab, the shoulder, the ribs. And I waited for you to wake.
