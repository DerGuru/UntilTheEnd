You lasted four hours.

Four hours of walking — slow, careful, each step a negotiation between forward motion and the broken infrastructure that permitted it. Then you stopped. Your body made the decision and informed you afterward. You sat on a root, your breathing went shallow and your right hand pressed against your ribs, which meant the end of today's travel.

We were in the forest again. Real forest with standing trees, intact canopy and filtered light. The destroyed clearing was long behind us. The air smelled like leaf litter and damp bark instead of shattered wood and exposed earth.

I built the fire. You sat against the root and watched the canopy and breathed.

Your notebook was in my pack. I took it out and held it where you could see it.

"Can I read this? All of it?" I asked.

You looked at the notebook. At me. At the notebook again. Something moved behind your eyes. Calculation. The assessment of someone deciding how much to permit.

"Yes," you said.

I sat on a root across from you. Opened it. Your handwriting was miniature. Dense. Someone who wrote faster than neatness permitted.

The first entry: *Day 1. Found her. Oak tree. 10 paces. No response. Qi-radius constant, approximately 3 meters. No food consumed. No sleep observed. No directional preference. Subject appears functional but unmotivated.*

Subject. You had called me subject.

I turned pages. Day by day. Word counts. Qi-measurements. Directional data. Everything quantified. Everything preserved.

I stopped at an entry. Read it twice.

"The path," I said. "A path through undergrowth. With a cairn."

"You were frustrated," you said. "The stream crossing. You were irritated about being slowed. Minutes later, a path appeared through dense undergrowth that should have required a detour. Old-looking. Worn edges. Compressed earth. And a cairn — three stones, mossy, years of growth. None of it had been there ten minutes before."

I turned more pages. Stopped again.

"I fell?" I asked.

You looked up. Something shifted in your expression.

"You wrote about — that I fell and something caught me."

"Eight hundred meters," you said. Slowly. Watching my face. "A cliff. You walked off the edge. Something arrested the fall at the last moment. Something like a qi-cushion, but not from your meridian system. From deeper."

I did not remember the cliff. Not the fall, not the arrest, not the height. I read your words describing it and they were words about someone else.

"We talked about this," you said. "At length. The cliff was why you started the poison experiment. It was the first evidence of the substrate — your word. The fall was the beginning of everything that followed."

The beginning. I looked at the entry. Your miniature script documenting an event that had apparently set the course we were still on. An event I could not access. Not the experience, not the conversation, not the decision it had produced.

"I don't remember any of it," I said.

You said nothing. But your eyes changed. The clinical detachment receding by a fraction, replaced by something I would not name.

I read. Turned. Read.

The observations accumulated. Your careful, hedged language. *Consistent with internal origin. Consistent with defensive response. Consistent with pattern-suppression.* You never wrote conclusions. Only observations. The discipline of someone who understood the difference.

"Something comes from inside me," I said. Reading your words, not my own. "Below the meridians. Below the cultivated structure."

"Yes."

"And something doesn't want me to understand it."

"That's what the data suggests." You shifted against the root. The ribs. "The headaches correlate with cognitive progress. The memory gaps correlate with insight. The erasures correlate with pattern recognition. It's — defensive."

Erasures. I looked at the word in your handwriting. Looked at the gaps in my own memory where the things you described should have been. The cliff. The path. The mechanisms. All documented in your hand because my mind could not hold them.

"After the poison," I said. The last entries. The pages where your handwriting stopped. "There's nothing here."

You were quiet. A different quiet than before.

"The shockwave knocked me unconscious," you said. "I woke up only the next evening. You were sitting by the fire. And to my question what had happened, only had one word." A pause. "Me."

I remembered that. But between the poison and the word was: nothing. A gap. Hours that I had lived through and that had been taken.

No — not nothing. A sensation. The feeling from the morning after — the strange, slow receding. A room emptying while I stood in it. Something had been there and then it wasn't. I remembered the *shape* of the loss but not what had been lost.

"Whatever I understood after the poison is gone," I said. Not a question. I had not written anything down because the notebook had been in the debris and I couldn't find paper and a pen to document my own mind before the erasure took it.

"What else is gone?" I thought aloud.

You were quiet. The quiet of someone choosing words.

"I don't know," you said. "I documented what I observed. But I wasn't always present. And the things that happen inside your mind —" You paused. "You don't share those."

True. I did not.

"I can tell you what you said and did," you said. "I can tell you the qi-readings and the behavioral shifts. What you thought — what you actually understood before the erasures — that's not in the notebook. That was yours. I couldn't record what I couldn't see, hear or detect otherwise."

"How much else have I lost?" I mused.

I looked at the pages. Your miniature script. The months of data. Everything you could see, preserved. Everything I could not hold, gone.

But reading your words — the observations, the patterns, the hedged conclusions — produced no headache. No pressure behind the eyes. No blur. I was reading your understanding and whatever operated inside me did not recognize external input as a threat.

I could not think the patterns into existence. But I could read them.

I closed the notebook. Held it in both hands. The cracked binding, the loose pages, the weight of someone else's documentation of my life.

"I'd like to keep reading," I said. "Later."

You nodded. The nod of a man who had written a notebook for exactly this purpose and who had waited for exactly this moment.

The light filtered through the canopy. You closed your eyes. The ribs. The exhaustion. Within minutes, you were asleep.

I sat with the notebook in my lap. Reading. Rereading. The filtered light shifting as the afternoon progressed toward evening. Your breathing was steady. The forest was quiet.

When the light began to dim I set the notebook aside and built the fire. The evening routine — wood, flame, water heated, the small domestic repetitions that structured time. I cooked the rice. Set your portion near the fire to stay warm. You woke when the smell reached you — the slow, reluctant waking of a body that wanted more sleep and was denied it by hunger.

You ate. Slowly. One-handed. The ribs dictating the terms. I did not eat. We did not speak. The conversation from the afternoon was enough. More than enough.

Evening. I placed my hand on your chest and sent the threads through the cleared paths. This time you did not flinch. The familiarity of the second night. The qi settled into the tissue around the ribs, warming, reducing. You breathed marginally easier afterward.

The night was cold again — open forest, clear sky through the gaps in the canopy. I fed qi into the fire the way I had the night before. The warmth wrapped the camp. You slept. I sat with the notebook and the fire and the knowledge that I was less than I had been and that the evidence was in my hands.
