The forest changed as we descended.

The volcanic soil from the crater region gave way to something older and darker — the loam of a woodland that had been growing undisturbed for centuries. The trees were taller here, the canopy dense enough to turn the afternoon into something that resembled evening. Light came through in columns where the coverage broke, the dust visible in the beams, the air carrying the smell of damp bark and the faint sweetness of decay that healthy forests produced.

You were walking better today. The slow descent helped — easier on the legs, easier on the breathing, the easy downhill gradient doing what your broken core couldn't. Your pen moved as you walked, the scratching steady, the dual operation of locomotion and documentation that you'd perfected across weeks of shared travel.

"The mushrooms are different here," you said.

I looked. You were right. The fungi on the trunks had shifted species — latticed white caps instead of the flat brown shelves we'd been passing for days. A different altitude, a different moisture profile, a different substrate beneath the bark. The forest was telling us how far we'd come in the only language forests used.

"You should eat something," you said.

"No."

"You haven't eaten since —"

"No."

You wrote it down. The refusal, the date, the time. Adding it to the catalog of things I did not do that a body should. The list was getting longer. You did not comment on the list's trajectory.

The trail widened where a stream crossed it — a shallow ford, the water clear enough to show the stones at the bottom, rounded by the patient friction of current against mineral. I stopped at the bank. Not to rest. The water was cold and moving and looking at it required nothing from the parts of me that were hurting.

You sat on a rock. Opened the provisions pac and ate some dried meat and a piece of old bread with the methodical efficiency of a man who treated meals as maintenance rather than pleasure. You chewed while you wrote. The multitasking of someone who had too much to document and too little time between events.

Birds. The canopy was full of them — the territorial calls of species I could identify but didn't bother to. Normal birds. Correct species for the altitude and the season. A small relief, the normalcy of fauna behaving as fauna should, after weeks of wrongs. Wrong stars and wrong winds and wrong everything.

I watched the stream while you ate. The forest breathed around us with the indifference of an ecosystem that did not care about substrates or memory erasure or the headaches of an immortal woman who couldn't stop investigating herself.

Everything was good, until it wasn't.

Southeast and northwest. Pincer formation. Professional. Then they came from two directions.

I turned toward them.

I wanted this.

"Eight," I said. "Formation."

Your pen stopped. You looked where I was looking and saw nothing — your broken core couldn't read qi signatures at this range. But you had learned to trust the count.

The four from the southeast group emerged from the tree line in coordinated formation. They were equipped with spiritual weapons and qi-armor. Practitioners who had been assigned a target and told the target was dangerous.

I moved.

The first cultivator's halberd came at me from the right. I went under it. Just enough to avoid it. His halberd passed above me. My hand passed through his guard. The meridian junction was there. I touched it. His qi failed. He fell.

The second attacked almost simultaneously. Her sword was faster than the halberd. They timed their attack well. It came close and I redirected it — not blocked, guided. The blade's momentum carried her forward, past me, off-balance. A finger against her qi point at shoulder completed what the redirection had started. She went down.

A smile appeared.

I smiled. Not the careful kind. The real one — the one that used everything.

Third. Fourth. Had the same fortune. I turned and pushed my fingers into their meridians. The southeast group: disabled in what you would later describe as "breathing twice."

The northwest group arrived as the southeast group went down. Another four. They had watched the first four fall. And they adjusted — wider formation, greater distance.

The smile widened.

The warmth came back. The old warmth, the one I remembered from battlefields.

I moved through them like a blur. Faster than even their cultivated eyes could follow. The first of the northwest group came at me with a spear. I caught it with one hand, the other hand on his chest, and pushed. The spear was not a problem. The cultivator was. He went down. I used the spears dull end to strike at the second's chest. When I hit the third's knee, it snapped. Splinters flying around us. The fourth's neck was hit with a short stick, that was all that was left of the spear. The northwest group: disabled even faster."

My hand went to my mouth. Wiping.

The disgust that followed was not at them. At the warmth. At the pleasure. At the fact that the pleasure was genuine.

I picked something up. A pendant, that had fallen from the leader of the northwest group, who was now on the ground, but conscious. The pendant small and made of metal. It was warm — warmer than it should have been, as if it held its own heat.

There were some marks like insignia. The same marks I had seen on the first group's equipment. The same organization.

And a scratch. Added recently. After the original inscription.

*The new one.*

I put the talisman in my robe.

You stood at the clearing's edge. You had seen the smile.

"You enjoyed that," you said.

I didn't answer. There was nothing to say that you hadn't already written in your head.

You did not write. Just stared at me for a moment. The look was not judgmental. It was not surprised. It was not even curious.

It was the loudest thing you had ever done.
