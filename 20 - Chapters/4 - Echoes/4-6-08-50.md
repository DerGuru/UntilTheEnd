You woke in the late afternoon.

Not all at once. Your breathing shifted first — a deeper intake, immediately punished by the ribs, immediately abandoned. Then a sound. Not a word. The sound a body made when it returned to a world it had not agreed to leave. Your fingers moved against the pack beneath your shoulder. Your eyes opened.

The left eye was partially obscured by the swelling from the temple laceration. The right eye was clear, focused, already doing what your eyes always did — surveying, noting, assembling.

You saw the fallen trees. The fissured ground. The lean-to of broken trunks above you, casting the shade I had put you in. The fire, low, at the edge of the clearing.

You saw me.

I was sitting beside you. My hands were clean. I had washed them in the stream hours ago — but my clothes still carried the stains. Brown on the sleeve where the fabric had been torn for your bandage. Brown on the hem, the knee. The scab on your temple was dark and tight. Your arm — reset, swollen, positioned against your body in the configuration that minimized pain.

You tried to sit up. The ribs stopped you. You lay back slowly and controlled — the motion of someone negotiating with damage.

"Don't," I said. Quiet. Not a command. A request.

You looked at me. The right eye steady. The left eye trying. The observation that was your nature — even now, even injured, even waking into a world you did not yet understand. You were already reading.

Your hand went to your hip. Where the notebook should have been. Your fingers found nothing, and I saw the flicker — the brief alarm of a man separated from his instrument.

It was beside me. I had found it in the debris — battered, the binding cracked, but intact. I had kept it out of your line of sight. Not hidden. Just not offered. You needed water and rest before you needed documentation.

"Notebook," you said. Your voice was rough. Dry. The sound of a throat that had not had water since before the experiment.

I handed you the water-skin. "Later."

You drank. Small sips — your body knew the ribs would not tolerate a full swallow. You handed it back. You looked at my clothes. At the stains. At the fire. At the destruction. The sequential observation of someone assembling a narrative from evidence. I watched you build it, piece by piece, from the outside in.

"How long?" you asked.

"Since yesterday. It's afternoon."

You processed this. The lost time. The gap. I could see the discomfort — not physical this time. The discomfort of a documenter who had missed the event he had come to document.

"What happened?"

"The poison was neutralized. Before it reached lethal concentration. Something in me neutralized it."

You were watching my face. Not my words — my face. Reading what the face carried rather than what the mouth delivered.

"And then?"

"The neutralization released a shockwave. Qi-pressure." I looked at the fallen trees. "You were at ten paces."

You looked at the trees. You looked at your own body. The connection formed behind your eyes — I could see it arrive. The evidence. The conclusion.

You were quiet for a long time. The fire crackled softly. A bird called from somewhere in the surviving trees — the same species that had been colonizing the wreckage all day. The late-afternoon light was lengthening, the shadows of the fallen trunks stretching across the fissured ground.

"What was that?" you asked.

Quiet. Steady. The question of a man who needed the phenomenon named.

I looked at you. Your pale face. The scab on the temple. The swollen arm. The ribs you were breathing around. Everything I had done to you, written on your body.

"Me."

One word. The smallest true answer.

You held my gaze. Five seconds. Ten. Long enough to weigh the word against everything you knew — the shockwave, the protection, the poison's failure, the centuries. Long enough to find that it fit.

You leaned back. Slowly. The ribs. You closed your eyes. You breathed — carefully, within the limits the damage imposed.

"You," you said. Slowly. Not a question. An echo.

You did not ask for more. No clarification. No elaboration. You asked nothing further.

The one word was enough. The word held the complete truth in its smallest volume.

The fire settled. The bird called once more and stopped. The light moved.

You breathed. I sat. The word remained between us.

You took that word with you. Never returned it. Never asked for a longer one.
