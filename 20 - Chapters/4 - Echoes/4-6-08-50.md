You woke in the late afternoon.

The transition: a change in your breathing, a deeper intake immediately punished by the ribs, a sound — not a word, not a groan, somewhere between. Your eyes opened. The left eye: partially obscured by the swelling from the temple laceration. The right eye: clear, focused, performing the immediate environmental survey that consciousness produced.

You saw the destroyed forest. The fallen trees. The fissured ground. The circle of devastation with the fire at its center.

You saw me.

I was sitting two paces away. Your blood on my hands — dried now, gone brown. Your bandage on your temple — my sleeve, stained, functional. Your arm — reset, swollen, positioned against your body in the configuration that minimized pain.

You tried to sit up. The ribs. You stopped. The arrested motion of someone whose body was communicating: no.

"Don't," I said.

You lay back. Your right eye tracked me. The observation that was your nature — the notation, the recording, the assessment that you performed on every input with the compulsive thoroughness that characterized: you.

Your hand went to your hip. Where the notebook should have been. Gone — scattered, blown away, somewhere in the debris field.

"Notebook," you said. Your voice was roughened. The respiratory obstruction from the blood had cleared but the vocal cords were affected by the dehydration and the concussion and the general systemic impact of being thrown into a tree.

"Later," I said.

You looked at me. At my hands. At the bandage. At the fire. The sequential observation of someone assembling a narrative from evidence.

"How long?" you asked.

"Overnight. It's afternoon."

You processed this. The lost time. The unconsciousness. The gap in your documentation. I watched you process it — the discomfort on your face, the discomfort of a documenter who had missed the most documentable event.

"What happened?" you asked.

"The poison was neutralized. Before it reached lethal concentration. Something in me neutralized it."

You were watching my face. Not my words — my face. Reading what the face said rather than what the mouth did.

"And then?"

"And then the neutralization released a shockwave. Qi-pressure. Approximately thirty-meter radius." I looked at the fallen trees. The evidence. "You were at ten meters."

You looked at the fallen trees. You looked at your own body. You connected the evidence. You saw the blood. You knew who put it there.

You said nothing for a long time. The fire crackled. A bird — one of the opportunistic species from the morning — called from a surviving tree at the destruction's edge.

"What was that?" you asked.

Quiet. Not afraid — precise. The question of a man who documented phenomena and needed the phenomenon identified.

I looked at you. Your pale face. Your bloody temple. Your swollen arm. Your broken ribs. The evidence of what had happened. The evidence of what I was.

"Me."

One word. One syllable. The complete answer to your question.

You stared at me. Five seconds. Ten. You stared long enough to weigh the word against everything you knew and find that it: fit. That the single word explained the shockwave and the protection and the poison's failure and the centuries of immortality and the deaths of everyone who had stood too close.

Me.

You leaned back. Slowly. The ribs. You closed your eyes. You breathed — carefully, the shallow rhythms that broken ribs imposed.

"You," you repeated slowly.

Not a question. An echo. The word returning to the space between us where it would remain.

You did not ask for clarification. You did not ask for elaboration. You did not ask what it meant in the mechanical sense, what the thing inside me was, how it operated, why it existed. You asked nothing.

Because the word was: enough. The word was the complete truth compressed into its smallest possible volume. Me. I did this. I am this. The protection, the destruction, the shockwave, the centuries. Me.



You lay on the ground in the circle of wreckage. I sat two paces away. Your blood on my hands. The fire burned the wood that my protection had created.

Your eyes went to the ground between us. The grass: green. Two paces of it — living, unseared, ordinary.

"The radius," you said. "It's gone."

You looked at the green grass. Looked at me. Filed the datum next to the one that preceded it.

Between us: one word, one absence. Both sufficient.

The silence held. Not the empty silence of my months alone. Not the one-sided silence of your early days of documentation. A different silence: the shared comprehension of two people who had arrived at the same understanding through different routes and who recognized that the understanding did not require further language.

You breathed. I waited. The fire burned. The word remained.

Me.

You took that word home. Never returned it. Never asked for a longer one.
