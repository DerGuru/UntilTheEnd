You wrote until the light changed.

Our conversation had ended where conversations with you usually end — in documentation. You had not asked the question's content because you understood that the content was not yet.

Not yet. The phrase you used for every phenomenon that resisted immediate classification. Not yet — currently unresolved but resolvable. I had borrowed your phrase without telling you I had borrowed it. Or the phrase had migrated, the way phrases migrated between people who spent enough time in proximity.

Evening came. You built a fire. You cooked. The standard procedure — the ginger, the rice, the scratched pot on the stones. The routine was your anchor.

You served. Two bowls. You placed mine at the usual distance, looked at the distance and the grass and then moved my bowl closer. Two paces. Inside what had been the radius. Testing your hypothesis, that the radius was gone.

We ate. The silence was the working kind — two people processing different versions of the same event. When you were finished, you set your bowl down. Picked it up again. Set it down again. Your hands needed occupation but the bowl was insufficient.

"I would like," you said, and paused. "I would like to reconstruct the event. If you are willing."

You had your notebook open. The pen ready. The posture of scholarship — and beneath the posture, the hands that were still not entirely steady.

"Why a pen?" I asked.

You looked at me. The question had interrupted the procedure. "What?"

"A pen. Not a brush."

You glanced at the pen in your hand as if noticing it for the first time. "Smaller script. More per page. A brush requires ink preparation, drying time, and horizontal surfaces." You turned the pen between your fingers. "A pen requires a pen."

A practical answer. I had expected a practical answer. What I had not expected was that I would ask. The question had arrived without planning — a small curiosity, surfacing on its own. I had not asked a genuine question since I had asked you about your shattered core, how it felt, when it broke. That had been about pain. This was about a pen. The distance between those two questions was the distance the cliff had moved me.

"From my position at the ridge," you continued, "I observed the following. You stepped forward. There was no acceleration phase — no running start, no push. You walked off the edge like you were simply taking one step further."

"Yes."

"You fell. The first seconds —" You consulted your notes. "The first seconds were slow. Relative to what I expected. You were close to the wall. I could see your robes. The wind caught them."

"At two seconds," I said. "The wind started at two seconds."

You wrote. "At what point did the robes flatten against the body?"

I considered. The memory was precise. The wind had pressed the fabric tight, clinging like wet cloth. "Four seconds. Perhaps late in the third."

"Consistent," you said. The word was professional. Clinical. You were holding yourself together with procedure. "I lost visual detail after approximately fifteen seconds. The distance — more than a li is substantial. You became a shape. A dark shape against grey stone. Falling."

The fire cracked. A log shifted.

"I counted," you said. "Twelve seconds to what I estimated as the midpoint. Then the counting lost its anchor. You were too small to track velocity against the wall."

"I counted the first nine," I said. "Then I stopped counting."

You looked up from the notebook. "Why?"

"Because the outside stopped changing. Terminal velocity. The wind was constant. The sound was constant. There was nothing left to count."

You wrote. Then stopped writing. "What did you do instead?"

"I turned inward."

The fire worked between us. You waited. The notebook lay open and the pen hovered, and you waited with the patience of a man who understood that some answers required the speaker to find the words.

"I felt something," I said. "During the fall. A pressure." I put my hand on my chest. Flat. The way you press down to check whether something beneath the skin is still there. "Here. Like a hand. From the inside."

You wrote. "The core?"

"Near the core. Around it. But not —" I shook my head. "Not qi. The qi was still doing what qi does. Circulating. Indifferent. This was something else. Something underneath."

"Underneath the qi?"

"Underneath everything."

You looked up. The pen hovered. "What was it?"

I held the silence for a moment. Not for effect. Because the answer was inadequate and I knew it was inadequate before I said it.

"Something else."

The fire lit your face from below. Shadows moved across your features. You were listening the way you listened when the anomaly exceeded your framework.

"Something below the qi," I said. "Below the channels. Below everything that your cultivation theory describes. A substrate. Something at the foundation."

You wrote the word. Underlined it. "Substrate. That is an unusual term for —"

"I know what it means. The thing beneath. The thing everything else rests on." I looked at the fire. "It is the closest word I have."

You accepted this. The pen moved. A scholar who recognized when a speaker's terminology, however imprecise, was more honest than a correction.

"The substrate stirred. The way —" I paused. The analogy I had felt during the fall. "Something shifting before waking. A preparatory movement."

"At what altitude?"

"I don't know. After between nine and fifteen seconds. Closer to fifteen. Near the bottom. The ground should have been close — seconds away. And instead of arriving, there was this."

You calculated. I could see you calculating — the numbers moving behind your eyes. "Fifteen seconds. That is five hundred to six hundred meters down. Perhaps two hundred and fifty remaining. At terminal velocity —" You looked at me. "Three seconds to impact."

"Yes."

"The substrate activated with about three seconds remaining."

"Warmth," I said. "From the center. From below the core. It expanded outward. Through channels — not the standard meridians. Different pathways. Older." I held my hands toward the fire. The warmth of the flames on my palms. "It moved through me the way water moves through a streambed. Using the structure. Belonging to none of it."

"And the deceleration?"

"The warmth became pressure. A hand pressing on the chest from inside. The force increased. And then — the wind changed. Less pressure against the face. The roar dropped. I opened my eyes and I was already much slower."

You tapped the pen against the page. "Air qi. Did you feel any ambient qi? Wind cultivators at higher stages can compress air beneath them — a cushion effect. Crude, but documented."

"No. There was no qi outside. No pull from the air. No compression beneath me. The force came from inside. From the chest outward." I paused. "The air did nothing. The air was just air."

You wrote. Crossed something out. Wrote again.

"From the ridge," you said, "I observed the following. You should have reached the base after approximately eighteen seconds. You did not. For seven seconds beyond that. You were still falling. Visibly. But your descent rate had changed. I could not determine the exact moment because at that distance, deceleration is difficult to distinguish from —" You stopped. Swallowed. "From the alternative."

"From impact."

"I watched for movement," you said. Your voice was level but I heard the effort it cost you. "At the base. For a very long time, I watched for movement."

"Seventeen minutes," I said.

"What?"

"I sat at the base for seventeen minutes. Shivering."

You looked at me. The pen had stopped.

"The landing," I said. "One hundred meters from the ground. Slower. Fifty meters — the warmth in my chest was intense. The hand pressed hard. Twenty meters — I was falling like a leaf. Then I floated. Eight meters above the rock. Suspended. For two or three seconds, nothing moved." I paused. "Then I descended. Gently my feet touching stone."

You didn't write.

"And then cold came," I said. "The warmth withdrew and was replaced by ice. In the joints. In the fingers. The price for whatever the substrate had spent. I shivered. For seventeen minutes."

The fire burned low between us. You set the pen down.

"The substrate," you said. "It acted autonomously."

"Yes."

"Without your direction."

"Without my knowledge. Without my direction."

"And the mechanism — the warmth, the pressure, the deceleration — you are describing a force that opposed gravity. Selectively. Applied to your body alone. Operated from within."

"Yes."

You were quiet for a while. The fire cracked. The smoke rose straight — no wind.

"I have documented seventeen anomalous events since the beginning of this study," you said. "I maintained composure during each one because composure is the prerequisite for accurate observation." You picked up a stick. Turned it in your hands. "This is different."

"Different how?"

"The other events were —" You searched. "Contained. A toxicity radius. An organism avoiding proximity." You broke the stick. The snap was loud in the quiet. You looked at the two halves. "The other events suggested anomaly," you said. "This suggests something with judgment."

I said nothing. He had arrived at the same conclusion I had reached at the bottom.

"Something decided," you said. Quietly. To the fire.

"Yes."

"Something that is not you."

"I don't know. It lives where I live. It uses what I use. But it decides without asking."

You placed the stick halves in the fire. Watched them catch.

"I didn't die," I said.

"You didn't know that."

The accuracy of this was absolute. I had not known. *Let's see* had been the thought. Not *I will survive.* Let's see. An experiment without predicted outcome.

"No," I said. "I didn't."

You set down the pen. Not on the notebook. Next to it. The gesture was deliberate — the scholar stepping outside the scholar.

"Then why did you do it?" you said.

I paused. The question was not accusatory. He honestly wanted to understand.

"Because I am old," I said. "Very old. Too old."

You waited. For the rest of the sentence. For the elaboration.

There was none. The reason was complete.

You looked at me then. The fire between us. Your face in the unsteady light. The look was not clinical. Not the study-look that preceded documentation. Something beneath the study. Something the documentation could not hold.

"Don't do it again," you said.

I did not promise. Promises from an immortal were structural lies — the forever-person could not guarantee the choices of the self that would exist in a thousand years. So I didn't and I said nothing.

The silence was the only honest response.
