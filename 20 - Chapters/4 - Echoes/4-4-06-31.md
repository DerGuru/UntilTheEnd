You were at the camp. Twenty paces from the cliff's edge. Your pack on the ground. Your notebook in your hands.

You had seen it.

Your face told the story before your mouth could. The wrong color. Not fear-pale — something permanent. The kind that meant: this changes things.

"What," you said. Then stopped. The sentence had not continued because continuing required language adequate to the phenomenon and the phenomenon had no adequate language.

You tried again.

"What was that?"

Three words. The same three words you had asked at other moments of anomaly — the branches, the path, the pulsing. But the delivery was different. The branches-voice had been curious. The path-voice had been analytical. The pulsing-voice had been watchful.

This voice was shattered.

You had watched a person step off an eight-hundred-meter cliff. You had watched the fall — seconds you could not have counted but could estimate from the visual of a human body diminishing against grey stone. You had watched the fall stop. The floating. The settling. The standing-at-the-bottom.

Your notebook was open and the pages were — I could see from here — half-filled with writing that was worse than your usual miniature precision. Larger. Uneven. The handwriting of hands that were unsteady because their owner's reality had been revised.

Your hand was still shaking. The pen trembled against the page.

"You jumped," you said.

"Yes."

"Eight hundred meters."

"Approximately."

The word hung. I let it.

"You're standing here."

"Yes."

We were asymmetric. You were shattered and I was calm and the calm was new. The old calm had been disconnection — the not-caring, the stillness that emptiness produced. This calm was the presence of something. Resolution. A mind that had found a question to replace a void and the question was a better occupant.

You wrote. The pen moving in the shaking rhythm. Your hand: unsteady. Your breathing: irregular, your body still running the threat-response the cliff-jump had triggered.

"How," you said.

I looked at you. The looking was deliberate — directed, intentional. I looked at you and the looking was: honest.

"I don't know," I said. "Not yet."

The not-yet was mine. The same not-yet that you used — the word that contained the assumption of eventual understanding. I had borrowed your word. Or the word had migrated, the way words migrated between people who spent enough time in proximity. Not yet. Currently unresolved but resolvable.

You closed the notebook. Your hand was still shaking. You put the notebook in your pack — done writing, not because the data was complete but because the data was too large for the hand to hold.

Evening came. You built a fire. You cooked. The standard procedure — the ginger, the rice, the scratched pot on the stones. The routine was your anchor.

You meditated. Louder than usual — the volume increase that indicated the noise-need had intensified. The mantras were faster. The humming more insistent. The breathing was theatrical in the way that desperation made things theatrical.

I sat at six paces. The fire between us. Your meditation filling the space with the sound I had complained about and endured and complained about again and that was now —

I knew the rhythm.

The rise and fall. The contour that was not melody but resembled it. The sequence that repeated every forty seconds. I knew it. Not from study. From exposure. From twenty-three nights of the ten-pace distance that had installed the pattern in my memory without my memory requesting the installation.

I knew when your breathing would deepen. I knew when the mantra would conclude and the humming would bridge. I knew when the humming would accelerate and the next mantra would begin.

The immortal who didn't care had accidentally memorized a stranger's meditation schedule. This was either the most pathetic thing that had happened in my existence or the second most pathetic, the first being that I noticed I had done it.

I noticed that I knew.

The noticing said: something in me still learned. Still absorbed. Still converted proximity into knowledge and knowledge into —

Familiarity. The knowledge that this sound came next and the knowledge that the sound's coming-next was: expected.

I expected.

Expected. As in: anticipated. The future contained a predictable element and the predictable was: your meditation rhythm. The future contained something I knew about before it happened and the knowing-before was —

Not hope. Not yet. Just: expectation. The first stirring of a mind that had decided the future was a space that contained things rather than a space that was empty.

You meditated. I expected. The rhythm continued. The fire burned. The night was cold and the cold was external and the internal was: one degree warmer than before.

You slept, eventually. The silence returned. The you-shaped hole in the silence that I had noticed before was still there.

You never knew I'd memorized your rhythm. Or you knew and pretended not to. Either way — it was the first thing I kept.

Keeping it felt like both progress and risk. Proximity became familiarity without asking permission. Familiarity became a thread.

I did not pull on it.

I sat in the silence. In the hole. In the one-degree-warmer-than-before.

The humming beneath my ribs continued. The substrate breathing. The echo.
