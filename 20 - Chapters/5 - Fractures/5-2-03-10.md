I made some light with qi, to carry on searching.

The sealed cylinders were scattered through the ruin — some still in wall recesses, some buried under collapsed shelving, some rolled into corners where gravity and centuries had nudged them. I worked through the debris methodically, checking each cylinder, breaking wax, unrolling, reading some lines, deciding. Most were more sect records. Administrative trivia preserved with more care than it deserved. I set those aside and moved on.

By the time the dark outside had shifted from black to the deep grey that precedes dawn, I'd opened almost more than a hundred cylinders and found two more scrolls that mattered.

The second was from a different era. About fifteen hundred years old. A woman — the language markers were clear, gendered pronouns embedded in a grammatical structure that predated the gender-neutral reforms of the Middle Period.

The margins were dense with notations. Again dates, locations, weather conditions, the subject's physical state before and after each observation. Neat, obsessive, the handwriting of someone who believed that context was as important as content. The kind of person who documented everything and trusted the record more than their own memory.

*I tried to die,* she wrote. *My body refused. As if it knew something I did not.*

Different words. Same meaning. The coincidence was too precise to be coincidence.

The third was older again — perhaps four thousand years, the script so archaic that I had to read the characters multiple times, translating through layers of linguistic sediment. The cultivator — I couldn't determine gender from the script — had documented the very same phenomena. Qi that obeyed will rather than technique, anomalies in the surrounding world, a body that continued against the mind's explicit wishes.

*The world around me obeys,* this one wrote. *I have never learned this. I have never asked for it. It obeys as if I am the law and the world merely follows and I do not know what I have done to deserve subjection so total that even the earth bends.*

Three cultivators. Three epochs. More than two thousand years of distance between the first and the last.

I laid the scrolls side by side on the cleared floor of the library. The morning light fell on them in parallel beams, pinning each one to the stone like pressed leaves between pages. You sat across from me, notebook open, watching me compare them with the focused attention of someone who understood that what was happening was important even if you didn't fully understand what it was.

Side by side, a pattern emerged that I hadn't noticed reading them individually. All three scrolls shared a structural quality — they read less like journals and more like field reports. Observations written by someone at a slight remove. The first-person passages, the cultivators' own words, were embedded in a framework of external documentation. As if each cultivator had someone with them. Someone watching. Someone writing it down.

"Parallels?" you asked.

"Different language. Identical experience. Three people across three millennia who had each arrived at this truth about themselves — their qi responds to will, not training. Their bodies sustain themselves against their wishes. And the world around them..." I paused. The word I wanted was *obeys,* but that word carried weight I wasn't ready to apply to myself. "Changes."

"Warping reality."

"Adjustment. The word warping implies violence. This is... softer. Retroactive. As if reality is being edited, not broken."

You wrote that down. I saw you underline *edited.*

"And all three are dead."

"All three stopped documenting. The scrolls end. Just like the inscriptions at the ruins — mid-sentence, mid-thought. No conclusion, no farewell, no sign of violence. They simply stop."

"Forgetting."

I looked at you.

"That's your theory," you said. Not accusatory — observational. "From the cave inscriptions. You said they forgot."

"Yes."

"Something made them forget what they were investigating. Erased the thought. The question. The connection."

"Yes."

"And you think whatever did that protects you too. Keeps you alive. And erases anyone who gets too close to understanding it."

I said nothing.

"Because if they understood it," you continued, your voice quiet and level, "they might try to stop it. And whatever it is doesn't want to be stopped. So it removes the threat. Not by killing — by forgetting. The cleanest erasure there is."

You were right. You were exactly right. And the fact that you had reached that conclusion from the evidence meant the evidence was clear enough for a non-cultivator scholar to read it, which meant it was not subtle at all, which meant either the truth was becoming harder to hide or I was becoming less capable of hiding it.

Or both.

"Eat something," you said.

There was fish. You'd caught it somewhere — the nearby stream, probably, during the hours I'd spent buried in scrolls, unreachable, insensible to anything that wasn't ink on ancient paper. You'd cleaned it, gutted it, cooked it over the fire I still hadn't seen. The smell was good. My fingers tightened on my chopsticks, the way competence in others makes your own hands feel emptier.

I hadn't fished in — a long time. I might never have fished. The memory was ambiguous — there was water and patience and something about a line, but the details had the quality of dreams rather than experience. I might have been remembering someone else.

I took the fish and ate. The flesh was firm, seasoned with something peppery you must have found at the stream bank. I ate without pleasure, without displeasure, with the steady blankness of someone filling a vessel. But I ate.

"So I'm not the only one," I said between bites.

You looked up from your notebook. "No."

"Others had this. The... whatever it is that keeps me alive. This qi obedience. This reality... adjustment."

"And they're all dead. Or they would still be present."

"Yes."

"Which means whatever keeps you alive didn't keep them alive."

"Or it did. For a while. And then stopped."

"Why would it stop?"

I set down the fish. The question was the right question — the one that mattered, the one that had been circling me since the first scroll like a hawk waiting for the right moment to dive. Why would protection stop? Why would a force that overrides the cultivator's own will to die suddenly allow death?

"I don't know," I said.

You picked up your chopsticks. Set them down. Picked them up again. A cycle I'd seen before — your version of thinking, the physical fidgeting of a mind working through a problem too large for stillness.

"Your body does it," you said. "Not your technique. Not your training. Your body sustains itself. Against your will."

"Yes."

"Then it's not external. It's you."

"It's something in me. That's not the same as being me."

"Isn't it?"

I looked at you across the fish bones and the scrolls and the dust and the centuries of evidence laid out between us like a bridge neither of us was sure could hold weight.

"No," I said. "Whatever this is — whatever refuses to let me die, whatever adjusts reality, whatever erased those researchers — it's not a choice they made or I make. It's not something I do. It's something that is done to me. Through me. As if I'm the instrument, not the musician."

"And the others were instruments too."

"Apparently."

"Instruments that broke."

Silence. The fire outside crackled. A bird sang — a real bird, the right species for this region, the right season, the right time of day. I noted the rightness. It didn't comfort me.

"Maybe," you said and your voice was careful, the way your voice is careful when you're placing something fragile on a surface you don't trust, "you're not different from them. Maybe you're just... newer. Maybe whatever wears out instruments hasn't worn you out yet."

I finished the fish. Set the plate down. Wiped my hands on my robe.

"Or maybe," I said, "I'm the one who broke all the others."

You stopped writing.

I stood. Went to the shelf. Pulled down the next cylinder.

The afternoon passed in scrolls and silence and the slow work of cross-referencing what I already had. The three scrolls against the seven sites. Different dates, different locations, the same symptoms, the same endings. The same phenomena, the same arc, the same abrupt stop — repeated across thousands of years with the consistency of a law rather than a pattern.

Ten total. Seven from the ruins. Three from the scrolls. Ten cultivators across millennia. Ten beginnings. Ten erasures.

And me. Still here. Reading their words. Tasting chalk and smelling incense and feeling the cold in the metal — a cold they must have known, in places they had walked.

A shelf settled deeper into its collapse. Wood groaned. Dust sifted down.

Still alive. That word carried more weight than it should have. Alive while ten others had stopped. Protected by a force I didn't understand, that didn't consult me, that operated with the cheerful indifference of something that knew better.

The hand that held me up had pushed them down. The protection and the erasure, I was beginning to suspect, were one and the same — separated only by whether the instrument was still useful.

I didn't say any of this. I rolled up the scrolls. Stacked them in a neat pile. My hands moved with the precision of someone who has handled ancient documents for longer than most libraries have existed.

You wrote in your notebook. The fire died to embers.

The light in the ruin shifted from gold to grey and finally to the blue that precedes total dark.
