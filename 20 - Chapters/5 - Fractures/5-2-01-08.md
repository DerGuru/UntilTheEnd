The library was buried in the side of a mountain like a secret someone had tried to tell and then thought better of.

The entrance was low — half-swallowed by root systems and collapsed earth, a horizontal gap between rock and overgrowth that required crouching. Vines had sealed the opening so thoroughly that from ten paces it looked like solid hillside. I'd walked past it twice before the qi told me what my eyes couldn't.

You pushed through the undergrowth behind me, thorns catching your sleeves, your pack scraping against the rock overhang. You didn't complain. You scraped dirt from the lintel stone with your thumbnail, found carved characters underneath, and wrote them down before you'd finished standing up.

The air hit us like a wall of age. Thick. Dry. Tasting of chalk so fine it was more like flour — the residue of centuries of stone slowly grinding itself into powder, of plaster crumbling, of walls exhaling their own substance into the dark. My teeth grit against it immediately. The metal fixtures on the collapsed shelving — iron brackets, hinges, the remains of a locking clasp — radiated cold. And underneath the dust, threading through the stale air like a living thing. Incense. Musty. Sweet. Without any determinable source.

The same three. Every site. Chalk, cold metal, incense. I'd stopped being surprised by them. I'd started expecting them, the way you expect a knock before a door opens.

The interior was more intact than I'd expected. The ceiling had given way in the main hall — stone and timber splayed across the floor in a pattern that spoke of one catastrophic moment rather than gradual decay — but the side rooms still held. Shelves, mostly collapsed, their contents scattered. Stone tablets, cracked but legible. And scrolls.

Hundreds of scrolls.

Most were ruined — moisture had seeped through rock fissures at some point, turning paper to pulp, turning ink to ghosts. But some had been stored in sealed containers, stone cylinders with wax caps and those had survived. A dozen. Maybe twenty. Enough.

You sneezed. Three times in quick succession, each one louder than the last, the kind of sneeze chain that builds momentum and leaves the victim blinking and exhausted.

I was already working.

Systematic. Row by row. Shelf by collapsed shelf. Each scroll removed, examined, set aside or discarded. The motion was mechanical — lift, unroll, check, decide. My fingers moved through the work with the efficiency of someone who has searched ruins before, who knows that patience is cheaper than haste and that the thing you're looking for is never in the first place you look.

You recovered, wiped your nose with your sleeve and joined me. You took the left side of the room. I took the right. We worked in silence punctuated only by the sound of stone on stone, paper unfurling and occasional muttered cursing when a scroll crumbled at the touch.

The light came through cracks in the walls — narrow beams that cut through the dust-thick air like blades, illuminating whatever they touched with the ruthless clarity of noon and leaving everything else in deep shadow. I held scrolls up to those beams one by one, reading through the grit and age.

Most were records. Sect business — rosters, inventories, technique manuals. The administrative skeleton of an organization that had once been alive with purpose and had died of whatever kills organizations. Time, infighting, irrelevance.

I finally found what I was looking for.
