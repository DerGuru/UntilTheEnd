Two words and a date.

I had seen them when you opened your notebook in the tea house. Some page before the map — the one the spine had exposed for the duration of a page settling against its neighbor, the involuntary disclosure that notebooks made when opened to a specific location.

Half a second. The time it took for paper to find its resting position against paper. The time it took for one page to slide past another and settle.

Half a second was enough.

The handwriting was yours. The characters were pressed harder. The strokes were shorter. The spacing between them was compressed, the words crowding each other the way words crowded when the hand writing them was not entirely under the control of the mind directing it.

Two words. Written on a page dated when we first visited the crater.

*Approached. Refused.*

Two words that had no context and required none. Two words that were a complete narrative compressed to its structural minimum. Someone came and asked, someone had declined. The grammar of an encounter reduced to its bones.

I had said nothing, because the notebook closed to the map and your finger traced our route and the conversation moved to the convergence and uninhabited mountain ranges and the question of what was drawing me south. The two words had slipped from the surface of attention into the space below where important things waited.

They waited through the tea. Through the headache. Through the wall of pressure that the word *enough* had built. Through the breaking of camp and the night march and the first hours of walking south in darkness, moving through terrain that didn't want to be found.

They waited until now.

We were walking. The trail had narrowed to a suggestion — animal tracks and the negative space between root systems. A landscape that did not accommodate human traffic. The moon was a sliver. The stars were wrong, one of them, the one I'd been watching, sitting a few degrees from where memory placed it yesterday.

I watched your back. You walked ahead of me, which was unusual — you preferred the position behind, the observer's vantage, the seat with a view of the subject. Tonight you'd moved forward without comment.

You were not scouting. You had seen my eyes move when the notebook opened. Half a second — but you knew what half a second was worth to someone like me. You knew I'd read the page. And now you couldn't walk behind me, couldn't sit in the observer's seat with the subject ahead of you, because the subject had seen something you hadn't meant to show.

The delegation had found us through their talismans, yes. Resonance collectors tuned to the anomaly's frequency, tracking the disturbances to their source. But talismans gave location, not context. For that, the Pencil had built a network. Informants. Contacts. The patient infrastructure of scholarship disguised as surveillance.

Networks operated through contacts. Contacts required approaches. Approaches required opportunities.

And the crater had been an opportunity.

I had been on the basalt, alone, attention directed downward. Someone must have felt the energy I was emanating. You had been at the rim. Alone. And a sect scout recognizes an opportunity the way a fisherman recognizes a current.

Someone had *approached* you. At the rim, while I pushed deeper, while the basalt cracked and the substrate corrected. Someone had looked at you and seen access. The soft point in the perimeter.

You had *refused*. The word that closed doors. The word that a pragmatic man used. Because the calculus didn't add up? Because the sect offered less than I provided? 

Or that was the pragmatic reading?

There were other readings.

Maybe because of loyalty, honor or the sentimental architecture of companionship.

You had told me nothing. Not at the crater, not during the weeks during the weeks after, when you held the notebook closer to the chest. Not after the delegation's visit, when the grey-robed woman had offered cooperation and the whole mechanism of their approach had become visible. Not in the tea house.

You had carried this alone — the private integrity of a choice that would never be verified because it was never shared.

I could have asked. But I said nothing.

The decision was immediate and complete. If I asked, I would make your silence visible. The observed act becoming a different act. 

No. Mine would be the silence that matched yours. The symmetry of two people not saying the same thing for different reasons. Your silence protected me from knowing I'd been vulnerable. My silence protected you from knowing I'd seen your protection.

You walked ahead. I walked behind. The trail narrowed and widened and narrowed again, the geography indifferent to the weight of what traveled through it.

The stars moved. The wrong one sat where it sat. We walked south.
