We walked back to camp in silence. I shuffled beside you in the outer robe, too large, too warm in some places and not warm enough in others, the world pressing against me from every direction with its new, exhausting detail.

The trail was different. The same trail we'd walked this morning, the same stones, the same switchbacks, the same pines leaning at the same angles. But I was walking it in a different body. The soles of my feet registered every pebble, every root, every transition from packed earth to loose scree. A sharp stone caught the arch of my left foot and I flinched — actually flinched, the involuntary reaction of a body that had discovered it could be hurt by the ground.

The air had weight. Not the metaphorical weight of atmosphere or mood but actual, physical presence — I could feel it entering my lungs, cool and dry, expanding the tissue, pressing against the inside of my ribs. Each breath was an event that carried the taste of the mountain of pine resin, stone dust and the faint sweetness of something flowering somewhere uphill that I hadn't noticed before.

It was exhilarating. It was exhausting.

A spider had built a web between two branches at head height. I stopped. The silk caught the light — individual threads, each one carrying a bead of dew, each bead refracting the sun into a spectrum large enough to see. The spider sat at the center, motionless, legs folded, waiting with the patience of things that had never been in a hurry.

I'd walked past ten thousand webs in my life. This was the first one I'd experienced.

You waited. Didn't ask why I'd stopped. Just stood there with both packs and your guilt and your patience and let me look at a spider.

We walked on. The pine needles underfoot were soft. Each one yielding under my weight with its own small give, a carpet of separate objects pretending to be a surface. The bark of the trees was rough when I brushed against it, textured, alive with the ridged architecture of years. An ant climbed one of the ridges. I watched it navigate the terrain of the trunk the way we navigated the terrain of the mountain. One obstacle at a time, with the blind commitment of something that knew where it was going and didn't need to understand why.

We arrived at the camp. The fire had gone out, so you knelt and rebuilt it. The wood caught. The warmth spread. I sat close enough that the heat reached my face and my hands. And the cold in my bones eased by a degree.

The fire was different too. Not the abstract warmth I'd known before — a state, a condition, a thing the body maintained from inside. This was directional. The side of my face toward the flames was warm. The side away from them was cold. The boundary between warm and cold ran down the center of my body like a line drawn by the fire's reach, precise, measurable, the physical truth of radiant heat.

I held my hands out. The heat reached my palms first, then the backs of my fingers, then the wrists. The progression was real. The progression had always been real. I just hadn't noticed before.

A bird landed on the pack you'd set down. Pecked at the strap. Found nothing edible and left. Its weight had been on the pack for three seconds and in those three seconds I'd heard its claws on the fabric, heard the small percussive tap of its beak against the buckle, heard the whisper of its wings as it launched. Three seconds of bird, rendered in full detail.

You sat across from me with your notebook open. You were writing — the fast and urgent handwriting at the clearing had softened. It had settled into something steadier. You wrote. Paused. Looked at me. Wrote again.

"I had to," you said. Not to me. To the page. To yourself. To whatever version of the morning you were trying to fix in ink before it became something you could live with.

"I know," I said.

You looked up. The words had landed wrong — or right. The *I know* that carried knowledge I shouldn't have had, that implied understanding of an event I couldn't remember.

"Do you —"

"No. I don't know what you did." I looked at my hands. At the robe. At the camp that looked exactly the way it had looked this morning, as if nothing had changed, as if the world hadn't cracked open and sealed itself shut while I wasn't watching. "But I know you did something. And I know it was necessary. And I think it's eating you."

You said nothing. Your pen stopped.

"And I also know that it worked. At least in part." I said. "Because I can still feel it."

You set the notebook down between us. Open. The page facing up, the fresh ink still damp. An offering, or a confession, or both.

I didn't read it. The content was dangerous. What was safe was the feeling and it didn't require reading.

Your guilt was there — would be there for for a while, the kind of guilt that doesn't dissolve but settles, becomes sediment, becomes part of the geological record of a person's internal landscape. But it was accompanied now by something else. The cautious, fragile hope of a man who had done a terrible thing and was beginning to see that the terrible thing had produced the result he had hoped for. At least in part.

The first crack that might lead somewhere.

We sat. The fire burned. The morning was still early and everything was precisely, painfully, overwhelmingly real.
