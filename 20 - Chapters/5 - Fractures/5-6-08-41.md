The fire settled into coals. I sat beside it and did not move.

The cold was still there. A persistent awareness. The temperature of the air against my skin, the temperature of the ground beneath me, the temperature gradient between side of my body towards the fire and the other side. What had once been mere awareness was now sensation.

I pulled your outer robe tighter. But it was insufficient. A mortal garment designed for mortal cold and the insufficiency of mortal solutions was a lesson I hadn't been prepared for.

Above me, the sky was clear. The afternoon light had turned the mountains sharp. Every ridge, every tree line, every shadow cut with the precision of things seen at altitude where the air is thin and offers nothing between the eye and what it sees.

The feeling pulsed.

There. Behind the sternum. In the space where anatomists put the heart and poets put the soul and I put the thing that had no name.

I tried to think about it.

A thought began to form, the way a sentence begins. Subject verb object, the standard architecture of comprehension. *I felt something when—*

Gone. Quick, efficient, the seamless intervention of the wall that had been running this pattern long enough to recognize threats by their opening syllables. The thought was taken before it could complete and in its place was the gap, the familiar absence, the smooth surface where content had been.

I tried again.

*During the—*

Gone.

*What you—*

Gone.

The wall was thorough. The wall was fast. The wall had no interest in letting me reconstruct what had happened through indirect approaches, circuitous logic, or the patient accumulation of fragments.

So I stopped trying to think.

I sat there, looking at the mountains, feeling the cold and instead of reaching for the feeling with my mind, I reached for it with the thing that wasn't my mind. The thing that lived in the chest. The thing that existed below thought and above reflex.

And the feeling responded.

It resonated, vibrated at the same frequency. It confirmed that it was there, that it was real. That it was mine.

Ancient.

The word arrived not as a thought but as a quality. Much like you perceive the age of a mountain, not through calculation but through impression. The feeling was old. The kind of old that doesn't have a number, the kind of old that predates the practice of counting.

Lonely.

Also a quality. Also not a thought. The feeling carried loneliness as stone carries cold. Inherently, structurally, as a property of its composition rather than a condition of its circumstances.

Certain.

This was the strongest quality. The certainty of the feeling — its absolute, unwavering confidence in its own existence — was what made it different from every other feeling I'd ever had. Emotions are uncertain. Emotions shift, flex, respond to circumstance, change with time and context and the accumulation of new information. This feeling didn't shift. This feeling was as fixed as any law I'd encountered. As gravity, as causality, as the principle that actions have consequences and consequences have weight.

I had always been here.

Another quality. An aspect of the feeling that arrived without words and existed without them and would continue existing if I never found words for it.

I put my hand on my chest. The heartbeat was there, steady, reliable, the mechanical rhythm of a muscle doing its involuntary work. Under the heartbeat, the feeling. Under the feeling, something deeper, something that I could spend years trying to reach, decades trying to understand and centuries learning to live with.

But right now it was enough to know it was there.

*Enough.*

The word had found me before. But this time it was affirmative.

Enough as in sufficient. Enough as in this will do. The feeling — nameless, formless and ancient as stone — was present and its presence was a thread, which could be followed. And following is forward motion and this is the direction I want to go.

The fire crackled. A coal shifted, sending a small cascade of sparks upward, bright, brief, the miniature display of combustion releasing its last energy. I watched the sparks rise and die and become invisible against the afternoon light.

Your breathing changed. Slightly deeper. Slightly less controlled. The real sleep of a body that had held tension for too long and had finally, reluctantly, surrendered to its demand for sleep. 

You had hurt me on purpose. You admitted as much. Yet I had forgiven you without knowing what I was forgiving. Still, the forgiveness was valid because it was based on trust rather than information. The trust, that you wouldn't have done it, if you had seen another way. And trust was one of the things the wall couldn't erase because trust wasn't a thought. 

Trust was a feeling.

The afternoon continued. The coals glowed. The mountains stood. The cold pressed through the robe, through cloth, through every thin barrier between body and world, and it reached me. Really reached me. The way cold reaches someone who has spent her life insulated from sensation and is now, for reasons she can't remember, uninsulated.

My hand stayed on my chest. The heartbeat. The feeling. The nameless frequency that hummed below awareness and above silence.

The afternoon softened. The shadows lengthened across the camp, across your sleeping face, across the coals that glowed quieter now. The mountains lost their sharp edges, their ridges blurring into the sky as the light drained from them in stages — gold, then copper, then the deep blue-grey of stone surrendering to evening.

The first star appeared. Then another. Then enough to stop counting.

They were right. I don't know how else to say it. They were where they were supposed to be, doing what they were supposed to do, burning with the patient indifference of things that had never needed permission to exist. They were right the way the stream had been right, the way the cold was right, the way the feeling behind my sternum was right — not good, not comforting, but correct. Present. Accounted for.

Quiet settled over the camp. Not silence — the mountain was never silent, not with the stream and the wind and the small sounds of cooling earth and settling wood. But a kind of agreement. The forest and the slope and the sky all arriving at the same volume, the same register, the same willingness to simply be there without demanding anything.

The moon rose over the ridge. Slow, certain, the way large things move — without effort, without announcement, just the gradual occupation of space that had been waiting. It cleared the treeline and the camp turned silver. Your bedroll. The pot by the woodpile. My hands on my chest. All of it washed in the colourless light of something borrowed from a star that was too far away to care.

It would be enough for now. For whatever came next. For the next morning, the next trail, the next question that I would forget or the next answer that would be taken.

The feeling would remain. The content would go. And the gap between knowing and feeling, which had been a prison, was becoming, slowly, tentatively, with the fragile momentum of something that might still be stopped —

larger.

I pulled the robe around me and looked at the fire. The coals were dim — patient, but fading. By morning they'd be ash.

I got up. Took two of the branches I'd gathered and laid them across the coals. Carefully angled so the air could reach them. The bark caught, then the wood beneath and the fire climbed back from ember to flame, small but definite, the kind of fire that would last until dawn if nothing disturbed it.

I lay down. The ground was hard, cold and real beneath my back. Your breathing was steady beside me. The moonlight lay across the camp like a second blanket, thinner, colder, but present.

I closed my eyes. The world didn't stop — the stream, the birds, the wind, the cold, the feeling — but it softened. The volume turned down by one degree. Then another.

Enough.
