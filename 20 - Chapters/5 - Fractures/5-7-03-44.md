I was walking and thinking — or trying to think. Pressing against the crack constantly now, the habitual exploration of the loose tooth, the reflexive check on whether the change was continuing. I pressed and the crack gave slightly and I leaned into the give, and—

Before the memory could form. Before the wall could activate. Before the guardian could identify the incoming thought and mark it for deletion. 

The headache changed. The hot wire behind my eyes that had been one note for weeks suddenly slid half a step to the side. Sharp, sudden, located precisely behind my left eye with the intensity of something aimed rather than general.

I stopped walking, pressed my hand to my face and the thought retreated. Untaken, of its own accord. As if the thought itself had reconsidered, had evaluated the approach, decided that this direction was not viable and had withdrawn to wherever thoughts go when they choose not to be thought.

Warning rather than deletion.

I'd felt this for months now, since the headaches had started punishing the thought before I could think it. I'd read it as escalation. As the defense getting smarter, crueler, tightening the perimeter. A strike.

But this didn't feel like a strike. This felt like someone stepping into a doorway. Blocking the path not with force, but with presence. Like A warning. The pointed, precisely located communication of something that didn't want me to remember and was asking me not to try.

Asking.

The word sat wrong somehow. Asking implied someone who asked. Asking implied preferences, strategies and the capacity to evaluate approaches in order to select the one most likely to achieve the goals. The wall hadn't asked, didn't ask, wouldn't ask. The wall erased. It was a reflex, a force, an automatic process that operated on rules.

But reflexes don't change strategy — yet something did.

The thought arrived and was not taken. It sat in my consciousness unmolested. Something inside me was concerned about me.

I pressed against the crack again. Experimentally. Gently. Like pressing a loose tooth, just enough to feel the movement, not enough to force the extraction.

The headache came. Behind the left eye. Sharp, exact and immediate.

I stopped pressing. The headache receded.

*Don't go there.* Got it.

It did not feel hostile or aggressive. It was almost — and the almost was the part that unsettled me — tender. The way you'd stop a child from touching a hot surface. With the urgent, loving refusal of someone who knows the consequence better than the person they're refusing.

"Something wrong?" you asked. You'd noticed the hand on my face. The trained observer, reading my body language the way you read texts. For meaning, for pattern, for the information beneath the surface.

"Nothing. Just the headache."

You didn't believe me. You took out your notebook and paused, the new hesitation, the request for consent that lived in the pause. Then opened it. Wrote.

I didn't tell you what I'd felt. The wall, for once, wasn't the reason. The thing I'd felt was private. It was mine. It was the first faint indication that whatever lived behind the crack wasn't a jailer but a guardian. It wasn't a censor but a caretaker. And it wasn't an enemy but a part of me that had been doing the only thing it knew how to do. 

Protecting.

Badly, perhaps. Bluntly. With the clumsy, heavy-handed, everything-must-go approach of a guardian that had one tool and used it on everything. But protecting nevertheless.

I walked. The headache faded. The crack pulsed. The fox-spirit padded behind us, amber-eyed, patient.

The trail opened into a clearing and the light hit full and bright and I braced for the impact. It came a bit softer than yesterday. The bird calls were still individual, still carrying pitch and rhythm and direction, but the sharpness had dulled to something almost bearable. My body was learning. The senses were still too much, still too detailed, but the too-much had shifted from drowning to wading. Progress, measured in the distance between unbearable and merely exhausting.

And somewhere beneath the wall, beneath the guardian, beneath the architecture of self-protection that had been my invisible companion since before I'd known it existed — something felt my recognition and responded.

A slight easing of the headache. A softening. As if something that had been bracing for a fight had realized the fight wasn't coming.

Not today. But I'd be back.
