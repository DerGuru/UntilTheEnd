The shrine sat in a fold of the mountain like something the land had tried to swallow and given up on. Half the roof was gone. Vines choked whatever was left, thick as rope, cracking through stone that had once been carved with care. The path leading up to it had no path anymore — just a suggestion where mud was thinner between the trees.

You stopped at the base and looked at your shoes.

"These were new," you said.

I didn't answer. I was already climbing.

The air changed the moment I crossed the threshold. Not the temperature — something else. A pressure behind the ears that had nothing to do with altitude. The qi here moved wrong. Not stagnant. Not absent. Skewed. Like water running uphill in a stream that looked perfectly still.

I knelt by the entrance wall and pressed my palm flat against the stone. The surface was cold. Not mountain-cold, which makes sense in autumn. Metal-cold, the kind that drinks warmth out of your skin and gives nothing back. Old fittings lined the doorframe, brackets for a gate long gone and every one of them felt like midwinter.

Chalk dust. I tasted it before I saw it — a grit between my teeth, fine and persistent, the residue of walls that had been grinding themselves to powder for centuries. I spat. It didn't help.

And underneath it all, faint as a thought you can't quite hold: incense. Musty. Sweet as rot can be sweet if you're far enough from it. No source. No burner, no ash, no stain on the stone. Ghost-smell. The kind that makes you turn your head and find nothing.

I wiped dust from the nearest relief panel with the heel of my hand. Slow, methodical strokes. Moss came away in clumps, revealing carved lines beneath. Geometric, precise, the work of someone who had understood symmetry not as decoration but as language.

"You could use a brush," you said from behind me. You'd made it up, too. Your breathing was slightly uneven, which you covered by adjusting your collar.

"Could." I kept scraping. Fingernails into grooves. The stone spoke through texture. Edges worn smooth here, still sharp there. Whoever had carved this had believed in what they were doing. You can always tell. Conviction leaves deeper marks than skill.

You wandered the perimeter while I worked. I heard you — boots on broken tile, the careful placement of someone testing each step before committing weight. You found a beam that had fallen at an angle and rested against the far wall. You must have leaned on it, because the wood groaned.

"Careful." I chided. "This place is old."

"I'm being careful."

"The ceiling over you is held up by optimism."

You moved.

I worked the wall for the better part of an hour. Layer by layer. Dust, lichen, moss, more dust. The carvings underneath told me nothing I could read yet, not because the symbols were foreign, but because too many were missing. Time had eaten them the way time eats everything. Patiently, starting at the edges.

But the qi. The qi was interesting.

It flowed toward me. Not along the natural channels in the stone — those ran east, following the mountain's spine, as water follows gravity. This undercurrent pulled south. Toward me. When I moved to the next panel, it adjusted. When I stepped outside to check the foundation, it followed.

That wasn't normal. Qi doesn't track people. Qi settles, pools, flows. It does what geography and spiritual density tell it to do. It doesn't decide.

It had an old feeling. Older than the shrine. The qi predated the stone it moved through, by centuries at least. Whatever had settled here had done so long before anyone thought to build walls around it.

I said nothing.

By noon, you had managed to cut your hand on a splintered beam. Not badly. A shallow slice across the back of your left hand, the kind that bleeds more than it should because the skin there is thin. 

I looked at the cut. "You should wrap it."

I did. Clean cloth from your pack, wound tight, three loops and a tuck. I didn't look at your face while I did it. My hands knew what to do — they'd been doing it for longer than this shrine had existed, longer than the mountain it sat on had been called by its current name. The wrapping was precise, mechanical, the kind of care that has nothing personal in it.

You studied your bandaged hand the way someone studies a sentence that almost makes sense. 

"Thank you," you said.

I was already back at the wall.

The afternoon brought wind. It came from the wrong direction — northwest instead of the usual east — and carried a smell of wet pine that didn't match the dry season. I noted it. Filed it away. Added it to the list that had started forming three days ago, when a riverbed we'd crossed had been two meters wider than my memory insisted it should be.

I wasn't keeping the list on paper. Not yet. Lists on paper can be found.

By evening, I'd cleared four panels. The carvings showed cultivation forms: Body positions, qi flow indicators, the visual shorthand that sects used before written manuals became standard. Old work. Pre-Consolidated Era, probably. The stone was the right age.

You built a fire in what might have been the shrine's inner courtyard. You set a pot over the flames and started cutting something that might have been a root vegetable into shapes that might have been intentional.

"Anything?" you asked.

"Nothing conclusive."

That was a lie. Or rather, it was the kind of truth that carries a lie inside it, like a shell carrying the sea. There was plenty. The qi flow was anomalous. The wind was wrong. The carvings felt almost recognizable. Not familiar, exactly. But not unfamiliar either. Like a room you've never visited that has furniture arranged just as you would arrange it.

I sat across the fire from you and watched the flames find their rhythm. The chalk dust had settled into the lines of my palms. The cold metal taste lingered at the back of my throat.

You slid a bowl toward me. I ignored it for a while. Then I ate.

You watched me eat the way a scholar watches an experiment — noting, not commenting. Your notebook sat by your knee, closed.

"You're different," you said.

"How."

"In the Echoes — in the crater months. You were closed. Sealed. Like a door someone had nailed shut." You paused. Chose your words with the care of someone defusing something fragile. "Now you're open. But it's worse."

I looked at you.

"You're not grieving anymore," you said. "You're hunting."

I set down the bowl.

The fire popped. A spark rose and died. Somewhere in the dark beyond the broken walls, an owl called, the two-note cry of a species that shouldn't have been this far south.

I added it to the list.
