When I came back from the river, you were cooking.

You looked up and did a quick but thorough assessment. The automatic scan of a man who had learned to read the difference between her-after-something-happened and her-after-nothing-happened. You looked at my face. My eyes. The set of my jaw. How I moved, which was different, though I didn't realize it yet, something in my posture having shifted during the minutes at the water, something in the way I carried weight having redistributed.

"Everything okay?" you asked.

"Yes."

You knew it was a lie. I saw the recognition. the brief flicker of analysis, the categorization of my response as incomplete and the decision to accept it anyway. You turned back to the cooking. Rice and something green. A plant you'd found near the river. Edible. The kind of foraging discovery that pleased you because it represented a welcome change in the routine of the practical.

I sat by the fire. The warmth was good. Real warmth, fire warmth, the kind that came from outside and had to be received. The cold in my bones eased. The evening chill retreated from my extremities in the slow, gradual yielding of temperature to temperature.

Xiǎohú appeared from wherever she'd been. She padded straight to me, pressed her flank against my shin and stayed. Warm. The particular warmth of a living body that has decided to be near yours.

I looked down and our looks met. Her amber eyes were calm. No evaluation, no assessment — just presence. The simple, uncomplicatable presence of an animal that has identified a person who needs company and has provided it without being asked.

I put my hand on her back. The fur was coarse on top, soft beneath, the dual-layer insulation of a creature built for mountain cold. She didn't flinch. Didn't move. Leaned into the touch, fractionally, the way animals lean when the contact is welcome.

The sensation was overwhelming in the way everything was overwhelming now, the texture of fur against palm, the warmth of the body beneath, the subtle rise and fall of breathing. But it was also simple. Hand on fur. Warmth against warmth. The oldest comfort.

The feeling was still there. Changed now, or rather, my relationship to it had changed. The nameless frequency, the bass note vibration that the deletion couldn't reach, was the same. But I heard it differently. Before the river, the feeling had been mysterious. Alien. Something present in me that I couldn't identify, couldn't categorize, couldn't file under any heading that my experience provided.

Now it was familiar.

Still beyond understanding. I still couldn't name or define it, I still couldn't describe it in terms that would survive the wall's scrutiny. But it felt familiar the way a voice is familiar when you hear it in a crowd — you don't know who's speaking, can't see their face, can't place them in your history, but you know you've heard them before. You know they belong to your life.

The feeling belonged to my life. Not as an addition — not something that had arrived and attached itself. It had been there. Always. The way a sound you've been hearing for so long you stopped noticing it has been there, and then one day you hear it again and wonder how you ever didn't.

You handed me a bowl. Rice with the green plant, which you'd wilted with steam and seasoned with the salt from the market. Simple. Good. The flavors were distinct. Individual compounds registering on my tongue, the bitterness of the green offset by the starch of the rice offset by the mineral sharpness of the salt. Every bite was an event. Every bite was too much information, taken in by a palate that was still adjusting to its new sensitivity.

I ate anyway. I was getting better and better at the too-much. Learning to let the input arrive without resisting it, the way a river learns the shape of new stones, not by fighting them but by flowing around them until the flow itself adjusts.

"I saw something at the river," I said.

You looked up. Careful. The new carefulness, the post-question carefulness that weighed every interaction against the cost of the last one.

"What?"

I considered. The wall was quiet. The guardian was quiet. Whatever watchfulness governed my access to truth was apparently uninterested in what I'd seen at the river, either because the content was below its threshold or because the content was too foreign for its classifications. But maybe whatever I'd seen was sanctioned, permitted, allowed.

"My reflection," I said. "It — did something."

You waited. Notebook still closed. Hands still.

"It smiled before I did."

You weighed this. The silence was the silence of deep thought, the quiet of a mind turning over implications, sifting through what you knew, tracing the consequences that any new revelation produces when added to a picture already heavy with unknowns.

"Half a second. Maybe less. It was smiling before I started to smile."

"And you weren't scared?"

"This time — no. It felt—" I searched. The word was there, somewhere, hiding behind inadequacy and imprecision. The fundamental insufficiency of language when applied to experiences that language wasn't designed for.

"Warm." I paused. "Like someone was glad to see me."

You were quiet for a long time. The fire crackled. The green plant wilted further in the residual steam. Xiǎohú lay at my feet, amber eyes closed.

"You're not going to write that down?" I asked.

You looked at me.

"No," you said.

"Why not?"

"Because some things aren't documentation. Some things are—" You paused. You pausing mid-sentence was rare enough to constitute an event. "Yours."

We went back to eating. The fire burned. The river ran a few steps away, the constant murmur of water over stone that had been the background of this place since before we'd arrived and would continue being the background after we left.

I didn't tell you all of it. Some things belonged only to me.

The stars appeared. The cold settled in and I pulled the robe tighter and felt the feeling pulse and let it. I didn't press against the crack and didn't try to think about what had happened or what it meant or what would come next.

I sat by the fire and was warm and cold at the same time.

The fire settled. A log broke in half. Sparks flew up and disappeared into the night. The crack pulsed. The feeling hummed. The world was still loud and sharp and full of details that I was still learning to process.

The river's voice was there, the fire's voice was there, the presence of the fox-spirit was there, the warmth in my chest was there.
