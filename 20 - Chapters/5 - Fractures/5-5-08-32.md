The sunrise came from the east, as sunrises do, as this one always had. You were already awake, already moving, already performing the routine of breaking camp that had become ritual. I was sitting with my back against a boulder, pressing my thumbs into my temples, waiting for the headache to decide whether today would be a burning day or a throbbing day.

The light hit the landscape. The valley below us opened up in the way that mountain valleys do when viewed from altitude. The slow revelation of depth and distance and the layered greens of forest meeting the browns of exposed rock meeting the blues of sky.

Except the landscape wasn't right.

The mountain on the opposite side of the valley — the one that had been there yesterday, whole and unremarkable — was missing a piece. Even from this distance I could see it. A section of the western flank, roughly three hundred meters wide, simply absent. The silhouette was wrong. The shape that my eyes expected, the shape that had been there every time I'd looked east, had a gap in it.

"Do you see that?" I said.

You were already looking. Your notebook was open. "The western flank. Something's missing."

The word *missing* might be wrong. From here, across the valley, the mountain didn't look damaged. It looked complete — just complete in a different shape than it had been yesterday. As if the silhouette had always looked like this.

"We need to get closer," you said.

We descended. The trail into the valley was steep and switchbacked, the kind of descent that punishes knees and rewards patience. The headache pulsed with every step. The stone shifted once under my foot — a loose rock on solid ground that shouldn't have moved — and I caught myself and kept walking.

At the valley floor we crossed a shallow stream. When we crossed the cold water on stones. We started up the opposite slope. The ascent took hours. The terrain was dense — undergrowth, loose scree, the kind of mountain face that exists to be climbed past rather than through.

By early afternoon, we reached the flank.

The edge was sharper than distance had suggested. The mountain simply stopped. One step landed on solid rock and soil. The normal geology of a mountainside that had been there for millennia. The next step would have been grass. The cut was horizontal, clean, as if someone had drawn a line and everything above had ceased to exist.

Grass grew to the plateau. Not the raw, disturbed grass of a fresh scar, but established grass, the kind that takes months to root and years to thicken, growing right up to the line where we stood. There were wildflowers. Yellow ones. Growing along the meadow in the casual abundance of things that had always been there. Also butterflies. Moving between the flowers with the lazy purposelessness of insects that exist in settled places — that have no reason to be anywhere other than where they are.

The mountain hadn't been destroyed. The mountain had never been. Forgotten.

You knelt at the edge. Touched the grass. Pulled a blade and examined it, the root system intact and healthy, weeks of growth at minimum. You held it between your fingers and your face had the expression of a man whose categories were failing.

"This grass didn't grow overnight," you said. "But the mountain was there yesterday."

You opened your notebook and began to sketch the outline. The mountain's shape, the missing section, the smooth boundary where absence began. Annotated the flowers. The grass depth. The butterflies. Your hand moved with the mechanical persistence of someone who had decided that documentation is the only response that doesn't involve screaming.

Evidence of nothing. Evidence of nothing having always been nothing.

The field had done this. The mountain had still been whole yesterday. But whatever the field had set in motion was still propagating. Still eating. The collapse had released something that hadn't finished working, and overnight, while we slept, it had erased a portion of the mountain the way my mind erased memories. Completely, seamlessly, without leaving evidence that anything had been there.

"Three hundred meters across," you said, measuring with your eye. "The depth —" You leaned forward, carefully, and looked along the face. "At least two hundred meters thick at this elevation."

Hundreds of thousands of tons of rock. Of stone and mineral and the compressed history of geological time. No debris in the valley. No dust in the air. No indication that the material existed anywhere else.

Just gone. As if it had never been.

"Eight minutes," you said. You were staring at the edge. "The field ran for eight minutes. And it had the power to conserve everything inside it. Where did it get the energy?"

I didn't answer.

You closed your notebook. Opened it again. Closed it. "What if the field was fueled by *this*." You gestured at the absence. "Matter into energy. The mountain consumed to keep the field running."

You said it like a question. Like something you wanted me to disprove.

I couldn't.

"It didn't break the mountain. It ate it. And what's left —" You looked at the grass growing where stone should be. "It removed it. The way —"

You stopped.

"Say it."

"The way you forget things." You looked at me. "Wholesale. Complete. With no trace that there was anything to forget."

My hands were cold. Not from the altitude. That was the cruelest part. The sky was clear, the light golden, the air cool and clean and smelling of pine resin and the mineral sharpness of high altitude. Birds sang. Wind moved through the pines. Everything was exactly as it should be.

Except for the hole in the mountain where the mountain should have been.

I sat down at the edge. The grass was soft and warm under my hands. The same grass that couldn't possibly be here, growing in soil that couldn't possibly exist, rooted in a reality that had been rewritten while I slept.

A stone shifted beneath my foot. A small thing, a loose stone on a slope, the kind that happens a thousand times a day on any mountain. My foot slipped.

But the stone shouldn't have shifted. The ground had been solid.

Your flask. A thin film of moisture seeping through the seam where the leather was stitched. Not much. A few drops. The flask was new, well-made, the kind of quality equipment that doesn't leak unless it's been damaged.

It hadn't been damaged.

It was just leaking. For no reason.

A loose stone that shouldn't have shifted. A flask that shouldn't have leaked.

I'd spent something enormous. Hundreds of thousands of tons of reality, erased, consumed, burned as fuel for something I hadn't asked for.

The bill was arriving. In small installments. A loose stone. A leaking flask. The trivial, accumulating math of things going slightly wrong.

"Let's move on," I said.

You looked at the edge one more time. The flowers. The butterflies that moved between them with the serene indifference of creatures that existed in a world where mountains had always looked like this.

You turned and we went.
