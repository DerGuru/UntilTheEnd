The truth came in. Everything, at once, from every direction, the total content of something that had been held back pouring through a gap that was too small for what it carried but still managed to pass.

I was—

I was once—

I was always—

I was here before—

I—

Five echoes. The same beginning, each one carrying a different ending that arrived too fast to read, too dense to separate, stacking on top of each other like pages being turned before the words could be absorbed. Additive. Each echo louder than the last, each one carrying more, each one filling space that the previous one had opened.

A place. A place that didn't exist. Not here, not in any geography I'd seen or mapped or walked through. A place that existed in a category I didn't have vocabulary for. Not a memory. Not a dream. A FACT. A place as real as mathematics, as real as the rules that governed the universe, needing neither visit nor verification.

A name. A name I'd never heard, spoken in a voice I'd never heard, in a language I'd never learned. But recognizable. As recognizable as your own name spoken by a stranger. Instant, automatic, bone-level recognition of something that belongs to you regardless of who says it.

And between the echoes — fast, almost lost in the flood — a flash of something specific. Scrolls. The ones I'd read in the library and dozens more, hundreds more. Written in hands that weren't mine, in scripts from eras I remembered but hadn't known I'd been *recorded* during, sealed in ruins I'd walked past without knowing what was inside. And beside each scroll, always, a presence. A face I couldn't see. Someone sitting close, watching, writing it down. Someone who kept the record I couldn't keep for myself.

The flash passed. Too fast to parse. Too fast to keep.

And a feeling. Beyond emotion, beyond the surface-level affect that made rain, split trees or moved mountains. Something deeper. Something structural. Something that existed at the foundation of whatever I was, in the bedrock below personality and memory and experience, in the place where being myself was rooted.

Ancient. The word is insufficient but it's the closest language gets.

I had always been here. 

I had always been. 

Always. Always. 

*Always.*

I saw all of this in that one moment, when crack appeared and the truth pushed through. The full truth, compressed into a window of time too small for comprehension, delivered to a consciousness too limited for the delivery.

Something happened to my eyes. A sting. Sharp, sudden, behind both eyes at once, as if someone had pressed needles into the back of my skull from the inside. I blinked — and the world shifted.

I was still standing in the clearing. Still facing you. But I wasn't looking through my eyes anymore. I was looking through something else's eyes, something that used my face the way a light uses a lantern, present behind the structure, visible through the openings, but not the structure itself.

And it saw you.

Not the way I saw you. Not the way a person sees another person — partial, filtered, shaped by context and history and the limitations of a mind that can only hold so much at once. This seeing was total. It saw every part of you. Every history. Every decision that had brought you to this clearing to stand five meters from something that wore a human shape and was, for one second, not wearing it very well.

I couldn't move. The thing looking through my eyes was so complete, so absolutely present in its attention that I was a passenger in my own body, watching it watch you with a focus that made everything I'd ever called *seeing* feel like squinting through fog.

One moment of eternity, as you described it later to me.

Then—

Everything was gone. The guardian, the wall — whatever it was — performing its most extreme operation. The crack had opened and the truth had come through and the truth was too much and the wall had done the only thing it could do.

It had erased the moment.

Erased. Completely, totally, absolutely. As if the last few seconds had been cut from a scroll and the edges on either side had been pressed together.

I looked down at my hands. At the grass. At you. Your face was wrong. Cracked. As if something behind it had broken and the face was holding the pieces together through will alone. "Weren't you going to ask me something?"

Your mouth opened. Closed.

"Then ask," I said.

Something moved across your face. Not pain. Defeat. The slow collapse of a man who had just heard the proof that everything he'd done had been undone. Your jaw loosened. Your shoulders dropped. You said nothing.

"What happened?" I asked.

The gap was total. I remembered walking here. We had a conversation. But I didn't remember everything after. How long have we been here? Couldn't be too long, because the sun was still in about the same place. But it could have been seconds. It could not have been more than an hour. Everything in between was possible.

And the in-between? Gone. All of it taken, erased and locked into the void where the guardian kept the things it decided I couldn't have.

But — there was a but. And the but was everything.

Somewhere deep, below the gap, in the place where the wall couldn't reach because the wall was built to block thoughts and this wasn't a thought, something remained.

A feeling.

Not quite an emotion. Not a mood or affect. Something more fundamental. A frequency. A resonance. A vibration in the depths of my being that had been activated during the moment of seeing and had not been deactivated when it was taken away.

I couldn't name it. I couldn't describe it. I couldn't even think clearly about it. Every time I tried, the thought dissolved, the wall intervened, the guardian did its work. But I could feel it. The way you feel a bass note in your chest, below the range of hearing, too low for ears, present only as vibration, as pressure, as the raw awareness that something is happening on a frequency you can receive but cannot decode.

I put my hand on my chest. The feeling was there. Under the ribs. Behind the sternum. In the space where the heart is and the lungs are and the fundamental truth of being alive is housed.

Something real. Something old. Something mine.

I didn't know what it was. I didn't know what it meant. And I didn't know what had caused it or why it was there or how long it would last.

But it was there.

And for the first time since the forgetting had begun. Since the wall had started building and the guardian had started taking. Since my own mind had started editing itself with the precision of something that knew exactly what it was doing. For the first time, something had survived.

Neither a thought nor a memory nor information.

Just a feeling.

But it was everything to me.
