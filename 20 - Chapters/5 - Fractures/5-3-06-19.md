We moved faster. Not running. Running implies being chased and we weren't — not yet, not exactly. But the pace changed. We covered ground with the restless efficiency of people who know that stillness has become more dangerous than motion, that every day spent in one place gives the world another day to adjust around them and that eventually the adjustments would bring someone to the door.

Three days of hard walking. South and west, into hill country that folded in on itself like crumpled paper, the kind of terrain where sight lines are short and trails dead-end against cliff faces and the geography itself seems designed for people who don't want to be found.

Good country for hiding. Bad country for research.

I hadn't touched a ruin in days. Days without a new site, without new findings, without the familiar tension that came from pressing my hands against old stone and feeling the foundation qi respond with the warmth of recognition.

The world didn't stop adjusting. That was the worst part. Even without my probing, without my investigation, reality continued its subtle, patient editorial work. Small things. Always small things. A tree species at the wrong altitude. A stream flowing a few degrees off the expected heading. A star that I was increasingly uncertain about, its position differing from my memory by an amount that might have been precession and might have been something else.

The ache continued. They came in patterns now. Neither random nor just in response to certain thoughts, but rhythmic, like a tide. Pain in the mornings. Pain before sleep. Persistent low-grade pressure that becomes part of your landscape, so constant that you stop noticing it and start building around it instead.

I rubbed my temples. You noticed. You always noticed.

"How long?" you asked.

"Since the library."

"Every day?"

"Most days."

"Have they changed?"

I considered. The episodes had, in fact, changed. Not in intensity but in character. The early ones had been sharp, reactive, the kind that came when my thinking approached dangerous territory and served as a clear, functional warning: *stop.* The current ones were different. Duller. Less targeted. More like a generalized weight behind the eyes, as if something inside my skull was taking up more space than it used to and the container was protesting.

"They're broader," I said. "Less exact."

"That means they're either getting worse or you're getting used to them."

"Or both."

We reached a small town at the edge of the hill country. The kind of place that exists for a single purpose — a river crossing, a mining depot or a market hub — and has no ambition beyond fulfilling that purpose. This one was a crossroads. North-south and east-west routes intersecting at a bridge over a narrow but forceful river. The buildings were practical, the people were practical and the tea house was two rooms with a sagging roof and tea that wasn't terrible.

I sat by the window while you ordered. Outside, the town moved through its late-afternoon routine. The merchants packing up stalls, children running in the diminishing light, a sect courier passing through at speed with dispatches that probably contained the same frightened reports we'd been hearing for weeks.

Normal life. The kind that continues regardless of what's happening to the qi currents or the spiritual balance or the academic machinations of a man called the Pencil. Normal life doesn't care about anomalies. Normal life cares about food, a roof and a place to sleep.

I watched the children play.

"You're staring at the children," you said.

"Am I?"

"Three minutes."

I hadn't been aware of the duration. The children were playing a game that involved kicking a ball between them with more enthusiasm than skill. They shouted. They laughed. They fell down and got up with the elastic resilience of bodies that haven't yet learned they're supposed to be careful.

"They're loud," I said.

"They're children."

"Loud children."

You set a cup of tea in front of me. It was cracked, a hairline fracture running from the rim to about halfway down, barely visible, an imperfection that a good ceramicist would have discarded and this tea house had decided was fine.

I traced the crack with my finger. Barely there, but once you recognized it, you couldn't not see it. The whole cup, its shape, its weight, its capacity to hold liquid, was defined by this fracture. Not broken. Not whole. Cracked.

The metaphor was too obvious. I drank the tea instead of thinking about it.

"The talismans tracked us once," you said. "They'll do it again. Moving faster doesn't help. It may have bought some time, but that is all it did."

I looked at you over the rim of the cracked cup.

"So, what do you suggest?"

"Stop running. Start planning." You opened your notebook. I recognized the page. Your anomaly map, a hand-drawn chart of our route with numbered markers at each documented anomaly site. Lines connected the markers. The pattern was visible even at arm's length. A zigzag — south- east, then south-west — but narrowing. Each leg shorter than the last, the deviations shrinking, the whole route funneling toward a point like a needle finding its thread.

"We're converging," you said. "Look."

I looked. You were right. The route I'd been following — the instinct that said *south, go south, the next site is south* — wasn't random. It was a pattern. Narrowing. Each zigzag tighter than the one before, the east-west swings compressing. Almost like a funnel.

I hadn't noticed. I, who noticed everything, wrong paths, wrong winds, wrong stars, hadn't noticed that my own trajectory was closing in on something. That I was being drawn. That the zigzag had a destination.

The headache pulsed. Once. Warning.

"Where does it converge?" I asked.

You traced the lines with your finger, extending the pattern beyond the last marked point. Your finger moved south, the zigzag flattening, narrowing, until it came to rest on a spot you'd marked with a small X.

"Here," you said. "About four days from now, at the current pace."

"What's there?"

"According to the maps? Nothing. Mountain range. Uninhabited. No sects, no villages, no recorded structures."

"That holds true also according to my memory."

You looked up.

I stared at the X on your map. The spot meant nothing visually. But something in my chest tightened when I looked at it. Something deep. Something that felt like recognition dressed in a coat it hadn't worn before.

"But I've been there," I said.

"When?"

"I don't remember."

You waited.

"But I've been there and I don't remember what's there, which is— " The pressure surged. Not a pulse. A wall. Solid, sudden, the worst it had been yet. A crushing weight that turned the light painful and the sound of children playing into a razor against the inside of my skull.

I pressed my hands against my temples. Closed my eyes. The world turned red behind my eyelids.

*Enough.*

The thought wasn't mine. Or rather it was mine, but from somewhere below me, below thinking, below deciding. A reflex. A ward. The same voice that said *Not now* and *Keep going,* the part of me that decided what I was allowed to think and what was sealed behind doors I couldn't find handles to.

*Enough.*

The pressure eased. The red faded. The children's voices returned to normal.

I opened my eyes. You were watching me with the expression of someone who has seen confirmation of something you already suspected and isn't sure whether to be relieved or afraid.

"The headaches are connected," you said. Not a question.

"They're a symptom."

"Of what?"

Of whatever closes the doors. Of whatever keeps the pages folded. Of whatever stopped the researchers mid-sentence, had erased their thoughts and left their work unfinished. Their lives, their curiosities, their brilliance, their stubborn human need to understand ground to nothing by a force that lived inside their cultivators and me. That did not allow them to see what it was.

"Of me and those cultivators, we found records of." I said.

You closed the notebook. Finished your tea and stood.

"Four days," you said. "We have four days to decide whether we walk to whatever's at the end of that line or turn around."

I looked out the window. The children had stopped playing. The ball sat in the dust, abandoned, perfectly still. Beyond the town, the hills rose in layers of green and the darkening blue of approaching dusk.

"We don't turn around," I said.

You nodded. As if you'd known. As if the question had been a formality, a courtesy extended to the possibility of caution before caution was rejected.

We left the tea house. The cracked cup sat on the table, empty, its fracture catching the last light.
