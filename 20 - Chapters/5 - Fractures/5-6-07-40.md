You were still writing when I stood up. My legs protested — stiff from sitting, the soles of my feet tender from the walk. The robe shifted around me, the hem dragging against the ground.

"I'll get some more firewood," I said.

You looked up. Your pen stopped mid-stroke. For a moment I thought you'd argue — insist on coming, or insist I stay, or find some reason to keep me within arm's reach where your guilt could monitor its handiwork. But you just nodded and went back to writing.

The forest around camp was familiar. I'd walked through it for days without registering it. Now it registered itself. The undergrowth was dense in patches, following some logic of light and soil that I'd never paid attention to. Mushrooms on a fallen trunk — small, pale, clustered in a logic of their own. A beetle crossing a patch of moss. The moss itself, damp, spongy, yielding under my feet in a way that was so different from dirt or stone or pine needles that my brain filed it as a separate category of ground.

I picked up branches as I found them. Dry, light, the bark peeling in strips. Each one had a weight, a texture, a temperature and a smell. And each of those properties arrived simultaneously, which was going to be a problem.

At first it was manageable. Trees. Ground. Air. Then a gust of wind brought the smell of something — resin, earth, water, the faint sweetness from uphill. The sun moved behind a cloud and the light changed. A bird called from somewhere to the left and the branch in my hand was rough under my fingers. The ground was cold under my feet and the robe was slipping off my shoulder. All of it arrived in the same instant, each sensation demanding the same attention, each one as loud as the others. There was no hierarchy, no filter, no way to say *this matters and this doesn't* because it all mattered equally and equally was too much.

I stopped. Closed my eyes. Breathed.

With my eyes closed, it was better. Fewer sensations. Only sound, touch and smell but not the relentless flood of visual detail. I stood there with my arms full of branches. I shut my eyes and sorted.

Wind was present. Constant. But can be ignored.

The Ground was cold. Noted. Filed.

The smell had complexity. But it was not urgent. Background noise.

I held branches. Stable. Done.

I opened my eyes. The world was still there, still loud, still detailed. But for a moment, I had chosen what to attend to. A small victory. The kind that doesn't feel like one until later.

I gathered more wood. Slowly. One branch at a time. One sensation at a time, as much as I could manage.

The stream was close. I could hear it. But hearing it was different now, layered, the sound not one sound but dozens of sounds pretending to be one. Water over stone, water over gravel, water against the bank, the deeper note of a pool somewhere downstream, the higher pitch where the current narrowed between two rocks.

I set the wood down at the camp and took the pot to the stream.

The water was clear. I could see the bottom — pebbles, dark and light, the slow movement of sediment where the current disturbed it. I knelt at the bank and lowered the pot into the stream.

The cold hit my hands before the water reached the rim. The temperature of the stream against my skin, the pressure of the current pushing against the pot, the light on the water's surface fracturing into moving patterns, the smell of wet stone rising from the bank, the softness of the mud under my knees, the pot's handle biting into my palms —

My fingers opened. Involuntary. The pot tilted, water rushing in lopsided, the weight shifting. I grabbed it with both hands and the cold water surged over my wrists and up my forearms. The sensation was so complete, so simultaneous, so *much* that my vision blurred and my hearing went flat. And for a moment I was nowhere at all, just a body being shouted at by the world from every direction.

I pulled the pot up, set it on the bank and sat down in the grass beside it.

The grass was wet. I felt every blade and I didn't care because sitting was better than kneeling and breathing was better than not. The stream was still making its layered, detailed, relentless sound and the sun was on my face. The water was dripping from my hands into the grass and I couldn't stop noticing.

I sat there. The stream ran. The pot sat beside me, half full, water dripping down its side.

I don't know how long it took before I heard your footsteps. You moved differently in the forest than on the trail — slower, more deliberate, placing your feet the way someone does when they're not sure what they'll find.

You found me sitting in wet grass with a pot of water and the expression of someone who had just lost an argument with themselves.

You didn't ask. You sat down beside me. Not close enough to crowd, not far enough to be careful. Just beside me, at the distance of someone who understood that presence was enough and questions were sometimes too much.

"Everything is too loud, too bright, too much." I said.

You nodded.

"The water was too cold — I almost couldn't hold the —" I stopped. The sentence was too complicated. The event was too simultaneous to render in the sequential medium of speech. "Everything arrived at once."

"It will get easier," you said.

I looked at you. The certainty in your voice was wrong — not false but borrowed, the certainty of someone repeating something they'd read or been told rather than something they'd experienced. You had never lost your senses and found them again. You were guessing.

But the guess was kind. And kindness, at that moment, was more useful than accuracy.

"How long?"

"I don't know."

That was better. That was honest. I could work with honest.

You picked up the pot. Tested the weight. Added a little more water from the stream, carefully. I watched the cold hit your hands and saw your fingers tighten against it, the small involuntary grip of a body that has learned to hold on.

We walked back. You carried the pot. I carried nothing, because my capacity for simultaneous sensation had been exceeded for the morning. The walk was short. The camp was close. The fire had settled into the patient burn of wood that nobody had fed. You fed it, and it responded.

You cooked. Rice and what was left of the dried vegetables from two camps ago. The pot steamed. The smell was simple and specific in a way that food hadn't been before — starch, salt, the mineral note of mountain water, the faintly scorched edge where the rice met the bottom of the pot.

After we ate, you cleaned the pot. Stacked the remaining wood. Straightened your bedroll, then mine, then yours again — the restless maintenance of a man running out of tasks.

"Sleep," I said.

"I'm fine."

"You look like you haven't slept in two days."

"One."

"Sleep."

You considered arguing. I could see the sentence forming, the counterargument assembling itself behind your eyes. Then the body won. The body usually wins, eventually. You lay down on your bedroll, pulled the blanket up and closed your eyes with the reluctant compliance of someone surrendering to a superior force.

You were asleep in minutes. The shallow, immediate sleep of exhaustion, the kind where the body doesn't bother with preamble, just drops. Your breathing changed. Your face changed. The tension released in stages — jaw first, then forehead, then the shoulders, the hands, until you were just a person sleeping in the afternoon sun and the guilt and the vigilance were, for a while, someone else's problem.
