You told me you wanted to explore the terrain the next morning. A practical, mundane task that required solitude and clear sightlines and the absence of a traveling companion who might accidentally rearrange the geography.

I didn't argue. The headache was bad enough that solitude sounded like medicine.

But you didn't just explore. You found an open clearing. Wide. About two hundred meters across. The same radius as the field, which was not coincidental on your part. You were thinking in terms of destruction's reach. You were thinking like someone who had watched reality break multiple times and was now preparing to break it once more, on purpose.

No mountains nearby. You walked the perimeter, evaluated the terrain, confirmed that there was no large geological feature within range that might be edited out of existence by whatever I did when pushed past limits. The missing flank was still visible on the horizon, the smooth absence where rock used to be and you looked at it while you planned. Your notes say that it made you hate the plan even more. But it also made you more certain that it was necessary.

The clearing had only few trees. And the ones that were there, were small. Saplings, young growth, flexible wood that might bend without shattering. You were calculating tolerable cost. *Tolerable cost.* For a man who documented butterflies.

You put your notebook in a crevice. Between two boulders, wrapped in a waterproof case and wedged it deep enough to survive whatever came. If it went wrong, which was a category you'd promoted from unlikely to probable, the documentation would survive. Someone would find it. Someone would know. Someone would read it and it wouldn't stop mid-word.

Then you came back with the casual stride of a man who had spent a morning walking and nothing more significant to report than the quality of the terrain and the direction of the wind. You sat down. You boiled water and made tea.

"Wei would have liked this place," you said.

I flinched. Briefly. I did it involuntarily, a contraction in the muscles of my torso, a tightening around the ache that Wei's name created, the bruise that never healed because healing it would mean accepting it and accepting it would mean something I wasn't yet ready for.

The pain was sharp. Clear. Present.

And then, thirty seconds later, I couldn't remember what you'd said.

You used his name as a key.

The sentence was gone. The words, the syntax, the casual delivery. All erased. Cleaned. The wall had done its work, the guardian had intervened, the wall had identified the content as dangerous and removed it with the clean precision that I'd learned to recognize and couldn't learn to prevent.

But the pain was still there.

I rubbed my chest. The gesture was unconscious, hand moving to the site of impact, pressing, as if pressure could locate the source. The pain had no address. The pain existed without its cause, an effect divorced from its origin, an echo without a sound.

"Something wrong?" you asked.

I looked at you. You were watching me with the attentive stillness of a naturalist observing a phenomenon. With the intent focus of someone who needed to see this, needed to confirm this, needed to know that your theory was correct before you committed to the consequences.

"I don't know," I said. "Something — I feel —"

I couldn't finish the sentence. What did I feel? Pain. Where? In my chest. Why? I had no idea. Literally no idea. The cause had been removed. But the effect remained. And the disconnection between them was maddening, the kind of maddening that made me want to press my temples and breathe and push against the wall and—

"*You* created the field," you followed.

Fear. Immediate, visceral, spiking through my body like ice water. Fear of what? The words registered, were processed and gone, taken, erased, the guardian moving faster now, faster than before, as if it recognized the pattern and was accelerating its response.

But the fear stayed.

I couldn't tell what you'd said. Couldn't retrieve the sentence, couldn't reconstruct it, couldn't even remember that you'd spoken. But I was afraid. Suddenly, completely, groundlessly. The fear of a person who knows something terrible is true and can't remember what the terrible thing is.

"I—" My hands were shaking. "Why am I afraid?"

Your face. I saw how much it cost you to sit there and watch this, but I couldn't tell why at the moment. You watched me feel things I couldn't understand, watched the disconnection between cause and effect play out in real time on a person you chose to walk beside. 

"The mountain," you said. "Turn around."

I turned.

The horizon. The mountain with the missing flank — the smooth absence, the flowers at the edge, the geometry of nothing where something had been.

My body reacted before my mind could process. I was on my feet. When had I stood? My hands were clenched and the ground beneath me was vibrating with a deep rumble. I was staring at the absent mountain and I didn't know why I was staring at it. But my body knew, my gut knew, something deep in my bones knew that the missing mountain was connected to me in a way that—

The thought dissolved. The knowledge evaporated. The cause was taken.

The feeling remained. Guilt. Responsibility. The weight of having done something that cannot be undone, pressing down on the inside of my chest like a hand.

I sat down. Slowly. The vibration stopped. The world settled.

You sat beside me. Not close — the careful meters, the safety distance that had become our default spacing. You were breathing too carefully, the controlled respiration of a man who was running a deliberate test on someone who trusted and you were horrified by your own methods.

"Three for three," you said. Quiet. To yourself.

"What?"

"Nothing." You looked at the ground. "I think I understand now. How the forgetting works."

"Tell me."

"I could, but you'll forget the explanation."

The absurdity of it broke through. The circular trap, knowing that knowing was useless because knowing would be taken, made me laugh. A dark, exhausted humorless laugh. Even it  wasn't funny. Even though, it was the most terrifying thing you could have said. I laughed because the alternative was to be silent and afraid.

"Then what?" I asked.

You looked at the mountain. At the absence. At the evidence of what I could do when something cut deep enough.

"I have to make you feel something so deeply that the feeling alone, even without content, or any explanation, would be enough." You paused. "I have to reach the part of you that the forgetting can't touch."

I should have heard the warning in that. Should have recognized the timbre of a man who was describing a plan he despised, who was outlining an act of necessary cruelty with the detachment of someone who had already committed to committing it.

I didn't hear it. I heard a man with a plan. And plans were the one thing I didn't have.

"When?" I asked.

"Tomorrow."

You stood. Walked to the fire and cooked dinner. Your hands were steady because you willed them to be. Its effort was visible in the set of your jaw and the hardness of your shoulders and the way you didn't look at me for the rest of the evening.

I ate. I slept. The headache pulsed.

Tomorrow came.
