"Yun." Your voice, quiet. You weren't looking at me. You were looking at three people near the salt stall. Not the traders — three others, standing with the equidistant spacing and complementary sightlines of people who had been trained to observe. Civilian clothing. No talismans. No weapons. Just the patient, cataloguing attention of professionals doing their job.

"I know," I said.

"They've been there since we came back from lunch."

The fox-spirit watched from the shadow of a storage building. The child was gone. The traders were still talking. The sun moved behind the ridge and the market's light changed. We were still standing there, two people among thirty buildings, observed by three others who had been assigned to watch us and were doing their job with the diligent patience of professionals.

We left the village. The fox-spirit emerged from shadow and fell into step beside us. The trail was there. East, still, always east. The village disappeared behind us. The mountains received us.

The sadness faded. Not completely — it settled, the way sediment settles in still water, present but no longer suspended. The trail helped. The rhythm of walking helped. The mountains, indifferent and enormous, helped most of all.

The three from the market were still behind us. They kept their distance — always the same distance, the kind of precision that comes from training rather than coincidence. Whatever instructions the Pencil had given after the field, after his people had retreated vomiting and shaken to the roots of their cultivated foundations, his instructions had clearly included *do not threaten*, *maintain distance* and *observe only.*

Your notebook was in your hand — open, now, the hesitation of recent days having settled into a pattern where you opened it when external observations required recording and closed it when the observations were about me.

"Lian Zhuo's people," you said. "The formation habits are consistent even in plain clothes."

"Formation habits?"

"They're equidistant. Their sightlines are complementary. Two watch us, one watches the environment." You paused. "Textbook observation pattern. Whoever trained them didn't account for the possibility of being observed by someone who reads tactical deployment as a hobby."

The observation was — almost — funny. The thin, dry humor of a man who had spent years in libraries studying exactly the kind of operational theory that these cultivators were executing and finding their fieldwork lacking.

"Should we—" You closed the notebook.

"No." I said. "Let them watch. As long as they keep their distance."

The pragmatism was calculated. Three sect cultivators at observation distance were a known quantity. Three sect cultivators who had been confronted and driven off were an unknown — cornered observers might escalate, might interpret confrontation as aggression, might do the thing that frightened people do when they feel their options narrowing. 

I didn't want things to escalate. Better to let them watch. Better to let them report. Better to let the Pencil's information stream continue flowing, because information flowing toward the sect was information not building into the kind of pressure that required explosive release.

Besides.

They had seen the field. Had seen what I did to mountains. And what could they report? Two people walking east? With no apparent goal or destination? No, the three observers were not a threat. They were witnesses. 

We walked. The observers followed. They would reposition, probably. Find a new vantage. Resume their patient documentation from a different angle. The sect had shifted from confrontation to study. The shift seemed permanent in the way that changed doctrines are permanent.

I was a phenomenon to be understood.

But that was a problem for later. For now, the observers kept their distance and the trail went east and the world continued to be sharp and loud and cold and full of things that happened in my disfavor with the steady, patient regularity of a bill being paid.

We made camp where the trail widened into a shelf of flat rock. Generations of travelers seemed in agreement, that this is where you stop. The earth was packed hard across a broad circle, beaten smooth by years of boots and bedrolls. A fire ring of stacked stones sat at the center, blackened, the gaps between them filled with old ash. Someone had dragged a log to one side — seat height, bark worn to a polish on top. The wind broke against the ridge above and passed over without touching the shelf. 

You built the fire in the ring, while I sat on the log and the fox-spirit walked into the camp. It came from the trail behind us, openly, the way an animal approaches a place it has decided belongs to it. It padded past you, close enough that its fur brushed your leg, and sat down beside the fire.

You froze. Your hand went to your belt — reflex, the instinct of a man who had spent years in places where things that appeared suddenly were rarely good news. Your weight shifted. Your eyes went wide.

The fox-spirit looked up at you with amber eyes. The expression, if a fox could have an expression, was one of mild evaluation. As if it had been considering this moment for some time and had decided that now was acceptable.

You frowned. "There's a fox-spirit at the fire."

"Yes?"

"It's been—"

"Following us. For a while. At apparently it had deemed you worthy of noticing it."

You stared at it. Frowning. It stared back at you. The standoff lasted three breaths. Then the fox-spirit yawned — the slow, deliberate yawn of an animal that has assessed a threat and found it beneath concern — and began cleaning its left paw.

Your hand dropped from your belt.

"It's not going to attack you," I said. "It decided to let you see it. That's the opposite of an attack."

"How long is 'a while'?"

"A few weeks. Since before the field."

"I've been—" You paused. "Something was following us. I could feel it. I thought it was the scouts."

"It wasn't the scouts. Well, not only them."

The fox-spirit finished its paw. Stood. Padded three steps closer to you and sat again. Close enough to touch, if you'd wanted to. You didn't. But you didn't step back either.

"What should I call you?" I said.

You looked at me. The fox-spirit looked at me. For a moment, the two of you wore the same expression — the shared confusion of beings who hadn't expected to be addressed.

I looked at you. "Not you."

I looked back at the fox-spirit. "You have amber eyes. What about Hǔpò?"

The fox-spirit hissed. Ears flat, teeth bared, the quick violence of an animal that has heard something offensive. The sound was brief and absolute.

"No to that one." I looked at the rust-colored fur. "Hóngyè?"

Same reaction. Hiss. Teeth. The amber eyes narrowed with what could only be described as contempt.

"Okay, okay." I chuckled. "What about Xiǎohú?"

The ears shifted. Forward to sideways, relaxed, the loose angle of an animal that has heard something and found it acceptable.

"Xiǎohú," I said again. "You like that?"

The fox-spirit stood. Padded toward me. Her tail swung in loose, unhurried arcs — not the stiff wag of excitement but the slow sweep of an animal at ease. She stopped in front of me and tipped her snout forward, pressing it against my hand. Brief. Warm. Then she sat and curled her tail around her paws.

"Xiǎohú, then." I said, giving the fox-spirit a short pet.

You looked at me. The look of a man being told that the invisible animal he hadn't known about had a name he hadn't been consulted on.

"You just — named a fox-spirit. Right now."

"She named herself. I just listed options."

"She?"

"She's smaller than a male would usually be. And she doesn't carry the scent. Male foxes mark — it's sharp, unmistakable. She doesn't."

I turned back to Xiǎohú. "Are you a vixen?"

Xiǎohú leaned forward and pressed her snout against my hand again. Her tail swept once across the ground — a single, loose brush through the dust.

I looked at you. "I think she's a vixen."

Xiǎohú looked at you. You looked at Xiǎohú. Something settled between the two of you — not trust, not yet, but the preliminary negotiations that precede trust, the mutual agreement that neither party intended immediate harm.

The fire popped. A coal shifted. Outside the circle of light, the observers' campfire flickered like an echo. And we were three now, visibly, where before we had been two and a secret.
