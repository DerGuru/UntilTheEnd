I couldn't sleep. The headache had settled into something permanent. Not sharp enough to incapacitate, but also not dull enough to ignore. The steady ache of a body arguing with itself.

I lay on my back and stared at the sky. The stars felt right. The constellation patterns matched my memory. The moon was in the correct position for this date, this latitude, this season. I checked each one with the attention of someone counting coins, wanting to be sure.

You slept. Your notebook was under your hand. Not beside you, not in your pack. Under your hand. As if you'd fallen asleep guarding it. As if the information inside was something that needed protection now in a way it hadn't before.

Memories came.

Not the kind I expected. Debris. Fragments of things that had no business being together, connected by nothing except the fact that they all belonged to me.

A village. Rooftops. Smoke. Not the smoke of cooking, the smoke of endings. I didn't know which village. I didn't know which ending.

A hand — my hand — pressing against a wall. Stone. Cool. Lines carved into the surface. I'd done this before. The same motion, the same search, the same fingers tracing grooves that someone had cut into the world and left for me to find. Or that I had cut and left for myself.

A face. Young. Male. Brown eyes. Open mouth. He was saying something. I couldn't hear the words. The face meant something — I could feel the meaning pressing against the inside of my chest like a fist — but the meaning was sealed behind something opaque and thick and absolutely, unwaveringly committed to staying closed.

My head pulsed.

The memories didn't fit together. They sat in my mind like shards of broken glass. Dangerous, sharp, refusing to reconstruct.

A path. Narrow, carved into a mountainside, switchbacking between two ridges. I knew this path. The loose scree at the second turn. The place where the trail narrowed to shoulder-width and the drop to the left was vertical, two hundred meters of nothing before the river. And the view. Three peaks to the south, unmistakable, the kind of formation you remember because nature gave it a face. The leftmost peak was blunt, flat-topped, the skull of something buried. The middle one rose to a narrow point, leaning east, like a finger indicating direction. The rightmost was split — cleaved down the center, two halves of what had once been whole, the gap between them wide enough that on clear days the sky showed through like a blue wound.

No other place in the world looked like that. I had walked thousands of paths across thousands of mountains and this view existed exactly once.

And on this path, at the third switchback, where the scree gave way to solid granite and the trail widened just enough for a single tree to grow from a crack in the rock. The tree was on the left. I was certain of that. The certainty lived in my body rather than my mind, muscle memory and spatial instinct.

But then there was another memory of the same path, with the same view. And the tree was on the right. I knew that too. Both things true. Each one possible on its own, but not together at the same time, yet they were — separated by a shift so quiet that nobody else would even notice the discrepancy.

Nobody except me.

The headache sharpened. I pressed my fist against my forehead and held it there.

*Not now.*

The gate again. The interior voice, the one that sounded like me but acted independently. Stop. Don't look. Let it go.

The fragments dissolved. Like sugar in water. Present, dissolved, detectable only by the fact that everything tasted different now.

I opened my eyes. The stars were still right.

I got up and walked to the stream.

The water was cold. Clear. I knelt and put my hands in it. Washed them. Not because they were dirty. Because the motion felt necessary.

But it was there. On my hands. The residue of something I'd almost held and had been made to drop.

I washed for five minutes. Maybe ten. The water ran over my skin and carried away nothing.

You found me there.

You stood behind me and waited in silence. The way you waited when I was trying to remember something and the memory was just out of reach. The way you waited when I was trying to understand something and the understanding was just out of reach.

I stopped washing and looked down at my hands. Clean. Empty.

"Something happened," I said.

"Yes."

"Something important."

"Yes."

"I don't remember."

You were quiet for a long time. The stream filled the silence with its own conversation: meaningless, persistent, the sound of water talking to stone about nothing especially.

"I wrote it down," you said.

I turned. You held out your notebook. Open. The page filled with your handwriting: precise, measured, the script of a man who understands that written records are the only defense against a world that keeps rewriting itself.

I read.

*Episode 1: explained pattern. She understood. 10 seconds later, forgotten. She asked "what were you saying."*

*Episode 2: repeated explanation. She understood. 2 minutes later, forgotten. Piece by piece. Like someone disassembling a structure from inside.*

*Episode 3: repeated explanation. She understood. During erasure — her eyes. Being dimmed. Active process. Not forgetting. DELETION.*

I read the entries again. Three times. The words were there. They meant things. They described a night, words, silences. An event I had apparently lived through.

I didn't remember any of it.

I could feel the outlines. Three of them. Three deletions.

I couldn't see what had been inside them.

I handed the notebook back. You took it. Closed it. Held it against your chest.

"The gap," I said. "In me. It's bigger now."

"I know."

"Whatever is in there — whatever is being protected — it's important."

"I know."

"And I can't reach it."

"Not yet."

"Maybe not ever."

You looked at me. The morning was arriving, a thin grey line along the eastern ridge. In the growing dawn, your face was tired. Older than it should have been.

"You'll reach it," you said. "Not through thinking. Not through me explaining. Thinking is what it blocks. Explaining is what it erases. Whatever lives in you — it sits between knowing and remembering and it decides what passes."

"Then how?"

You looked at the stream.

"I don't know yet," you said.

I stood. Wiped my hands on my robe. Walked back to the fire.

The embers were dying. The scrolls were still there. The sand drawings, faded now, softened by night wind, barely legible, traced their patterns in the dirt.

I looked at them. At the scrolls. At your notebook, held against your chest like armor.

"Why are these still here?" I said.

You followed my gaze. The scrolls. The maps. The evidence.

"Whatever does this to me — it can move chalk. It can shift paths. It can rearrange terrain." I gestured at the spread of documents. "It can edit reality. So why not edit *these*? Why leave the evidence intact?"

You looked at the scrolls. At the sand drawings. At the notebook in your hands.

"You're right," you said slowly. "The scrolls survived for thousands of years. My notebook is untouched." You turned the notebook over, examining it as if seeing it for the first time. "If whatever is in you can change the physical world — why not destroy the records?"

"It doesn't make sense."

"Unless it can't."

"It bends trees."

"But trees don't contain information about you."

We looked at each other. The stream talked to its stones. The grey light thickened.

"Or it doesn't want to," you said. "Not can't. Doesn't want to. The distinction matters."

"Assuming it has its own will. Why wouldn't it want to? It keeps me from remembering. It keeps me from understanding. Why would it want to preserve evidence that could lead me there?"

You opened your mouth. Closed it. The expression of someone who has arrived at the edge of a thought and found the ground missing.

"I don't know," you said. "But there's something there. Some condition. Some rule that determines what gets changed and what doesn't. The chalk was changed. The paths were changed. Your *memory* was changed. But the physical evidence — paper, ink, stone carvings — stays."

I turned my hands over. The hands that had washed in the stream for ten minutes and come out clean and empty.

"There's a pattern," I said. "In what it changes and what it leaves alone. If I could see that pattern —"

The headache pulsed. A warning. The gate, pressing down.

"Later," you said. You'd seen it — the flinch, the narrowing. "We'll come back to it."

The bowl of rice from last night sat where you had left it. Cold now. The grains stuck together in a single congealed mass.

I left it there.

The gap pulsed.

I went to sleep.
