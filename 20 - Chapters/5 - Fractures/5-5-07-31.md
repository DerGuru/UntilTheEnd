I woke to the sound of you breathing.

Waking-breathing. Controlled. Deliberate. The breathing of a man who was lying in his bedroll with his eyes open, waiting for dawn to formalize what his body had already decided.

You'd been awake for a while. Lying in the dark, breathing carefully, thinking whatever thoughts a mortal thinks when you've spent the night three meters from something that can rewrite reality.

I didn't announce my waking. I lay still and watched the sky turn from black to the deep blue that precedes dawn. The only honest color — the one that exists between definitions, admitting to being neither night nor day.

The world looked wrong.

A subtle wrongness, the kind you notice in dreams — furniture in the right place, light falling from the wrong side, and you know, without being able to explain, that you're not where you should be.

Colors were too much. The pre-dawn blue was too blue. The grey of the ashes in the fire pit was too precisely grey. The green of the pine needles against the brightening sky was the deliberate green of things being green on purpose.

Shadows were wrong too. Correct in position. I checked, I tracked the angle of the emerging light against the objects that cast shadows and the geometry was correct. But the shadows moved strangely. When I shifted my gaze, there was a delay — the shadow adjusting a fraction of a second after the light changed. As if referencing the light rather than responding to it.

The world was self-correcting. Still absorbing what I'd done to it. Still fitting itself back into the shape of normal reality after being held in the shape of mine.

I sat up. The headache was there. Had been there, had been continuous since the field, a hot wire behind my eyes that pulsed with my heartbeat. I pressed my temples. Breathed and stood up.

You were ahead of me. Already packed, while I was contemplating the wrongness of the world around us. Already standing at the edge of our campsite with something in your hand that caught the early light with a thin metallic gleam.

A knife. The small utility blade you carried for cutting food, for trimming rope, for the mundane tasks that a mortal performs with tools because he doesn't have qi to do them without.

You were looking at your left hand. Specifically at your left index finger. Your right hand held the knife against the pad of the finger, the blade's edge pressing just hard enough to dimple the skin without breaking it.

I watched, but didn't speak.

You pressed the knife. A small cut. Precise, controlled. The deliberate incision of someone conducting an experiment. Blood appeared immediately, a thin red line, bright against the pale skin of your finger.

You stared at the blood and waited.

The blood didn't move. Didn't reverse. Didn't flow back into the wound through skin that rebuilt itself. It simply bled. Normal. Expected. The ordinary response of skin meeting a blade.

You exhaled. Long and shaking. Your shoulders dropped and your jaw unclenched, but your hands kept trembling.

You put the knife away. Found a cloth in your pack, the same cloth you used for cleaning utensils and wrapped your finger. The blood soaked through the fabric in a small, spreading stain that darkened and dried and looked exactly like what it was.

"It stays," you said, nodded and tucked the wrapped finger against your palm. "Good."

We ate in the colors-too-bright morning. Rice, cold from last night, eaten with fingers because neither of us had bothered to heat water to clean the bowls or perform any of the small rituals of preparation that made meals feel like meals instead of fuel. You ate with your right hand. Your left hand, the injured finger, rested on your knee.

The cut would scar. Barely. It was small, shallow, the kind of wound that heals in a few days and leaves a thin white line that fades over time until it eventually becomes invisible to everyone who isn't looking for it. But you would look for it. I knew this. You would look at that scar the way soldiers look at their scars. With the recognition of someone who survived something and needs the proof.

"The shadows are wrong," I said.

You looked at the ground. Looked at a tree. Looked at the angular shadow it cast against the brightening earth.

"Delayed," you said. You'd noticed. Of course you'd noticed. "As if they're being generated rather than cast."

Assessment. The scholar's reflex, converting observation into analysis, filing the impossible under *documented* and moving on.

"Will it stop?"

"I don't know."

The world continued adjusting around us. I could feel it — small corrections, tiny shifts, the reality equivalent of a body settling after exertion. The colors were still too vivid, but less so than an hour ago. The shadows were still delayed, but the delay was shortening. By afternoon, perhaps, the world would look normal. By tomorrow, maybe it would be normal. The self-correction was happening, the way a lake returns to stillness after a stone, ripple by ripple, circle by circle, the disturbance propagating outward until it reaches the edges and dissipates. Except I was still in the lake. And I was still a stone. Bopping up and down, sending out ripples that were hitting the edges of the world and causing new disturbances.

I packed and we started walking.

The trail was there — still going east, still pulled by whatever internal compass had decided that isolation was the appropriate direction. I'd stopped fighting it. Not because I agreed, but because the alternative — forcing south, wrestling with terrain that rearranged itself to prevent my passage, burning energy on a war with geography — was exhausting in a way that I wasn't willing to endure now.

So east we went. Into the mountains. Away from people who might be standing next to trees when I got angry. We walked in silence. The loaded kind — too much to say and no words adequate to say it.

I tried to describe what the field had felt like from the inside.

"It was like holding —" I stopped. Holding wasn't right. Holding implies hands. Implies something outside yourself that you grip. "More like being — no. Like becoming the —" Wrong again. The field hadn't been something I'd become. It had been something I'd done, except *done* implies action and this had been reflexive, automatic, as unconscious as blinking. "The weight," I said. "There was weight. The pressure of keeping everything —"

I stopped. Started again.

"Imagine you're —" No. But there was no analogy. Every comparison I reached for collapsed under the distance between the experience and the language available to describe it.

"The world was —" I pressed my palms against my temples. "It was like —"

Silence.

"You don't need to explain it."

"I want to."

"I know. But wanting to explain and being able to explain are different things. And forcing the explanation might —" You glanced at the nearest tree. The glance was involuntary, instinctive, the new reflex of a man who had learned to check whether the trees near you were intact.

"Might break something. Might cost something. Might translate the frustration of inadequate language into the force of splitting wood."

I shut my mouth.

By midday, the colors had settled. The shadows were tracking correctly. The world had finished its adjustments, had accepted interruption, had filed it away the way the earth files away earthquakes: damage noted, stability restored, history continuing.

But it didn't feel right. The wind touched my skin and I flinched — not from cold, from proximity. As if the air had gotten closer. The ground under my boots transmitted vibrations it shouldn't have. Your footsteps, yes, but also deeper things. The slow grinding of rock against rock somewhere below. The movement of water in soil. The pulse of roots drawing moisture upward through channels too fine to see. I could feel the mountain breathing.

A bird crossed our path — a sparrow, small and brown and ordinary — and I knew its heartbeat. Felt it. A rapid flutter against my awareness like a fingertip tapping glass. It landed on a branch six meters away and I felt the branch bend under its weight. Felt the tree absorb the impact.

I stopped walking.

"What?" you said.

"Nothing." I started walking again. But it wasn't nothing. The boundary between me and the world had thinned. Before the field, my perception had ended at my skin. Now everything within a certain radius registered — texture, weight, temperature, movement. The world pressed against me from every direction, feeding me information I hadn't asked for and couldn't turn off. The relationship between what I was and what the world was, the negotiation between my interior and the external, the contract that governed which of my emotions became force and which stayed private.

The contract had been rewritten and the terms felt worse.
