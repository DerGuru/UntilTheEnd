"You're afraid," I said.

I saw it in the way you handed me the rice bowl at dinner. The pause — fractional, almost invisible. You caught yourself. Completed the gesture. The bowl changed hands. But the pause had been there.

You ate. Chewed. Swallowed. The deliberateness of someone composing a response.

"Yes."

"Since when?"

"Since the trees." You set your bowl down. "The rain — I could stand in that. Standing in the rain is not comfortable, but it is endurable." You looked at the fire. "Splitting three trees from the inside with a thought is not."

I'd split three trees with anger I hadn't aimed. Had made it rain from a clear sky. Had made the earth move because I was frustrated about scrolls.

"If it helps, I'm afraid of me too," I said.

You looked up. Something shifted in your expression — not relief. As if my admission had changed a calculation from *doesn't know* to *knows and it doesn't help.*

We both didn't sleep well that night, so we broke camp before dawn. You made tea in the dark — by feel, by routine, the water heated with a thread of my qi I fed into the kettle. Your core couldn't sustain a flame anymore. Mine could sustain a sun. The least it could do was boil water. No jasmine left. Just plain leaves from a pouch that was running low. The tea tasted like hot water. We drank it anyway, crouched by the dead fire, our breath visible in the pre-dawn cold.

Neither of us spoke. A bird started up somewhere in the canopy — tentative, testing the morning, deciding whether it was safe. It sang three notes. Stopped. Tried again. The forest's version of clearing your throat.

Moving was better than sitting.

The trail should have taken us south. We'd studied your map, annotated in your careful hand with the route changes the world kept making. South, toward the river valley where other people lived.

But the trail didn't go south.

It had, yesterday. South, clearly marked, cutting between two ridges toward the valley floor. This morning, the trail went east. Into the mountains. Toward higher ground, thinner air, less life, less buffer, more isolation.

"The path changed," you sighed. Flat. Not surprised. Beyond surprise, now, in the territory where anomaly was simply the operating condition.

I looked at the trail. East. Into the mountains. Into the place where my emotions would have fewer things to break and more empty space to fill.

I hadn't wanted to go east. I hadn't consciously redirected anything. But the part of me that operated without consultation had apparently decided that proximity to people was a variable to be minimized.

"We go south," I said. "Off-trail."

We went south. Off-trail. Through undergrowth that hadn't been walked. Over terrain that hadn't been crossed. Around obstacles that nobody had bothered to clear because nobody had ever needed this route before.

An hour later, we found a trail.

Going east.

You stopped. Looked at the trail. Looked at me.

"You're doing this," you said.

"I'm not — I don't want to go east."

"What you want and what's happening appear to be different categories."

You were right. The gap between my conscious intention and the world's response was the thing that scared me most. I could survive being powerful. But surviving being out of control — being a force that acted according to rules I couldn't read — that sat in my stomach like cold iron.

We tried south three more times. Each time, the terrain funneled us. Not violently — no walls, no barriers, no dramatic geological intervention. Just the gentle persuasion of a landscape that kept offering east as the path of least resistance and making south increasingly difficult. A ravine here. A landslide there. A thicket of thorn-brush so dense that non-qi-enhanced cutting would take hours.

By afternoon, we were going east.

I hadn't decided to go east. The world had decided for me. Or — the part of me that was becoming the world had decided for both of us.

"The sect," I said. We'd stopped at a stream. The water was cold. It tasted of minerals and the metallic aftertaste that mountain water carries through old stone. "They've been tracking me. They'll know where we are."

"Yes."

"And they'll follow."

"Also yes."

You sat on a flat rock and pulled off your boots. Your feet were blistered — the terrain punishing a body that ran on endurance rather than cultivation. You examined the damage with the clinical detachment of a man cataloguing data. From your pack, a small pouch: mineral powder, the kind travelling merchants sold at crossroads for a few copper. You dusted it over the raw skin, hissing once through your teeth, then wrapped strips of cloth around the worst spots with the practiced efficiency of someone who had done this before and expected to do it again.

"Let me," I said.

You looked up.

"The blisters. A thread of qi. Ten seconds."

You considered. The consideration lasted longer than it should have. Weighing something — not the offer, but what accepting it would mean. What it would cost the architecture of self-sufficiency you'd built over months of walking behind someone who could sustain a sun while you couldn't light a candle.

Finally you nodded. "Okay. Thank you."

I knelt. Placed two fingers against the sole of your left foot. The qi moved — narrow, precise, the smallest application I could manage. Heat without burning. The blistered skin smoothed, the fluid reabsorbed, the raw tissue closing over like water settling after a stone. Your right foot, came next. Ten seconds each.

You flexed your toes. Tested the weight. Put the boots back on.

"Thank you," you said. Quietly. The tone of someone who has accepted something and is already recalculating the balance.

I drank. The water tasted wrong. Not poisoned — but wrong the way things had tasted since the night I'd forgotten. Slightly off. Slightly distant.

"We could separate," I said.

You refilled your flask. Capped it. Secured it.

"No," you said.

"You'd be safer."

"Probably."

"You'd be invisible. Mortal. Nobody would—"

"No!" You didn't raise your voice, but the statement was absolute.

I rubbed my temples.

"Let's move on. We are going east, apparently." The corner of your mouth did something that might have been humor if it hadn't been so thin.

We walked east.

Around midday, we passed a meadow. Small, sheltered, the kind of accidental clearing that happens when a stream carves a wide enough bed to let sunlight reach the ground. Wildflowers — blue and white, the alpine kind that grows close to the soil and blooms in defiance of altitude. A butterfly worked the blossoms with the focused industry of something that had no idea the world was wrong.

You stopped. Looked at the meadow, at the flowers. Looked at me.

"What?" I said.

"Nothing." You adjusted your pack. "It's just — flowers."

Flowers. In the middle of a mountain range that my unconscious mind was reshaping for purposes I couldn't identify, while a sect of cultivators tracked my emotional outbursts through resonance talismans, and the mechanism that kept me alive was actively preventing me from understanding itself — flowers.

They were very blue.

We kept walking.

The mountains received us with patient indifference. The air thinned. The vegetation thinned. Everything felt slightly unreal — as if the landscape was a draft that hadn't been finalized.

I walked. You walked behind me.

Your footsteps sounded different. Lighter. Ready.
