On the third day of walking, the trail opened onto a crossroads town — the kind of place that exists because two trails meet and people who travel need to eat, sleep and buy things. A market square that was, for reasons no one had bothered to explain, a near-perfect rhombus. Thirty buildings, maybe forty, clustered around the intersection with the pragmatic density of structures built for function rather than aesthetics.

People.

The first people who weren't cultivators or companions in weeks. The first people who didn't know who or what I was. People who didn't carry talismans or instruments. People who didn't have the professional wariness of those who monitored reality anomalies for a living. These were farmers. Traders. Simple folk. A woman sold vegetables from a stall she'd set up at dawn. An old man who sharpened tools at the corner of the intersection. Children that moved between adults with the purposeful aimlessness of small humans performing the important work of being young.

I walked among them and felt everything.

The new sensitivity made the village overwhelming. Not dangerously, not like cultivation energy or combat. The mundane, detailed, sensory overwhelm of a person experiencing normal life at full volume for assumably the first time. The smell of cooking fires, distinct, individual, each one carrying the flavor of whatever was being cooked in whatever pot with whatever fuel. The sound of voices, conversations overlapping, laughter, argument, the bass murmur of commerce and the treble clarity of children. The texture of the market square underfoot, packed earth, worn smooth by generations of feet, each step transmitting the information of density and moisture. Loaded with the history of a surface that had been walked on ten thousand times.

Almost too much but manageable — barely. On the trail I'd learned to filter: one bird at a time, one scent, one texture. The trail offered them in sequence. The market offered them all at once. But the muscle was there now, the one I'd built over three days of walking. It strained. It held.

You were in your element. Markets were your natural ground. You'd sold the herbs I'd pointed out on the trail — roots and stems that grew only above the qi line, in places most gatherers never reached because most gatherers didn't travel weeks beyond the last settlement. I knew them by sight, by smell, by the subtle hum they carried. The apothecary at the edge of the market had gone very still when you'd opened the pouch and had paid without haggling. We first spilt the money und then split up. You moved through the stalls with the efficient purposefulness of a man whose pouch was heavy for the first time in weeks. Rice. Dried vegetables. A spool of heavy thread for mending. Salt.

I bought fruit. A basket of small, hard pears from a woman whose stall was organized with the military precision of someone who took produce seriously. She counted my coins, copper, the small denominations that moved in rural marketplaces and frowned.

"You're three short," she said.

I counted again. She was right. The sum was wrong, three coins fewer than it should have been.

"Sorry." I found the additional coins and paid.

The pears were beautiful. Smooth-skinned, green-gold, the variety that grows in mountain valleys and carries the altitude in its flesh. Dense, firm, sweet only because sweetness has to be earned from thin soil and cold nights.

I bit into one. The flesh inside was brown. Mealy. Rotted from the center outward, the exterior perfect and the interior past salvage.

I looked at it. The brown flesh, the untouched skin.

I threw it away. Tried another. Also brown. A third. Brown.

Three pears. Three perfect exteriors. Three rotten interiors. The probability of this — randomness selecting three bad fruits from a basket of thirty — was low enough to be suspicious and high enough to be deniable. 

The words from the crack came back. *The debt. The cost. Every scar in the fabric of what should have been.* I hadn't had a name for this before. Now I did. Debt. And now that I had the name, I could see the shape. Never impossible. Never dramatic. Just persistent, nagging, the steady erosion of probability in my disfavor.

I found you by the fabric stall. You were holding your outer robe, my outer robe now technically. The one I'd been wearing since the clearing. You were discussing repairs with the vendor. A tear in the shoulder seam. It also needed to be shortened. Simple work for someone with the right tools.

The vendor took the robe. Threaded a needle. Made three precise stitches.

The thread snapped.

New thread. New stitches. Snap.

Third attempt. Heavier thread. Slower stitches. The needle went in, the thread pulled through, the seam began to close.

The fabric tore. Along a different seam. A new tear, two centimeters from the repair, opening with the quiet sound of material giving up.

The vendor stared at the robe. The expression of a professional encountering a failure that couldn't be explained by skill or materials or any of the variables that professionals control.

"I'll try—"

"Don't," you said. You took the robe back. Folded it. The tear was visible, a thin line of separation in the grey fabric, another entry in the growing catalogue of small things going wrong.

You looked at me. I looked at the rotten pears in the waste pile.

"We'll buy a new one," you said. The tone of someone solving a problem by refusing to argue with it.

The woman three stalls down sold traveling clothes. Not fine work but sturdy fabric, simple cuts, the kind of garments made for people who wore things out and replaced them without sentiment. You held up a grey robe, sized it against your shoulders, held up another, sized it against mine. One for you, one for me, so we'd both have an outer layer again.

You paid. Unfolded yours and put it on.

The seam at the left shoulder split on the first movement. Not dramatically — a quiet separation, thread pulling free of fabric with the small, definitive sound of something that had been made correctly and had failed anyway.

You stood very still. The new robe hung open at the shoulder, the tear identical in location to the one on the old robe, as if the failure had transferred rather than been repaired. As if the problem had never been the garment.

You took it off. I put mine on. It held. The seams stayed. The fabric didn't tear.

You looked at the robe in your hands. Looked at me in the one that worked. The calculation was visible — not suspicion, not yet, just the quiet arithmetic of a man who had noticed that the things near me broke and the things on me didn't.

You went back and bought a second robe. Put it on carefully, the way a person handles something they expect to fail. It held. The seams stayed.

Three robes. You'd meant to buy one.

The connection was clear. The same debt, the same slow extraction, the same precise and impersonal ledger that took what it was owed in installments small enough to be individually trivial and collectively significant.

We ate lunch at a stall. Rice and braised greens, the kind of simple food that exists in small markets because complexity requires time and ingredients and a settled kitchen. The food was adequate. Warm. Real as only market food is real, made by a person, for a person, in the transaction of nourishment for currency that has been the foundation of civilization since civilization decided to have a foundation.

The greens were slightly bitter. Not wrong, just the slightly-too-much edge of vegetables that had been stored a day too long. The rice was fine. The rice was always fine. Rice was apparently beneath the debt's notice, or the debt had a sense of proportion that I didn't fully credit it for.

I ate. You ate. The fox-spirit waited at the market's edge, unwilling to enter the human space, watching with amber eyes from the shadow of a storage building.

After lunch, we walked through the market again. You still had to get some salt. I bought nothing. The rotten pears had exhausted my interest in retail. We moved between stalls, between people and between conversations that meant nothing to us and everything to the people having them. The anonymity of public spaces where nobody looks at you because nobody has reason to.

There were two traders at the salt stall. One lean, one wide, the complementary geometry of a business partnership that had survived on the road longer than it had survived in any fixed location. The lean one was talking. The wide one was listening with the professional patience of someone who had heard this particular story many times before and knew his role was to listen anyway.

"—froze them where they stood. Both armies. A hundred men on each side and she looked at them and they couldn't move. Not a muscle. That's how she ended it."

"Which war?" the wide one asked.

"The village war near the southern pass. Two villages fighting over a boundary stone."

"Two villages don't have armies."

"They had enough."

I stopped walking. The lean trader was gesticulating — the grand, practiced gesture of someone who told stories for the social currency they provided.

"There was no freezing," I said.

Both traders turned. The abruptness of the correction was — I could feel it — socially inappropriate. A woman they'd never seen, interrupting a story she had no business knowing, correcting details she had no reason to possess.

"She told them the rock wasn't a boundary marker," I said. "It was a glacial erratic. It had been sitting in that field since before either village existed. Since before anyone had *fields*. And their fields didn't even overlap. So she told them to stop fighting about it. And they felt foolish enough to stop."

The lean trader stared. The wide one made a sound — half laugh, half exhale.

"That's not how I heard it," the lean one said.

"That's how it happened."

"And you know this because —"

"I've been to both villages. They think the freezing version is hilarious."

I walked on. The irritation was small — the feeling of hearing your own history told wrong by someone who wasn't there, the particular helplessness of being the only witness to a correction that nobody wanted. I lived trough it a hundred times before. It was the kind of thing that happened when you were a person who knew things and other people didn't care about those things. The world was full of stories and most of them were wrong and there was nothing to be done about it.

A child appeared at the market's edge.

Small — six, maybe seven, the age where children are fully mobile but not fully socialized, old enough to navigate a crowd independently and young enough to not understand why adults don't say the things that are obviously true.

The girl was looking at me. The unblinking, uncomfortably direct look of a child who has noticed something and hasn't yet learned that this is supposed to be discreet.

I noticed her noticing me. She kept looking until she walked over. She stopped two meters from me and looked up.

"Why are you so sad?" she asked.

"I'm not sad," I said.

"Yes you are."

Was I sad?

The crack pulsed. The feeling hummed. Somewhere deep, beneath the wall, beneath the guardian, beneath the new gentle warning that had replaced the old brutal deletion — somewhere, something ached with the quality of loss.

I was sad. I had lost something. Or someone? I didn't know what I'd lost. The content was gone. The memory was gone. The wall had seen to that. But the sadness remained.

"I don't know," I shrugged perplexed.

She tilted her head. The gesture of a child turning over an answer that doesn't fit the expected format.

Your hand reached my shoulder. Gentle. "Come on," you said.

I let you guide me away. Away from the child, away from the mirror, away from the simple question that had cut deeper than any cultivation technique. 

The child waved. The small, casual wave of someone who has said what they wanted to say and is done.

I didn't wave back. I should have. But my hands were at my sides and my chest was full of something that might have been sadness. 

You walked closer after that.

*Why are you so sad?*

I didn't have an answer.
