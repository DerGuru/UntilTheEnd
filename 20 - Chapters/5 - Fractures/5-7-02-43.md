You'd been carrying something all morning. I could see the weight: in your posture, in the way your pack sat on your shoulders, in the tightness of your jaw.

"You said something," you started. "During the — during what happened."

I looked at you. "I don't remember anything happening."

"I know."

The trail was narrow here, winding between old pines, the kind of trail that forces single file and makes conversation a thing shouted over shoulders. You were behind me. I slowed to walk beside you where the path widened, because whatever you were about to say deserved eye contact, even if you seemed to be avoiding it.

"What did I say?"

You reached into your pack. The notebook — the waterproof case, the worn pages, the careful handwriting that documented everything because documentation was survival. You opened it to a page near the back, where the writing was different from your usual precise, organized script. Hurried. Uneven. Written with the shaking hand of someone who knew you had to record what you'd heard before it was gone, before your own memory softened the edges.

"These aren't your words," you said. "Not your syntax. Not how you speak. It was—" You searched. "Formal. Old. Like someone reading from a text that was written before the language finished becoming itself."

You read.

Quietly, without projection or performance. In the careful voice of someone reading a translation that might not survive the journey between languages.

The words were mine and not mine.

I heard them. Recognized them the way you recognize a room you've been in once, years ago. The shape is right, the proportions are right, the feeling of the space is right, but the details are wrong, or missing, or belonged to a version of the room that no longer exists.

Old words. Words that used formal constructions I didn't use, that arranged meaning in patterns that my everyday speech didn't follow, that carried the weight of language used by someone who had been speaking for much longer than a human lifetime.

I can't reproduce them here. The wall would take them if I tried, yes. But more than that, they weren't meant to be reproduced. They were meant to be heard once, in the voice of a man reading them from a shaking page, on a mountain trail, with a fox-spirit three meters behind and pines overhead and the morning light doing its precise, detailed work on every surface.

They were about existence. About being. About the experience of being something that had always been and would always be and knew this about itself without needing to be told.

They were about loneliness.

Deeper than the social loneliness of a person without company. The structural loneliness of something singular, the loneliness of the only thing of its kind, the loneliness of something that couldn't be companioned because there was nothing else like it to be a companion.

They were about choosing. About choosing to be small. About choosing to forget. About choosing to live in a body and a time and a place that were limited because limitation was the only way to experience what unlimited things cannot experience.

Finitude. The words — my words, the words of whatever part of me had spoken through the crack — were about the beauty of finitude. Of endings. Of things that mattered because they stopped.

Your voice trailed off. You closed the notebook. Looked at me.

I had stopped walking.

"That sounds—" I started. The sentence stalled. The wall had nothing to do with it; the wall was quiet, apparently uninterested in emotional responses to information that had already been deleted. The sentence stalled because I was looking for a word and the word was hiding.

"Right," I said. "It sounds right. I don't know why."

The feeling in my chest responded. Without identification, without the sudden click of recognition or the dramatic revelation of meaning that stories promise when characters encounter truth. With resonance. The deep, structural vibration of a tuning fork meeting its frequency. The words were true. I didn't know them, couldn't claim them, couldn't reconstruct the mind that had produced them or the moment in which they'd been spoken. But they were true in the way that mathematics is true — independent of the person perceiving them, valid regardless of context.

"Who said that?" I asked.

"You did."

"Not me. Not—" I gestured at myself. At the body, the face, the person standing on a mountain trail wearing a stolen outer robe and carrying a headache and feeling cold for the first time in her life. "Not this."

"No," you agreed. "Not this."

The crack pulsed. I pressed against it, the mental equivalent of pushing a loose tooth, the habitual gesture of someone exploring a change. The crack gave. Barely. Too little to see through. But enough to feel the draft and the draft carried a temperature and the temperature was old.

Ancient. Lonely. Certain.

The same qualities as the feeling. The same resonance. The same bass note vibration that lived below thought and above silence and hummed with the confidence of something that knew what it was even if I didn't.

"Something behind the crack said those words," I said.

"Yes."

"And it's me."

"Yes."

"But I don't know it."

"Also yes."

"That's enough for now," you said.

You closed the notebook. Folded it into the case. Secured the case in your pack.

No pressing. No follow-up.

"The fox is still following us," you said.

I looked back. The fox-spirit was there, three meters behind, amber eyes, the patient accompaniment of a creature that had decided we were going somewhere worth going.

"Yes," I said.

We walked. The words settled. The crack pulsed. The feeling hummed.

And somewhere deep — deeper than the wall, deeper than the guardian, deeper than the architecture of self-protection that had been building and breaking and rebuilding since the beginning — something shifted. A vibration. Low. Steady.

The fox-spirit's ears flicked forward.
