I woke warm. The cold was still there, underneath, the way it had been since the clearing. But the fire I'd fed before sleeping had done its work. The coals still glowed, a low orange pulse in the grey, and the heat reached my face and hands the way a familiar voice reaches you before you open your eyes. Present. Steady. Proof that something you'd taken care of had lasted through the night.

Your robe was around me. The smell of it — woodsmoke and dust and the faint trace of the pine resin you'd used on your boots — was still unfamiliar. I'd had it for one day and already the weight of it on my shoulders felt like something I'd notice the absence of. The ground was hard beneath my back, but the hardness was known. The cold was known. The weight of the robe was not known — not yet — but it was learning to be.

For a moment, it was almost comfortable.

Then the morning came in full.

The cold sharpened. The bird calls that had been soft background in the last seconds of sleep separated into individual voices, each one carrying pitch and rhythm and direction. The light pressed through my eyelids — the pre-dawn grey that used to be mere backdrop was now sensation sharp and exhausting. The new condition I needed to learn, how to deal with.

I opened my eyes. The world was there. Even sharper and louder. More detailed than any world had a right to be at this hour.

The crack was there too.

Like a loose tooth. I'd had a loose tooth as a child, one of the early ones, the kind that loosened gradually and then sat in the gum for days, moving slightly with every touch of the tongue, not painful but insistent, demanding attention through the simple fact of its wrongness. The crack was like that. A thing I could press against — mentally, with the interior equivalent of a tongue — and feel move. Feel give. Feel the resistance of something that was still attached. But eventually wouldn't be anymore?

I pressed against it. Gently. Just to feel it, just to confirm it's still loose, just to verify that the change is real and continuing.

The crack responded. With a draft. The interior equivalent of air through a gap, the faintest difference in pressure, the subtlest indication that there was another side and that the other side had its own atmosphere.

Something on the other side was breathing.

I sat up and the robe fell from my shoulders.

Movement at the edge of vision. A fox-spirit.

Small. Reddish-brown, with a white chin and ears too large for its skull. The kind of minor spirit beast that lives in the gap between wildlife and cultivation — too simple for real intelligence, but sensitive enough to read qi the way a dog reads scent. I'd seen it once before, weeks ago, stirring in the undergrowth when my emotions had done something loud to the landscape. It had stared at me from its hiding place, head tilted, ears forward, and then vanished into the brush before I could decide whether it mattered.

Now it was lying in the grass three meters from my bedroll. Not hiding. Not running. Just there, the way a cat lies when it had chosen a spot and the spot happens to be yours.

It had come closer. Considerably.

I didn't move toward it. Didn't reach out. Just sat with the robe pooled around me. The cold on my arms and a fox-spirit close enough to touch, watching me with amber eyes that seemed to have made a decision about what I was. Spirit beasts aren't the usual animals. They read qi the way people read faces — and whatever this one read in me overnight had changed its assessment from *keep distance* to *stay*.

You were already up and moved around camp with the practiced economy of a man performing the morning routine, but differently. Softer. Your gestures had a care to them that hadn't been there before the clearing. Some kind of deliberateness, as if you were handling fragile things near a fragile person and wanted to make sure every motion communicated gentleness.

You walked to the fire kit. Then to the water skin. Then to the packs. And each time your path curved around the spot where the fox-spirit lay — a wide arc, unconscious, the kind of detour a body makes around something it senses but the mind hasn't registered yet.

You didn't look at it. Didn't pause. Your eyes passed over the patch of grass as if it held nothing but grass.

That was interesting. The fox-spirit had chosen for you, not to see it. An old trick — minor spirit beasts rarely had the qi for true concealment, but a fox could blur the edges of its presence well enough to slide past mortal perception. But your body knew. The arc in your path said so.

The careful way you moved said also something else entirely. You'd paid a price. The question — the one I couldn't remember — had cost you something. Something behind your eyes that hadn't been there before the clearing.

You handed me the tea. And I was grateful for its warmth. The gesture was the same as always, but the feeling behind it was different. The tea was still tea, but the act of giving it had changed. Like you were deliberately offering something that might help. A small kindness that said, without words, *I want you to be okay.*

"How are you?" you asked. The question was genuine. You wanted to know. You needed to know, the way a surgeon needs to know whether the patient survived the surgery.

"I can still feel the crack," I said.

You nodded. Set down your own cup. Reached for the notebook but hesitated. You didn't open it. This was new. Before the clearing, the notebook would have been open already, the pen moving, the observation transmuting into record with the automatic efficiency of a habit built on documentation. Now you hesitated. As if recording me had become something that required something that hadn't been necessary before.

"Like a door that's swollen shut," I continued. "I can feel it move when I lean into it. But it won't open."

"Does it hurt?"

I considered. "Not yet."

You took a breath. Let it out. The exhale carried something. The residue of something you wanted to say and were choosing not to, the disciplined silence of a man who had learned that sometimes the kindest thing you could do was not provide information.

We packed. The fox-spirit watched, amber eyes tracking our movements with the close interest of something that had decided we were worth watching. When we lifted our packs and turned toward the trail, it stood. Stretched — the long, full-body stretch of a creature waking fully. And followed.

Ten meters behind. The distance of curiosity without commitment.

We walked. East — still east, always east, the world's insistence on directing me toward isolation continuing with the quiet authority of a current beneath the surface. But the walking felt different now. Not the mechanical forward motion of our previous days, not the determined, head-down, one-foot-then-the-other progression of someone moving because stopping was worse.

This was walking with something in my chest. A draft where there used to be a wall. A fox where there used to be empty grass. A world that tasted like itself for the first time.

Behind us, three sect cultivators appeared at the tree line, watching us with the patient attention of people who had upgraded their classification of me from threat to phenomenon.

Forward. The only direction that mattered.
