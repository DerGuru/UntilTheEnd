The morning was cold and clear and the kind of beautiful that mountains do best. No drama, no spectacle, just rock, sky and light. Xiǎohú stretched and yawned. 

You packed efficiently. You were always efficient, but this morning the efficiency had a different quality. A finality, a sense of completion. As if something had ended during the night. The morning was about beginning what came next.

You closed your notebook.

You secured it in the waterproof case and put the case in your pack. The notebook was full. Every page, front and back, the dense, precise handwriting of a man who treated blank space as wasted potential, carried the accumulated record of everything that had happened since we'd entered each other's lives for the third time.

Everything you'd observed. Everything you'd measured. Everything you'd asked me and everything I'd forgotten. Everything the world had done in response to whatever I was becoming.

The notebook was complete.

You'd done everything you could. The documentation was thorough, the observations were precise, the analysis was as far as mortal analysis could go. You'd pushed me to the crack. You'd gotten the feeling through the wall. You'd sacrificed something important. Innocence, the clean conscience of a man who had never deliberately caused harm. But your sacrifice had produced a result.

From here, it was mine.

Mine. The crack was mine. The feeling was mine. The reflection that smiled before I smiled was mine. The next step, whatever it was, wherever it led, however much it cost — that was mine to take.

You knew this. I saw it in the economy of your movements, in the closed notebook, in the way you shouldered your pack and looked at the trail and waited for me to choose the direction.

I went forward. The world's insistence on directing me toward isolation had softened since the river. The paths were paths again, going where they geographically went, behaving like terrain rather than like forced arguments.

Forward was enough. Forward was what I had.

I walked. You walked beside me. Three meters, the old distance, the companionable distance that had survived every impossible thing that had happened. Xiǎohú trotted beside us. The world was sharp and loud and cold and full of details.

The sky was clear. The trail wound between pines, dark green against grey rock, the stubborn persistence of trees that don't shed what they have. The air tasted of stone and altitude and the emptiness of high places where the atmosphere admits to being thin.

Somewhere behind us, the sect observers followed. Still there. Still patient.

Let them watch.

My head hurt. The river had quieted it for a while but it hadn't lasted. The headache was part of me now, the way the cold was part of me, the way the sharpness was part of me.

I rubbed my temples. Walked.

The world turned. The mountains stood. The trail went forward. And forward was where I went.

When we made camp again, you cooked. Rice, with some dried mushrooms from the market and a handful of wild greens. The food was warm and adequate. I tasted every component of it with the overwhelming, beautiful, exhausting sensitivity that had become my relationship with physical experience.

The fire burned. Burned well. Lit at the first try. The debt apparently satisfied for the moment, having moved on to other accounts or simply pausing before the next installment.

I looked at the fire. At the coals. At the small, patient geometry of heat doing its work.

Then I looked up. At the sky. At the stars. At the void between them that wasn't empty but full of things too far away to be perceived by eyes that hadn't been designed for that kind of seeing.

Something up there. Or behind me. Or inside me. Or all three, the distinctions collapsing into irrelevance in the face of a truth I could feel but not articulate.

I put my hand on my chest. The heartbeat. The feeling. The warmth of being myself in a world that was still, despite everything, willing to hold me.

Xiǎohú curled near the fire, amber eyes reflecting flame. 

We sat in the light and the dark, in the warm and the cold, in the known and the unknown.

Tomorrow we would walk again. The crack would pulse. The feeling would hum. The world would be sharper than it had been and louder than it should be and full of small unfairnesses that accumulated like rain in a basin.

But tonight — right now — the fire was warm and the rice was good and the stars didn't care what I had done to a mountain and Xiǎohú was sleeping. And somewhere deep — deeper than the wall, deeper than the crack, in the bedrock of whatever I was — something shifted. The feeling pulsed. Stronger than before. Stronger than it should have been.

The headache spiked. My hand went to my temple. The fire dimmed — or my eyes did.

You looked at me with the attention of a man who had just noticed something in my face that I couldn't feel.

"Yun," you said. Quiet. Careful.

"What?"

You didn't answer. You looked at my hands. At the ground beneath them. At the air around my shoulders.

Xiǎohú opened one eye. Her ears flattened.

I didn't know what they were seeing. I didn't know what was changing. The crack pulsed. It carried something heavier than warmth, something that pressed against the inside of my ribs with the weight of a question I couldn't ask and an answer I couldn't survive.

The fire cracked. The coals shifted. The stars burned with the indifferent patience of things that had seen this before.

My hands were shaking.
