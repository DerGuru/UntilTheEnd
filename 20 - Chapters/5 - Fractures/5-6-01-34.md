I sat cross-legged on the rock shelf above our camp, eyes closed, breathing regulated, doing everything that the cultivation texts said to do when seeking internal clarity. Breathe. Center. Focus. Turn awareness inward. Find the point where consciousness meets the deeper architecture. Observe.

A wall. Dressed as emptiness. A closed door painted to look like an open field.

I tried anyway. Every morning, for the past days, I'd sat here and tried. The rational approach, to reconstruct what you had told me. What had been said. What it meant. What truths had been offered and taken. If I thought hard enough, carefully enough, approached from the right angle with the right technique, maybe the wall would thin. Maybe a crack would appear. Maybe some fragment of what had been erased would surface.

Think. Remember — briefly, a flicker, like a word on the tip of the tongue, present for a fraction of a second before it dissolved. Forget. The frustration of losing it. The ground tension, the seismic whisper beneath my feet that said the frustration was translating again. Breathe. Suppress. Try again.

Think. Remember. Forget. Frustrate. Try again.

Each attempt ended exactly where the last one had, with the same wall, the same gap that pulsed behind my eyes like a second heartbeat.

I saw you watching. Trying, to be present without registering, occupying space without demanding attention. You sat thirty meters below, at the base of the slope, ostensibly reading. Your notebook was open. But your eyes weren't on it.

You were studying me.

Not the way you studied texts or maps or the tactical details of terrain. You were studying me the way a sculptor studies a cracked stone, with the patient attention of someone searching for the fault line, the flaw, the place where pressure had done its hidden work.

I was on the rock shelf, closed my eyes again and started a new cycle of think-remember-forget-frustration with the stubborn persistence of someone who has no other strategy and therefore commits fully to the one that doesn't work.

You know what they say about stubbornly trying the same thing over and over, expecting different results? It's the definition of insanity.

Here is what I know now, from what you told me later and what I've pieced together.

You watched for two hours. Two hours of me sitting still, breathing, trying, failing. You recorded the cycle. Timed it, even. About ninety seconds, on average from attempt to failure. A neat package of intention and erasure that repeated with the regularity of tides.

You had opened your notebook to the pages from the past weeks. Your observations. Your records. The careful documentation of everything I'd done, everything I'd said, everything that had happened *to*, *around* and *because* of me since we'd entered these mountains.

You were looking for a pattern. Not in the events — you had those mapped, cross-referenced, annotated. You were looking for a pattern in what survived.

The trees I'd split: I remembered being angry and the trees split. Remembered standing there, remembered the sound. But the how — the mechanics, the thing inside me that had reached out and pulled wood apart along the grain — that was gone. What remained was the anger. Clean, contextless, hovering in my chest without an anchor.

The rain: I remembered the grief and the rain falling from a clear sky. Remembered being wet. But the connection between my grief and the weather, the moment where one became the other — erased. The sadness was still there. The sadness had nowhere to go.

The tremor: I remembered my frustration and the ground shaking. Remembered setting down the scrolls, stone move. But the way it had translated from emotion into force — I couldn't reconstruct it. The frustration remained. The path it had taken was blank.

The field: I remembered the field, mostly, in the way you remember a fever dream, but the mechanics, the raw sensation of locking reality, the understanding of what I'd done — those were fading. Blurring. The edges softening every day, the details dissolving. But the horror of it remained. The feeling of being something, I didn't want to be, remained.

The thoughts were deleted. The emotions were not.

Whatever governed the erasure — whatever architecture lay below my awareness, whatever guardian operated beneath my consent — it deleted content but not affect. It erased what I knew but left the tremor in my hands, the tightness behind my ribs. Every experience was being stripped of its meaning while the emotional residue was left intact, like removing the text of a book but leaving the stains where tears had fallen on the pages.

This was not random. This was design.

And if the design preserved emotions while deleting thoughts, then the path to reaching me — to getting truth past the wall, past the erasure, past the guardian that stood between me and understanding — was not through thinking.

It was through feeling.

You sat with this realization for forty minutes. Forty minutes of staring in thoughts at your notebook, understanding what it meant, understanding what you would have to do and hating every implication.

Because making someone feel something deeply, viscerally, below the threshold of thought, is not a kindness. Making someone feel the thing that you needed me to feel was an act that required cruelty. Which was not in your repertoire and adding it to your repertoire was a cost you weren't ready to pay.

You closed your notebook.

You didn't tell me. Not yet. You stood, stretched and walked back to the camp with the casual gait of someone who had not just decided to do the worst thing you would ever do to someone you cared about. You boiled water. You prepared rice. You performed the routine of evening with the precise movements of a man who needed your hands busy while your mind worked.

I came down from the rock shelf at dusk. Tired. Headache pounding. The exhaustion of failed meditation, not physical, not even mental, but something deeper, something that felt like having pushed against a locked door for hours and having nothing to show for it except sore shoulders and the knowledge that the door was still locked.

"Anything?" you asked.

"No." I answered tiredly.

You handed me rice. I ate. The food was warm and tasted of nothing especially and served its purpose, which was to keep my body functional while my mind ran the same failing pattern.

"It doesn't work," I said. It was the verbal equivalent of pressing a bruise, confirming the pain was still there, confirming nothing had changed, confirming the cycle.

"No," you agreed. "It doesn't."

Something in your voice. A color I hadn't heard before, underneath the agreement.

I almost asked. Almost pushed. The question was right there — *What aren't you telling me?* — formed, ready and loaded with the accusatory weight of a person who has learned that the people around her keep saying things she forgets.

But I didn't ask. I ate my rice, worked at your meridians, while my headache throbbed. 

The night came down like a curtain and behind it, somewhere in the space between your silence and my exhaustion, a plan was forming that I didn't know about and wouldn't have approved of. But when it arrived, it would shatter something that had needed shattering since before either of us had understood what the word meant.
