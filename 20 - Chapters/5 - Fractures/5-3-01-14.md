The world was coming apart.

We heard the first reports in a trading town three days south of the library — a narrow place built along a river crossing, the kind of settlement that exists because geography put it at the intersection of two roads and commerce filled the gap. Merchants, travelers, a few lower-ranked cultivators passing through. The usual traffic.

Except the usual traffic was frightened.

A tea seller told us about a village to the east where the qi had reversed direction overnight. Foundation-level cultivators — people who'd been quietly building their channels for decades — woke up with their cultivation destabilized. Meridians collapsed. Stored qi scattered. Some lost everything they'd built. Some even died.

A traveling merchant described lights in the sky above the northern mountains. Qi currents that were normally invisible had become visible — threads of energy writhing across the clouds like veins laid bare beneath split skin. They lasted hours. He'd watched from a hilltop and said it looked like the sky was bleeding.

A sect courier, passing through with dispatches, mentioned that two regional sects had sealed their gates. No entry, no exit. They were calling it a spiritual climate event — the bureaucratic language of people who have no idea what's happening and need a phrase that sounds like they do.

You sat at the tea house table and listened to all of it with the composed attention of someone cataloguing evidence.

"The whole region," you said, after the courier had left. "Can you determine how far?"

I'd been feeling it for days. The qi disturbances weren't local anymore — they rippled outward from... from the places I'd been. Not immediately. Not obviously. But the timing matched, the geography correlated and the pattern was unmistakable once you knew what to look for.

"Not just the region. But weaker the farther you go," I said.

You looked at me across the teacups. There was a question in your eyes that you didn't ask, because you already knew the answer and asking would have forced me to say it in a public tea house surrounded by frightened merchants, which was a particularly poor idea even by our standards.

You drank your tea instead. I drank mine. The tea seller came by twice to see if we wanted more and twice I waved him away, because whatever was happening in the world outside this window was not going to be improved by putting more tea into our cups.

The sky that evening was wrong. Not the stars — those were correct — but the light. The sunset lasted too long, the colors shifted through a spectrum that didn't match the season and for about twenty minutes, the entire western horizon looked like a bruise. Purple shading to green, with a thin line of gold along the bottom that had no business being there.

You stood at the window of our room and watched it.

"Beautiful," you said.

"Dangerous."

"Can't it be both?"

I didn't answer. I guess it could.

The next morning brought more reports. A road that had been straight for a century now curved around a hill that travelers swore hadn't been there last week. A waterfall running upward. A forest grove that had doubled in size overnight, with new trees looking as old and established as the originals.

Anomalies. My signature. Radiating outward like waves from a stone dropped in water, except the stone was me and the water was reality and the waves were getting bigger with every site I visited, every question I asked, every step closer I took to understanding what I was.

I was making it worse.

I watched the road ahead. Dust. Hills. The world going on regardless.

The realization arrived without drama. No epiphany, no thunderclap, no moment of devastating clarity. Just the quiet click of a piece falling into place — the same cold click I'd been hearing since the first shrine, since the first wrong path, since the first stone that hummed when I touched it.

I was the source. My investigation was amplifying the disturbances. Every time I probed, every time I pushed, every time I reached for the truth, reality buckled a little more. The adjustments were cascading and the cascades were reaching further. And people were dying.

People were dying because I was looking for answers.

I said nothing to you. You already came to the same conclusion — I could see it in the way you studied maps now, tracing our path with your finger, noting the correlation between our route and the epicenters of reported anomalies. You hadn't said it. You were waiting for me to say it.

I wasn't ready to say it.
