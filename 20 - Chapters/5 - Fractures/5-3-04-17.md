We were walking through a stretch of open grassland. Flat, exposed, the kind of terrain that makes you visible from every direction and offers nothing to hide behind. Late afternoon. The sky was clear. Normal wind from the east.

Then the wind stopped.

Not gradually. Not the way wind tapers off as weather patterns shift. It stopped the way sound stops when someone covers your ears. Completely, immediately, with a suddenness that makes the silence physical. The grass, which had been flowing in long silver waves, went rigid. Every blade pointing straight up, frozen mid-sway.

The buildup prickled through my feet before I saw it. The qi in the earth, normally quiescent in grassland, spread thin and even like a calm sea, was concentrating. Pooling. Gathering toward a point about forty meters ahead of us, where the ground began to hum.

"Move," I said.

You didn't. You were staring at the sky, where the qi currents had become visible. The same phenomenon the merchant had described in the northern mountains. Threads of energy, normally invisible, drawn out of their channels by the concentration below, pulled toward the focal point like iron filings toward a magnet. They writhed. They pulsed. They were beautiful in the way that natural disasters are beautiful, which is to say from a distance — that we didn't have.

"Xu Ran. Move."

You moved. Too late.

The release was silent. No explosion, no crack, no dramatic sound. Just a pulse, a wave of qi pressure that expanded from the focal point like a shockwave underwater, invisible but absolute, pushing through the air with a force that had nothing to do with wind and everything to do with the fundamental fabric of reality being compressed and released in a space too small for the energy involved.

The grass flattened in a perfect circle. Forty meters of radius, pressed to the earth as if by an oversized invisible elephant foot. The air became thick with pressure, the qi so dense it was almost solid, almost breathable, almost a substance you could touch.

You were inside the circle.

For a moment, less than a heartbeat, less than the space between two thoughts, the world stuttered. Like a bird stutters in flight when a wing catches wrong and the body drops a centimeter before correcting. Reality hesitated. The pressure wave, which was rolling toward you with the serene inevitability of an avalanche, seemed to — consider.

As if something was trying to protect you.

The hesitation was real. A tug caught in my chest, a pull in the qi that lived in the deepest layers of my core, a reaching that wasn't mine but came from me, as if the thing that kept me alive was trying to extend its reach, to grab you, to include you in whatever bubble of privileged existence surrounded me.

It failed.

The pull wasn't strong enough. The hesitation broke. The pressure wave continued and you were in its path and your body — once Nascent Soul, now merely cultivator-trained, developed but limited, the body of a man who had strength but not immunity — was about to receive a force that didn't care about your strengths.

I moved.

Movement. Pure, mechanical, older than language, the reflex of a body that has been keeping people alive for longer than it can remember. One step. One gesture. One hand, extended, palm flat, between you and the wave.

The wave hit my palm and dissolved. The qi scattered, not absorbed, not redirected, but unmade, broken apart at the foundational level, reduced from a wave to noise in the space of a breath. The pressure evaporated. The grass sprang back. The air thinned.

You had dropped to the ground. Your own legs had given out, the involuntary collapse of a body that had braced for impact and then received nothing, which is sometimes worse because the bracing has to go somewhere.

I pulled you up. One hand on your arm. You were lighter than I expected, or I was stronger, which was more likely and the difference between the two was the difference between your world and mine.

"Are you okay?" I said.

You weren't. Your hands shook. Your color was wrong. Pale, the white-grey of someone whose body has braced for a death that didn't come, all that readiness with nowhere to go. Your breathing came in short bursts that you tried to control and couldn't.

"You're fine," I said, because saying it might make it true and because I had no other words available.

You sat on the flattened grass and pressed your palms against your knees and breathed until the breathing became voluntary again instead of frantic. I sat across from you. Waiting. Watching you wrestle with something that won't fit into the shapes you've been using to understand the world.

The smell of turned earth. Dust. The sharp ozone-tang of qi that has been released in quantity. Sharp, metallic, the smell of static electricity with a texture beneath it that normal ozone doesn't have.

And underneath the ozone, the smell triggered something.

Dust. Turned earth. The raw quality of air after impact.

I'd smelled this before.

The memory arrived without invitation. Slower this time, more deliberate, as if the part of me that stored memories had decided this one deserved a proper introduction.

Wei. First year. The spirit beast that had come too close to the village. The one that shouldn't have been there. Wrong territory, wrong season, driven from its usual range by something neither of us could name. Wei had been standing in the path. Too slow to move. 

I'd moved then, too. Same reflex. Same blind urgency. Same hand between the vulnerable thing and the thing that could destroy it. I'd killed the spirit beast with a gesture that had cost me nothing and had cost the surrounding forest a season of growth. The qi backlash had drained the soil for fifty meters in every direction.

The cost. Always the cost.

*Again,* I thought. The word was a weight. *Again. The same pattern. The same geometry. Someone I am close to, standing where harm arrives and me between them and the aftermath always worse than the event.*

Wei had looked at me that day with the expression of a child who has just learned something about the world that the world would rather he hadn't. Worse than fear. Awe.

You were looking at me the same way now.

"You stopped it," you said.

"Yes."

"With one hand."

"Yes."

"That wave would have — " You stopped. Calculated. Reconsidered, but did not finish the sentence. 

"Yes."

You looked at your own hands. Steady now, or nearly. The shaking had subsided to a fine tremor that you masked by gripping your knee.

"For you," you said. "How significant was it?"

I looked at the flattened grass. The circle was already recovering, blades straightening, the earth undisturbed, as if the pressure wave had been a dream the ground had already forgotten.

"Manageable," I said.

"Like when you stopped the tribulation, when I was about to enter Nascent Soul?"

"Similar in scale, but different in kind."

You nodded. Slowly. The nod of a man manifesting his understanding. The woman sitting across from you had stopped a force that could have killed you. Twice. With one hand. Without seeming effort. And she was now watching you with the cold attention of someone examining a blade for cracks after a strike.

The gap between us — the gap that had always been there, that we'd been stepping around for months — was visible now. Not a gap. A chasm. You were mortal. I was whatever I was. You lived in a world governed by force and consequence and the reasonable expectation that sufficient force produces sufficient damage. I lived in a world where force was a suggestion and damage was something that happened to other people.

I watched your hands stop shaking.

You didn't speak.

You just sat there, adjusting and when it was complete, or complete enough, you stood, shouldered your pack and looked south.

"How far to the next town?" you asked.

Your voice was steady. Your hands were still.

Something in my chest — something that had nothing to do with qi or cultivation or the deep, nameless thing that kept me alive — loosened. Not much. A centimeter of slack in a rope.

"Half a day," I said.

We walked.
