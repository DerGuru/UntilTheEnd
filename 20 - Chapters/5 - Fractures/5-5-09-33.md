Two days passed. The trail kept going east and we kept following it. The small wrongnesses kept accumulating. A root caught my foot where no root should have been. A branch gave way under your hand when you reached for balance. A ledge crumbled at the exact point where we'd planned to rest. The persistent, nagging rhythm of a world that had decided to be slightly less cooperative.

The flask kept leaking. I emptied it, dried it, refilled it. It leaked again. Same seam. Same thin film of moisture. I checked the stitching. Intact, tight, no visible flaw. The leak had no mechanical cause. The leak existed because things were going wrong, one small thing at a time, in the steady rhythm of something being repaid.

The first evening, you sat cross-legged by the fire with the flask in your lap, a needle between your teeth, and the heavy thread from your repair kit laid out on a flat stone. You turned the flask over. Ran your thumb along the seam. Held it up to the firelight and squinted at it with the focused displeasure of a man who could not find the thing he was looking for and resented the thing for hiding.

"The stitching is fine," I said.

"The stitching is fine," you agreed, around the needle.

You re-stitched it anyway and tied it off. You filled the flask from the stream and set it upright on the stone.

We both watched it.

For two minutes, the flask sat there, sealed, re-stitched, repaired. Then the thin film appeared. Same seam. Same slow weep of moisture through leather that had no reason to weep.

"I'll try wax tomorrow," you said.

You wouldn't try wax tomorrow. We both knew this. The flask wasn't leaking because of a flaw in the stitching or a gap in the leather or any cause that wax or thread or competence could address. But saying *I'll try wax tomorrow* was easier than saying *the universe has decided your flask leaks now and there's nothing either of us can do about it,* and easier was what we had.

On the second evening, you built the fire. You were good at fires. I'd watched you build a hundred fires. You'd never failed. You struck the steel. Sparks landed on tinder. And the tinder didn't catch.

You adjusted. Blew gently. Reconfigured the kindling. Drier pieces, smaller pieces, positioned to catch the spark from a better angle. Struck again.

Nothing.

Your jaw tightened. The familiar tightening of frustration that you managed with discipline, as you managed everything — by acknowledging the emotion, categorizing it and proceeding through it with the methodical patience of someone who did not permit obstacles to become excuses.

Third try. New tinder. Fresh sparks. Careful breath.

Nothing.

Fourth try. You rearranged everything. Stripped the bark thinner. Built a nest of the driest fibers you could find, the kind that should catch from a hard look, let alone a steel spark. Struck. The sparks landed true. The fibers glowed for half a second, a faint orange thread of heat — and went out.

I watched you a while longer until you sat back. You looked at the cold tinder. Looked at your hands. Looked at the fire steel as if it had personally betrayed you.

Then you looked at me.

You didn't ask. You just looked. 

"Let me," I said.

You moved. I knelt where you'd been kneeling. The tinder nest was good — it should have caught three tries ago. I reached for the smallest thread of qi I could manage, the barest whisper of intent, and fed it into the kindling. Not fire. Just heat. Just the suggestion of combustion, the nudge that the tinder had been refusing to accept from steel and spark.

The fire caught. First hesitantly then eagerly. As if it had been waiting for permission rather than ignition.

You watched it burn. Your face was neutral in the way that faces are neutral when the person behind them is working very hard to make them so.

I watched the flames grow from the initial hesitant flicker to the steady, self-sustaining burn that meant the fire would last. The wood crackled. The warmth expanded. Normal. Ordinary. The most basic human technology: controlled combustion, heat, light, the domestication of a force that predated every other tool.

"It's happening to you too," I said.

You looked up from the fire. The flames reflected in your eyes — orange, shifting, alive in the way that fire is alive, which is not how people are alive but close enough to be mistaken.

"The stone yesterday. The branch today. The fire."

"I noticed."

"I'm sorry."

You considered this. The apology. The weight of it.

"Don't apologize for things you can't control," you said. "Save the apologies for things you choose."

You cooked. I watched the fire and thought about the mountain.

Not the missing piece. I'd spent enough time sitting with the knowledge that I'd removed three hundred meters of geography from the world's inventory. I thought about the mountain that remained. The part that was still there. Still standing. Still being a mountain as mountains are supposed to be.

The mountain hadn't moved. Hadn't crumbled. Hadn't collapsed into the gap I'd left. It had simply continued, accommodating the absence the way old things accommodate damage. By being.

There was a lesson there. Probably. But I wasn't in the mood for lessons.

You handed me a bowl. Rice with dried mushrooms, a variation, a small one, the kind of minor adjustment to routine that you introduced periodically because you understood that monotony was its own form of erosion. The mushrooms were chewy and tasted of earth, smoke and a deep umami.

I ate mechanically. Fuel. Maintenance. 

You ate too. Your injured finger, had scabbed over. The scab was dark and small and completely ordinary, the kind of minor wound that every body accumulates and heals and forgets. You hadn't forgotten it. Your eyes went to it periodically, checking, confirming, reassuring yourself that the damage was still there, that the scab was real, that the world was playing by its normal rules.

"I could fix that," I said.

You looked at me and back at your finger.

"No."

"It would take a second."

"I know." You turned the scab toward the firelight and studied it with attention. As if the small dark ridge of dried blood contained information worth extracting. "I'd rather keep it."

I waited.

"It reminds me," you said, "I can be hurt. I can heal. The scab is proof of both." You flexed the finger. The scab stretched but held. "I'd like to keep the proof."

As if your qi-system wasn't already a monument to the fact that you could be hurt. The thought occurred to me instantly, but I kept it to myself. You'd made your point. The scab would stay.

After dinner, we sat for your daily round of qi-plumbing. The fire burned. The night settled around us with the patient darkness of altitude, where the sky is close and the stars are sharp and the sense of being exposed, small, visible, unprotected, is acute.

I should have thought about what I was. That was the question that had been driving everything. The investigations, the ruins, the scrolls, the cultivation techniques that defied categorization. *What am I?* A question that assumed an answer existed, that assumed the answer was knowable, that assumed knowing would help.

But sitting there, watching the fire, eating rice, feeling the headache pulse and knowing that a section of mountain was missing because of me, the question changed.

It wasn't just *what am I?* anymore.

It added *How much can I destroy?* to the list.

I analyzed in the cold, grounded way that a person with a weapon assesses its range. I had split trees. Had made weather. Had locked reality. Had erased a mountain flank from existence so completely that the world didn't remember it had been there.

The scale was increasing. Each event was larger than the last. The trajectory was clear. Upward. And the question of where the trajectory ended was not philosophical. It was geographical.

How many mountains. How many valleys. How many villages with red circles on a sect map.

But beneath that question, quieter, more personal, more terrifying in its simplicity came another one.

*Can I stop?*

Not *will I stop* — that was about choice, which was becoming academic. Can. The bare physical question. Did whatever I was becoming have an off switch, a limit. Would I get to a point where the escalation reached its ceiling and the power stabilized? Where the world stopped being a thing I accidentally edited?

I didn't know.

*Not now,* the thought came and I let it come. It settled into the place where the other thoughts lived — the growing archive of things I wasn't ready to examine, yet. The shelf where I put the questions that didn't have answers and might not ever have answers. And their not-having-answers was itself a kind of answer.

"I'm eating," I said, to nobody. To you, who were there. To the mountain, which was partially there.

Your mouth did the thin thing. Not humor. Not quite. The acknowledgment of humor's possibility, seen from a distance, through fog.

"You're eating," you confirmed.

"Yes. I am eating."

"Good."

I finished the rice and set down the bowl. The fire crackled and spat a spark that landed on the ground where it died.

Tomorrow we would walk. East, most likely, because east was where the world wanted me to go and I'd stopped arguing. We would walk and the small wrongnesses would accumulate like rain in a basin, filling slowly, rising, approaching a level that I couldn't yet see.

But for now — right now — I was eating. I was warm. The fire was lit, even if it had taken multiple tries and some qi. The stars were out and they didn't care what I'd done to a mountain or what a mountain had done to me.

Stars are good at not caring. It's their primary function.

My head hurt.
