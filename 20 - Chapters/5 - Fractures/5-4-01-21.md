We stopped near midnight. A clearing at the edge of a ridge, sheltered by an oak broad enough that its canopy swallowed the firelight before it reached the sky. The ground beneath it was dry, layered with seasons of fallen leaves compressed into a surface that gave slightly underfoot, like walking on leather.

You built the fire. Efficient, practiced — the ritual of months. Kindling first, then the arrangement of larger pieces that maximized burn time over brightness. A traveler's fire, not a beacon. The flames caught and steadied, and within minutes we had warmth.

The oak creaked. Not from wind — from the heat reaching wood that had been cooling all day. The sound was slow, deep, the complaint of something large adjusting to a change too small for anything smaller to notice. I pressed my back against the trunk and felt the vibration through the bark, the faint pulse of a living thing responding.

Insects arrived. Moths first, drawn to the light with the blind devotion of creatures whose navigation relied on sources they couldn't distinguish from stars. Then smaller things — gnats, midges, the unnamed fleet of night-fliers that materialized wherever warmth disrupted the dark. They circled the flames in orbits that tightened until wings met heat and the orbit ended.

Above us, clouds. Low and thick, the kind that erased the sky and turned the night into a ceiling. No stars. No moon. The world contracted to the small radius of firelight, and beyond it, nothing. The darkness beyond the clearing full of sound. Rustling in the undergrowth. The distant call of something that hunted by ear. The creak of branches settling into positions the wind had pushed them from. The forest doing its night work, indifferent to the two figures and their small fire.

The air smelled of damp earth, leaf rot and the mineral sharpness of stone cooling after a day of sun. Underneath it, your fire had its own signature — the wood you'd chosen was slightly green, which meant more smoke, less heat, but also less light escaping upward.

I sat with my back against the oak. The bark was rough, furrowed, the texture of something that had been growing for longer than most buildings had been standing. You sat across the fire, your pack open, your notebook already out.

Neither of us spoke for a while. The fire did the talking — the pop of sap, the whisper of flame against wood, the occasional collapse of an ember that sent sparks spiraling upward into the canopy where they died against leaves. You heated water. The kettle's low hum joined the fire's monologue. Tea — the last of the jasmine we'd bought.

"We should talk about what we have," you said.

I looked at you through the fire. The flames moved between us, distorting your face in intervals — light, shadow, light.

"All right."

You had spread everything you had across the flat ground beside our fire like a calligrapher laying out brushes before the first stroke. Your copies of the scroll passages, arranged chronologically. Your anomaly map with its tightening zigzag-pattern. The qi signature comparisons — your own notation, meticulous, cross-referenced. The correlation charts. The timeline of the researchers and their identical endings. All of it laid out on loose earth in the firelight, organized with the precision of someone who understands that presentation is itself an argument.

You'd been working on this. Not just recording — synthesizing. While I'd been walking, pushing, probing ruins, you'd been building a picture from the evidence I gathered, the observations I shared and the silences I left, which you'd learned to read as carefully as the words.

You pushed a bowl of rice toward me. I set it beside my knee. The steam rose, thinned, disappeared. You watched it cool. I watched the evidence.

The fire painted shadows on your arranged evidence. The scroll copies fluttered slightly in the night air, their edges curling, the ink catching the light in amber tones that made three-thousand-year-old words look warm.

"I can feel it," I said.

You looked up from the map you'd been adjusting.

"The answer. It's here. In all of this." I gestured at the spread. "The evidence is sufficient. The pattern is clear. I know what it points to. I just can't —" I stopped. Closed my eyes. The sensation was maddening — like a word on the tip of your tongue multiplied by a thousand, a meaning that sat just below the surface of thought, a shape visible but blurred through water and unreachable. As if reaching for it made it retreat.

"I know," you said quietly. "That's why I did this."

You stood. Lifted a stick. And began to draw.
