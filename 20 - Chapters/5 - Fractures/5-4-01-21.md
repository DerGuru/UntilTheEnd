Everything was there.

You had spread it across the flat ground beside our fire like a calligrapher laying out brushes before the first stroke. Your copies of the scroll passages, arranged chronologically. Your anomaly map with its tightening spiral. The qi signature comparisons — your own notation, meticulous, cross-referenced. The correlation charts. The timeline of the seven researchers and their identical endings. All of it laid out on packed earth in the firelight, organized with the precision of someone who understands that presentation is itself an argument.

You'd been working on this. Not just recording — synthesizing. While I'd been walking, pushing, probing ruins, you'd been building a picture from the evidence I gathered and the observations I shared and the silences I left, which you'd learned to read as carefully as the words.

I stared into the fire.

You pushed a bowl of rice toward me. I took it. Ate. Mechanically, steadily, without pleasure or resistance — the eating of someone who has accepted food as fuel rather than experience. Progress from the woman who'd had to be tricked into swallowing soup eight months ago. You noted this. I saw you note it. Neither of us commented.

The fire painted shadows on your arranged evidence. The scroll copies fluttered slightly in the night air, their edges curling, the ink catching the light in amber tones that made three-thousand-year-old words look warm.

"I can feel it," I said.

You looked up from the map you'd been adjusting.

"The answer. It's here. In all of this." I gestured at the spread. "The evidence is sufficient. The pattern is clear. I know what it points to. I just can't —" I stopped. Closed my eyes. The sensation was maddening — like a word on the tip of your tongue multiplied by a thousand, a meaning that sat just below the surface of thought, visible through the water but unreachable, as if reaching for it was what made it retreat.

"I know," you said quietly. "That's why I did this."

You stood. Lifted a stick. And began to draw.
