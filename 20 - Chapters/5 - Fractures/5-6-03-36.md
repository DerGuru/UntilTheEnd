I hadn't slept. The night had been long and detailed in the way that sleepless nights are detailed — every sound amplified, every shift in the dark registering, the mind running loops that went nowhere and started over. At some point I'd stopped trying and just lay there, watching the stars move with the imperceptible slowness of things that have nowhere to be.

The fire was already going when I gave up pretending. The pot was on. Water heating. The routine of a camp where someone competent had decided the day would begin with order.

I lay in my bedroll and watched you work. The light was early and grey, the kind that doesn't commit to color yet, that holds the world in the flat neutrality of a sky still deciding what it wants to be. You moved between the fire and the packs with the quiet efficiency that was your natural state. Water measured. Rice portioned. But the rhythm was off. You checked the fire twice, adjusted a log that didn't need adjusting, walked to the edge of camp and back for no reason I could see. Your hands kept finding things to do — retying a knot on your pack, straightening the bedroll you'd already straightened, brushing dirt from a surface that wasn't dirty. The efficiency was there but the stillness underneath it wasn't.

I sat up. The headache was there immediately, the loyal companion that arrived each morning before my thoughts did. I pressed my thumbs into my temples and breathed. Waited for it to settle into its cruising altitude.

"Morning," I said through the pain.

"Morning." You handed me tea without turning around. The cup was warm. The tea was adequate. Not good, not bad — the tea of a man whose attention was elsewhere, who had measured the leaves by muscle memory while his mind ran calculations I couldn't see.

I held the cup and watched the steam curl in the still air. The valley was quiet. The kind of quiet that mountains produce in the hour before the wind finds its direction — no rustling, no birdsong yet, just the low murmur of the fire and the distant, almost subliminal sound of water moving somewhere below us.

You sat down across from me. Ate rice from the pot, mechanically, with the chopsticks held in the precise grip that you never varied. You chewed. Swallowed. Stared at a point slightly to the left of my shoulder.

"You slept badly," I said.

"I slept fine."

You hadn't slept fine. The skin beneath your eyes was darker than yesterday, the jawline harder, the posture of a man who had spent the night not sleeping and was now performing the morning as if sleep had happened. I didn't push. We all have our performances.

I ate my rice. No mushrooms today. So it was plain, white, the taste of nothing in particular and the texture of sustenance. I still scraped the bowl clean, because that was what you did when food was finite and the next market was unknown.

A bird landed on a boulder to the north. Small, brown, the anonymous kind that exists in mountains without bothering to be identified. It hopped twice, looked at us and flew away.

"There's a clearing," you said. "A little to the east. I found it yesterday."

"The one you were exploring?"

"Yes." You set down your bowl. "I'd like for us to go there this morning."

The way you said it. The careful, weighted delivery of someone who had rehearsed the sentence and was now performing it. The performance was almost good enough to hide the rehearsal.

Almost.

"Alright," I said.

You nodded, stood and cleaned the pot with the methodical attention of a man who needed to clean a pot more than the pot needed cleaning. I watched you scrub the inside with sand from the streambed, rinse it, check it, scrub again. The pot was clean after the first pass. The second and third were for you.

We packed. You pulled on your boots — the old pair with the cracked sole that you'd sealed with pine resin and stubbornness.

The morning had warmed by a degree. The grey had shifted toward something that might, eventually, with commitment, become blue. The valley below was still in shadow, the sunlight reaching only the upper ridges, painting them gold while the lower world waited.

You led me to the clearing — open, wide, treeless in the center, the kind of space that exists in mountains when a flat shelf of rock lies close enough to the surface that soil never accumulates deeply enough for large growth. Grass, short and wind-flattened. Small stones. The horizon in all directions, the sky pressing down with the leaden weight of high altitude.

My body knew before I did. The moment we stepped into the open, something shifted — a tightness in the chest, a sharpening at the edges of vision, the animal recognition of a space that had been chosen rather than arrived at. My pulse picked up in anticipation. Not much. But enough that the headache responded, throbbing in time with the acceleration.

The grass under my feet was individual. Each blade distinct, pressing against the sole with its own pressure, its own angle. The air tasted of stone and altitude and something metallic that might have been adrenaline or might have been the morning. A mouse darted across the ground two meters ahead of me, a grey streak low against the grass, and I tracked it the way you track movement when your body has decided, without consulting you, that everything in the environment is relevant.

I noticed the absence of big trees. Noticed the openness. The clearing was a stage. My eyes measured the distance to the nearest significant structure — a boulder field, forty meters north — and found it adequate.

Adequate for what, I didn't ask myself. My hands already knew the answer. They were cold.

I breathed. Deliberately. The way the cultivation texts taught — in through the nose, slow, measured, letting the air fill the lower lungs first, then the upper. Out through the mouth. Again. The pulse resisted, then yielded. The sharpness at the edges of my vision softened by a fraction. The mouse was just a mouse again.

It wasn't enough to calm, but I regained some control. The difference between a fire burning and a fire being watched.

I stopped in the center. The grass was damp beneath our feet, the morning dew soaking through to skin. Morning light, low and angular, casting shadows too long for the objects that made them.

You put your pack down at the edge of the clearing and removed all the things you normally carried close — the water flask, the small knife, the fire steel. You set them on the ground, right next to the pack, with the deliberate placement of someone keeping a workspace clear.

Then you came facing me and stopped with five meters between us.

The distance was odd. Not the traveling distance, not the companionable three meters. Five. The extra two meters were intentional, calculated, the space between a man and something that might react.

"I need to ask you a question," you said.

Your voice was steady. Your hands were not. They hung at your sides and the tremor was visible — not the gross shaking of fear but the fine vibration of controlled tension, the body's protest against a mind that had decided to override its survival instincts.

"Ask away."

I said it simply. Openly. The way you say things to someone you trust and I trusted you entirely.

You knew this. It was written on your face — not the knowledge itself but the weight of it, the quiet agony of a man who was about to use someone's trust as a weapon against them because the weapon was necessary and the target was behind walls that nothing else could breach.

"It's going to hurt," you said. An apology, offered in advance, for a wound you were about to inflict deliberately.

"That's how you know it works." I said, feigning bravado. The truth was that I had no idea how you were going to do this. No idea what you were going to do to me. No idea how much it would hurt, or how long the pain would last, or what the consequences would be. I just knew that whatever it was, it had to be done.

You closed your eyes. Your jaw worked, the motion of someone biting down on the last chance to stop, chewing it, swallowing it. You opened your eyes again.

"Yun."

I looked at you. The name was not even my real name. But it still worked like a tether. The one thing that could anchor me to the moment when everything else was about to change. You looked at me like a man standing at the edge of something irreversible, the moment before the stone is dropped and the ripples begin and everything downstream changes.

"Have you killed Wei?"

The world stopped. The wind forgot to move and the grass to bend. And my own heartbeat seemed to forget its next beat and everything else. The clearing, the sky, the man five meters away, the mouse somewhere in the grass, the mountains on every horizon — everything held its breath.

My hearing cut out. One moment the clearing had sound — wind, grass, the small ambient noise of a world existing — and then nothing. Silence so total that the only thing left was my own breathing, loud, close, the ragged in-and-out of air through a throat that had forgotten how to do this quietly.

The light went wrong. Too bright. The morning sun, which had been gentle, unremarkable, now burned through my vision like a blade, bleaching the grass white, turning the sky into a flat sheet of glare. Yet still the world seemed to sink into darkness, consuming the all of the too bright light.

I felt a shivering cold and boiling hot at the same time. My hands ice. My face burning. The skin of my arms prickling with goosebumps while sweat gathered at the back of my neck. The body couldn't decide which emergency it was having so it had all of them at once.

*Have you*

The words were in the air. I could feel them there, each one separate, each one waiting to be assembled into meaning. Four words. A question. Simple syntax. Subject, verb, object. The kind of sentence a child could parse and my mind struggled to find a meaning for.

*killed*

The third word arrived and my mind reached for it, reached for the connection, tried to attach it to the first two, tried to build the sentence that would make them mean something —

*Wei?*

The fourth word landed and the sentence was complete, assembled, the meaning almost —

The wall struck with the speed of something that had been watching the sentence build, word by word, and had waited until the last possible moment — the fraction of a second before comprehension — to intervene. The meaning that had been forming, the understanding that had been assembling itself from four simple words, was taken mid-construction. Ripped away like a page torn from a book before the last line could be read.

But the feeling was there.

The emotion that lived below the wall, below the defense, in the deep architecture where it existed without content. Where affect existed without cause. The feeling had already arrived. Before the wall could close. Before the guardian could intervene. Before the deepest defense could identify the threat and silence it.

And it had a shape. The shape was the answer to the question and the answer was—

A crack.

Not in the ground. Not in the air. Not in any external surface. Inside. In the wall. In the defense. A single, thin, hair-fine crack, running from wherever the feeling had landed to wherever the surface was, propagating through the structure of my self-protection with the quiet inexorability of ice in stone.

The crack was small, but it was enough.

And through the crack, for one moment, I saw everything.
