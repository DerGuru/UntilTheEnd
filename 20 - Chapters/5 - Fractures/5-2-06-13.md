You fell asleep first. Your breathing evened. Your hand, which had been resting on your notebook, relaxed.

I stayed awake.

There was a fourth scroll. I hadn't mentioned it.

I'd found it during the night search, wedged behind a collapsed shelf in the deepest alcove — a cylinder smaller than the others, sealed not with wax but with something harder, darker. I'd broken it open, read the first three lines by qi-light, and known immediately that this one was different. Older. More dangerous. More mine.

I'd slipped it into my robe before you woke. Now I unrolled it by firelight. The paper was impossibly thin, translucent almost, the characters visible from both sides like shadows trapped between layers of glass. The ink had aged to a deep bronze that caught the light with an almost metallic sheen.

Old. Older than old. This was pre-written-history old, the kind of document that shouldn't exist because the civilization that produced it had left no other records, no buildings, no evidence of having existed at all beyond this single scroll sealed in a stone cylinder in a library that had been forgotten before most current nations had been founded.

The first lines were observational. Precise, almost austere.

*Forty-three deviations in two lunar cycles. The qi responds before I act. The world adjusts to accommodate my presence. I have documented each instance. They are consistent.*

Forty-three deviations. Two months. A pace that made my fourteen anomalies in three weeks look almost identical.

I read on. The cultivator — the oldest voice in the collection, the first to notice what I was noticing — had been thorough. More thorough than the others. The documentation was dense, systematic, the work of someone who had approached mystery with the tools of science rather than the reflexes of superstition.

*I have concluded that the source is internal. The deviations correlate with my emotional state, my physical position and most critically, my intentions. When I intend to go somewhere, the path adjusts. When I wish for rain, the clouds shift. When I expect safety, threats diminish. The causality is reversed. The world does not shape my experience. My experience shapes the world.*

My hands tightened on the scroll.

*I have further concluded that this effect is not voluntary. I have attempted to control it. I cannot. The adjustments occur below consciousness, below intention, in a stratum of will so deep that the concept of "choice" does not apply. It is as automatic as breathing. As involuntary as a heartbeat. And as impossible to stop.*

I set the scroll on my knee. The fire was dying. You breathed.

The cultivator's words were my words. In the literal sense. These were things I could have written, in this order, with this logic, arriving at these conclusions through these steps. The thinking was mine. The structure was mine. The way the mind moved from observation to hypothesis to conclusion — that architecture of reasoning — was so familiar that reading it felt less like discovery and more like remembering.

I read the final section.

*The question is not what I am. The question is what happens when I understand what I am. I have observed a pattern. The closer I come to comprehension, the more the world resists. My thoughts scatter. My focus breaks. I reach for the conclusion and find my hand empty, my mind blank, my notes unreadable.*

*Something does not want me to know.*

*I am not certain it is something external. I am increasingly certain, I*

The scroll ended. Same as all the others. No final character. No conclusion. Just the last word, *I*, hanging in the silence like a note held past the end of a song.

*I*

I rolled the scroll. Slowly. The paper was so old that haste would destroy it and this scroll, this voice from the deepest past, deserved care. It deserved to be held gently, the way you hold something that has traveled farther than you thought objects could travel and arrived intact.

I placed it back in the cylinder, sealed it. And put it back into my pack.

*I am the source. And something in me does not want me to know.*

The rain began.

Not gently. All at once, the way mountain weather decides. Not a transition but a declaration. One moment dry. The next, a curtain of water so dense the fire hissed and failed in seconds.

You woke. Rolled over. Pulled your blanket over your head. Went back to sleep with the unperturbed efficiency of someone who has categorized rain under *problems that are not mine.*

I stood.

The rain tried to hit my face and I let it. Cultivators at my level don't get wet, not involuntarily, the qi forms a barrier as natural and unconscious as blinking. I let the barrier drop. The way you open a window when the room is stale — you don't want the cold, but you need the air.

The qi shield dissolved and the rain arrived, full and heavy and real, soaking through cloth to skin, through skin to muscle, through muscle to bone. The water was cold. Not unpleasant cold, not the dead cold of metal fittings, but alive cold, the temperature of clouds and altitude and the indifference of weather to human affairs. It ran down my skin under my robe, soaked through fabric that should have repelled it.

I stood in it.

The water ran down my face like something being washed away. My hair plastered to my skull. My robe became a second skin, dark and heavy, clinging to shoulders and arms and the architecture of a body that had existed for longer than it had any right to.

I tilted my face into the rain and kept my eyes open.

I wanted something else than the cold efficiency that had carried me through three weeks of ruins and scrolls and evidence that pointed at a truth so large that looking at it was like looking at the sun.

I wanted to feel the rain. The cold. The heaviness. The way water insists on going down, on finding the lowest point. Obeying a gravity that has nothing to do with will or intention or the deep, unconscious workings of whatever I was.

The rain didn't care about me. That was the point. Everything else — the qi, the world, the paths that bent, the forests that grew where clearings had been — everything else responded to me, adjusted for me, obeyed without being asked. The rain didn't, at least I thought it did not. The rain was just water. It fell because falling is what water does, not because something in my bones wished it so.

The sky was black and close and featureless. The rain fell from it like a curtain and for one moment, I wasn't a cultivator or a mystery or a source.

I was just wet.

A puddle formed at my feet. The water collected in a depression in the rock, shallow and clear and my face appeared in it. I saw myself. Distorted by raindrops. Each drop that hit the surface broke my face apart, fragmented it, scattered me into circles of ripple and reformation and each time I came back together I was slightly different, slightly reassembled, a mosaic version of myself that the rain kept breaking and remaking.

A raindrop hit the center of my reflection. My face dissolved and I looked away.

The rain continued. I stood in it until the cold reached my bones — cold and deep. Affecting tissue that hadn't been affected by anything external in a long time.

Then I sat back down. Water pooling around me. Dripping from my sleeves. The scroll cylinder sat in my pack, untouched by rain.

You were still asleep. Your blanket had slipped and I pulled it back over your head without thinking about it.

The rain fell.

I sat in the dark, wet and cold and alive and thought about many thousand years of cultivators who had stood where I stood, asked what I asked and then forgotten.

The question crystallized. It had been forming since the first scroll. But now it had edges. Now it had weight.

*If others had what I have and they all died — why am I still here?*

I wasn't stronger. I had the same qi, the same experiences.

Luck?

I didn't believe in luck.

Then why?

I sat with the question. The rain answered nothing. The dark answered nothing. Your breathing answered nothing.

But the question was mine now. Solid. Heavy. Real.

And whatever it was that kept stopping questions — whatever invisible hand had folded those pages shut, mid-sentence, mid-thought, mid-character — it hadn't stopped this one.

Not yet.
