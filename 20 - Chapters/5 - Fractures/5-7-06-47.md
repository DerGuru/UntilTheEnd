Xiǎohú didn't walk behind us anymore. She walked around us — circling, darting, the restless orbit of an animal that had spent weeks concealing herself and had decided concealment was no longer necessary. She investigated a bush. Found it boring. Investigated a rock. Found it beneath her. Trotted ahead, paused, looked back at us with the amber-eyed impatience of a creature that moved at a speed humans didn't match and found the discrepancy personally offensive.

You watched her. The wariness from last night had faded into something closer to fascination — the expression of a man cataloguing a new variable.

Xiǎohú disappeared into the undergrowth. Reappeared ten meters ahead. Disappeared again. The rustle of her passage through dry leaves was the only evidence she was there at all. That, and the occasional amber glance from between branches, checking that the slow creatures were still following.

A bird called. Xiǎohú's ears swiveled. She froze — the full-body stillness of a predator locking onto sound. Two seconds. Three. The bird called again. Xiǎohú rushed ahead into the underbrush.

I heard the rustle of Xiǎohú's fur against the dry brush. I heard your footsteps — the left one slightly heavier, the way it always was when the trail inclined. I heard all of it and none of it drowned out the rest. The muscle that had strained and held in the market — it wasn't straining anymore. The input was the same. The volume was the same. But somewhere between the village and here, filtering had stopped being an effort and started being habit. The world was still loud. I was just better at listening.

By midday the trail dropped toward water. We heard the river before we saw it — the low, continuous voice of moving water finding its way through stone. Xiǎohú heard it first, of course. Her ears had turned toward it a full minute before the sound reached us. She was already at the bank when we arrived, drinking with the quick, precise laps of an animal.

The river was narrow, cold and clear. A mountain stream that had grown into something wider, fed by tributaries and snowmelt, running over smooth stone with the cold clarity of water that has never been near agriculture or settlement. The stone beneath was visible. The bottom was a mosaic of river-tumbled rock, each piece worn smooth by the patient erasure of current against surface.

You set up camp on the bank. Xiǎohú finished drinking and found an place in the sun, where she lay with amber eyes half-closed, performing the boneless relaxation of creatures that are fully present and fully at peace.

I went to the water and knelt. The stones at the river's edge were cold, genuinely cold, the temperature registering against my knees with the new, insistent sensitivity that made every physical contact an experience. I leaned forward. Cupped water. Brought it to my face.

Cold. Achingly, beautifully cold. The water against my skin was a conversation. The river saying *this is what I am* and my face answering *this is what it feels like* and both statements being true.

I washed my face. Water over forehead, over cheeks, over closed eyes. I opened them and saw my reflection on the water.

The river's surface — still in the shallow pool near the bank, sheltered from the current by a natural formation of stone — held my face with the fidelity of a mirror. Not perfect. Still slightly distorted. The edges soft, the details approximate. But recognizable. Unmistakably me.

Tired. That was the first thing, that came to mind as I saw my face the water. It was the face of a woman who had not slept well or long enough for too long. Whose body was performing the mechanics of rest without achieving its purpose. Lines that hadn't been there a month ago. Shadows under the eyes that spoke of headaches a, my heightened sensitivity and the exhaustion that was taking in too much through channels that hadn't been shaped for this volume.

Thin. Thinner than I'd been. My cheekbones were more prominent. The jaw sharper. The face paring down to essentials, losing its softness.

Real. That was the last thing. The face in the water was real. Not the curated self-image that the mind constructs, not the version of yourself that you imagine when you imagine yourself. The actual, unmediated, honestly-rendered face of a person seen from the outside. Flawed. Particular. Human.

I smiled.

Not on purpose. Not because anything was funny, or happy, or worthy of the expression. The smile arrived the way the rain had arrived, without intention, without cause, summoned by some internal process I couldn't name. Maybe the child. Maybe the rice. Maybe the cold water on my skin. Maybe just the simple, animal satisfaction of being alive near a river on an afternoon where nothing terrible was happening yet.

But the reflection had smiled first.

The smile on the face in the water preceded my smile by a fraction of a second, half a heartbeat, a blink's worth of time, the irreducible minimum of *before.* My lips began to move. The muscles of my face engaged, the contraction that produces the expression that humans call smiling. And the face in the water was already doing it.

Already smiling.

Already there. Ahead of me. As if it had known.

I froze. The smile — my smile, the one that had been forming — stopped. My face went still. The expression I'd been constructing halted mid-completion, the muscles locked, the gesture suspended.

The reflection kept smiling.

The same face. The same features. But a different expression, than it should have. The reflection was smiling while I was not and the disconnection was—

Somehow not frightening.

I waited for the fear. Expected it. The pattern of the past weeks had been. Impossible thing happens, fear follows, the wall activates and the content is taken. The sequence was reliable. The sequence was the way things went.

But the fear didn't come.

The reflection smiled at me and what spread through my chest was warmth. Not temperature-warmth, not the new sensation of heat and cold that had become my condition since the breakthrough. Something deeper. The deep, structural, full-body warmth that comes from being looked at by something that wants you to be okay.

I titled my head. The reflection's smile was gentle. Relieved. That was the part that undid me, the part that I couldn't process and didn't try to.

The crack pulsed. The feeling hummed. Something moved through me — too large to be a thought, too warm to be a memory.

The headache came. Sharp, precise, the familiar warning. The wall had noticed. Whatever I'd been about to understand was already dissolving, the shape of it collapsing even as I reached for it. I could feel the edges of something enormous retreating behind the architecture that kept me from knowing what I was.

But the warmth stayed. The wall could take the thought. It couldn't take the feeling.

The reflection's smile faded. Slowly. The corners of the mouth turned down. The eyes changed. The face in the water looked at me with an expression of sadness. Deep and old, the kind that has lived in a body so long it has become structural.

Then that faded too. The expression relaxing, the reflection returning to be just that. As if whatever had looked through my eyes had said what it needed to say and was stepping back.

My face in the water was only my face again. Tired. Thin. Real. No longer smiling, on either side.

I sat back. My knees were wet. My hands were wet. The water in my cupped showed me nothing but the distorted reflection of sky.

I breathed.

The feeling in my chest, the nameless frequency, the vibration that the deletion couldn't reach, was different. Warmer. As if something that had been running cold had, for the first time, been acknowledged.

I stayed by the river for a long time. The water ran. The stones were cold. The sun did its patient work on the sky.

When I stood, my legs were stiff and my face was dry. The crack in the wall had not closed but had not widened either. It was there. Stable. Present.

I wiped my hands on my robe and walked back to camp.
