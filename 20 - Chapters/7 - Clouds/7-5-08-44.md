A signature.

On the ridge. East. Two hundred paces. She knows it the way the body knows weather — not through analysis but through the recognition that precedes analysis, the pre-verbal identification of a presence the nervous system has catalogued.

Old. Her kind of old.

She stands. The Wei memory is still warm. The bird is still calling. The fear is still sitting where it sits.

She walks east. The fox-spirit stays on the rock, watching with amber eyes, the animal assessment of a situation that does not require an animal's participation.

He is standing on the ridge. Not sitting — standing, the posture of someone who has been standing for the duration she has been in the cave and for whom standing and sitting are equivalent states. He has been here longer than she. The knowledge arrives without evidence. He was on this mountain before her, the way he was on the western hill before the battle, the way he was in the courtyard before she sensed him. Always: already there.

Two fingers. Raised. Index and middle. The old greeting — the one that hasn't been used in centuries, the greeting of equals meeting on a road.

She stops. Ten paces.

She does not return it.

His face. She has seen it twice before — once through qi-perception at two hundred paces, once in the briefness of a courtyard encounter years ago. Here, in daylight, in the grey overcast of altitude, the face is: old. Not aged — habituated. The face of a person for whom time is furniture rather than weather.

He lowers his hand.

"You held," he says. Low. The acoustic register that old beings use when volume has lost its purpose. "Seven seconds."

He felt it. From wherever he stood, whatever distance — the seven seconds when her qi lapsed and her cells asked their question. He felt it the way she would feel a stone dropped into still water from the other side of the lake. Unmistakable.

"What pulled you back?"

Her voice meets the mountain air. "The wall."

"Whose wall?"

The question sits in the grey light. Simple. The simplest question she has been asked — simpler than any of Xu Ran's, simpler than any she has asked herself. The kind of question that does not require an answer because the question IS the answer.

She does not respond. He does not need her to.

Wind passes between them. Cold. The punctuation of altitude.

"I watched you protect a settlement once," he says. "Every wall. Every window. Every path."

A pause. The duration of one breath.

"Everything except."

The word arrives in her chest where the fear sits. Except. The word that has followed her from the dead grass, from the body, from the night she spent placing barriers on every surface except the one that mattered.

"You are protecting everything again," he says. "Except."

She looks at him. The looking of one old thing at another old thing — not hostile, not warm, the looking that exists between beings who share the specific loneliness of having outlived the context for loneliness.

He looks back. Equal. Ancient. Unhurried.

"A hand that grips long enough," he says, "forgets that gripping is a choice."

The sentence arrives not as instruction. Not as wisdom delivered from above. As observation — the way a physician names a condition. Not to cure. To identify.

She breathes. The sentence sits in the air between them where the bird was calling.

"You knew," she says. "What I was. Before I did."

"Yes."

"Why didn't you tell me?"

He looks at her. The look contains something adjacent to regret — adjacent, not the thing itself, the territory near regret that beings of their magnitude occupy because regret requires the belief that intervention would have helped. And they know better.

"Would you have believed someone else's answer about who you are?"

She exhales. The answer is no. The answer has always been no. Every piece of understanding she possesses was earned by arriving at it herself — through Xu Ran's questions, through the cave, through seven seconds, through the body in the dirt. None of it could have been given. All of it had to be found.

He nods. Once. The nod of conclusion — the gesture that replaces paragraphs.

He turns. No salute this time. No two-finger farewell. The nod was the greeting and the nod was the farewell and the farewell is his back and his back is receding along the ridge and the ridge absorbs him the way ridges absorb weather — gradually, and then completely.

His signature diminishes. Fades. Goes.

Gone. The way signatures go when the being behind them has decided to stop being present. Not hidden — absent. The distinction between concealment and departure, between a door closed and a room emptied.

She stands on the ridge. Alone again. The fox-spirit is a rust-colored shape on the rock below. The bird has stopped calling. The wind continues because wind always continues.

A hand that grips long enough forgets that gripping is a choice.

She looks at her hands. Open. On the ridge. In the grey light. The hands that have held for millennia — held qi, held barriers, held the wall, held herself in place against the current of time that everything else rides.

Gripping is a choice.

The sentence does not solve. Does not cure. Does not transform. The sentence sits — where the fear sits, where the Wei-memory sits, where all the important things sit, in the chest, below the sternum, above the diaphragm.

She walks back to the rock. The fox-spirit lifts its head. The bird resumes. The mountain does not comment.

The sentence stays.
