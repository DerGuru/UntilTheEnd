The guilt was a circle.

I'd been walking it for two days — the mental circumnavigation that guilt produced, the track worn by repetitive thinking, the path that went nowhere because it went everywhere and the everywhere was always the same place. Destruction. Cause. Self. Repeat.

You saw it before I named it.

"Stop," you said.

We were on the trail. The afternoon sun was behind clouds that might have been mine or might have been weather. The uncertainty had become the background radiation of every observation. Xiǎohú was ahead of us, a rust-colored shape on the grey trail, moving with the efficiency of an animal that knew where it was going even when no one else did.

"Stop what?" I said.

"That leads nowhere."

"What does?"

"The thing you're doing. The loop. I can see it — you've been walking the same thought for two days. It starts with the nest. Goes to Wei. Goes to every other loss. Arrives at guilt. Returns to the nest. Starts again."

The precision was offensive. The irritation of being accurately described by someone who had been watching with more attention than I'd authorized. The documentary eye seeing the structure of the spiral when the person spiraling could only see the motion.

"How would you know?" I said.

The question was defensive. The deployment of challenge-as-shield, the rhetorical maneuver that substituted aggression for vulnerability. I knew how you knew. You knew because you were observant, because the signs were visible, because two days of circular thinking produced behavioral patterns that a trained observer could read like text.

But your answer was different.

"Because I think that way," you said.

The words landed with the weight of confession. The flat confession, absent the tearful revelation and wounded disclosure that narrative demanded and life rarely provided. The factual. Your particular register of truth-telling that placed information in the air without decoration and let it stand.

"Guilt as circle," you said. "I know that pattern. The loop. Event, cause, self, repeat. The mental pattern that converts regret into repetition and repetition into — nothing. It doesn't arrive anywhere. It doesn't resolve. It just —"

You made a gesture. Circular. Your hand describing the trajectory of a thought that went around and around and never touched ground.

"You?" I said.

"Yes."

"About what?"

The pause. A different kind. The silence of a man negotiating between privacy and relevance, deciding how much personal truth was required to serve the pedagogical moment.

You decided on less.

"That doesn't matter right now," you said. "What matters is: the guilt goes in circles. It's movement disguised as progress. Energy without work. Heat without light."

"So what do you do?" I asked.

You looked at me. The direct gaze that you used when you were about to say something that was both helpful and disappointing, the visual equivalent of medicine that tasted bad and worked.

"I cook rice," you said.

You set down your pack. You started a fire. You put the pot on. You began the procedure: the water, the grain, the proportions that produced edible results, the mechanical sequence that you performed with the methodical attention of a man who understood that the procedure WAS the point.

I watched.

"That's it?" I said.

"That's it."

"You — cook rice."

"Yes."

"And the guilt?"

"Stays. But stays smaller. Stays — contained. The rice doesn't fix anything. The rice is rice. But the making of rice requires attention and attention can only be in one place and if attention is on the rice then attention is not on the loop."

The fire was catching. The pot was heating. The water was beginning the slow transition from cold to warm to hot, the thermal narrative that preceded boiling, the patient escalation that couldn't be rushed.

You added the grain. You stirred. You watched the water the way you watched everything, with the completeness of a man who had decided that this, right now, was the most important thing happening and the decision was absolute.

I sat. You hadn't told me to and the thought-loop hadn't released me. But the fire was warm and the rice was cooking and the domestic normalcy of a man preparing food on a mountain trail was — something. Less than a solution, less than an answer, but something.

The guilt was still there. The nest. The three eggs. Wei's silence. The thousand names, the thousand faces, the thousand small destructions that I had committed unknowingly across millennia. All of it was still there, still circling, still the mental perpetual motion machine that guilt constructed when given enough material.

But you were cooking rice.

And the rice required a pot and the pot required fire and the fire required attention and attention was redirected. Not stopped. Not fixed. Redirected. The water flowing into a different channel, not because the old channel was blocked but because a new one was available and gravity was indifferent to which channel it used.

Your past. The thing you hadn't disclosed. It sat in the air between us, visible by its absence, the shape of an untold story outlined by the space it occupied. You knew the guilt-loop because you walked it too. You knew the circle because he'd been a resident. The rice wasn't theoretical advice. It was practiced knowledge, the wisdom that came from repetition rather than insight.

You cooked rice because he'd needed to cook rice. For yourself. For your own loop. Your own circle. Your own —

I didn't ask. The not-asking was its own kind of respect, the acknowledgment that some silences were inhabited and the inhabitants had the right to their tenancy.

The rice was ready. You portioned it. Two bowls. The equality of shared meals, the democratic distribution of nutrition that rendered all other hierarchies temporarily irrelevant.

"Here," you said.

You held out the bowl. The rice was steaming. The smell was — rice. The unremarkable scent of a grain that had been cooked in a pot on a fire on a mountain by a man who was trying to help a woman stop thinking in circles.

I took the bowl.

The loop didn't stop. But the loop had a competitor now and the competitor was holding a spoon.
