The atmosphere changed.

Not weather — although the weather changed too, the cooperation of sky and feeling that I'd stopped being able to distinguish. The atmosphere between us. The interpersonal climate that existed in the space between two people who shared a campsite and a purpose and the knowledge that the shared purpose was approaching its structural expiration.

You were packing.

Not the daily packing — the routine consolidation of tools and supplies that preceded each morning's departure. This was different. This was sorting. Arranging. The organizational behavior of a man who was preparing for a different kind of departure, the inventory-taking that preceded not a day's walk but a — longer absence.

You sorted your notes. Paper by paper. The individual sheets that had accumulated outside the notebook — the overflow, the surplus documentation that the notebook couldn't contain, the textual excess of a story that was larger than the binding designed to hold it. You stacked them. Aligned the edges. Placed them in the case with the care that indicated not routine but ritual.

Your notebook. The notebook. You picked it up. Held it. The covers were — I'd seen them before, described them before, registered them before. But now, in the context of your packing, they were different. The worn leather. The stained pages. The material exhaustion of an object that had been used for its entire existence at the maximum intensity its construction allowed.

Written-full. Stained. Irreplaceable.

Three words. The notebook's entire biography.

You tucked it under your arm. The possessive gesture of a man holding something that was both your and another's, the physical claim on an object that documented someone else's life and was therefore owned by the act of documentation rather than the content documented.

I watched you pack.

"I can't teach you what you need to learn," you said.

You were kneeling. Arranging the pack. Your back wasn't to me — not quite — but your face was angled away, the partial avoidance of someone who needed to say something and needed to not-see the face you were saying it to.

"Nobody can."

"I know," I said.

"The last piece — that's only for you. I'm a distraction."

The word landed. Distraction. The lexical choice that reduced your presence to a function — the subsidiary role, the secondary character, the person-as-obstacle in the narrative framework where the protagonist's journey required solitude.

"You're not a distraction," I said.

You turned. The look on your face was — precise. Sad the way accurate assessments were sad. Sad the way correct diagnoses were sad. The sadness of precision.

"Yes, I am," you said. "The best one you ever had."

The humor was — present. Under the surface. Your register of humor operating at depth rather than surface, the comedy that lived beneath the tragedy because you knew both languages and could speak them in the same breath.

Almost a smile. His. Not mine. The asymmetric expression of a man who understood your own joke and understood that the joke's truth was its weight, that the funny was inseparable from the painful and the painful was inseparable from the necessary and the necessary was — this.

You continued packing.

I sat by the dying fire and watched a man prepare to leave and said nothing because everything I could say was either too much or not enough and the space between those two extremes was too narrow for language.

Xiǎohú watched too. Amber eyes on the man with the pack, the man with the notebook, the man who was sorting papers as though the order of paper sheets mattered and it did and the fact that it did was the beautiful futility of a human being imposing structure on material that would outlast the structure and the human being and the reason for both.

The evening came. The fire died. We didn't cook.

We sat in the darkness and didn't speak and the not-speaking contained everything that speaking would have contained and more, because silence could hold what words collapsed under and the weight was — significant.

Tomorrow.

The word appeared in the space between us. Neither of us said it. Both of us knew it.
