"Start," you said.

We were sitting. The where didn't matter — camp, mountain path, the side of a trail that led upward into the ranges where the vegetation thinned and the air became something you noticed instead of something you breathed. The where didn't matter because the where was not what you were asking about.

"With what?" I said.

You had your notebook open. The pen resting across the page, a scholar preparing to document. You looked at me the way a physician looks at a patient who has been postponing the examination — patient, firm, ready.

"Tell me who you are."

Silence.

Not the comfortable silence of two people who have run out of things to say. The structural silence — the load-bearing kind, the silence that exists because the weight of what needs to be said is too heavy for words to carry without preparation. The silence that precedes the lifting of something immense.

I looked at the sky. Clouds — thin, scattered, the high-altitude wisps that existed more as suggestions than as weather. They moved. Slowly, indifferently, with the unhurried drift of things that had no destination, no schedule and no responsibility to anyone watching.

"I'm tired," I said.

Truth. Plain, undressed, the kind that arrives without strategy. Simple, factual, body-deep truth. I was tired. Had been tired since before the twist, since before the eons of accumulated existence had compacted themselves into a weight that sat between my lungs and my spine and made every breath a small act of labor.

"I know," you said. "Tell me why."

Your voice — the register you used when you were pushing, the tone that lived halfway between clinical inquiry and genuine concern, the vocal instrument of a man who had learned that the right question at the right moment was more powerful than any answer. You were pushing because that was your function. That was what you were for.

I breathed.

The metallic taste was there — faint, persistent, the low metallic hum that had been background noise for weeks. My teeth vibrated with it. A frequency I couldn't identify and couldn't silence, like the resonance of a bell struck too long ago to hear but not long enough ago to forget.

"I am mortal," I said.

The words came out. Left my mouth. Entered the air. Occupied the space between us — the meter of mountain atmosphere that separated the woman who had created immortality from the man who was writing it down. The words were correct. The syntax was accurate. The statement was — 

Nothing.

The words hit the interior landscape the way rain hits stone — surface contact, immediate runoff, zero penetration. I am mortal. The sentence was a string of sounds that my mouth could produce, my ears could hear and my mind could comprehend. But it reached the place where words became conviction and it stopped. Just — stopped. The way water stops at a cliff's edge. The way light stops at an opaque surface. Naturally. Completely. Without drama.

Every fiber. Every cell. Every particle of qi that constituted the architecture of whatever I was — all of it knew. Knew with a certainty so deep it had become structural, had become the foundation upon which everything else was built. I am immortal. Not believed. Not thought. KNOWN. The way bone knows weight, the way the lungs know air. Below belief. Below thought. In the true self.

You were watching. The pen hadn't moved — you'd been waiting to write something and there was nothing to write, because nothing had happened.

"What did you feel?" you asked.

"Nothing."

"Nothing as in emptiness?"

"Nothing as in nothing happened. The words went in and came out the other side. My body doesn't believe them."

You considered. Your face was doing its thing — the micro-adjustments, the calibrations, the visible processing of a mind that heated information, hammered it, tested its edges before setting it down. You wrote something. Short. Probably: *Attempt 1. Result: none.*

"Again," you said.

"It won't —"

"Again."

I looked at the clouds. They had moved since I'd last looked — not far, not meaningfully, but moved. The clouds didn't care about my attempts at mortality. The clouds were clouds. They formed, they drifted, they dissolved. Free of conviction. Free of effort. Just weather, doing what weather did, while I sat below and tried to convince my own bones of something they refused to hear.

"I am mortal," I said again.

Nothing.

Xiǎohú was lying nearby, amber eyes open, watching me with the evaluative patience of an animal that had seen many things and was adding this to the catalog. She yawned. The yawn of a creature completely uninterested in the spiritual mechanics of an immortal's identity crisis.

You wrote.

I sat.

The clouds moved.
