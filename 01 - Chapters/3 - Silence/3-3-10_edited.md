# Chapter 29

Night.

He slept. The sleep that followed victory — deeper, heavier, the weight of a body that had fought and won and celebrated and now processed all three through unconsciousness.

His face: relaxed. The scar on his shoulder — visible above the blanket, the diagonal line that was thirty hours old and already part of his body's vocabulary. This is Wei. This is what Wei has done.

I knelt.

The nightly arrangement. Knees on floor, hand hovering, the gap between palm and chest. The ritual that had become routine and the routine that had become the architecture of my nights — monitor, count, catalog.

His core.

Like a small star. Not metaphor — description. A source of light, warm and golden and persistent and, in the most literal sense available, luminous.

The glow pushed through his shirt. Through the fabric, through the threads, through material that was supposed to be opaque and wasn't opaque to this frequency. The pitch climbing. The climbing being the data.

I counted.

Three.

Four.

Three.

Four.

Irregular.

New.

The irregularity was the new information. The twitches — the micro-contractions of his core's stability, the moments when healthy qi-flow stuttered — had been consistent. A rhythm. Two per minute, then three, then four. Regular, predictable, the kind of escalation that could be tracked.

This — the alternating, the three-four-three-four — was not regular. Was not rhythm. Was the body's attempt to maintain equilibrium shifting from instinct to effort, from effortless stability to labored, irregular, visible work. A system straining harder to produce the same result.

I pulled my hand back.

His blanket. I tucked it. Higher. Around his shoulders, around his chest. The wrapping that was the care gesture and the care gesture being the physical expression of the thing I couldn't say and the thing being the count.

Like tucking a child. Like tucking everyone. I had done this a thousand times. Furs and blankets and woven mats — whatever the era provided for covering sleeping bodies. This is what I do. I stay. I cover. I watch.

I went outside.

Moon. Stars. The settlement: dark, except for ember-glow of banked fires and the light from one window three houses down.

Lao Chen's door was open. He sat in lamplight, oiling a blade that would never cut anything.

"The boy fought well?" he said. Without looking up.

"He didn't die."

"High praise, from you." A pause. The cloth moved over metal. "He'll want to fight more now. After tasting victory."

"Probably."

"And you'll let him."

It wasn't a question. I didn't answer it.

He held the blade up. Turned it. Lamplight caught the edge.

"Good steel," he said. Meaning something else.

I walked to the garden. Three pots. Moonlit. The dock grass, the nettle-cousin, the lemongrass. Vertical, committed, wrong-wrong-right. Wei's work.

Two stones. The blade fragment. Moon on metal.

I stood.

Breathed. The breath of someone who didn't need to breathe and whose breathing was therefore choice, and the choice being the performance of humanity.

*Irregular. That's new. His core is no longer compensating evenly. The overload distributing unevenly across the qi-channels.*

I lifted my tea. Cold. Drank.

*That means when the break comes it won't be clean. It won't be a shutdown. It will be—*

I stopped.

Stars. This latitude, this season, this night. Indifferent. Light that had traveled for years to arrive at a garden where a woman stood with cold tea and a count and a thought she wouldn't finish.

*Wei is different. He must be different.*

*Everyone I've lost was different.*

That one arrived uninvited. The thought I hadn't planned, from the place where unauthorized thoughts lived — the truth. The unrehearsed truth that existed beneath the principles and the philosophies and the gambles and that said: you have been here before and before is the pattern and the pattern is the prediction and the prediction is the thing you're holding.

I drank my tea. Watched the stars. Watched the garden. Watched the settlement sleep.

Inside: the small star. His core. Glowing, twitching, three-four-three-four, irregular, bright, beautiful.

I stayed.

Morning would come. He would wake. He would stretch and grin and run to Lao Chen's and not look back.

And I would sit with the count and the trajectory and the thought I wouldn't finish.

And the night would end.

And begin again.
