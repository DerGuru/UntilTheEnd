# Chapter 61

Night.

No fire.

The clearing was dark. The forest was dark. The sky above the canopy — dark, starless, the clouds low enough that no light came through. The brown circle beneath me was invisible in that darkness. The death-radius, continuing unseen.

I sat. In the dark. Without fire.

The cold came first. Ground-cold. The kind that seeped through cloth and skin into the bone beneath, the kind that did not announce itself but accumulated, degree by degree, until the body began to shake.

My hands shook.

I pressed them flat against the dead earth. The soil was dry. Powdery. Nothing grew here. The texture was wrong — earth should hold moisture, should have the give of root systems beneath. This felt like ash.

Somewhere to the left, a branch cracked. Small. An animal — a night-bird settling, or something larger shifting weight in the undergrowth. The sound was loud in the silence. Loud because no one said *did you hear that* and no one reached for a weapon and no one whispered *just a fox, go back to sleep.*

No one expected fire now.

Wind moved through the clearing. Not strong — enough to shift the canopy above, enough to make the darkness move. Cold air against the back of my neck. I did not pull my collar up. I did not adjust.

I sat.

My body wanted fire. My hands knew the motions — gather, strip, arrange, strike. My fingers twitched against the dead soil.

I did not move.

Then — not a thought. An imprint. Not recalled but felt, the way a hand remembers the weight of a tool it has held for years.

He would have hated this.

Not the dark. Not the cold. The *sitting.* The posture of a person who had stopped performing the basic operations that survival required.

"What do you mean, you give up? Are you STUPID?"

Not a voice. An imprint. His shape.

"Get up."

The wind again. Colder now. My jaw was clenched — I noticed it the way one notices a cramp. The muscles tight, the teeth pressing together. My body had decided something before my mind caught up.

I stood.

The motion was stiff. My knees protested. The cold had settled into the joints, and standing was a negotiation between intent and muscle that the muscle nearly won. But I stood.

The clearing looked the same from above as from below — dark. The brown circle was still invisible. The trees were shapes without detail. Nothing had changed.

I stood in the dark and breathed. The air tasted of pine and wet stone and something else — something vegetal and decomposing, the forest floor doing what forest floors did. Breaking things down. Continuing.

An insect landed on my wrist. Small, barely a pressure. I felt it walk — six points of contact moving across the skin of my forearm. It did not die. Too small, maybe. Or too brief. It lifted off, and the absence of its weight was louder than its presence.

I did not light a fire.

But I was standing. And standing was not sitting. And the difference between the two was the choice that the imprint demanded, and the demand was: not this. Not you. Not like this.

Refusal.

Not hope. Not the word people used when the future seemed possible. Just — refusal. The refusal to give the night the satisfaction of keeping me on the ground.

The wind shifted. Somewhere east, water moved over rocks — I could hear it now that I was standing, now that my ears were higher than the undergrowth that had muffled it. Water, continuing. The forest, continuing. The insects, the wind, the slow decomposition of leaves — all of it continuing without permission or purpose.

I breathed.

Not tonight. But maybe.
