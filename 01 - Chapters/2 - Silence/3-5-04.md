# Chapter 40

"Then I'll train slower."

Morning. He stood in the doorway. Already dressed. Already decided. The grammar of someone who had received information, processed it overnight, and produced a solution.

"What?" I asked.

"You said too fast. So: slower."

He looked at me. The look of someone who believed in the relationship between problem and solution. Their belief was rational and linear and fourteen.

"That's not—"

"What?"

"—so simple."

"Why not?"

Because his core couldn't slow down. Because the acceleration wasn't from his training — it was from me. Because he'd been broken before he knew what breaking meant. Because the instability was structural, not behavioral, and structural problems didn't respond to behavioral solutions and behavioral solutions were the only solutions he knew how to apply.

"Train slower," I said.

Because what else. What else was I supposed to say. The truth — the full truth, the causal truth, the guilt-truth? He wouldn't understand the mechanism. And understanding the mechanism wouldn't change the mechanism. And changing the mechanism required an intervention that I could perform and that performing required acknowledging and acknowledging was the door I would not open.

He went outside. Trained. Slower. The deliberate deceleration of movements that wanted to be fast, the conscious restraint of a body whose default was full-speed. Their full-speed was being overridden by will. He moved through the forms at half-tempo, concentrated, his face set with the determination of someone who had identified the problem as velocity and velocity as the variable and the variable as controllable.

It wouldn't work. I knew this. The way I knew the progression and the spiral and the twitches. His training speed was not the cause. The cause was the core. The core was the clock. The clock was independent of the training.

But I watched him train slower and I said nothing more because saying more required the truth and the truth was the thing.

He finished. Stood. Wiped sweat. Looked at me for assessment.

"Better?" he asked.

"Yes," I lied.

He went back to the forms. Slower still. Even more controlled. The body of a boy who believed that discipline was the solution to everything. Their belief was the thing that made him Wei. Their Wei-ness was the thing and the thing was—

I stood at the edge. Watching.

The trigger should have fired.

A student. In danger. The configuration of: young, talented, trained by me, now facing the consequence of the training. The emotional-cognitive signature that had produced memories throughout everything since the mountain — reliably, predictably, the way tides produced, the way triggers produced the firing and the firing produced the image and the image produced the lesson and the lesson produced the sentence that began: *There was someone else who—*

Nothing came.

I waited. The way you wait for something you expect — the internal posture of readiness, the cognitive door held open for the arrival of the memory that would connect past to present and present to pattern and pattern to lesson.

Nothing.

Silence. In the space where memories lived. In the archive that had been accessible and reliable and that had provided, every time, the parallel. The echo. The other-person-who.

Empty.

No one. No face. No name. No era. No: *before, someone else, the same grin, the same symptoms, the same—*

Nothing.

Wei had no parallel. Or: I couldn't find one. Because he was different — the word I kept using, the word that was hope disguised as observation, the word that said: he will not follow the pattern because the pattern doesn't apply because he is the exception because the exception is the—

Or: I was too close. The proximity that had been the teaching tool and the loving instrument and the enabling catalyst was now the thing that obstructed the comparison. I couldn't see him clearly enough to compare him. He was too near. Too present. Too much the him that occupied the space where general patterns lived. Their occupation displaced the patterns and the displacement was the silence.

No memory came.

For the first time in this story: the archive was empty.

I stood. Watched him train. Slower. Concentrated. The forms at half-speed, the deliberate restraint that wouldn't work but that he believed would work. Their believing was the faith and the faith was the thing and the thing was him.

The silence where the memory should have been. The gap where the parallel should have been. The absence that was not nothing but something — the something of a record that had been searched and found empty. Their emptiness was the answer and the answer was: you have never been here before. Not exactly here. Not with exactly him.

He finished. Looked at me.

"Better?"

"Yes."

The second lie. Or the same lie repeated. The lie that said: your slower training is working. The lie that said: the solution you've identified is the correct solution. The lie that was the kindness and the kindness was the cruelty and the cruelty was the love and the love was the thing I had admitted to the stars and that the stars had received without comment and that the comment-less reception was the universe's position and the position was: indifferent.

He went inside. To eat. To rest. To be fourteen and slower and believing.

I stood. In the silence where the memory should have been. In the empty archive. In the space where the pattern had always been and was now not.

*He's different.*

The sentence that had been hope. That was now fact. That was now problem.

Different. With no parallel. With no roadmap. With no prior case to consult.

For the first time: I didn't know what would happen next.
