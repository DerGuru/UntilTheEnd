# Chapter 18

Night.

Wei slept. A boy with days full enough to produce exhaustion, and exhaustion was permission: rest, deep, total, the surrender that only safety allowed.

His core.

I felt it from the doorway. Before I crossed the room. Before I knelt. Two weeks ago, I'd felt it from a meter. A week ago, from two. Tonight: from the doorway. Four meters.

The range was expanding.

I crossed. Knelt. The nightly arrangement — my knees on the floor beside his bed, my hand hovering, the two-centimeter gap between contact and assessment. I don't touch. I measure.

Light. Through his shirt. Golden, warm, steady — the candle-behind-paper quality that was beautiful and was also the indicator of output exceeding containment. The body was fourteen. Fourteen was the vessel. The vessel was insufficient.

Four twitches per minute.

I counted. Automatic. Four contractions of qi-stability. Four moments where his core stuttered, flickered, reset. Four interruptions in smooth emission — indicating fatigue, indicating progression.

Four. Yesterday: three. The day before: three. Two weeks ago: two.

The arithmetic. Two, two, two, three, three, three, three, four. The slope: positive. Slow. Consistent. This was not random. This was progression, and progression has an endpoint.

I withdrew my hand.

"You're hovering again," Wei mumbled. Eyes closed. Not asleep — not quite.

"I'm checking."

"You check every night."

"Yes."

"And?"

"And go to sleep."

He turned onto his side. The glow dimmed slightly — side-sleeping compressed the broadcast, changed the angle. A half-measure. Like holding a candle sideways.

I went outside. The door — quiet, careful. The motive was love. Love was a word I did not use. The not-using was the defense.

Night air. Cool. The settlement sleeping — dark houses, dark paths, the occasional ember-glow of a fire banked for morning. Somewhere: a dog. Somewhere: a stream.

The qi-field.

I extended my perception. A hundred meters. Enough to feel the settlement's perimeter. Lao Chen, asleep, his qi like old iron — dull, stable, someone who had peaked decades ago and settled into peace. Frau Huang: bright, loud even in sleep. Shen: dim, muted.

I stopped.

The field. Beyond the settlement. Something.

Not a presence — not a person, not a cultivator standing in the dark with intent. Something subtler. The qi-equivalent of being watched — a shift in ambient pressure when attention was directed at a location. This settlement. The boy it contained.

Weeks ago — at the market — the attention had been directional. Three cultivators, three lines of interest converging on Wei. Navigation. Triangulation. People looking at a thing.

This was different. This was ambient. The field itself altered by the looking, the way a lake's surface changes not because something touched it but because something is watching it. The watching had weight.

Not me. I checked — the habit of millennia. My presence was suppressed, compressed, the signature of void. No. Not me.

Him.

The attention was on the boy. On the golden light broadcasting through cotton. On the signal that he was and that I could not stop him from being.

Before: I hid. They sought me. I moved. They lost me. Simple — predator and prey, and I was the better hider because hiding was ancient and ancient was better.

But if they sought him, then hiding was his problem. His solution. His skill. And his skill was: none. He had qi that screamed. He had a core that glowed through fabric. Bright and dangerous, and brightness was the signal.

I could hide myself. I could not hide him. Not without suppressing his qi permanently — and permanent suppression would stunt his growth. Damage was unacceptable.

I went back inside.

Stood over him. Sleeping. The glow softer now, the core cycling down. But still visible. Still broadcasting. Still the candle behind paper.

Four twitches.

I knelt. Pulled his blanket higher. The gesture — the same gesture, every night, the blanket and the tucking and the care that was physical because physical was the only language available when the other languages required saying the things.

"Yun." Barely a word. Eyes still closed.

"Yes."

"Something's out there. Isn't it."

Not a question. His breathing was still slow. But he knew. The way animals know weather — not through data, through the skin.

I pulled the blanket higher. "Go to sleep, Wei."

He slept.

Tomorrow he would wake. Would stretch, yawn, run to Lao Chen's. Would train, cook, exist at the velocity of fourteen. Would be bright. Would broadcast.

And the thing beyond the perimeter would still be watching.
