# Chapter 41

The air tasted like metal.

Brief — a flash of iron on the tongue, the ferric quality that lasted a fraction of a second and then didn't. Like tasting a memory of blood. Like the ghost-sensation of a flavor that existed at a frequency too brief for confirmation but too present for doubt.

I stood outside. Afternoon. The sun was warm and normal and the normal was irrelevant because beneath the normal: the tremor. Not earthquakes — not surface vibration, not the geological instability of the qi-source that Wei had repaired. Deeper. In the bones. The resonance that happened when something immensely powerful moved through a region and the region responded to the magnitude of the thing by vibrating at the structural level.

An Immortal-stage cultivator was moving into the valley.

The signature was not direct — at this distance, my perception caught the secondary effects rather than the presence itself. The displacement. The atmospheric response to mass, like water responding to a ship: not seeing the ship, seeing the wake. The wake was: metal taste, bone-tremor, the subtle-but-unmistakable quality of air that had been compressed by proximity to something that was orders of magnitude beyond anything the local environment contained.

I hadn't felt one in centuries. The rarity of this tier — the Immortal-and-beyond, the cultivators whose power existed not as strength but as weather, whose presence altered environments the way climate altered ecosystems. They moved rarely. They moved for reasons. Their reasons were not small.

I looked west. Saw: nothing. The mountains, the forest, the sky. The visible spectrum contained no evidence.

I felt: everything. The slow swell. Like an ocean rising — not a wave, not a storm. Just: more water. The gradual increase in ambient pressure that said: something is coming, something is here or nearly here, and the here-ness is the thing.

One sentence of evidence. The metal taste. Then: gone.

I went inside. Said nothing. The thing I was getting better at.

---

Evening. Dinner.

Wei sat at the table. He'd trained slower today — the deliberate, conscious deceleration that was his solution to the problem I'd half-described and that wasn't working because it couldn't work and the couldn't was the structure and the structure was the core and the core was the thing.

He was calm. Relaxed. The post-training settl of someone whose body had been used appropriately and was now requesting fuel.

Rice. The bowl in front of him. Chopsticks in his left hand — he was left-handed for eating, right-handed for fighting, the bilateral arrangement that I'd never corrected because bilateral was efficient.

He reached.

The bowl. His right hand — the non-chopstick hand. Grip the ceramic. Hold it steady. Simple.

His hand went through the bowl.

Not physically through — the grip didn't engage. His fingers closed on the shape of holding. But the holding produced nothing. As though something between his wanting and his hand had gone hollow for a fraction of a second — the fingers being there but not being his, the cold gap between reaching and having.

The bowl tipped. Rice hit the floor. The sound of ceramic on wood followed by the scatter of grains across surface.

Silence.

Wei stared at his hand. Open. He closed it. Opened it. Closed it. Testing. The hand worked now. That made the not-working worse.

"Just tired."

Fifth time. The phrase had lost its meaning. What remained was the sound — hollow, practiced, the shape a mouth made when the truth wouldn't fit.

I continued eating. My chopsticks: stable. My hands: steady. The steadiness was the contrast and the contrast was sitting across from me with rice on the floor.

He picked up the bowl. Scooped the rice back in — most of it. The practicality of someone who didn't waste food because food had once been scarce.

He ate. With his left hand. The right hand: in his lap. Hidden.

I ate. Across from him. With my stable hands and my full information and my silence.

The taste of metal was gone. The bone-tremor: continuing, barely felt, the background vibration of something massive moving into position. The Immortal-stage presence settling into the region the way weather settled — slowly, imperceptibly, altering the conditions without announcing the alteration.

Two threats. External: the sect, the signatures multiplying at the tree line. Internal: his core, his hand, the bowl on the floor.

I reached for it — the memory. The name, the face, the other one who had—

Gone. The thought ended at the edge of something smooth and blank. Cold pooled at the base of my skull. Vertigo, brief. The taste of metal again, or the ghost of it.

After grip-failure—

Nothing. The thought ended. I let it end.

I stopped eating. Put my chopsticks down. Neatly. Aligned.

"More rice?" I asked.

He looked at me. Across the table. His right hand still in his lap.

"Yeah."

I served him. From the pot. Into the bowl his hand had dropped. The rice: warm, fragrant, normal. The act: the thing I could do when the other things were counting and silence.

He ate. With his left hand. His right hand: in his lap.

I sat across from him and held my chopsticks and held the silence and held the bone-tremor and the sound of the word — *tired* — becoming hollow.
