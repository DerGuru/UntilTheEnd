# Chapter 12

It happened in the transition.

They were still at the sword stall — Wei and Shen Liang, thirty minutes deep into the kind of conversation that boys had with mentors who weren't their mentors.

Wei shifted. Demonstrated something — a stance, the opening position of a form. He moved into it efficiently, without preamble, with the economy that came from repetition.

And Shen Liang said it.

Casual. Almost offhand. As though it had just occurred to him, which it hadn't — nothing that Shen Liang said just occurred to him.

"You correct your hip on the transition step. Almost no one does that without a teacher."

Wei stopped.

The sword vendor paused mid-polish on a polearm. A customer at the next stall turned her head. The sentence had landed in the ambient noise of the market the way a stone lands in still water — small, but with rings.

I watched from the herb stall. Chrysanthemum in my hand. My hand: steady. My chest: not.

The hip correction. The subtle, nearly invisible adjustment that Wei made between the third and fourth positions — the micro-rotation of the pelvis that aligned the strike vector with the spine. I had never explicitly taught it. My method was demonstration; his was observation. The result was a correction so subtle that I hadn't been sure he'd absorbed it until three months ago when I'd seen it and hadn't mentioned it.

Shen Liang had seen it. In five minutes. In a market. He had identified a nuance that existed in the space between instruction and absorption, that was invisible to everyone except people who knew exactly what to look for, and he had named it.

"I—" Wei started. Stopped. Started again. "I didn't know I did that."

"It's instinct. A good one." Shen Liang's smile was warm — genuinely, or the simulation of genuinely that was indistinguishable from the real thing. "Whoever taught you — your acquaintance — they move well. And you learned how they move."

"Yeah." Quiet.

A donkey pulling a vegetable cart wedged itself between the sword stall and the fabric vendor. The driver shouted. The donkey didn't care. Shen Liang stepped aside smoothly — the fluid economy of someone whose body was always calculating space. Wei hopped back onto a crate, then down again. The interruption passed.

Shen Liang picked up exactly where he'd left off. No lost thread. No wasted beat.

"Your talent is wasted here." He said it simply. Without pressure. The absence of pressure that was itself a form of pressure — truth stated calmly being more persuasive than urgently. "The Iron Lotus has masters who specialize in blade forms. Precision work. The kind of thing that turns instinct into technique."

"I'm not—" Wei hesitated.

"Think about it." Shen Liang stepped back. Easy. Giving space, which was the thing pressureless selling provided. "I'm at the sect gate most mornings. If you want to see the training grounds."

He left. Clean. No lingering. The departure of someone who understood that the best hook was the one you let the fish approach.

Wei stood. Alone at the sword stall. The vendor: had been watching, understood nothing, returned to his blades.

The herb vendor beside me held up a bundle of dried something. "Your boy?"

"Acquaintance," I said.

She gave me the look that all market women gave when someone said something obviously wrong. I bought the chrysanthemum. She overcharged. I let her.

I crossed the aisle. Stood beside him.

He looked at me. His face — the face of someone who had just been seen by a new pair of eyes.

"Ready to go?" I asked.

"Yeah." A beat. "He was — nice."

"Was he?"

"You don't think so?"

"I didn't talk to him."

"You were listening."

True. And he knew it. And I knew he knew it. The layered awareness that was intimacy by another name and that worked because neither of us named it.

We walked home. Him three steps ahead. The chrysanthemum — purchased, unnecessary, unwanted — in my bag.

On the path, Wei demonstrated the transition step. Third to fourth position. He did it three times. The hip correction — there, visible, perfect each time.

"Look," he said. "I do correct my hip."

I looked.

"You always have," I said.

He grinned. The grin that said: someone noticed. The grin that didn't say, but that I heard: someone else noticed first.

The path home was two hours. Neither of us spoke for the first thirty minutes. Neither of us needed to. The silence was the space where things settled — his discovery, my inventory, his excitement, my count. Three cultivators. One recruiter. One hip correction.

One boy, broadcast.
