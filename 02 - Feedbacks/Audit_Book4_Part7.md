# Audit: Book 4 "Echoes" — Part 7 (Resolution)

**Auditor:** Senior Editor
**Date:** 2026-02-13
**Files Reviewed:** 4-7-01.md through 4-7-07.md
**Reference Docs:** Writing Rules.md (41 rules), StyleDNA.md, 4-7-Szenen.md

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Part 7 is structurally sound — all major beats land, the ending works emotionally, and the book closes with genuine directional energy. **But the prose is in crisis.** Three systemic defects saturate every chapter:

1. **"specific" tic: 72 occurrences** (+ 1 "particular" = 73 total). This is the WORST count in the entire series so far (P1=69, P2=159, P3=129, P4=102, P5=164, P6=83). For a Part that's only ~10k words, that's approximately 1 "specific" every 137 words. The word has become a nervous filler — a crutch the prose leans on instead of trusting its descriptions.

2. **POV catastrophe.** 1st person narration ("I") switches to 3rd person ("She") approximately **99 times** across 7 chapters. This is not occasional drift — it is a systematic fracture of the narrative frame. Yun tells her story in "I", then the text describes her in "She" within the same paragraph, sometimes the same sentence. This is the worst POV violation in the series.

3. **Post-show analytical cascades.** Every shown moment is immediately followed by 1-4 paragraphs of clinical dissection: anatomy terms (orbicularis oculi, zygomatic activation, saccadic instability, tympanic membrane), pseudo-scientific labeling ("the specific volitional quality of someone who moved food closer"), and colon-predicate constructions ("the X was: Y"). The prose shows — then tells you what it showed — then labels the telling.

## PART-LEVEL PATTERN SUMMARY

### A. The "specific" Pandemic
**Total: 72 "specific" + 1 "particular" = 73 across Part 7.**
Per-chapter: 12, 10, 10, 9, 12, 10, 9. Remarkably uniform distribution — the tic is structural, not occasional. Nearly every paragraph contains one. The word has replaced actual description: instead of writing what makes something distinctive, the text labels it "specific" and moves on.

### B. The POV Fracture
**~99 instances of "She" referring to Yun in a 1st-person narration.** This is not stylistic choice — it's a consistent failure to maintain the POV. Some chapters (4-7-03, 4-7-04) are majority 3rd-person. The I→She oscillation creates a dissociative quality that might be thematically appropriate (Yun is "abgeklemmt"), but it's never framed that way. It reads as a drafting error, not a narrative strategy.

### C. The Analytical Cascade
The prose follows a consistent pattern:
1. **Show** (physical detail, gesture, dialog) — usually 1-2 sentences, often excellent
2. **Annotate** (clinical description of what was shown) — 1-2 sentences with medical/scientific terminology
3. **Label** (colon-predicate construction: "The X was: Y") — 1 sentence
4. **Interpret** (paragraph explaining the significance) — 2-4 sentences

This 4-step cascade appears 30-40 times across the Part. It triples the word count and halves the impact. The showing is almost always good enough to stand alone.

### D. The Colon-Predicate Construction ("was: Y")
Extremely frequent across all chapters: "was: mechanical", "was: hers", "was: different", "was: data", "was: there", "was: simply there", "was: comfort", "was: enough". This construction is an affectation that distances the reader from the moment. Used once per chapter, it's a stylistic signature. Used 5-8 times per chapter, it's a compulsion.

### E. Modern Register Contamination
Despite Rule 41 prohibiting modern terminology:
- **substrate**: ~14 occurrences (established as Xu Ran's word — somewhat defensible, but overused)
- **data/data point**: 5 occurrences
- **mechanism**: 2
- **system** (cognitive system, vestibular system, memory system): 4
- **systematic**: 1
- Medical jargon: saccadic, vestibular, subcutaneous, hemorrhage, ambulatory, orbicularis oculi, zygomatic, tympanic membrane, chromatic, nomenclature, cognitive (~12 terms)
- Signal processing: "interference pattern", "signal", "static"

**Total modern register violations: ~38**
