# Royal Road Review — *Until the End*

**Reviewed at:** Chapter 293 (Book 5, Chapter 38)  
**Overall:** ★★★★★ (5/5)  
**Style:** ★★★★★  
**Story:** ★★★★½  
**Grammar:** ★★★★★  
**Character:** ★★★★★  

---

**Attention: this will contain spoilers for the first five books, but I will try to keep them vague enough that they won't ruin the experience if you decide to read.**

Alright. I need to be honest with you. I started this expecting another OP MC cultivation power fantasy — you know the type. Ancient cultivator wakes up, levels up fast, face-slaps young masters, rinse repeat for 3000 chapters. The synopsis sounded like it could go either way.

This is not that. This is something else entirely, and I'm still not sure the genre tag "xianxia" actually covers it.

Instead it is a first-person narrative told by Yun, a female cultivator so absurdly powerful and ancient that she existed before written language. She finds a starving twelve-year-old kid named Wei in a forest, saves him from a spirit boar she kills with a flick, and then spends the next chapters pretending she doesn't care about him while very obviously caring about him. That's Book 1. 

The tension in all of these books is *never* "can the MC win the fight." She can delete anything. The tension is always "will she act" and "at what cost." That shouldn't work for as many chapters as it does, but it works because the writing is just that good. Every chapter where Yun tells herself "not my problem" while very clearly making it her problem earns the reader's trust that the author knows what he's doing.

**Style — 5 stars, and I don't give these away.**

The prose in this story is operating on a different level from anything else I've read on RR. Yun's voice is dry, precise, ancient, and occasionally funny in a way that sneaks up on you. Lines like "The basket, wisely, did not argue" had me grinning. Lines like "I watched a man smile through blood because his son was watching" had me staring at my ceiling. The writing *trusts* the reader constantly — no info dumps, no "as you know" exposition, no hand-holding. The cultivation system, the worldbuilding, the power levels — all delivered through Yun's ancient boredom with it. It feels organic. It feels real.

There's also this thing where Yun occasionally addresses a mysterious "you" — not the reader exactly, but someone. It adds this layer of intimacy and mystery that I haven't seen anyone attempt in web fiction, let alone pull off.

One warning: if you need action every three chapters, this story will test you. Especially in Book 1's middle section (the herb-sorting training arc) and Book 4's opening (the grief arc). The payoff is always there, but you have to trust the process. I did. It was worth it every single time.

**Story — 4.5 stars.**

Here's where I need to be fair. The *overall* story arc across five books is phenomenal. It goes: meeting → bond → road → loss → grief → investigation → existential horror. Each book escalates what the story is *about* without feeling like a genre switch. Book 1 is a warm, quiet mentor story. Book 3 kills the student. Book 4 is a grief novel. Book 5 is borderline cosmic horror where the MC discovers she might be the thing destroying the world around her. That progression is masterful.

But.

There are stretches — particularly in the travel sections of Book 2 (walk, camp, train, threat, walk) and the early investigation sites in Book 5 (shrine, library, cave, observatory — same template four times) — where the structural repetition is noticeable. Each individual scene is beautifully written, but when you're reading chapter-by-chapter on a serial schedule, the fourth variation of "arrive at ruin, taste chalk, find old inscription, find same ending" tests your patience a little.

Also: Yun keeps secrets from Wei for the entire duration of his life. She never tells him that her proximity is what's destroying his core. He dies not knowing. That's a bold, devastating choice — and it works thematically. But I'd be lying if I said there weren't multiple chapters where I wanted to reach through the screen and shake her. The narrative *knows* this is frustrating (Yun literally asks herself the same question), but knowing it and resolving it are different things. Book 3's ending hits you like a freight train partly *because* of this frustration. So... it works? But it cost me half a star because reading chapters 32 through 71 of Book 2 while screaming "JUST TELL HIM" in my head was genuinely agonizing in a way that wasn't always the fun kind.

**Grammar — 5 stars.**

Near-flawless. I caught a handful of typos across almost 300 chapters ("sice" for "since," "fife" for "five," "Wie" for a pronoun). That's an insane error rate for a web serial. The sentence structure is varied, controlled, and clearly deliberate. Someone who knows their craft wrote this.

**Character — 5 stars. This is where the story lives and dies, and it lives.**

**Yun** is the best first-person narrator I've encountered in web fiction. Full stop. She's ancient, powerful beyond comprehension, detached, occasionally funny, deeply lonely, and in massive denial about caring. Her internal contradictions are the engine of the entire series. The way she says "not my problem" with decreasing conviction over 66 chapters in Book 1 is character work that most published novels don't achieve. By Book 5, watching her discover that her own mind is being edited in real time — that she can understand something, nod, say "yes," and then lose the comprehension while Xu Ran watches — is genuine psychological horror. She never becomes a different person. She becomes *more* of who she already was, which is harder to write.

**Wei.** Oh, Wei. A twelve-year-old with no cultivation talent, too loud, too stubborn, too kind. He collects the wrong mushrooms. He tells his lynx-fight story with escalating exaggeration. He runs toward pain. He doesn't live past Book 3. And I am wrecked. The thing about Wei is that his death *earns* the devastation. He doesn't die for shock value. He dies being exactly who he is — pushing a deaf kid out of a kill-box, standing in open ground, his core breaking because the woman who loves him is the reason it was unstable in the first place. The congee chapter in Book 1 (his mother walking up the slope with thin rice porridge, the ginger floating like "small, pale moons") is still the single chapter that hits me hardest. The kid was a star.

**Xu Ran.** Started as a background element — the broken genius from the tribulation in Book 1. Became a window into the outside world in Book 2. Became something incredible in Books 4-5. He's Yun's external memory, her methodology, and eventually the person who asks her the cruelest, most necessary question anyone has ever asked her: "Have you killed Wei?" The moment he cuts his own finger after the field incident and says "I'd like to keep the proof" — refusing to let the immortal undo his damage — that's when I knew this character was special.

**What I'm waiting for (the tension at chapter 38 that keeps me reading):**

- What the hell is the substrate? Something ancient living inside Yun that won't let her die and actively erases her understanding of itself? That's terrifying.
- Lian Zhuo (the Pencil) is sitting out there somewhere with data on a reality-locking field and a plan. That man is going to cause problems.
- The "Have you killed Wei?" moment cracked the wall for one second and Yun saw *everything* — then it was erased. But something survived. A feeling. A frequency. She can feel cold now. She asked for a robe. For the first time in millennia. Where does this go?
- The Hermit Master pulled Yun's perception at the exact moment Wei died. That's still unexplained. I have theories and none of them are comforting.
- Ten past cultivators with Yun's condition. Ten past companion-scribes. All ended. Xu Ran is the eleventh. I am stressed about this.

**Bottom line:**

This is not a typical cultivation novel. If you want power fantasies, tournament arcs, and face-slapping, this is not your story. If you want character work that will ruin your week, prose that reads like literary fiction wearing xianxia clothes, a mystery that deepens with every book, and an emotional gut-punch roughly every 15 chapters, this is the best thing on this platform.

I'm a guy who reads maybe 30-40 web novels a year. Most of them I enjoy and forget. This one I think about when I'm doing dishes. The congee. The lemongrass. The three deletions. "I'm cold." Two words that contain four thousand years of isolation cracking open.

Read it. Be patient with it. Let it build. It will destroy you, and you will thank the author for it.
